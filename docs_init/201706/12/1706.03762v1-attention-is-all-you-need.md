## Original Abstract
The dominant sequence transduction models are based on complex recurrent or convolutional neural networks in an encoder-decoder configuration. The best performing models also connect the encoder and decoder through an attention mechanism. We propose a new simple network architecture, the Transformer, based solely on attention mechanisms, dispensing with recurrence and convolutions entirely. Experiments on two machine translation tasks show these models to be superior in quality while being more parallelizable and requiring significantly less time to train. Our model achieves 28.4 BLEU on the WMT 2014 English-to-German translation task, improving over the existing best results, including ensembles by over 2 BLEU. On the WMT 2014 English-to-French translation task, our model establishes a new single-model state-of-the-art BLEU score of 41.0 after training for 3.5 days on eight GPUs, a small fraction of the training costs of the best models from the literature. We show that the Transformer generalizes well to other tasks by applying it successfully to English constituency parsing both with large and limited training data.

### 1. Core problem and overall meaning (motivation and background)
*   **Core problem**: Traditional sequence transduction models (such as machine translation) rely mainly on recurrent neural networks (RNN/LSTM) or convolutional neural networks (CNN).
    *   **RNN limitations**: inherently sequential computation that cannot be parallelized, leading to low training efficiency on long sequences, and difficulty capturing long-range dependencies.
    *   **CNN limitations**: parallelizable, but building long-range dependencies requires stacking many layers, with compute cost growing with distance.
*   **Overall meaning**: The paper proposes a new architecture that completely discards recurrence and convolution and relies only on **self-attention**. The goal is to improve translation quality while achieving high parallelism and significantly shorter training time.

### 2. Methodology: core ideas and key techniques
The Transformer uses the classic encoder-decoder structure, with core components:
*   **Scaled Dot-Product Attention**:
    *   Weights are computed from the dot product of Query and Key, scaled by $\sqrt{d_k}$ to prevent vanishing gradients, and finally applied to the Value.
*   **Multi-Head Attention**:
    *   The core idea is to split the model into multiple "heads" that learn information in different subspaces in parallel, then concatenate the results. This lets the model attend to information from different positions and representation dimensions simultaneously.
*   **Positional Encoding**:
    *   Since the model has no recurrence, the authors encode positions using sine and cosine functions at different frequencies and add them to the input embeddings to inject order information.
*   **Feed-Forward Network (FFN)**:
    *   Each layer contains a fully connected feed-forward network applied independently and identically at each position.
*   **Residual connections and layer normalization**:
    *   Each sub-layer uses a residual connection followed by layer normalization to keep training stable in deep networks.

### 7. Strengths
*   **Highly parallelizable**: breaks the sequential constraint of RNNs and greatly improves computational efficiency.
*   **Long-range dependency modeling**: self-attention makes the "path length" between any two positions a constant $O(1)$, effectively addressing long-text modeling.

### 8. Limitations
*   **Computational complexity**: self-attention scales quadratically with sequence length $n$ ($O(n^2)$), creating heavy memory pressure for very long inputs (such as whole books or high-resolution images).
*   **Risk of losing local information**: although multi-head attention mitigates this, the inductive bias toward local structure (such as neighboring phrases) is weaker than in CNNs.
*   **Inference cost**: at the generation stage (decoder), autoregressive generation means inference speed is still limited by sequence length.
