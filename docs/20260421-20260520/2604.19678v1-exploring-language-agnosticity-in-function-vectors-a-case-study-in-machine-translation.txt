# Exploring Language-Agnosticity in Function Vectors: A Case Study in Machine Translation

Abstract 

Function vectors (FVs) are vector representations of tasks extracted from model activations during in-context learning. While prior work has shown that multilingual model representations can be language-agnostic, it re-mains unclear whether the same holds for function vectors. We study whether FVs exhibit language-agnosticity, using machine translation as a case study. Across three decoder-only multilingual LLMs, we find that translation FVs extracted from a single English →X direction transfer to other target languages, consistently improving the rank of correct trans-lation tokens across multiple unseen languages. Ablation results show that removing the FV degrades translation across languages with limited impact on unrelated tasks. We further show that base-model FVs transfer to instruction-tuned variants and partially generalize from word-level to sentence-level translation. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have become central to multilingual NLP, showing strong performance across a wide range of cross-lingual and multilingual tasks (Brown et al., 2020). A growing body of mechanistic work has begun to ask how these models represent and process information across languages (Resck et al., 2025; Ferrando et al., 2024). One recurring finding is that many internal representations are language-agnostic : they encode task-relevant content, such as semantic meaning, independent of language identity, so that similar concepts in different languages are mapped to nearby states in activation space. This property has been documented at several levels of abstraction. At the representation level, work on multilingual BERT showed that substantial cross-lingual alignment can emerge without explicit parallel supervision, enabling zero-shot transfer across languages and scripts (Pires et al., 2019; Wu & Dredze, 2019). More recent studies on autoregres-sive LLMs have confirmed that concept representations can be largely language-agnostic, with intermediate activations partially separating “what is being said” from “in which language” it is expressed (Wendler et al., 2024; Dumas et al., 2025). Beyond static repre-sentations, language-agnosticity has also been observed at the level of grammatical con-cepts (Brinkmann et al., 2025) and at the level of circuits where prior work suggests that some internal mechanisms are shared across languages (Ferrando & Costa-jussà, 2024; Zhang et al., 2025b). A natural next step is to ask whether this language-agnosticity also holds for task-level representations such as function vectors. Function vectors (Todd et al., 2024) offer a direct way to test this: they are compact task representations, extracted from a few in-context demonstrations, that can be added to the residual stream to induce a target behavior. 2 Related work 

Multilingual language models. Multilingual language models are trained on text from many languages, often with deliberate choices about how training data are distributed across them. Some models use relatively balanced multilingual corpora in order to support cross-lingual transfer, including mT5 (Xue et al., 2021), and Aya (Üstün et al., 2024). In contrast, models such as Llama-3 (Grattafiori et al., 2024) and Gemma-2 (Rivière et al., 2024) are trained on heavily English-dominant data, yet still exhibit strong multilingual capabilities. These different pretraining choices are often assumed to affect the emergence of multilingual and cross-lingual representations (Conneau et al., 2020; Pires et al., 2019; Blevins et al., 2022). 

Language-agnosticity in multilingual LLMs. Dumas et al. (2025) provide causal evidence for language-agnostic concept representations in decoder-only LLMs using activation patch-ing in word-to-word translation. They show that concept and language information can be manipulated independently, and that averaging concept representations across languages preserves or even improves translation performance, suggesting a shared conceptual space rather than fully language-specific encodings. 2Preprint These findings extend beyond lexical semantics to grammatical structure. Brinkmann et al. (2025) use sparse autoencoders to study morphosyntactic concepts and show that properties such as number, gender, and tense are often encoded in feature directions shared across many typologically diverse languages. Shared structure has also been identified at the circuit level. Ferrando & Costa-jussà (2024) analyze subject–verb agreement in English and Spanish and find that the two languages rely on highly similar circuits, including a language-independent “subject number” direction in the residual stream that transfers causally across languages through activation steering. Fierro et al. (2025) show that some recall mechanisms generalize across languages while others remain language-dependent. In decoder-only multilingual LLMs, the last-token representation behaves like a function vector that first encodes the relation to be retrieved and later composes in the language, whereas the final extraction step remains language-specific. 

Interpretability of machine translation in LLMs. Earlier work on understanding transla-tion in neural models focused on observational analyses such as neuron activation patterns and representation probing (Mu et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Wendler et al., 2024). More recent studies have moved toward causal, circuit-level analyses that identify the specific components responsible for translation behavior. Zhang et al. (2025a) study word-level translation in decoder-only LLMs using subspace-intervened path patching. They find that translation behavior depends on a sparse subset of components, with attention heads and MLPs playing distinct functional roles. They further show that fine-tuning these translation-relevant components can improve translation performance while largely preserving general capabilities. Lasnier et al. (2026) extend this line of work to sentence-level few-shot MT. Using activation patching on clean and corrupted prompts, they decompose MT into two separable subtasks: target language identification and sentence equivalence. They find that these subtasks are mediated by distinct sparse sets of attention heads across many transla-tion directions. Based on these heads, they construct steering vectors for instruction-free MT and use ablations to show that the identified components are causally important.

4 Methodology 

4.1 Extracting FV for Machine Translation 

To clarify how the general FV framework applies to translation, we now detail the structure of the ICL prompts and the corruption scheme specific to word-level machine translation. 3Preprint Each ICL prompt pti consists of N demonstration pairs followed by a single query word: 

pti = (xi1, yi1), (xi2, yi2), . . . , (xiN , yiN ), xiq 

,where each xik is a word in the source language (e.g., English) and yik is its translation in the target language (e.g., French). For instance, consider an English →French prompt of the following form: 

Q: eggs\nA: oeufs\n\nQ: good\nA: bon\n\nQ: sugar\nA: sucre\n\nQ: loan\nA: 

Given this prompt, the model must infer the translation mapping from the demonstrated pairs and produce prêt , the correct French translation of the query word loan . The correct answer token y used in the FV scoring procedure is the gold target token. 1

To form the corrupted prompts ˜p ∈ ˜Pt, the source-side words and the query remain fixed while the target words {yi1, . . . , yiN } are randomly permuted across the N demonstrations, breaking the source–target mapping. The FV extraction then proceeds as described in Section 3. 

4.2 Models and Data 

We focus on decoder-only autoregressive LLMs and evaluate three models: Gemma-2-2B (Rivière et al., 2024), Llama-3.2-3B (Grattafiori et al., 2024; Meta, 2024), and Tiny Aya (Üstün et al., 2024; Cohere, 2026). We choose these models because they provide multilingual coverage at small parameter scales, which enables systematic FV extraction and intervention sweeps within our computational budget. We use the English–French, English–German, and English–Spanish word-pair datasets from Todd et al. (2024) to compute the translation FVs, with 4705, 5154, and 5200 pairs, respectively.

A natural follow-up question is why a single-direction FV promotes translations across languages. One explanation is that the FV relies on attention heads that are themselves shared across translation directions. To test this, we compare the top-ranked heads selected by the FV scoring procedure across several English →X translation FVs. We quantify overlap among the top-10 FV-ranked heads across directions, reporting both pairwise overlap and the number of heads shared across all directions. Across models, the three translation FVs show strong overlap in their top-ranked heads, with 6 to 8 of the top 10 heads shared across all three FVs, depending on the model. This suggests that part of the FV-associated head set is shared across directions. This finding is consistent with concurrent work that has reported overlap of translation-relevant heads across directions using causal analyses outside the FV framework (Zhang et al., 2025a).

5.4 Sentence-level FV intervention 

Our main analyses use word-level prompts because they provide a controlled setting for measuring token-level effects. A natural question is whether the same FV intervention car-ries over to sentence-to-sentence translation; we therefore conduct an additional evaluation in this setting. To test this, we sample 150 English source sentences from FLORES-200 (Costa-jussà et al., 2022) and apply the translation FV during generation. During the initial forward pass, the FV is added to the residual stream at all input positions; during autoregressive decoding, it is added only at the newly generated position at each step. Because our goal is to evaluate target-agnostic translation effects, the output language is not fixed in advance. Instead, we use GPT-4o to identify the language of each generated output post hoc, align it with the corresponding FLORES-200 reference in the detected language, and evaluate translation quality using BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and XCOMET (Guerreiro et al., 2024). 

Figure 7: Sentence-level translation performance for Llama-3.2-3B. Left: XCOMET scores. Right: BLEU scores. Figure 7 (left) suggests that sentence-level FV intervention transfers only partially from the controlled word-level setting. In Figure 7, the XCOMET distributions for all three conditions are concentrated toward the high end of the scale, indicating that many outputs preserve substantial semantic adequacy. The instruction-prompting baseline shows the strongest concentration near the top of the distribution, while both FV-based conditions exhibit a broader spread and a heavier lower-score tail. This suggests that the translation FV can often steer the model toward semantically valid translation behavior at the sentence level, but less consistently than explicit instruction prompting. A similar pattern appears in Figure 7 (right), where BLEU scores are spread across wider range and heavily concentrated near low values for all methods. This indicates that even when the generated output is semantically close to the reference, it often differs in surface form from the reference translation. We believe this gap between XCOMET and BLEU is expected in our setup: the target language is not fixed beforehand, outputs are matched post hoc based on language detection, and sentence-level translations may vary substantially in wording while remaining semantically correct. Under these conditions, BLEU provides a stricter view of reference overlap, whereas XCOMET better captures whether the intended meaning was preserved. Overall, these results show that the translation FV retains some target-agnostic translation effect beyond isolated word prompts, but the effect is weaker and less reliable in full sentence generation. This is consistent with the greater difficulty of sentence-level translation, which requires not only lexical mapping but also fluent generation, reordering, and longer-range compositional planning. 

6 Conclusions 

We investigated whether function vectors exhibit language-agnosticity, using machine trans-lation as a case study. Across three multilingual LLMs, we found that translation FVs 8Preprint extracted from a single English →X direction consistently promote correct translations into multiple target languages, with effects concentrated in a shared band of middle layers. Ab-lating the FV degrades translation across languages while leaving general task performance intact, confirming that the FV captures a translation-specific subspace. The cross-lingual effect is further supported by substantial overlap in the top-ranked FV heads across transla-tion directions. Beyond word-level experiments, we showed that base-model FVs transfer to instruction-tuned models and partially generalize to sentence-level translation, suggest-ing that the captured translation computation is robust beyond its extraction conditions. Together, these results extend the language-agnosticity finding, previously documented for representations, grammatical concepts, and circuits, to function vectors.
