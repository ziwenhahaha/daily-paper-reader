## Abstract 

Diffusion large language models (dLLMs) have emerged as a compelling alternative to autore-gressive (AR) LLMs, owing to their capacity for parallel token generation. This paradigm is par-ticularly well-suited for code generation, where holistic structural planning and non-sequential re-finement are critical. Despite this potential, tailor-ing dLLMs for CUDA kernel generation remains challenging, obstructed not only by the high spe-cialization but also by the severe lack of high-quality training data. To address these challenges, we construct CuKe , an augmented supervised fine-tuning dataset optimized for high-performance CU DA ke rnels. On top of it, we propose a bi -phase curated reinforcement l earning ( BiC-RL )framework consisting of a CUDA kernel infilling stage and an end-to-end CUDA kernel generation stage. Leveraging this training framework, we introduce DICE , a series of di ffusion large lan-guage models designed for CUDA k e rnel genera-tion, spanning three parameter scales, 1.7B, 4B, and 8B. Extensive experiments on KernelBench demonstrate that DICE significantly outperforms both autoregressive and diffusion LLMs of com-parable scale, establishing a new state-of-the-art for CUDA kernel generation. 

## 1. Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have shown powerful abil-ity in a range of language tasks, and the dominant paradigm for LLMs is based on autoregressive modeling (Achiam et al., 2023; Grattafiori et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025; Comanici et al., 2025) for a long time. Recently, diffusion large language models (dLLMs) (Nie et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025; Xie et al., 2025b;

Peng et al., 2025) have emerged as a compelling alternative, achieving comparable or even better performance than simi-larly sized AR LLMs and faster inference speed. Different from the token-by-token generation mechanism introduced by AR LLMs, dLLMs mask the generated sequence and then refine the sequence through iterative denoising steps, which can account for global information and provide re-markably fast inference speed (DeepMind, 2025; Labs et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025). Although dLLMs are rarely designed explicitly for code generation, their native bidirectional attention and iterative refinement paradigm align well with the inherently non-sequential nature of code generation (Gong et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025d; Ni et al., 2025b), which often demands re-peated back-and-forth refinement (Xie et al., 2025a). Com-mercial dLLMs, such as Gemini Diffusion (DeepMind, 2025), Mercury Coder (Labs et al., 2025), and Seed Diffu-sion (Song et al., 2025), have demonstrated that diffusion-based code models not only match AR LLMs in accuracy across a wide range of standard code evaluation benchmarks, but also deliver substantially faster inference speed. Despite the remarkable abilities shown by open-source dLLMs designed for code generation (Gong et al., 2025; Xie et al., 2025b; Peng et al., 2025), the specialized and computationally intensive task of kernel generation remains underexplored in the dLLM field, in stark contrast to the fruitful explorations on AR LLMs (Ouyang et al., 2025; Baronio et al., 2025; Fisches et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025c). Recent studies (Ni et al., 2025a; Prabhudesai et al., 2025) demonstrate the superior data efficiency of dLLMs, espe-cially under data-bound conditions. Given the scarcity of high-quality kernel data (Kong et al., 2025), dLLMs are uniquely positioned to leverage their architectural advan-tages for this task. Aiming to bridge the gap, this paper introduces DICE , a series of dLLMs tailored for CUDA kernel generation, spanning parameter sizes of 1.7B, 4B, and 8B. ## 3. CuKe Dataset Construction 

To address the scarcity of high-performance data in the field of CUDA kernel generation, we introduce CuKe, an augmented dataset that is substantially improved on top of the ConCuR dataset (Kong et al., 2025). We enhance the original dataset from the following two dimensions: 

High-Performance Sample. While the SFT dataset of cudaLLM 2 provides numerous PyTorch-CUDA pairs, we notice that only 86% exhibit a speedup over their PyTorch equivalents. Furthermore, speedups near 1.0× are often statistically unreliable in GPU environments, as they can be confounded by pseudo-speedup factors such as kernel launch overhead, cache states, or measurement jitter. To ensure that our model learns CUDA kernels with the actual speedup effect, we apply a strict 2.0× speedup threshold as a robust filter. This filtration process guarantees that every data pair represents a substantial and verifiable performance improvement over standard PyTorch implementations. The application of this acceleration threshold yields a refined set of 1,425 high-quality PyTorch-CUDA pairs, ensuring that the model is aligned with truly optimized kernels rather than marginal or statistically noisy improvements. 

Structural Diversity. Beyond isolated operations, we argue that kernel generation should target the core components of AI systems. To this end, we incorporate complex structures from mainstream LLMs, such as Attention submodules and MLP blocks. To enhance the adaptability of models, we vary the input dimensions for these structures, as the un-derlying CUDA optimization logic often shifts significantly with tensor shapes. We curate 291 such structural samples and utilize Mercury Coder (Labs et al., 2025) to generate optimized CUDA kernels. Each candidate is executed up to five times, and only those demonstrating a confirmed speedup are retained. This validation yields 36 functionally verified instances that achieved demonstrable acceleration. Combining the samples from these two diverse dimensions results in a final, high-quality training set of 6,303 samples.

4.1.2. B IC-RL To further extend the capabilities of DICE in CUDA kernel generation and mitigate the deceptive behavior, we propose a bi-phase curated reinforcement learning framework built upon TraceRL (Wang et al., 2025). This framework operates in two dimensions. In the data dimension, we leverage Mercury Coder (Labs et al., 2025) to categorize the training data into hierarchical difficulty levels, progressing from fundamental operations to complex model structures. In the training dimension, the reinforcement learning process is split into two sequential stages: an initial CUDA kernel infilling stage, followed by an end-to-end CUDA kernel generation stage. This progressive paradigm ensures that the model masters the core implementation of the kernel before transitioning to end-to-end kernel generation. 

Kernel In-filling Stage. Recent works (Woo et al., 2025) and community blog 1 have highlighted the presence of hack-ing shortcuts in generated kernels. We characterize this phenomenon as deceptive behavior , a prevalent issue in gen-erated kernels that remains nontrivial for automated evalua-tion scripts to detect. Specifically, we identify three primary types: ① the model adheres to prompt-provided example kernel while defaulting to high-level PyTorch functions for the forward pass, effectively bypassing the kernel; ② the model generates a valid custom CUDA kernel but fails to implement the necessary invocation logic; and ③ the custom kernel is omitted from the forward function, precluding its execution during compilation. To address these challenges, we decompose the optimized CUDA kernel into a tripartite structure: the prefix, the core implementation, and the suffix. As illustrated in Fig-ure 3, the prefix establishes the execution environment and global dependencies, primarily consisting of library im-ports (e.g., torch , cpp extension ) that define the nec-

And the core implementation part is the main logic of the CUDA kernel ( elementwise add source func-tion in this example) that the model needs to generate. By providing this scaffold, we ensure the model is grounded in the software stack and architectural constraints, allowing it to focus exclusively on the core logic while precluding the possibility of deceptive behavior. 

Kernel Generation Stage. Once the model has mastered the optimization of core logic for CUDA kernels during the infilling stage, we transition to a full-scale generation objec-tive. In this subsequent phase, we train the model to perform end-to-end generation; given a high-level PyTorch reference, the model must generate the complete implementation of the corresponding CUDA kernel, including all necessary invocation logic. This progression ensures that the founda-tional knowledge acquired during infilling is successfully scaled to the complete CUDA kernel generation.

## 6. Related Work 

Diffusion Large Language Models. The paradigm of large language models has been dominated by autoregres-sive (Achiam et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025) for a long time, but the emergence of diffusion large lan-guage models (Nie et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025; DeepMind, 2025; Labs et al., 2025; Ye et al., 2025) has broken this, offering a new choice for language models. Unlike autore-gressive methods, which use a strict left-to-right, token-by-token decoding process, dLLMs first generate multiple masks, then gradually denoise the masked sequence dur-ing decoding, which allows for simultaneous sampling and decoding of multiple tokens. Moreover, dLLMs employ a bidirectional attention mechanism to more comprehen-sively understand the context. Advancing models, such as LLaDA (Nie et al., 2025), an 8B-parameter diffusion lan-guage model trained from scratch, and Dream (Ye et al., 2025), a 7B-parameter model initialized from pre-trained AR-based model weights. SDAR (Cheng et al., 2025) syn-ergistically combines the advantages of diffusion and au-toregressive. TraDo (Wang et al., 2025) proposes TraceRL, deriving the first long-CoT dLLM, which achieves strong performance over models of comparable parameter size. More recently, WeDLM (Liu et al., 2025) represents the first dLLM to surpass industrial autoregressive engines while maintaining high performance on reasoning tasks. 

dLLMs for Code Generation. Code generation is a cru-cial and relatively challenging task for dLLMs, while it is still under exploration. CodeFusion (Singh et al., 2023) is the first to propose a diffusion-based language model for code generation, but it is limited to small-scale models and simple tasks. Recently, DiffuCoder (Gong et al., 2025) demonstrates remarkable capabilities in code generation. Dream-Coder (Xie et al., 2025b) further improves the per-formance, and Stable-DiffCoder (Fan et al., 2026) achieves the current best performance. Open-dCoder (Peng et al., 2025) competes with much larger dLLMs, despite being only 0.5B parameters. Moreover, commercial dLLMs, like Gemini Diffusion (DeepMind, 2025), Mercury Coder (Labs et al., 2025), and Seed Diffusion (Song et al., 2025), have demonstrated that dLLMs can achieve performance compa-rable to leading autoregressive code models while offering significantly faster generation speeds. However, when it comes to more challenging domains such as kernel genera-tion, the potential of dLLMs remains largely unexplored. 

LLM Kernel Generation. The advancement of LLMs has shifted the focus from general programming to hardware-specific task, kernel generation. Central to this progress is the development of specialized benchmarks, such as Ker-nelBench (Ouyang et al., 2025), MultiKernelBench (Wen et al., 2025), TritonBench (Li et al., 2025b), and FlashInfer-Bench (Xing et al., 2026), which provide standardized envi-ronments for evaluating functional correctness and execu-tion efficiency. Beyond evaluation, recent research empha-sizes improving generation quality through sophisticated training paradigms and reasoning alignment. Specifically, Kevin (Baronio et al., 2025), TritonRL (Woo et al., 2025), and AutoTriton (Li et al., 2025c) employ reinforcement learning to navigate models to effectively generate high-performance GPU kernels. More recently, agentic systems such as Astra (Wei et al., 2025), CudaForge (Zhang et al., 2025), and KernelEvolve (Liao et al., 2025) have introduced iterative refinement workflows to achieve near-expert per-formance. On the other hand, Kong et al. (2025) notices the data scarcity issue of high-quality kernels and proposes Con-CuR, an SFT dataset especially for CUDA kernel generation. Despite this progress toward kernel generation, how dLLMs can be effectively leveraged to address this challenging task remains an underexplored domain. 

## 7. Conclusion 

In this paper, we curate the CuKe dataset to provide a ro-bust foundation for supervised fine-tuning, mitigating the scarcity of high-quality training data in this domain. Then we introduce BiC-RL , which is specifically designed for CUDA kernel generation. Combining kernel infilling and end-to-end kernel generation stages can effectively improve training efficiency and enhance convergence. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that each training stage contributes significantly to stable convergence, and their combination yields substantial performance improvements. By integrat-ing these components, we develop DICE , a family of diffu-sion large language models spanning three parameter scales, opening up a new perspective for this specialized area. 8DICE: Diffusion Large Language Models Excel at Generating CUDA Kernels

## B. Experimental Details 

B.1. Supervised Fine-tuning Details 

We choose the SDAR model family (Cheng et al., 2025) as our base models, which contain models across three parameter scales (1.7B, 4B, and 8B). As mentioned in Section 5.1, we conduct supervised fine-tuning on our curated CuKe dataset for three epochs using 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUS, with a learning rate of 1.0 × 10 −5.

B.2. RL Sampling Details 

During the reinforcement learning stage, we employ a block size of 4 and the dynamic decoding strategy introduced in SDAR (Cheng et al., 2025), with a threshold τ = 0 .9. The inference hyperparameters are set at top − p = 1 .0, top − k = 0 ,and temperature = 1 .0. We train for 20 steps in the kernel infilling phase and 100 steps during the end-to-end kernel generation phase. For each step, we sample 64 problems, each with 16 generated responses.
