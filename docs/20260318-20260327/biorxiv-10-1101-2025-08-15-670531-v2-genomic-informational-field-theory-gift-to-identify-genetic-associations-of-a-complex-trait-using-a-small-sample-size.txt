ABSTRACT 18 

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) are commonly used to investigate the genetic basis of complex traits. 19 

However, to be adequately powered they typically require large sample sizes to provide precise inferences. To 20 

address this challenge, this paper introduces GIFT, a novel data analytic method that enhances the power of 21 

genetic analyses, enabling the use of smaller datasets without compromising precision. In a small cohort of 157 22 

ponies, GIFT was applied to examine the complex trait of “height at withers”, comparing its performance to 23 

traditional GWAS. GIFT enabled the identification genetic loci linked to insulin physiology validating, in turn, 24 

a long-standing hypothesis that “height at withers” is associated with insulin physiology in equids, potentially 25 

promoting equine metabolic syndrome (EMS). By redefining correlations between single nucleotide 26 

polymorphisms (SNPs), GIFT provides new insights into linkage disequilibrium and reveals underlying gene 27 

network structures. This, in turn, enables the distinction between core and peripheral genes within these 28 

networks. By reducing the time and cost associated with large-scale genotype–phenotype mapping studies 29 

without sacrificing statistical robustness, GIFT broadens access to quantitative genetic research, allowing 30 

smaller-scale studies to investigate the genetic architecture of complex traits with greater resolution. 31 

NEW & NOTEWORTHY 32 

Inferring genotype–phenotype associations typically requires large sample sizes, limiting many genetic studies. 33 

GIFT, a novel data analytics tool, overcomes this by enabling accurate association mapping in small datasets. 34 

We further show how GIFT’s extended framework infers linkage disequilibrium and gene networks, 35 

distinguishing core from peripheral genes involved in complex traits. This advancement enhances understanding 36 

of biological architecture and enables high-resolution genetic research in limited cohorts, offering a powerful, 37 

cost-effective alternative to traditional large-scale approaches. 38

INTRODUCTION 42 

Identifying associations between phenotype and genotype is the fundamental basis of genetic analysis and 43 

GWAS is currently the method of choice for mapping genotype to phenotype in large populations. It is expected 44 

that large-scale GWASs will elucidate the genetic basis of complex traits. The need for "large-scale" GWAS 45 

arises from the statistical requirements of the method requiring very large populations to produce valid 46 

inferences when effect sizes are small. A prime example of large-scale GWAS applied to a complex trait is the 47 

genetic analysis of the phenotype "standing height" in humans. A recent GWAS meta-analysis involving an 48 

unprecedented sample size of 5.4 million individuals successfully produced a saturated map of genetic variants 49 

associated with human height (1) . Although this represents a landmark achievement in genetics, it also 50 

highlights the limitations of GWAS, whose success relies heavily on data acquisition to generate adequately 51 

powered studies, which can be a limitation to individual investigations or non-consortium science. While human 52 

genetic studies benefit from substantial funding and large-scale GWAS capabilities, research on non-human 53 

species, especially those facing extinction, often lacks the necessary resources to conduct similar studies (2) . As 54 

a result, there is a need to explore alternative methods, increasing the investigative power of biological datasets 55 

when sample sizes are small. 56 

GWAS is based on the statistical framework developed by Fisher more than 100 years ago. It partitions 57 

genotypic values and performs linear regression of phenotype on marker allelic dosage. Regression coefficients 58 

estimate the average allele effect sizes, and the regression variance is the additive genetic variance due to the 59 

locus. However, there is ongoing debates about whether the statistical method underpinning GWAS is optimal 60 

for understanding complex traits (3, 4) .61 

In GWAS, statistical associations rely on frequency or count plots like bar charts or histograms, which 62 

categorize data into bins. These plots, known as distribution density functions (DDFs), form the basis of 63 

frequentist probability, determining key statistical measures such as averages and variance that are used to infer 64 

genotype-phenotype associations. However, this approach involves the loss of significant amounts of 65 

information from datasets. To comprehend this point, consider a population where phenotypic data are measured 66 

with high precision, making each data point unique. When grouped into bins to form DDFs, individual 67 

distinctions are lost since it is not possible to differentiate data within a given category, even though the data 68 

were initially differentiated. While averages and variances derived from DDFs appear informative, they are 69 

inherently based on this reduced representation limiting access to fine-grained information. Increasing the 70 

sample size might improve the precision of statistical summaries, i.e., average and variance, but it does not 71 

recover the lost individual details. 72 

This raises a fundamental question: Is more data always beneficial if the method inherently disregards fine-73 

grained information? 74 

GIFT is a novel data analytics method designed to overcome this conceptual limitation by analyzing the full 75 

biodiversity of data without requiring data categorization. Through theoretical simulations (5, 6) and analysis 76 

of various datasets with sample sizes comparable to those used in GWASs (7) , GIFT has been shown to enhance 77 

both investigative and discriminative powers of genetic studies. However, the ability of GIFT to operate 78 

effectively with smaller sample sizes and provide essential genetic information has not yet been shown. 79 

Herein, using a small cohort of 157 ponies and focusing on the complex trait "height at withers" the investigative 80 

powers of GWAS and GIFT are compared. Several reasons motivated the focus on "height at withers". 81 

First, like "standing height" in humans, “height at withers” in equids is a classic complex trait positively selected 82 

during domestication and correlated with other morphometric traits (8–10) . With a well-established heritability, 83 

GWASs have investigated its genetic background (9, 11–24) . Thus, GIFT-derived variants can be benchmarked 84 

against GWAS results in the equine species and compared to similar phenotype, i.e., “body size” or “body 85 

height”, across different species. 86 

Second, “height at withers” is suspected to be associated with equine metabolic syndrome (EMS) (11) . EMS 87 

describes a cluster of metabolic disturbances predisposing horses to laminitis, a condition where insulin 88 

dysregulation plays a central role (11, 16, 25, 26) . While a GWAS identified HMGA2 (High mobility group 89 

AT-hook protein 2) and IRAK3 (Interleukin-1 receptor-associated kinase 3) as associated with “height at 90 

withers” in a cohort of 236 ponies, no other genes were linked to insulin physiology (11) . However, since a 91 

retrospective correlation was identified between blood insulin concentrations and HMGA2 allelic dosages (11) ,92 

it was hypothesized that GIFT could reveal further genetic insights into the relationship between insulin 93 

physiology and “height at withers” (27) .94

Third, it is postulated that complex traits can be omnigenic involving core and peripheral genes (28) . Validating 95 

this new paradigm using GWAS would necessitate large sample sizes to detail any information on gene 96 

networks. However, since GIFT provides a more detailed genetic analysis, it was hypothesized that redefining 97 

SNP and gene pairwise correlations using GIFT could offer a fresh perspective on linkage disequilibrium (LD) 98 

and gene networks informing, in turn, on the potential validity of the omnigenic paradigm. 99 

The results herein demonstrate that GIFT’s approach is adequate when small sample sizes are used, yielding 100 

new insights into the genetic architecture of complex traits. 101

GIFT 132 

The same genotype and phenotypic residuals, as filtered by GWAS, were used for GIFT. Regarding the 133 

representation of GIFT, upon selecting a SNP the different genotypes were assigned arbitrary values of +1, 0, 134 

and -1, respectively. More specifically, the assignment of values +1, 0, and -1 were done as a function of the 135 

base pairs as follows: AA=TT=+1, GG=CC=-1, and 0 otherwise. The genetic microstates +1, -1 and 0 are 136 

respectively color coded in red, blue and grey in the figures to facilitate the representation of genetic paths. With 137 

this convention, any barcode can be represented by a string of numbers from which a GIFT analysis can be 138 

performed providing a level of significance noted pୋ୍୊୘ for the SNP considered, as explained in (7) and further 139 

developed mathematically in (27) . The level of significance ( pୋ୍୊୘ ) can then be plotted using a Manhattan plot 140 

and the same significance thresholds determined using 99% and 95% Bonferroni correction as in GWAS. For 141 

readers unfamiliar with GIFT, Supplemental_Text_S1.doc introduces the methodology underpinning GIFT. 142 

Enrichment and correlation analyses 143 

Enrichment analyses were performed using STRING, Uniprot, Ensembl, Gephi and the GWAS catalog (29–33) 144 

as cited in the text. Pearson correlations between genetic paths were performed using R-studio and the measure 145 

of centrality using Matlab. 146

RESULTS 147 

Manhattan Plots using GWAS or GIFT 148 

GIFT is a method designed to decouple the notion of precision in phenotypic measurements from that of sample 149 

size, providing genotype-phenotype associations without relying on data categorization or grouping. 150 

Measurements of “height at withers” obtained by Knowles et al. (25) were pre-corrected for fixed effects and 151 

relatedness. Further to this correction, phenotypic residual values were demonstrated to be adequately normally 152 

distributed using Kolmogorov-Smirnov test and usable by GWAS and GIFT (Supplemental_Figure_S2.doc). 153 

Association analyses using GWAS and GIFT based on the same phenotypic residual values were then performed 154 

and plotted under the form of Manhattan plots. Fig.1 presents the Manhattan plots for both GWAS (Fig.1A) and 155 

GIFT (Fig.1B). Concentrating on the highest threshold levels for GWAS and GIFT, the significant SNPs were 156 

then mapped to the reference horse genome assembly (EquCab3.0) available on Ensembl to retrieve the 157 

corresponding gene Id and gene name when available. The results summarized in Table 1 and Table 2 clearly 158 

demonstrate that SNPs located on chromosome 6 are highly significant. However, while GWAS identifies 5 159 

significant SNPs associated with the HMGA2 gene, GIFT detects up to 18 significant SNPs for the same gene 160 

on chromosome 6. Notably, GIFT also highlights significant SNPs on chromosome 2 that are not detected by 161 

GWAS. Figures 1C and 1D provide a comparative visualization of two representative SNPs using both GWAS 162 

and GIFT. Figure 1C shows a significant SNP on chromosome 6 identified by both methods, whereas Figure 163 

1D presents one significant SNP on chromosome 2, uniquely identified by GIFT. Interestingly, the SNP on 164 

chromosome 2 exhibiting a sigmoidal genetic path (Figure 1D, right panels) lacks a traditionally defined gene 165 

effect size (Figure 1D, left panel). 166 

While the Bonferroni correction provides strong protection against type I errors, it is highly conservative, 167 

particularly in GWAS using relatively small sample sizes, and may therefore miss biologically relevant 168 

associations. In contrast, the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure, which controls the false discovery rate (FDR) 169 

using the expected proportion of false positives among significant results, is preferred for small genetic 170 

population studies and exploratory analyses, as it maximizes discovery while controlling error rates. 171 

Accordingly, p-values from both GWAS and GIFT were adjusted using the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure 172 

and compared. As shown in Fig. 1E and Fig. 1F for GWAS and GIFT, respectively, a large proportion of the 173 

signals detected by GIFT remain significant after FDR correction (adjusted p-value < 0.05). This result indicates 174 

that, under the same error-control criteria, GIFT is more sensitive than GWAS in identifying significant 175 

associations in studies with small sample sizes. 176 

To further confirm that the significant SNPs identified by GIFT in Fig.1B result from the unique organization 177 

of their microstates rather than random chance, the microstates of each significant SNP from Fig.1B were 178 

randomly permuted 1,000 times. After each permutation, the significance level, i.e., the value of 179 

−Log ଵ଴ (p ୋ୍୊୘ ), was determined. The statistical summary of these significant values obtained from 1,000 180 

random permutations was presented as a boxplot (Fig.1G). These random permutations represent the 181 

distribution expected under a null hypothesis in which no genotype–phenotype association exists for the SNP 182 

considered. These values were then compared to the original significance value of the same SNP and to the 183 

Bonferroni corrections applied, as determined in Fig.1B, and represented as a blue dot in Fig.1G. Fig.1G 184 

confirms that GIFT’s identification of genotype–phenotype associations is not due to random chance. 185 

Finally, to assess whether GIFT can operate effectively with smaller sample sizes, the original dataset (N=157) 186 

was randomly subsampled to N=140, 120, and 100. For each subsample, the values of −Log ଵ଴ (p ୋ୍୊୘ )187 

corresponding to the most significant SNPs in Fig.1B were recalculated. This procedure was repeated 10 times 188 

for each new sample size to compute the average and standard deviation of −Log ଵ଴ (p ୋ୍୊୘ ) values obtained for 189 

the top SNPs (Fig.1E). Additionally, for each subsampling the microstates were randomly permuted 1,000 times 190 

to extract an average value for −Log ଵ଴ (p ୋ୍୊୘ ). As each subsampling was performed 10 times the statistics 191 

(average and standard deviation) of −Log ଵ଴ (p ୋ୍୊୘ ) values upon subsampling and random permutation of 192 

microstates were reported as a boxplot for each SNP. As shown in Fig.1F, although a decrease in the magnitude 193 

of −Log ଵ଴ (p ୋ୍୊୘ ) is clearly observed with smaller sample sizes, there is no overlap of standard deviations 194 

obtained between the subsampling and the null hypothesis. 195

In a second step, the genes listed in Tables 1 and 2 were cross-referenced in PubMed to determine whether their 210

HMGA2 (High Mobility Group AT-hook 2) is a non-histone chromatin-associated protein that binds AT-rich 214 

DNA sequences to modulate gene expression, acting as an architectural transcription factor (37) , including the 215 

expression of peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor γ (PPARγ) leading to the expression and release from 216 

adipose tissue of adiponectin, an insulin-sensitizing adipokine (38) . While HMGA2 itself has not been directly 217 

linked to metabolic dysregulation, in obesity and type 2 diabetes the impairment of PPARγ activity selectively 218 

downregulates insulin-sensitizing genes, most notably adiponectin, thereby contributing directly to systemic 219 

insulin resistance (39) .220 

A multi-omic mapping of human pancreatic islet stress responses has identified NCKAP5 as a gene dynamically 221 

regulated by both endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress and pro-inflammatory cytokines, two pathophysiological 222 

stressors implicated in type 2 diabetes (T2D) pathogenesis (40) .223 

CBR4, a member of the carbonyl reductase family, regulates fatty acid biosynthesis through its interaction with 224 

fatty acid synthase (FASN), a multifunctional enzyme central to energy storage and signal transduction. FASN 225 

has been implicated in activation of the PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling axis, which governs cellular growth and 226 

metabolism (41) . Mechanistically, CBR4 engages the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway to promote the degradation 227 

of FASN, resulting in reduced FASN protein levels and subsequently diminished AKT (protein kinase B) 228 

activation (42, 43) . This cascade of events ultimately leads to the inhibition of the mTOR (mechanistic target of 229 

rapamycin) signaling pathway. Given that mTOR is directly activated by insulin through the canonical 230 

PI3K/AKT cascade, this regulatory axis positions CBR4 as a modulator of insulin-mediated signals that 231 

integrate cellular energy status (43) .232 

PALLD (Palladin), a cytoskeletal protein, is essential for active actin remodelling and the formation of 233 

membrane ruffles (44) , a process integral to insulin signalling due to its role in the membrane translocation of 234 

specific GLUT family members (45) . Furthermore, a genome-wide functional screen identified PALLD as a 235 

novel negative regulator of insulin signaling, demonstrating that its overexpression inhibits insulin-stimulated 236 

AKT phosphorylation and reduces insulin receptor (IR) abundance, while its knockdown produces the opposite 237 

effects (46) .238 

SUMO1 is a central component of the SUMOylation pathway. This post-translational modification process 239 

critically regulates metabolic homeostasis by modifying key transcription factors that influence lipid 240 

biosynthesis, adipogenesis, and energy balance. SUMO1 covalently attaches to specific lysine residues on target 241 

proteins, thereby modulating their stability, subcellular localisation, and transcriptional activity in response to 242 

metabolic cues (47, 48) . A pivotal target of SUMO1 is the nuclear receptor PPARγ, a master regulator of insulin 243 

sensitivity (49) . Functional studies using SUMO1-knockout mice revealed that these animals gain less weight 244 

on a high-fat diet and show attenuated expression of PPARγ target genes, demonstrating that SUMO1 is required 245 

for full PPARγ transcriptional activity (50) . Collectively, these findings establish SUMO1 as a critical modulator 246 

of transcriptional networks governing adipogenesis, lipid metabolism, and energy balance, with direct 247 

implications for insulin sensitivity and the pathogenesis of metabolic disorders. 248

mechanistic link to the canonical insulin/IGF signaling cascade (51, 52) . Interestingly, human subjects with 252

GIFT’s determination of LD 271 

At the core of GIFT lies the concept of the genetic path, represented by ∆θ in Fig.1C and Fig.1D (right panels) 272 

for each SNP in the genome. The overall pattern of ∆θ s reflects the ordering of genetic microstates when the 273 

sample is ordered by phenotypic residual values. Consequently, SNPs similarly related to the phenotype exhibit 274 

comparable large ∆θ patterns. Conversely, SNPs that are unrelated to the phenotype give small and noisy ∆θ 275 

curves (see supplemental_Text_S1). This property is essential to determine precise correlations between loci, 276

DISCUSSION 371 

As statistical methods, GWAS and GIFT enable the determination of genotype-phenotype associations. The 372 

present study aimed to compare GWAS and GIFT results in identifying genes significantly associated with the 373 

trait “height at withers” in ponies, while simultaneously exploring potential links between this trait and equine 374 

metabolic syndrome (EMS), in a manner similar to the study by Norton et al., which was conducted on a larger 375 

sample size of 236 ponies (11) .376 

Taken together, the results obtained: (i) confirm that GIFT extracts significantly more genetic information than 377 

GWAS, while also corroborating GWAS finding (HMGA2); and (ii) suggest that the genes identified by GIFT 378 

are strongly implicated in metabolic dysregulation involving insulin-related signaling pathways. Although the 379 

absence of SNPs associated with the insulin gene in either the GIFT or GWAS results may appear unexpected, 380 

this can be explained by the gene array used, which lacked coverage of the genomic regions corresponding to 381 

the insulin gene. Additionally, making use of GIFT’s framework, LD was redefined demonstrating the 382 

possibility to extract isomorphic gene networks upon which centrality measures can be defined demonstrating 383 

that HMGA2 is indeed a central gene for the trait “height at withers”. Whilst this marks the first application of 384 

GIFT in equids with potential relevance to EMS, GIFT raises questions in relation to how genetic data must be 385 

interpreted. 386 

The specific representation of scientific datasets shapes the foundation and structure of scientific knowledge 387 

bases. However, when the representation shifts, the underlying scientific concepts must also be redefined. When 388 

DDFs are used, their statistical summaries often based on their leading-order moments such as averages and 389 

variances, are expected to capture key scientific insights. In genetics, these statistical measures are closely tied 390 

to quantifiable concepts such as gene effect size and dominance for the average, and heritability for the variance. 391 

However, as the conceptual framework introduced by GIFT establishes the genetic path as a fundamental entity, 392 

one that cannot be reduced to conventional statistical summaries, this shift has important implications. Indeed, 393 

with GIFT a SNP can be significantly associated with a phenotype even when its effect size cannot be defined 394 

(see Fig.1D). Accordingly, if heredity is defined solely through variance, which itself is determined in relation 395 

to a mean or effect size, how can heredity be conceptualized for a significant SNP that lacks a measurable effect 396 

size (see Fig.1D)? These observations do not dispute the potential for any SNP to be associated with a trait or 397 

to exhibit heritability. Instead, they highlight the need to consider SNPs collectively rather than in isolation, 398 

positioning them within intricate dependencies and higher-order relationships that may elude traditional 399 

variance- or average-based approaches. 400 

Traditionally, GWAS relies on genotyping a subset of SNPs, known as tag SNPs, which are in strong LD with 401 

many nearby SNPs. When a tag SNP shows a strong association with a trait, it suggests that a variant in LD 402 

with it may be associated with the trait. However, LD is typically determined based on bulk allele (or microstate) 403 

frequency differences calculated across the entire population. By using the detailed genetic paths provided by 404 

GIFT, it becomes now possible to refine the correlations between adjacent SNPs along a given chromosome, 405 

thereby offering a new perspective on LD. Note that, given the comprehensive information provided by GIFT, 406

Accordingly, the results demonstrate that the emergence of high-order isomorphic relationships becomes visible 409

is far more organized than initially thought. 411 

GIFT's ability to enhance the investigative power of datasets, in turn, enables discussion of a recently proposed 412 

concept in genetics: the omnigenic paradigm. The omnigenic paradigm for complex traits in genetics is a model 413 

that suggests that most genes in the genome contribute, directly or indirectly, to complex traits. This concept 414 

was first proposed by Boyle et al. and challenges the traditional polygenic model based on GWAS, which 415 

focuses on a limited set of key genes that cumulatively influence phenotypic trait (28) . The key concepts of 416 

the omnigenic paradigm suggest that a small subset of core genes directly regulates the complex trait, while 417 

peripheral genes influence it indirectly through interactions with core genes within regulatory networks. While 418 

the theoretical and analytical frameworks, along with the present results from GIFT, support an omnigenic-like 419 

representation of complex traits, where an increasing number of SNPs are associated, it is important to 420 

acknowledge certain limitations. GIFT reinforces the concept of core versus peripheral genes, as illustrated in 421 

Fig.4B. However, contrary to the initial assumption that most genes in the genome contribute directly or 422 

indirectly to complex traits, GIFT suggests that only statistically significant genes are involved. In other words, 423 

it is unlikely that most genes contribute by default. In this context, GIFT can be seen as a hybrid conceptual 424 

framework for complex traits positioned between GWAS and the omnigenic paradigm. 425 

Finally, it is essential to acknowledge the limitations of the present study. While GIFT is a powerful tool for 426 

identifying genes associated with complex traits, true validation of the results presented here would require an 427 

independent validation cohort, as a single statistical association study cannot establish causality. Rather, such 428 

studies highlight potential candidate genes that may influence the trait. Furthermore, genes identified as 429 

significant require functional validation. In the absence of these additional validations, association results 430 

remain putative. 431
