Abstract 39 

Thermophilic bacteria offer major advantages for industrial biotechnology, yet most remain genetically 40 

intractable because cellular defense systems block efficient DNA acquisition. Here, we present a 41 

programmable domestication strategy that converts wild Geobacillus strains into genetically tractable 42 

thermophilic hosts. We developed the Domestication of Non-Model Bacteria (DNMB) Suite, a multi-43 

omics-guided computational framework that systematically identifies genetic barriers to transformation. 44 

DNMB analysis revealed that non-canonical nuclease-based defense systems, including Wadjet II, 45 

constitute dominant barriers to DNA uptake in previously intractable Geobacillus strains. Targeted 46 

deletion of these loci increased transformation efficiency by up to six orders of magnitude. We further 47 

established a hierarchical thermophilic engineering toolkit that integrates plasmid artificial modification, 48 

conjugation-assisted DNA delivery, and genome editing using an endogenous CRISPR-Cas9 system. 49 

The resulting domesticated strains support stable heterologous expression and tunable genetic control at 50 

elevated temperatures. Together, these results establish a generalizable framework for transforming 51 

genetically intractable thermophiles into programmable industrial chassis. 52

Introduction 57 

Microbial platform organisms play an important role in modern biotechnology, enabling the sustainable 58 

production of fuels, chemicals, pharmaceuticals, enzymes, and food ingredients 1, 2 . Traditionally, 59 

microbial cell factories have relied on mesophilic hosts such as Escherichia coli , Bacillus subtilis , and 60 

Saccharomyces cerevisiae , because of their genetic tractability, extensive molecular toolkits, and well-61 

characterized physiology 3-5 . However, these organisms often encounter limitations under industrial 62 

conditions, including contamination risks, restricted substrate solubility, and reduced enzyme stability at 63 

elevated temperatures 6, 7 . Thermophilic microorganisms offer a compelling alternative, as elevated 64 

process temperatures can accelerate reaction kinetics, increase substrate and product solubility, and 65 

reduce contamination during large-scale bioprocessing 8-10 . Consequently, there has been growing 66 

interest in developing thermophiles as next-generation industrial hosts 11 .67 

Despite these advantages, most thermophilic bacteria remain genetically intractable. Effective 68 

genetic engineering requires efficient DNA delivery, stable plasmid maintenance, and predictable gene 69 

expression, yet these capabilities are poorly developed in many thermophiles 12 . Among thermophilic 70 

genera, Geobacillus species are particularly promising due to their rapid growth, environmental 71 

resilience, and natural ability to secrete industrially relevant hydrolases such as proteases, lipases, and 72 

cellulases 13-16 . Notably, G. stearothermophilus and G. thermodenitrificans are designated as Qualified 73 

Presumption of Safety (QPS) and Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) organisms, making them 74 

attractive candidates for food-grade thermophilic bioproduction 17 . Nevertheless, domestication of wild-75 

type Geobacillus strains has been hindered by the scarcity of versatile genetic tools and by substantial 76 

strain-to-strain variability in both genotype and phenotype 18-20 . As a result, engineering efforts have 77 

largely been confined to a few tractable species, including G. kaustophilus, G. thermodenitrificans, and 78 

Parageobacillus thermoglucosidasius 21 .79

Restriction–modification (R–M) systems have long been recognized as major barriers to DNA 80 

transformation in bacteria 22 . Recent advances in single-molecule real-time (SMRT) sequencing and 81 

methylome analysis have revealed extensive diversity in DNA modification patterns and highly strain-82 

specific defense architectures 23 . However, these approaches often fail to detect less-characterized non-83 

canonical defense systems that can impose equally strong, or even dominant, constraints on genetic 84 

manipulation. Such cryptic defense modules are frequently embedded within defense islands associated 85 

with mobile genetic elements but remain poorly annotated in many thermophilic genomes, thereby 86 

hindering systematic engineering of non-model microorganisms 24 .87 

Here, we show that cryptic non-canonical defense systems, including a Wadjet II-like complex, 88 

Gabija, and pAgo modules, constitute major barriers to horizontal DNA acquisition in wild-type G. 89 

stearothermophilus strains EF60045 and SJEF4-2 25 . These loci have largely escaped conventional 90 

genome annotation and are refractory to traditional plasmid artificial modification strategies that mimic 91 

host DNA methylation patterns 26 . To systematically uncover and dismantle these barriers, we developed 92 

the Domestication of Non-Model Bacteria (DNMB) Suite, a modular multi-omics framework integrating 93 

comparative genomics, transcriptomics, and motif-based defense mining. DNMB enabled precise 94 

mapping of defense islands, comprehensive functional annotation, and rational host redesign, including 95 

codon optimization, ribosome binding site engineering, and construction of Geobacillus -specific E. coli -96 

shuttle vectors with native replication origins and tunable promoters. We further engineered an 97 

endogenous CRISPR-Cas9 system (GeoCas9EF) and incorporated it into a plasmid artificial 98 

modification-assisted conjugative engineering (PACE) workflow to enable efficient genome editing 22, 27 .99 

Systematic locus-by-locus removal of defense modules, rather than canonical R–M loci, resulted in 100 

transformation efficiency increases of up to six orders of magnitude and markedly improved genetic 101 

stability. To demonstrate the utility of this domesticated thermophilic chassis, we reprogrammed G. 102

Results 107 

The DNMB Suite: A modular computational framework for deconstructing domestication 108 

barriers in non-model bacteria 109 

Domestication of wild thermophilic bacteria is frequently impeded by cryptic genetic barriers, including 110 

incomplete genome annotations, poorly characterized defense systems, and the lack of predictive tools 111 

for assessing engineering tractability. To systematically identify these constraints, we developed the 112 

DNMB Suite, a modular R-based computational framework that integrates comparative genomics and 113 

functional annotation to uncover genetic determinants limiting DNA uptake and heterologous expression 114 

(Fig. 1a ). 115 

The DNMB pipeline converts GenBank files into structured annotation tables and enriches gene-116 

and protein-level features using eggNOG-mapper and InterProScan. A key component of the framework 117 

is a gene/protein identifier harmonization module that reconciles inconsistencies in gene symbols, 118 

protein names, and accession identifiers across databases, enabling reliable cross-genome comparisons. 119 

This normalized step proved essential for accurately tracking homologous loci and defense modules 120 

across diverse strains. 121 

To resolve strain-specific defense architectures, DNMB integrates multiple layers of genomic layers, 122 

including gene neighborhood organization, operon structure, motif distribution, and methylation-related 123 

features. Candidate defense islands were identified using DefenseFinder and REBASE and visualized 124 

with DefenseViz (Fig. 1a; DataSets 1 –2 ). This analysis revealed a diverse combination of restriction– 125 

modification (R–M) systems, anti-conjugation loci, CRISPR–Cas elements, and additional defense 126 

modules across Geobacillus stearothermophilus isolates, indicating substantial heterogeneity in genetic 127 

accessibility barriers. 128

Beyond defense profiling, DNMB evaluates translational constraints affecting heterologous gene 129 

expression. Codon usage was quantified using complementary metrics including effective codon usage, 130 

frequency, and relative synonymous codon usage (RSCU), and compared with predicted tRNA gene 131 

copy numbers and amino-acid composition to identify potential translational bottlenecks ( Fig. 1b ). An 132 

ribosomal binding site (RBS)-scanning module was further implemented to detect Shine-Dalgarno 133 

sequences by aligning the reverse complements of the 3’ terminus of 16S rRNA to upstream coding 134 

regions. Application of this module identified a conserved consensus motif (AAGGGAGGN) and a 135 

preferred ~7-nt spacing between the RBS and start codon in Geobacillus , consistent with efficient native 136 

translation initiation ( Fig. 1c ). 137 

Together, these analyses systematically dissect genetic barriers to engineering by integrating 138 

annotation normalization, defense system detection, regulatory motif discovery, and translational 139 

optimization within a unified framework. The DNMB Suite enabled prioritization of strain-specific 140 

domestication targets and directly guided subsequent engineering of genetically tractable G. 141 

stearothermophilus hosts described below. 142

Beyond canonical restriction systems, DNMB-based defense profiling identified a broad diversity 178

Gabija, SoFic, SspBCDE, CBASS, and pAgo systems ( Fig. 2 ). Individual genomes encoded between 2 180

modular and polygenic immune architectures. Strikingly, strains lacking Wadjet-II, including P. 182

canonical and non-canonical defense systems constitutes a major barrier to genetic accessibility. By 188 

integrating comparative genomics, methylome profiling, and functional validation, the DNMB Suite 189 

provides a predictive framework for identifying candidate defense loci that limit transformation and for 190 

rationally prioritizing targets for genome engineering in thermophilic chassis strains. 191

CRISPR-based genome editing reveals non-canonical defense systems as dominant barriers to 228 

thermophile domestication 229 

To further enhance DNA delivery, we implemented plasmid artificial modification-assisted conjugative 230 

engineering (PACE), in which methylation-matched plasmids generated in engineered donor strains are 231 

transferred to Geobacillus recipients via pRK24-mediated conjugation ( Figs. 4a and S4 ). This approach 232 

enabled efficient restriction evasion during DNA transfer. Conjugative delivery of an sfGFP reporter 233 

plasmid into SJEF4-2 occurred at high frequency (3.3 ×10 -3 recipients -1 ), whereas EF60045 yielded only 234 

extremely low but reproducible transfer events (~9 ×10 -9 recipients -1 ), representing a difference of more 235 

than six orders of magnitude under identical donor conditions. Transconjugant colonies frequently 236 

displayed heterogeneous fluorescence, including mixtures of fluorescent and non-fluorescent cells, 237 

indicating post-entry plasmid silencing or degradation. These results demonstrate that intracellular 238 

defense mechanisms continue to restrict plasmid establishment even after successful DNA transfer. 239 

Because efficient DNA delivery alone did not confer genetic tractability, we next established a 240 

native CRISPR-based genome editing platform to interrogate endogenous defense barriers. Genome 241 

analysis revealed that G. stearothermophilus EF60045 encodes a Type II-C CRISPR-Cas system 242

A hierarchical thermophilic genetic toolkit enables programmable engineering of G. 293 

stearothermophilus 294 

With major defense barriers removed, we next asked whether these thermophilic hosts could be 295 

transformed into programmable engineering platforms. To this end, we established a hierarchical 296 

molecular toolkit for G. stearothermophilus that integrates tunable replication modules, quantitative 297 

promoter libraries, inducible regulatory elements, and scalable genome editing components, enabling 298 

precise control of gene dosage and expression in thermophilic chassis. 299

To demonstrate the utility of the domesticated Geobacillus chassis for metabolic engineering and 345

incapable of utilizing a given sugar as a sole carbon source will growth only if an introduced enzyme 348 

converts that substrate into metabolically usable intermediates. Genome analysis revealed that SJEF4-2 349 

encodes multiple carbohydrate utilization pathways, including the araBAD operon for L-arabinose ( L-350 

Ara) metabolism, the galKETR operon of the Leloir pathway, and a putative dgoDKAx cluster 351

confirmed robust utilization of L-Ara and D-Gal but no growth on D-Tag, which instead inhibited growth 353 

relative to control conditions (casamino acid-only), indicating the absence of a native D-Tag catabolic 354 

route ( Fig. S9a–b ). 355

Discussion 382 

Developing genetically tractable microbial hosts remains a major obstacle to exploiting non-model 383 

organisms, particularly extremophiles, for functional genomics and biotechnology. Despite their unique 384 

metabolic capabilities and industrial potential, most thermophilic bacteria remain genetically 385 

inaccessible due to multilayered defense systems and the absence of systematic domestication strategies. 386 

Here, we present a systematic framework for the programmable domestication of thermophilic bacteria 387 

that integrates computational genome analysis, targeted removal of genetic barriers, and modular 388 

engineering tools to convert previously intractable strains into versatile genetic platforms ( Fig. 6a ). 389 

Central to this effort is the DNMB pipeline, which consolidates task-oriented scripts and curated 390 

reference databases into an integrated analytical suite capable of rapidly identifying defense systems and 391 

genetic bottlenecks ( Figs. 1–2 ). By enabling rational prioritization of engineering targets, this 392 

framework transforms domestication from a trial-and-error process into a predictable workflow for 393 

engineering thermophilic hosts ( Fig. 6 ). Importantly, this framework is not limited to Geobacillus but 394 

provides a generalizable strategy for identifying and overcoming genetic barriers across diverse 395 

thermophilic taxa. 396 

R–M systems are widely regarded as the principal barriers to horizontal DNA transfer in bacteria 30 .397 

Consistent with this view, in vivo methylation strategies were initially required to enable efficient DNA 398 

delivery into Geobacillus strains. Heterologous expression of methylases provided a practical entry 399 

point for genetic manipulation, and notably, the Geobacillus -common Type III methylase (GCC 6m AT) 400 

alone was sufficient to methylate target sequences, challenging the assumption that both modification 401 

and restriction subunits are required for enzymatic activity ( Figs. 3 and S1c ) 31 . However, methylation 402 

alone proved insufficient to fully overcome genetic barriers. Several adenine methylases (MS.SJEF4-2I, 403 

M.AlwI, M.BstEII, MS.60045II, MS.GK426II) exhibited toxicity in E. coli , likely reflecting widespread 404

responses 32 . These effects necessitated tightly regulated expression systems (e.g., rhamnose-inducible 406 

promoters) to mitigate methylation-induced toxicity ( Figs. 3 and S2-3 ) 33 . Collectively, these findings 407 

indicate that R–M evasion alone cannot ensure genetic accessibility and that genetic intractability arises 408 

from multilayered host defenses rather than a single dominant mechanism. 409 

Beyond classical R–M systems, our results reveal that non-canonical defense systems constitute 410 

dominant barriers to genetic domestication in thermophilic Geobacillus (Fig. 2 ). Systematic deletion of 411 

recently described defense modules 34-37 , including Wadjet II, Gabija, and BREX III, dramatically 412 

increased transformation efficiencies ( Fig. 4f ). In particular, removal of native plasmids together with 413 

Wadjet II produced the largest improvement in conjugation efficiency, identifying this system as a 414 

critical barrier to DNA acquisition ( Figs. 4f and S7 ). These observations reinforce emerging evidence 415 

that bacterial defense architectures are multilayered and highly strain-specific, often involving 416 

mechanisms distinct from classical restriction systems. 417 

The Wadjet defense system belongs to a family of SMC-like complexes that recognize circular DNA 418 

substrates and cleave them via a toprim-like nuclease subunit (JetD) 36, 38-40 . Its widespread distribution 419 

across ( Para )Geobacillus species, except for Parageobacillus and G. thermodenitrificans , suggests a 420 

major role in restricting foreign DNA in thermophilic environments ( Fig. 2 ). The coexistence of Wadjet 421 

systems with large endogenous plasmids (>30 kb) in many strains raises the possibility of size- or 422 

topology-dependent discrimination that allows certain DNA molecules to evade cleavage. Structural 423 

variation within SMC-like domains may influence substrate recognition 41 , and the susceptibility of 424 

related complexes to DNA-mimic proteins suggests potential avenues for transient inhibition of Wadjet 425 

activity 42 . Alternatively, controlled activation of the JetD nuclease could be exploited as a426 

programmable DNA elimination system. 427

coordinated optimization of both intracellular defense systems and cell envelope properties. 436 

In parallel with removing genetic barriers, we established an integrated thermophilic engineering 437 

toolkit enabling iterative genome modification and tunable gene expression ( Figs. S5 and S8 ). 438 

Conjugation proved particularly effective due to the transfer of single-stranded DNA that is readily 439 

recombined by native repair pathways 45, 46, 47 . Paired with this delivery method, the native Type II 440

Beyond this proof-of-concept application, the domesticated Geobacillus chassis supports large-scale 461 

genome engineering. The high efficiency of homologous recombination using relatively short homology 462 

arms (~500 bp) suggests that genome-wide editing strategies may be feasible. Coupled with automated 463 

design tools, this system could enable the construction of comprehensive knockout libraries and 464 

systematic pathway rewiring for functional genomics and strain optimization. 465 

Finally, the engineered strain retains broad degradative capabilities toward diverse substrates, 466 

including proteins, lipids, and complex polysaccharides, while operating at temperatures incompatible 467 

with mesophilic hosts ( Figs. 6b and 9d ). These features, combined with improved genetic accessibility, 468 

position Geobacillus as an attractive chassis for high-temperature biomanufacturing and extremophile 469

thermophilic biocatalysis, and sustainable manufacturing processes where contamination resistance and 471
