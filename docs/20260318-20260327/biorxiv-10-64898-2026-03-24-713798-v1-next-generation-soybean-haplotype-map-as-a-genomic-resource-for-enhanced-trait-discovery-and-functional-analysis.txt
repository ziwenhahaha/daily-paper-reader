Abstract 20 

We present a global soybean haplotype map generated from whole-genome sequencing of 1,278 21 

Glycine max and Glycine soja accessions, comprising 11.37 million SNPs and 2.05 million short 22 

insertions and deletions. This map (GmHapMap-II) captures unprecedented worldwide genetic 23 

diversity, reflecting the broad extent of the global soybean gene pool. Population structure 24 

analyses revealed six geographically distinct subpopulations that affected the linkage and shaped 25 

the recombination. The haplotype variation map was used to identify novel genomic regions 26 

associated with crude protein content on chromosome 15 that were not detected by a lower SNP 27 

density array. LD-based haplotype analysis revealed a superior haplotype for crude protein 28 

content. The constructed haplotype map enabled detailed characterization of haplotype diversity 29 

and copy number polymorphism at the SCN-associated rhg-1 and Rhg-4 loci, revealing both 30 

novel haplotype structures and germplasm lines with elevated CNV relative to previously 31 

characterized genotypes. We employed the HapMap matrix for a multi-class variations ML-32 

based genomic prediction approach to predict phenotypes for SCN and catalogued the gene-33 

centric haplotypes in a user-friendly database. The analysis revealed the extent of deleterious 34 

alleles present in the soybean germplasm and how breeders have deployed beneficial alleles and 35 

purged deleterious ones. The haplotype map will serve as a major genomic resource for trait-36 

based mapping, enhancing efforts in the genomics-enabled development of improved cultivars. 37

Introduction 39 

Soybean [ Glycine max (L.) Merr.] is a crop of significant economic importance as it serves as a 40 

primary source for both animal feed protein and oil content. The allopolyploid genome, 41 

sequenced for the first time in 2010, comprises approximately 1.1 billion base pairs across 20 42 

chromosomes, with roughly 58,000 protein-coding genes (Schmutz et al., 2010). This genomic 43 

architecture, combined with the plant's capacity for symbiotic nitrogen fixation and production of 44 

valuable proteins and oils, makes soybean an essential subject for studying plant evolution, 45 

adaptation and improving crop yields through biotechnology. Soybean production is severely 46 

hampered by biotic and abiotic stresses, and it is crucial to identify beneficial superior alleles for 47 

improved varieties (Fang et al., 2026; Tian et al., 2025). Meeting projected 2050 protein 48 

demands requires exploiting the substantial genetic diversity preserved in germplasm collections, 49 

yet this diversity remains poorly characterized at the genomic level. While previous 50 

resequencing efforts have catalogued millions of single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), 51 

reliance on a single linear reference genome has limited detection of structural variants and 52 

resulted in incomplete linkage disequilibrium, reducing statistical power in genome-wide 53 

association studies and leaving substantial trait heritability unexplained (Zhou et al., 2015; Fang 54 

et al., 2017). Future progress in soybean breeding relies on deeper insights into genetic diversity, 55 

especially at the allelic level, present in worldwide germplasm resources. Genomic resources 56 

play an integral part in this strategy, and generating comprehensive resources can lead to more 57 

informed decisions in breeding based on genomics (Varshney et al., 2021). 58 

> 59

Recent advances in long-read sequencing technologies have enabled dramatic improvements in 60 

reference genome quality. The improved soybean reference assembly Wm82.a5, constructed 61 

using long-read technology, exhibits substantially enhanced contiguity and completeness 62 

compared to previous versions, enabling more comprehensive detection of structural variants and 63 

complex genomic rearrangements (Garg et al., 2023). However, the full potential of this 64 

improved assembly for population-scale variant discovery and trait dissection remains 65 

unrealized. The declining cost of genome sequencing, driven by advances in next-generation 66 

sequencing technologies, has opened unprecedented opportunities to comprehensively identify 67 

genetic variants across complete genomes from multiple individuals within a species. For 68 

soybean, numerous germplasm scale sequencing efforts have been accomplished, resulting in 69 

uncovering genetic relationships and identifying millions of variations in local and global 70 

germplasms. This involved a large number of individuals sequenced from the USA (Bayer et al., 71 

2022; Valliyodan et al., 2021; Valliyodan et al., 2016), China (Lam et al., 2010; Liu et al., 2020; 72 

Zhou et al., 2015; Fang et al., 2017), Canada (Torkamaneh et al., 2021; Torkamaneh et al., 73 

2017), Korea (Kim et al., 2021). Despite all these efforts, most genetic and association analyses 74 

are conducted using the Soy50KSNP array panel due to its ease of use and manageable size of 75 

the genotype data (Song et al., 2013). This has yielded several important and notable quantitative 76 

trait loci (QTLs) used for candidate gene discovery in seed composition and abiotic/biotic 77 

stresses (Hwang et al., 2014; Han et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2019). However, functional gene 78 

characterization studies and gene validation require complete sequence information, which is 79 

lacking in low-density genotyping arrays. To overcome this, there is a need to develop a 80

> 87

Haplotype maps provide a systematic framework for characterizing genomic variation patterns 88 

within a species by identifying sequence variants, their allele frequencies, and the linkage 89 

disequilibrium structure between variants across populations (Chia et al., 2012). Haplotypes, 90 

which are specific genomic blocks, emerge when sequence variants are inherited together in a 91 

highly correlated fashion, a phenomenon known as linkage disequilibrium (LD). In our quest to 92 

update and expand the reference set of segregating diversity in soybean, we integrated previously 93 

reported resequencing panels with soybean lines from six previous large-scale sequencing 94 

efforts. This includes resequencing data from soybean lines, originating on six continents, 95 

associated with domestication panels and improvement, including wild relatives, landraces, 96 

breeding lines and elite cultivars. The main objective of this study was to develop a haplotype 97 

map that improves the resolution and mapping power for genome-wide association studies 98 

conducted in important traits, including the seed composition traits. We added a resequencing of 99 

an additional 142 soybean lines at high coverage, including a diverse panel of ancestral lines, 100 

milestone lines, lines associated with seed composition traits displaying varying seed protein 101 

content and high sucrose-associated lines. 102 

> 103

Our comprehensive variant catalog revealed extensive variations in landraces and wild species 104 

lines. The haplotype map provided higher resolution for association and fine mapping 105 

uncovering more significant genomic regions associated with protein content revealing superior 106 

haplotypes for important traits. Gene-centric haplotype analysis identified one unique functional 107 

haplotype across rhg-1 gene associated with SCN resistance. We demonstrate practical breeding 108 

applications through genomic prediction and optimal haplotype identification. We have 109 

developed a soybean variation and haplotype database providing a user-friendly interface for 110 

extracting specific haplotypes from a large set of variation catalogues. This high-resolution 111 

haplotype map provides a foundational resource for soybean genetics and breeding, enabling 112 

improved genomic selection, targeted allele mining, and accelerated genetic improvement of this 113 

critical global protein crop. 114

Results 116 

A global soybean HapMap 117 

To create a global haplotype map for soybean (GmHapMap-II), we analyzed 1,278 re-sequenced 118 

soybean lines. This collection comprised 1,116 previously sequenced accessions, representing 119 

national and regional core collections, together with 162 newly sequenced accessions specifically 120 

for this study. Together, these samples are considered representative of the global soybean gene 121 

pool ( Figure 1A ). 122 

The sequencing effort produced ~206.15 billion paired-end high-quality reads (ranging 123 

from 100 to 150 base pairs), totaling ~27.45 trillion base pairs ( Supplementary Table S1 ). This 124 

provided an average sequencing depth of 19.3X. To ensure consistent variant calling, all data 125 

was processed using a unified pipeline based on GATK4. We collated whole-genome 126 

resequencing data for a total of 1,278 soybean lines, representing a global collection of soybeans 127 

from countries including the USA, China, Korea, Japan, Brazil and Australia, among the major 128 

ones ( Figure 1A; Supplementary Table S1; Supplementary Table S2 ). The lines include a 129 

collection of 1,119 cultivated lines and 159 G. soja lines which constituted 701 landraces, 268 130 

cultivar lines, 28 breeder lines, 141 wild-type and 140 others. Short-reads data from these 131 

accessions were mapped to the near-gapless Williams 82 v5 genome (Garg et al., 2023), which 132 

yielded an average mapping coverage of 98.9% of the genome ( Supplementary Table S2 ). 133 

Analysis of the germplasm collection revealed subpopulation admixture in the US national set, 134 

which covers a larger genetic diversity ( Figure 1B ). The variant calling resulted in the 135 

identification of 50.97 million raw SNPs which were further filtered down with GATK hard 136 

filtration parameters to 11.37 million (MAF>0.01) high-quality SNPs for most downstream 137 

population analyses (Figure 1C; Supplementary Table S3 ). Over for all the soybean samples, 138 

the mean SNPs per sample was ~1,664,955. The highest number of SNPs was observed for PI 139 

424007 (3,822,504) and the least for PI 548631 (62,403; Supplementary Table S4 ). The 140 

annotations of these variants showed that the highest proportion of SNPs were present in the 141 

intergenic regions (9,600,721; 84.47%) followed by intronic SNPs (1,346,700; 11.85%; 142 

Supplementary Table S5 ). The missense variations were comparatively lower in frequency 143 

undermining the phenomenon of conserved sequences within the gene boundaries. The analysis 144 

revealed high effect variations such as start lost, stop gained and stop lost were present in fewer 145 

variants and affected a total of 419 genes. Further examining these high-effect variations, we 146 

identified a total of 13,841 high-effect SNPs across the soybean germplasm lines, with the 147 

highest number observed on Chr 18 (1165; Supplementary Table S6 ). Our analysis of high-148 

effect SNPs and InDels, including loss-of-function (LOF) variants, showed that several of these 149 

variants affect important trait-associated genes, such as disease resistance genes, genes 150 

associated with response to cold, and seed composition-associated genes, such as SWEET 151 

transporter genes. Most of these SNPs had low MAF, suggesting that these variants have less 152 

profound deleterious effects in breeding programs. 153

The USDA germplasm collection accessions were classified into six subpopulations, most of 156 

which could be linked to geographic origins ( Supplementary Table S1 ). Four major clusters 157 

were identified including modern varieties of diverse origins from different countries, global 158 

landraces collection, breeding lines and G. soja lines ( Figure 1D; Supplementary Figure S1 ). 159 

We estimated the LD decay across three subpopulations viz landraces, cultivars and wilds to 160 

assess the frequency of recombination ( Supplementary Figure S2 ). Wild population lines 161 

showed the fastest LD decay, dropping rapidly from ~0.4 to below 0.1 within the first 50 Kb and 162 

reaching near-baseline levels (~0.05) by 100-150 Kb, indicating higher recombination rates and 163 

genetic diversity. The landrace population exhibited intermediate LD decay starting around 0.5 164 

and declining more gradually than the wilds. They maintained moderate LD (~0.15-0.2) even at 165 

300 Kb, showing a balanced genetic structure between wild and cultivated collections. The 166 

cultivar population, on the other hand, exhibited the slowest LD decay beginning at the highest 167 

(~0.6) and maintaining elevated LD throughout, still showing substantial LD (~0.2) at 300 Kb 168 

distance indicating extensive linkage blocks and reduced recombination. 169 

> 170

Haplotype map and blocks structure in the wilds, landraces and cultivars sub-populations 171 

Based on the LD-based haplotype analysis, we observed haplotype blocks ranging from 4,378 to 172 

20,874 among the wilds, cultivars and landraces populations ( Supplementary Figure S3 ). The 173 

mean block size was 18.15, 31.10 and 45.36 kb, respectively, in the wilds, cultivars and 174 

landraces. Similarly, the mean SNPs per block were maximum in cultivars (45.4 SNPs/block) 175 

and least amongst the wilds (18.2 SNPs/block). Total coverage ranged from 79.5 to 696.4 Mb 176 

moving from wilds to cultivars. The haplotype block sharing revealed that the blocks most 177 

shared were between the cultivars and landraces (9,825), followed by landraces and G. soja 178 

(1,223) and the least between cultivars and G. soja (582). 179 

> 180

Tag SNPs analysis 181 

Tagged SNPs represent a set of SNPs loosely linked to other SNPs within the LD block. The 182 

tagged SNPs analysis resulted in the identification of ~1.3 million genome-wide SNPs 183 

(Supplementary Figure S4 ). The tag SNPs exhibited an even distribution across common 184 

(>20%), intermediate (10-20%) and low frequency (5-10%) variants, with roughly 350K-480K 185 

SNPs in each major category. Despite LD-based pruning typically removing rare variants, the 186 

analysis retained 519 rare variants (1-5% MAF), indicating a good representation of less frequent 187 

alleles that still contribute to haplotype diversity within the population ( Supplementary Figure 188 

S5 ). Only 3 SNPs with MAF <1% were retained, which is expected since very rare variants are 189 

often population-specific and don't serve as effective tags across diverse populations. The SNPs 190 

distribution suggests that the LD pruning successfully identified a comprehensive set of tag 191 

SNPs that capture both common population-wide variation and intermediate-frequency variants 192 

important for population structure and association studies. With ~1.3 million total tag SNPs 193 

spanning the full MAF spectrum, this dataset should be well-powered for genome-wide 194 

association study (GWAS), population genetics and genomic prediction applications for soybean. 195

GWAS analysis with Global HapMap-II data 197 

Low-density SNP marker arrays have been typically deployed by soybean researchers to study 198 

the genetic control of variations in important traits. Since the release of the first soybean genome 199 

sequence, the density of publicly available markers for association panels has grown from 3K 200 

microsatellite markers to microarray-based markers to hundreds to thousands and tens of 201 

millions of SNPs generated via whole genome resequencing of large germplasm collections. 202 

Several QTLs have been mapped for important traits including the seed composition traits and 203 

have resulted in candidate genes for selection in breeding. The utilization of high-density SNP 204 

arrays presents an advantageous framework for the re-evaluation and refined mapping of QTLs 205 

through integration with previously acquired phenotypic datasets. 206 

We employed the phenotypic data for seed protein content, oil, stem growth habit, 207 

lodging, and palmitic acid obtained from GRIN database for lines part of the GmHapMap-II 208 

collection ( Supplementary Table S7 ). A genome-wide association study conducted with the 209 

new HapMap data identified two QTLs on Chr 20 and Chr 15 for seed protein content ( Figure 210 

2A ). We deployed the SoySNP50K as the genotyping array, using the same phenotypic data, to 211 

investigate the effect of genotype marker density on detecting the causal genomic segment and 212 

underlying genes associated with these traits ( Figure 2B ). For seed protein content, we detected 213 

an additional associated genomic segment on Chr 15 with the HapMap array not detected by the 214 

SoySNP50K array ( Supplementary Table S8 ). The lead SNP on 3,888,132 bp (MAF=0.18) 215 

contained two alleles “G” and “A” with the former allele leading to higher protein content and 216 

the latter allele for lower content ( Figure 2C ). We deployed the LD based approach to narrow 217 

down the genomic region to 3.22 Kb which contained a total of 20 SNPs (3,885,344 - 3,888,571 218 

bp; r2>=0.85) and investigated this region for candidate genes mapping the gene with function 219 

sugar transporter SWEET (Glyma.15G049200; Wm8a.5 ID - Wm82_49162) within the linkage 220 

block identified on Chr 15 ( Figure 2D; Supplementary Table S8 ). Similarly, for oil, we 221 

mapped the same genomic region of 3.22 Kb (3,885,344-3,888,571 bp; r2>=0.85) on Chr 15 for 222 

association with oil as mapped for protein content ( Supplementary Figure S6; Supplementary 223 

Table S9 ). The lead SNP 3,888,099 bp (MAF=0.18) was part of the same LD block as crude 224 

protein content and harbored two alleles “T” and “C”. The gene expression analysis of several 225 

soybean samples revealed that this gene was specifically expressed in the seed coat and seed 226 

tissues, with relatively little expression in other major tissues ( Figure 2E ). Overall, the newly 227 

developed HapMap increases the power to detect causal genes at higher marker resolution. 228 

GWAS analysis was also conducted for other important domestication-associated traits, such as 229 

lodging, stem growth habit and seed composition trait palmitic acid. A major QTL region on Chr 230 

19 was identified for lodging resistance with the lead SNP anchored on 48,319,877 bp and two 231 

alleles A and C were responsible for lodging and no-lodging, respectively ( Supplementary 232 

Figure S7; Supplementary Table S10 ). LD-based analysis revealed a region harboring nine 233 

candidate genes (Gm_Wm82_54928 - Gm_Wm82_54936) with functional annotation as 234 

flowering locus protein, F-box/LRR plant protein and iron-sulfur cluster assembly protein, etc. 235 

For the palmitic acid, we identified a major effect QTL on Chr 5 with the lead SNP position of 236

Superior haplotypes for protein content 242 

We investigated the genic variations for gene Glyma.15G049200 to analyse the haplotype 243 

patterns across the germplasm collection. A total of seven polymorphic sites were identified 244 

within the gene region, including one 3-bp InDel (3,888,412 bp) and multiple SNPs. Notably, 245 

one SNP (3,888,459 bp; G->A) was located in the coding region. Haplotype analysis revealed 246 

eleven distinct haplotypes, though five (H1-H5) accounted for majority of the variations 247 

(Supplementary Table S13 ). The dominant haplotype H1 was present in 822 lines, while H2 248 

occurred in 173 lines ( Figure 3A ). The remaining haplotypes were considerably rarer, with H3 249 

found in 53 lines, H4 in 12 lines, and H5 in 9 lines. Haplotypes H6-H11 were each present in 250 

fewer than 5 accessions. 251 

> 252

To identify functional variants, we tested for associations between haplotypes and protein 253 

content. This analysis revealed striking phenotypic differences among haplotypes. H2 showed 254 

significantly elevated protein levels compared to H1 (p < 2.22e-16), with H3 exhibiting 255 

intermediate values (Figure 3B, 3C; Supplementary Table S14 ). The protein content 256 

differences followed a clear gradient, with H1 associated with the lowest values, H2 and H3 with 257 

progressively higher levels, and the rare haplotypes showing variable patterns. We also examined 258 

oil content for the same haplotypes and found that H1 corresponded with higher oil levels while 259 

H2 showed reduced oil content, consistent with the well-documented negative correlation 260 

between seed protein and oil at this locus (Fleige et al., 2022; Goettel et al., 2023; 261 

Supplementary Figure S10 ). Geographic distribution of haplotypes revealed strong regional 262 

patterns. Chinese germplasm (n=418) displayed the greatest haplotype diversity, harboring H1 263 

through H8, though H1 remained predominant at 76.8% ( Supplementary Figure S11 ). In 264 

contrast, U.S. accessions (n=156) were nearly fixed for H1 (94.2%), with only a minor 265 

representation of H2 followed by H3. Japanese and South Korean materials showed intermediate 266 

diversity levels, with H1 frequencies of 55.9% and 41.7%, respectively, and substantial 267 

representation of H2 and H3. Several smaller collections from Brazil, Canada, Taiwan, 268 

Indonesia, Moldova, and Vietnam were completely fixed for H1 ( Figure 3D ). This geographic 269 

structure suggests that different breeding programs or environmental conditions have maintained 270 

varying levels of diversity at this locus. 271

Additive Effects of Two QTLs Control Seed Protein and Oil Content 290 

Two major QTLs on Chrs 15 and 20 were identified controlling seed protein and oil content with 291 

additive effects. Analysis of homozygous genotypes (n=80-671 per class) revealed that Chr 20 is 292 

the primary QTL, contributing +2.55% to protein content per Alt/Alt genotype, while Chr 15 acts 293 

as a minor modifier with a +0.65% effect ( Figure 4A and 4B ). Both QTLs exhibited a strong 294 

negative correlation between protein and oil, with each 1% increase in protein associated with 295 

approximately 2% decrease in oil content. The reference genotype (Ref/Ref at both loci, 70% of 296 

germplasm) produced the highest oil content (18.3%) with the lowest protein (43.8%), while the 297 

double alternate genotype (Alt/Alt at both loci, 8% of germplasm) showed the inverse pattern 298 

with 47.0% protein and 12.3% oil. Two intermediate genotypes (Chr 15=Alt/Alt × Chr 299 

20=Ref/Ref and Chr 15=Ref/Ref × Chr 20=Alt/Alt) offered balanced seed composition with 300 

44.7-46.6% protein and 14.9-16.8% oil, representing viable targets for breeding programs 301 

requiring moderate improvements in both traits. A simple additive model (R²=0.135) accurately 302 

predicted protein content across all four homozygous genotype combinations, indicating these 303 

QTLs act independently and are suitable for marker-assisted selection (MAS) ( Figure 4C ). The 304 

substantial within-genotype variation suggests additional minor QTLs contribute to the observed 305 

phenotypic diversity in seed composition traits. 306

Wild soybean ( G. soja , n=146) exhibited near-complete fixation for H1 (rhg1-c) at 99.3%, with 339 

H2 detected at a very low frequency of 0.7% and complete absence of H3 and H4 haplotypes, 340 

indicating that resistance haplotypes are largely absent from the wild progenitor species. 341 

Cultivated soybean ( G. max , n=1052) showed H1 at 90.5%, H2 at 6.6%, H3 at 2.9%, and H4 at 342 

0.1%. The markedly lower frequency of alternative resistance haplotypes in wild germplasm 343 

compared to cultivated soybean suggests their predominantly post-domestication origin, either 344 

through spontaneous mutation during cultivation or selection of extremely rare variants not 345 

captured in the wild sample. Examination of haplotype frequencies across different germplasm 346 

categories revealed the impact of breeding selection on haplotype distribution ( Figure 5D ). 347 

Landrace accessions (n=895) displayed H1 at 90.5%, H2 at 6.4%, H3 at 3%, and H4 at 0.1%. 348 

This composition represents the baseline diversity present in traditional farmer-selected 349 

germplasm prior to the advent of modern systematic breeding. Modern cultivars (n=157) 350 

exhibited H1 at 90.4% and H2 at 7.6%, with H3 at 1.9% and a complete absence of H4. The H2 351 

frequency in cultivars represents a ~1.2-fold increase compared to landrace germplasm, 352 

indicating enrichment through modern breeding selection for SCN resistance. Across the 353 

cultivated germplasm collection, approximately 9-10% carried alternative resistance haplotypes 354 

(H2, H3, or H4), while ~90% retained the susceptible H1 (rhg1-c) reference type. 355

occurred in different subpopulations of the germplasm collection. To evaluate how human 470

mainly observed in the G. soja lines. 521

Discussion 538 

Using whole-genome sequencing data from a large collection of 1,278 soybean accessions, we 539 

developed a global soybean haplotype map that provides a comprehensive resource for genetic 540 

studies and genomics-enabled breeding. Since the release of the first soybean reference genome, 541 

several high-quality soybean genomes have been furnished owing to long-read sequencing, 542 

demonstrating significant improvements in genome quality (Schmutz et al., 2010; Garg et al., 543 

2023). Although several earlier variation and haplotype maps have been generated for soybean, 544 

these efforts primarily examined local germplasm panels and relied on older, less contiguous 545 

genome assemblies (Kim et al., 2021, Torkamaneh et al., 2021, Valliyodan et al., 2021, Yang et 546 

al., 2022, Liu et al., 2022). By assembling a diverse panel of 1,278 globally distributed cultivated 547 

and G. soja accessions and aligning all data to a modern high-quality genome, we produced a 548 

unified and high quality catalog of 11.37 million SNPs and 2.05 million InDels. Population 549 

structure analyses clearly separated domesticated and wild accessions and further resolved wild 550 

soybean into geographically defined clusters, consistent with patterns seen in other major crops. 551 

Linkage disequilibrium patterns confirmed reduced recombination in cultivated relative to wild 552 

soybean (Song et al., 2015), with wild accessions displaying rapid LD decay and contributing 553 

diversity absent from cultivated germplasm (Khan et al., 2024). 554

> (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission. The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted March 26, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.24.713798 doi: bioRxiv preprint 21

Sequencing reads from all 1,278 accessions were processed through a standardized 716 

bioinformatics workflow built around GATK4 to generate a comprehensive catalog of genomic 717 

variants. Raw paired-end reads of 100-150 bp length were first subjected to quality trimming 718 

using Trimmomatic v0.39, which removed low-quality reads and clipped bases falling below 719 

quality thresholds (Bolger et al., 2014). Read quality was evaluated using the Raspberry tool v0.2 720 

(Katta et al., 2015). Quality-filtered reads were subsequently aligned to the G. max reference 721 

genome version 5 using BWA-MEM v0.7.19 (Li et al., 2013; Garg et al., 2023). Resulting SAM 722 

alignment files were converted, coordinate-sorted, and stored in BAM format using the Picard 723 

toolkit v3.0.0 (Picard toolkit, 2019). Per-sample variant detection was carried out using the 724 

HaplotypeCaller module of GATK v4.2.6.1 (McKenna et al., 2010). The resulting variant dataset 725 

was subsequently phased and missing genotypes were imputed using BEAGLE v5.1 (Browning 726 

and Browning, 2016). Functional annotation of identified variants was performed with SnpEff 727 

v3.0 (Cingolani et al., 2012). For phylogenetic reconstruction, genome-wide SNPs were pre-728 

filtered using SNPhylo (Lee et al., 2014) with the parameters -m 0.01, -M 0.01, and -l 0.5 to 729 

reduce redundancy, and the retained SNPs were then used to infer a maximum-likelihood 730 

phylogeny with FastTree2 v2.1.10 SSE3 (Price et al., 2010) using the -nt -gtr settings. 731 

> 732

Phenotype collection and metadata 733 

Phenotypic data for protein content, oil content, stem growth habit, lodging score, palmitic acid 734 

concentration, and SCN HG type reactions used in this study were sourced from the USDA 735 

Soybean Germplasm Collection general evaluation trials, which encompass morphological, 736 

agronomic, and seed composition datasets. These field-based evaluations were carried out across 737 

multiple locations stratified by maturity group, with some accessions evaluated over multiple 738 

growing seasons. Total protein and oil concentrations were quantified using methods appropriate 739 

to seed coat pigmentation. For accessions with yellow seed coats, protein and oil content were 740 

determined by near-infrared reflectance spectroscopy applied to whole seed samples. Accessions 741 

with dark or pigmented seed coats were analyzed separately, with total protein quantified via the 742 

Kjeldahl method and seed oil content measured using the Butt method (Bandillo et al., 2015). 743 

> 744

Genome-wide association analysis 745 

Genome-wide association analysis was conducted using two complementary statistical 746 

approaches, the mixed linear model (MLM) and FarmCPU, both executed within the rMVP 747 

framework (Yin et al., 2021). Associations were tested for protein content, oil content, stem 748 

growth habit, lodging, and palmitic acid concentration using imputed genotype data across all 749 

accessions with available phenotypic records. To account for confounding due to population 750 

stratification, the first three principal components derived from genome-wide marker data were 751 

incorporated as covariates. The genome-wide significance threshold was set at a Bonferroni-752 

corrected value of 0.05 divided by the total number of markers tested. 753

Linkage disequilibrium and haplotype analyses 755 

Linkage disequilibrium heatmaps were constructed using LDBlockShow (Dong et al., 2020), 756 

computing both R² and D' statistics, with haplotype block boundaries defined using the Gabriel 757 

et al. (2002) confidence interval method for D'-based visualization. Input for the accompanying 758 

Manhattan plot was derived from FarmCPU-based protein GWAS results obtained from filtered, 759 

unimputed SNP data. Haplotype block structure was characterized using the confidence interval 760 

approach of Gabriel et al. (2002) as implemented in PLINK v1.9 (Purcell et al., 2007) with 761 

default settings. Pairwise R², D', and allele frequency estimates were extracted for relevant 762 

genomic sites using PLINK v1.9, whereby sites in linkage disequilibrium with the GWAS-763 

identified SNP were first identified using the --show-tags flag with a --tag-r2 threshold of 0.9, 764 

followed by generation of detailed pairwise statistics for the tagged sites using the --r2 dprime 765 

option. The gene-based haplotype analysis was performed with geneHapR package (Zhang et al., 766 

2023). Functional impacts of nucleotide variants on proteins were assessed using SIFT 4G 767 

v.2.0.0, with variants scoring below 0.05 classified as putatively deleterious (Kumar et al., 2009). 768 

> 769

Multi-omics genomic prediction for SCN trait 770 

Genomic prediction was performed using a multi-omics machine learning approach. Haplotype 771 

features were combined with structural variation (CNV) data to create a comprehensive feature 772 

matrix. Population stratification was corrected using principal component analysis. Model 773 

selection included seven machine learning algorithms with cross-validation to identify optimal 774 

predictors. 775
