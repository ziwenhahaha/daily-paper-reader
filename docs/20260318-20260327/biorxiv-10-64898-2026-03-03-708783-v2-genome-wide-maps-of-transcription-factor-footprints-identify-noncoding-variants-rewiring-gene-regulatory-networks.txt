## Abstract 13 

Genome-wide association studies have identified millions of noncoding loci linked to human 14 

traits, yet how these variants alter gene regulation remains a major challenge, particularly for 15 

rare variants where whole-genome sequencing cohorts and high-resolution functional 16 

annotations remain limited. Here we show that single-molecule deaminase footprinting 17 

(FOODIE) in K562 cells captures up to 103-fold heritability enrichment for erythroid traits 18 

despite covering 0.12% of the genome. We introduce varTFBridge, integrating FOODIE 19 

footprinting with AlphaGenome variant effect prediction to identify causal noncoding variants 20 

altering transcription factor (TF)-mediated regulation. Applied to 490,640 UK Biobank 21 

genomes across 13 erythrocyte traits, varTFBridge prioritises 113 high-confidence regulatory 22 

variants (104 common, 9 rare), encompassing 2,173 linkages along the variant–TF binding– 23 

gene–trait cascade across 64 TFs and 108 genes. varTFBridge recapitulates rs112233623 24 

and resolves its mechanism: GATA1/TAL1 co-binding disruption at a CCND3 enhancer 25 

altering red blood cell count and volume. 26

## Introduction 32 

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified millions of trait-associated 33 

variants, yet most reside in noncoding regions and remain functionally uncharacterized 1–4 .34 

Fine-mapping has improved the localization of causal variants, but the underlying regulatory 35 

mechanisms are often unresolved 5–9 . Moreover, rare variants, which can pinpoint causal 36 

genes with large effect sizes 10 , have been studied primarily in coding regions owing to the 37 

difficulty of interpreting noncoding effects 11 . Characterizing noncoding variants and 38 

establishing functional links between these loci, their target genes, and the regulatory 39 

landscape thus remains a central challenge. 40 

Transcription factors (TFs) bind specific noncoding genomic regions to regulate gene 41 

expression 12,13 , and variants within TF binding motifs can disrupt or create binding sites, 42 

thereby altering target gene expression and contributing to disease 14–16 . Understanding 43 

these mechanisms has direct therapeutic relevance: the gene-editing therapy Casgevy 17 

44 

targets a BCL11A enhancer identified through GWAS to reactivate fetal hemoglobin for the 45 

treatment of β-thalassemia 18–20 . The release of whole-genome sequencing (WGS) data from 46 

nearly 500,000 UK Biobank participants 21 , encompassing approximately 1.5 billion variants, 47 

now provides an opportunity to systematically investigate how noncoding regulatory variants 48 

modulate disease risk. 49 

Recently, single-molecule deaminase footprinting (FOODIE) has enabled genome-wide 50 

measurement of TF binding at near-single-base resolution 22 providing direct, cell-type-51 

specific maps of TF occupancy that can pinpoint the exact binding sites affected by 52 

noncoding variants 23 . Concurrently, AlphaGenome 24,25 , a deep-learning model trained on 53 

thousands of epigenomic profiles, has demonstrated strong performance in predicting how 54 

single-nucleotide variants alter chromatin accessibility, histone modifications, and TF binding 55 

across cell types 26 . Together, FOODIE’s ability to resolve TF binding at base-pair resolution 56 

and AlphaGenome’s capacity to predict variant effects across multiple epigenomic layers 57 

offer complementary strengths for decoding the regulatory mechanisms of noncoding 58 

variants. However, no integrative framework currently combines these approaches. 59 

Here we present varTFBridge, a framework that integrates high-resolution FOODIE 60 

footprinting data with AlphaGenome variant effect prediction to prioritize causal noncoding 61 

variants across the allele frequency spectrum. The framework identifies trait-associated 62 

variants within TF footprints through GWAS fine-mapping for common variants and, for the 63 

first time, footprint-wide burden testing for rare variants, then maps their functional 64 

consequences using motif-based binding prediction, TF footprint–gene linking, and 65 

Alphagenome variant effect scoring. We first show that FOODIE footprints outperform 66

## Results 77 

The varTFBridge framework 78 

We developed varTFBridge, an integrated workflow that uses whole-genome sequencing 79 

data from 490,640 UK Biobank individuals 21 to identify TF-mediated noncoding regulatory 80 

variants for blood traits in a cell-type-specific context. The workflow proceeds in two stages: 81 

(1) identification of trait-associated common and rare variants, and (2) functional dissection 82 

of putative causal variants at the level of TF binding sites and target genes. 83 

In the variant association stage (Fig. 1c–d), we performed GWAS followed by genome-wide 84 

fine-mapping (GWFM) with SBayesRC 27 for common variants (MAF ≥ 0.1%) to pinpoint 85 

putative causal variants within cell-type-specific TF footprints (Methods). For rare variants 86 

(MAF < 0.1%), we conducted footprint-based burden tests by collapsing variants within each 87 

footprint, followed by leave-one-variant-out analysis 28 to isolate the specific driver variant 88 

within each significant footprint (Methods). 89 

In the functional dissection stage, we used FIMO 29 to scan JASPAR 30 TF binding motifs 90 

within footprints and assessed how putative causal variants affect predicted binding affinity 91 

(termed VAR2TFBS; Methods). We adapted the ABC-Max model 8,31 by integrating TF 92 

footprints (termed ABC-FP-Max; Methods) to link variants to target genes (Fig. 1e). Finally, 93 

we employed AlphaGenome to predict variant effects across multiple epigenomic layers, 94 

including histone modifications, TF binding, and chromatin accessibility (Methods). We then 95 

integrated these predictions through convergent-evidence filtering, requiring concordant 96 

support from motif disruption, TF expression, and variant-to-gene linkage, to produce a high-97 

confidence variant–TF binding–gene–trait regulatory resource (Methods). We applied this 98 

framework to 13 erythroid traits in K562 cells. 99

FOODIE footprints improve TF binding measurement and enhancer–gene prediction 101 

We performed FOODIE single-molecule footprinting in K562 and GM12878 cells using an 102 

updated kit (Vazyme TD721), generating genome-wide TF footprint profiles at near-single-103 

base resolution (Fig. 1a–b). To evaluate FOODIE’s capacity to capture TF binding alongside 104 

existing assays, we applied the footprint-calling protocol to K562 FOODIE data (Methods), 105 

yielding 188,484 footprints with a median length of 19 bp. Comparison with DNase and 106 

ATAC-seq data in K562 from ENCODE showed that 91.8% of footprints overlap at least one 107 

chromatin accessibility peak and 61% are supported by both, consistent with TF footprints 108 

predominantly residing in open chromatin (Fig. 2a). 109 

We compared FOODIE, DNase, and ATAC-derived footprints against K562 ChIP-seq data 110 

(249 TFs; 317 ENCODE experiments 32 ) using fold excess overlap 14 (Fig. 2b). DNase and 111 

ATAC footprints were called using footprint-tools 12 and HINT 33 , respectively. FOODIE 112 

footprints exhibited the highest excess overlap with ChIP-seq (8.3×, SD 2.2), followed by 113 

DNase (7.9×, SD 2.1), ATAC footprints (6.8×, SD 1.7), and ATAC peaks (6.8×, SD 1.7), 114 

confirming that FOODIE more accurately captures cell-type-specific TF binding. 115 

To link variants within TF footprints to target genes, we adapted the Activity-by-Contact 116 

(ABC) framework 31 to develop ABC-FP. Because individual FOODIE footprints are narrow 117 

(median ~15 bp), we extended them by 50 bp and merged nearby footprints to form 70,670 118 

regulatory elements (median length 534 bp) suitable for element-level activity quantification. 119 

Following the ABC protocol 34 , we calculated element activity (ATAC-seq and H3K27ac ChIP-120 

seq) and Hi-C contact frequency to all genes within 5 Mb, weighting each footprint’s 121 

regulatory effect by its chromatin contact frequency with target gene promoters (Methods). 122 

We benchmarked ABC-FP against a K562 CRISPR perturbation dataset 35,36 containing 123 

7,238 element–gene pairs and 418 validated positives. FOODIE footprints were enriched 124 

within CRISPR-validated enhancers (odds ratio 3.51; Supplementary Figure 1). ABC-FP with 125 

ATAC, H3K27ac, and K562-specific Hi-C achieved the highest accuracy (AUPRC = 0.649; 126 

Fig. 2c). Because cell-type-specific Hi-C data are unavailable for most cell types, we also 127 

tested a cell-type-agnostic reference Hi-C map 8; ABC-FP still achieved AUPRC = 0.597, 128 

surpassing the original ATAC-based ABC score (0.58). 129 

These results demonstrate that FOODIE footprints outperform ATAC-seq in capturing TF 130 

binding and predicting enhancer–gene interactions. However, ChIP-seq peaks are not a true 131 

gold standard for TF binding 37 , as peak regions (200–500 bp) exceed canonical footprints 132 

(6–20 bp) 14 , potentially underestimating enrichment. We therefore turned to heritability 133 

analyses to assess whether FOODIE footprints are enriched for trait-relevant genetic 134 

variation. 135

FOODIE footprints are disproportionately enriched in the heritability of erythrocyte 138 

traits 139 

Given the erythroid differentiation potential of K562 cells 38 , we examined heritability 140 

enrichment of K562 FOODIE footprints across 28 UK Biobank blood traits (13 erythroid, 4 141 

platelet, 9 myeloid, 2 lymphoid) using stratified LD score regression (S-LDSC) with the 142 

baseline-LD model v2.0 39 (Methods). We benchmarked FOODIE against DNase-seq and 143 

ATAC-seq footprints, as well as ATAC-seq peaks. Following previous study 14 , we extended 144 

TF footprint annotations by 50 bp to ensure sufficient SNP coverage for robust S-LDSC 145 

estimation; ATAC-seq peaks were used without extension. 146 

K562 FOODIE footprints yielded the highest heritability enrichment in 28 of 29 blood traits, 147 

significantly exceeding ATAC and DNase footprints in erythroid and myeloid traits (Fig. 2d). 148 

FOODIE showed median 40-fold and 21-fold enrichment for erythroid and myeloid traits, 149 

respectively, compared with 12-fold for lymphoid traits, consistent with the erythroid identity 150 

of K562. FOODIE footprint regions comprise 0.52% of the genome yet exhibit 71-fold 151 

enrichment for mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration (MCHC), explaining 40.27% of 152 

the trait’s heritability, compared with 31-fold (DNase footprint) and 23-fold (ATAC footprint) 153 

(Supplementary Data 6). 154

compared with ~71-fold after extension), but enrichment for non-erythroid traits, particularly 157

## Discussion 347 

Despite nearly two decades of GWAS, functionally characterizing noncoding variants and 348 

mapping them to target genes remain a major bottleneck. This challenge is compounded for 349 

rare variants, which require higher-resolution functional annotations than coding-region 350 

burden tests. Here, we show that FOODIE footprints provide the necessary resolution: they 351 

improve TF binding measurement and target gene prediction over ATAC-seq and DNase-seq 352 

and yield substantially higher heritability enrichment for erythroid traits in K562 cells, with 353 

raw footprints covering 0.12% of the genome yet capturing up to 103-fold enrichment. 354 

Building on these properties, varTFBridge integrates FOODIE footprinting with motif 355 

scanning, enhancer–gene linkage, and deep-learning variant effect prediction to map 356 

noncoding variants to TF-mediated regulatory mechanisms across the allele frequency 357 

spectrum. 358

recover known biology and generate testable regulatory hypotheses. 364 

varTFBridge is, to our knowledge, the first framework to systematically investigate both 365 

common and rare noncoding variants through TF footprinting. The combination of 366 

VAR2TFBS, ABC-FP-Max, and AlphaGenome provides synergistic advantages. While 367 

AlphaGenome cannot predict the effects of distal variants on target genes, ABC-FP-Max 368 

provides robust variant-to-gene links up to 5 Mb away. Conversely, AlphaGenome’s TF 369 

binding predictions are limited to 128 bp resolution due to ChIP-seq constraints, whereas 370 

VAR2TFBS achieves base-resolution predictions through FIMO motif scanning within 371 

FOODIE footprints. When these independent approaches converge on the same prediction, 372 

confidence in the regulatory hypothesis increases substantially, making it a high-priority 373 

target for experimental follow-up. 374 

Our study has several limitations that highlight areas for future work. First, varTFBridge only 375 

considers variants residing within TF binding sites, thereby missing variants flanking the core 376 

motif that might also affect TF binding affinity. Second, some co-regulators do not bind 377 

directly to the DNA sequence; our model remains limited in predicting this type of co-378 

regulation, though a recent model, Chromnitron 49 , might help bridge this gap. Third, 379 

varTFBridge assumes a single causal gene per variant, although enhancers containing 380 

disease variants often have highly pleiotropic effects. Fourth, AlphaGenome can only predict 381 

a limited number of TFs, which hinders the extension of our framework to other tissues and 382 

cell lines. Integrating ChromBPNet 50 into our framework could potentially address this 383 

limitation, as it can be easily trained on FOODIE data from new cell lines and tissues to 384 

predict variant effects. Lastly, while our multi-evidence approach substantially increases the 385 

credibility of predictions, the variant–TF binding–gene hypotheses generated by varTFBridge 386 

remain computational predictions that require experimental validation. We provide this 387 

resource as a prioritized starting point, not a substitute, for targeted functional studies. 388 

In summary, our approach paves the way for a comprehensive mapping of noncoding 389 

variants across the allele frequency spectrum onto TF-mediated gene regulatory networks. 390

Cell Preparation 403 

Human leukemia cell line K562 and human lymphoblastoid cell line GM12878 were grown 404

FOODIE library preparation 409 

The FOODIE libraries were constructed using the commercial Hyperactive FOODIE Library 410 

Prep Kit (cat. no. TD721, Vazyme) following the manufacturer’s instruction, which is similar 411 

to our previously reported protocol 22 . Briefly, approximately 50,000 cells were counted, 412 

washed with TW Buffer, and centrifuged. The cell pellet was resuspended in pre-chilled Lysis 413 

Buffer to facilitate permeabilization. Following permeabilization, nuclei were collected via 414 

centrifugation and resuspended in Tagmentation Mix for the Tn5 transposition reaction. The 415 

reaction was incubated at 37 °C for 30 min on a thermomixer with shaking at 800 rpm. 416 

Subsequently, the tagmentation reaction was terminated by adding pre-chilled Wash Buffer, 417 

followed by centrifugation. The isolated nuclei were then resuspended in a Deamination Mix 418 

supplemented with TruePrep DddB Enzyme (cat. no. S802-01, Vazyme) and incubated at 419 

37 °C for 10 min. The reaction was halted by the addition of Stop Buffer. Finally, DNA was 420 

purified using the provided magnetic beads, followed by PCR amplification using the kit-421 

supplied DNA polymerase and primers. 422

deduplicated with Bismark (v0.24.2) 52 . Customized scripts were then applied to perform 426

Genome-wide association testing of erythroid traits in UK Biobank 438 

We applied the same GWAS framework as described in our previous analysis 53 for UK 439 

Biobank. In addition to UK Biobank’s standard central quality control, we restricted analyses 440 

to participants of white European genetic ancestry. This was determined using k-means 441 

clustering of the first four principal components derived from genome-wide SNP genotypes. 442 

Individuals assigned to the white European cluster, but who self-reported a non-European 443

removed variants with minor allele frequency (MAF) < 0.1%. Genome-wide association 447

Genome-wide fine-mapping for identifying credible set variants 459 

Credible set variants were derived from genome-wide fine-mapping (GWFM) with the 460

ABC-FP-Max predictions for linking variants to the target gene 508 

To link variants to their target genes using ABC-FP-Max, we first adapted the existing ABC 509 

model (accessible at https://github.com/broadinstitute/ABC-Enhancer-Gene-Prediction) to 510 

predict footprint–gene connections within specific cell types, thereby constructing ABC-FP 511 

models. The original ABC model was designed to link chromatin accessibility elements to 512 

genes, initially by calling peaks in chromatin accessibility data using MACS2. In contrast, for 513 

connecting FOODIE footprints to genes, we replaced the peak-calling step with FOODIE 514 

footprint calling. Following footprint identification, we extended each footprint by 50-bp on 515 

either side and merged them into a single element, recognizing that closely located footprints 516 

might co-regulate the same genes and larger windows can better count reads in ATAC-seq 517 

and H3K27ac ChIP-seq. Subsequently, we followed the original ABC models to estimate 518 

chromatin accessibility from ATAC-seq data on these footprint-based elements and to 519 

assess chromatin conformation (Hi-C) between these elements and the promoters of 520 

potential target genes, as previously described. Specifically, the activity of each footprint-521 

based element is calculated based on ATAC-seq experiments and then quantile-normalized 522 

to the distribution observed in K562 cells. The footprint-gene contact is determined using 523 

cell-type specific Hi-C data from 4DN (4D Nucleome Data Portal). Finally, we compute the 524 

ABC-Max score for each footprint-gene pair as the product of activity and contact, 525 

normalized by the product of activity and contact for all elements within 5 Mb of that gene. 526

footprint–gene connections with an ABC-FP score ≥ 0.015 and all distal or proximal 529 

promoter–gene connections with an ABC score ≥ 0.1 (following the ABC-Max model setting). 530

VAR2TFBS for predicting variant effects on TF binding sites 544 

Quantifying the impact of genetic variants within FOODIE footprints necessitates assessing 545

identifies statistically significant hits by scanning Position Weight Matrix (PWM) of a certain 548 

TF against one-hot encoded DNA sequences. This process involves convolving PWMs 549 

across DNA sequences and converting the resulting scores into p-values using an exact 550 

background distribution derived from the PWM. In this study, we collected the PWMs from 551 

JASPAR 2024 core non-redundant vertebrates 552 

(https://jaspar.elixir.no/download/data/2024/CORE/JASPAR2024_CORE_vertebrates_non-553 

redundant_pfms_meme.txt). To assess transcription factor (TF) binding within a FOODIE 554 

footprint, the sequence is first extended by 30 bp on each side. This extended reference 555 

sequence is then subjected to FIMO scanning, with TF binding sites identified by considering 556 

all Position Weight Matrices (PWMs) exhibiting a motif p-value less than 1 × 10 !).557

(p-value < 1 × 10 !)) in the alternate sequence; increase, where an existing TFBS shows a 561 

decreased (more significant) p-value in the alternate sequence; disruption, defined as an 562 

existing TFBS in the reference sequence having a p-value ≥ 1 × 10 !) in the alternate 563
