Introduction 40 

41 The CRISPR/Cas system has revolutionized biological research by enabling precise genome 42 manipulation with unprecedented ease and efficiency ( Le Cong et al., 2013, Mali et al., 2013 ). 43 Advanced programmable genome editing technologies, including CRISPR-based Base Editing 44 (BE) and the more recently developed Prime Editing (PE), have substantially expanded the 45 scope of precise nuclear genome engineering ( Gaudelli et al., 2017; Anzalone et al., 2019 ). In 46 parallel, CRISPR-free systems such as RNA-free DddA-derived cytosine base editors (DdCBE) 47 have enabled targeted mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) editing, further broadening the landscape of 48 programmable genetic manipulation ( Mok et al., 2020 ). Despite these advances, the detection 49 and classification of editing events, including insertions and deletions (indels), complex 50 rearrangements, and off-target effects, often require specialized computational expertise and 51 access to advanced bioinformatics tools. This creates a bottleneck for many laboratories, 52 particularly those who are not using genome editing approaches on a regular basis. 53 Existing tools for analyzing CRISPR/Cas editing data typically require local installation and 54 command-line execution, often distributed via Python environments, containerized solutions 55 such as Docker, or integrated into workflow managers like Nextflow ( Clement et al., 2019; Amit 56 

et al., 2021; Sanvicente-García et al., 2023; Di Tommaso et al., 2017 ). While powerful, these 57 approaches present practical barriers for users without computational expertise and can 58 complicate rapid exploratory analyses. Web-based implementations of some of these tools 59 (Clement et al., 2019, Park et al., 2017 ) provide greater accessibility but are often constrained in 60 throughput, for example by limiting the number of samples processed per run or supporting only 61 single-sample analysis. 62

CleanFinder: a modular framework for genome editing outcome analysis 99 The modular architecture of CleanFinder is summarized in Figure 1. The platform is organized 100 into four functional layers: user-defined options, processing logic, analytical outputs, and 101 auxiliary modules. Input parameters include organism or reference locus, optional gRNA 102 information, and customizable alignment settings. Sequencing reads derived from any major 103 NGS platform (Illumina, PacBio, or ONT) are automatically parsed and directed to biologically 104 informed analysis modes (e.g., Cas9/Cas12, BE, or PE). A default high-resolution alignment 105 workflow is complemented by a heuristic turbo mode for exploratory and fast analyses. 106 The results layer provides structured summaries of editing outcomes ( Figure 2 ), including 107 genotype distributions, indel spectra, frameshift classification, and repair pathway inference via 108 RIMA and Repairome signatures ( Wimberger et al 2023, Lopez de Alba et al., 2025 ). Outputs 109 are accessible through interactive visualization and exportable reports. Additional modules 110 extend interpretability through allele-aware dropout detection and an integrated genome viewer 111 that contextualizes editing events within gene structure. A companion Python implementation 112 enables scalable batch processing. 113

Anchor-Guided Semi-Global Alignment for High-Resolution Genotyping 133 134 CleanFinder employs a constrained semi-global alignment strategy ( Figure 2A ), a dynamic 135 programming approach in which terminal gaps are not penalized ( Durbin et al., 1998 ). Classical 136 local alignment approaches such as Smith–Waterman are optimized to identify the highest-137 scoring subsequence between two sequences, but may truncate flanking context and fragment 138 complex editing events ( Smith et al., 1981 ). Conversely, strict global alignment enforces end-to-139 end matching and does not naturally accommodate terminal overhangs, which are common in 140 long-read sequencing data ( Needleman et al., 1970 ). 141 142

Cross-Platform Validation Across Diverse Nuclease Systems 185 186 To evaluate CleanFinder across distinct CRISPR nucleases and editing strategies, we analyzed 187 Cas9- and Cas12-mediated genome editing experiments in human iPSCs, encompassing both 188 knockout (KO) and knock-in (KI) designs ( Figure S1, S2 ). Across both Cas9 and Cas12 189 systems, CleanFinder consistently resolved deletion size, frame status, microhomology length, 190 and PAM proximity. Heatmap visualizations demonstrate genotype quantification across 191 independent clones and editing efficiency ( Figure S1A ). Representative aligned sequences 192 illustrate MMEJ-associated deletions in VHL and DMD knockout experiments, with locus-193 dependent differences in microhomology usage and PAM-proximal versus PAM-distal deletion 194 patterns that reflect nuclease cleavage architecture. In contrast, knock-in experiments at the 195 

LRPPRC and XPA loci showed precise homology-directed repair (HDR) signatures with 196 accurate reconstruction of intended integrations and minimal indel-associated microhomology 197 (Figure S1B ). These analyses confirm that CleanFinder supports structured classification of 198 genome editing outcomes across nuclease systems and distinguishes NHEJ/MMEJ-driven 199 knockout events from HDR-mediated knock-in designs in iPSCs. 200 201

k-mer–guided pre-alignment filtering improves computational efficiency 237 238 To reduce computational overhead during read alignment, we implemented a k-mer–guided pre-239 alignment filter that restricts full semi-global alignment to reads containing short exact matches 240 to the target locus ( Figure S4 ). As illustrated schematically ( Fig. S4A ) , reads lacking the 241 required k-mer seed are discarded prior to dynamic programming alignment, whereas candidate 242 reads proceed to full classification. This reduces computational load by limiting alignment to 243 locus-relevant reads. 244 Benchmarking demonstrated a substantial increase in processing speed for both Illumina and 245 ONT datasets ( Fig. S4B ). For Illumina reads, median throughput increased from approximately 246 8.4 thousand reads per second (normal mode) to 18 thousand reads per second (fast mode). 247 For ONT datasets, throughput increased from approximately 0.5 thousand reads per second to 248 1.89 thousand reads per second. The magnitude of speed improvement was therefore 249 particularly pronounced for long-read data. 250 These values represent empirical benchmarks under representative conditions. Processing 251 speed depends on dataset characteristics, including read length, file size, and, critically, the 252 proportion of reads unrelated to the targeted locus. In multiplexed or off-target–rich datasets, the 253 k-mer filter discards unrelated reads early in the workflow, leading to disproportionately greater 254 acceleration. 255 256

14 299 Prime editing (PE) enables programmable introduction of precise nucleotide substitutions, 300 insertions, and deletions without relying on co-transfection of donor templates ( Anzalone et al., 301 

2019, Chen et al., 2023, Binder et al., 2024 ). Depending on configuration, PE can operate in a 302 nickase-based format (e.g., PE2/PE3) or in nuclease-enabled forms that increase editing 303 efficiency but also generate more complex allelic architectures ( Adikusuma et al., 2021, Peterka 304 

et al., 2022 ). These configurations frequently produce heterogeneous outcomes, including 305 partial edits, insertion–substitution combinations, short indels, or shifted integration boundaries 306 that complicate alignment and classification. 307 To handle more complex PE outcomes, such as difficult-to-align insertions arising from local 308 mismatches, short indels, or integration site shifts, CleanFinder implements a dual competitive 309 reference scoring framework ( Figure 4A ). Each read is aligned against both the wild-type (WT) 310 reference and the intended prime-edited (PE) reference. Classification is determined by 311 comparative alignment scoring, enabling robust discrimination between WT alleles, precisely 312 edited PE alleles, and intermediate or bystander variants. 313 314 We first examined substitution-only designs at the XPA and POU5F1 loci in human iPSCs 315 (Figure 4B ). These edits introduced one or two defined nucleotide substitutions within the 316 programmable window, which were resolved at single-nucleotide resolution and clearly 317 distinguished from wild-type alleles. Turbo Mode 431 432 In parallel, we developed CleanFinder Turbo, a browser-native module designed specifically for 433 rapid screening and interactive exploration ( Figure S7D–E ). CleanFinder Turbo uses a heuristic 434 read-localization strategy: in this context, heuristic means that read placement and classification 435 are determined using fast, rule-based approximations (anchor detection with bounded 436 mismatches) rather than computing an optimal dynamic-programming alignment for every read. 437 Conceptually, this mirrors the broader class of seed/anchor-based heuristic strategies used to 438 accelerate sequence mapping in general-purpose tools such as BLAST and short-read aligners 439 (Altschul et al., 1990 ). Practically, Turbo extracts forward anchors from the reference and 440 computes reverse-complement anchors to enable strand-agnostic detection. Reads are 441 screened by mismatch-bounded anchor scanning, and classification proceeds from the 442 extracted internal segment and length delta relative to the expected reference middle region. 443 This design minimizes per-read computation and enables processing rates on the order of 444 ~10^5 reads per second, making Turbo well-suited for primary screening and multi-well plate-445 scale quality control without the full alignment detail, repair pathway inference, or advanced 446 visualization modules available in the main application. 447 448

Discussion 479 

480 Genome editing experiments increasingly generate heterogeneous allelic architectures that 481 extend beyond “simple” insertion–deletion events at a defined cut site. Composite indel– 482 substitution events, templated insertions generated by prime editing, locus-specific repair 483 biases, long-read sequencing noise, and amplification artifacts collectively challenge 484 conventional amplicon-based analysis workflows. 485 The analytical landscape for genome editing is currently fragmented. Existing command-line 486 tools, while powerful, often require specialized bioinformatics expertise, creating a bottleneck for 487 many laboratories ( Clement et al., 2019; Amit et al., 2021; Tommaso et al., 2017 ). More 488 accessible web-based tools, particularly those running on a server, lack scalability and might 489 raise concerns about data privacy ( Clement et al., 2019, Park et al., 2017 ). While other browser-490 native or locally implemented tools offer improved data protection, they are frequently optimized 491 only for short-read Illumina genotyping, with either absent ( Schmid-Burgk et al., 2014 ) or only 492 partial adaptation ( Nguyen et al., 2022 ) to the distinct error profiles of long-read platforms like 493 ONT sequencing. 494 495 In this study, we present CleanFinder as a modular and scalable analytical framework designed 496 to address this diversity through anchor-guided localization, constrained semi-global alignment, 497 dual-reference scoring for complex edits, integrated repair pathway annotation, and allele-aware 498 quality control. 499 In our previous attempt ( Nguyen et al., 2022 ) and during the early development stages of 500 CleanFinder, edit reconstruction relied primarily on adaptive local alignment centered on the 501

While local alignment (Smith–Waterman, 1981 ) provides sensitive 502 detection of high-scoring edit-containing subsequences, its design inherently reports only the 503 best local match and may therefore truncate flanking sequence context. In the setting of 504 complex editing outcomes, such as large insertions, composite indel–substitution events, or 505 long-read sequencing data, this behavior required additional post-alignment layers to 506 reconstruct full-length edit structure and ensure accurate classification. Furthermore, classic 507 local alignment is computationally intensive. Conversely, conventional global (Needleman– 508 Wunsch) alignment enforces end-to-end matching of both sequences and does not naturally 509 accommodate terminal overhangs, limiting its suitability for long, error-prone ONT reads where 510 adapters and large indels are common ( Needleman et al., 1970 ) ( Table S2 ). 511 To overcome the structural and computational limitations of the earlier buffer-based approach, 512 we adopted a constrained semi-global (“glocal”) alignment strategy ( Durbin et al., 1998 ). In this 513 framework, short, high-confidence anchor sequences first localize each read to a candidate 514 reference window. Semi-global alignment is then performed within this window without 515 penalizing terminal gaps, preserving full-length read continuity while tolerating variable read 516 boundaries and large indels. By restricting alignment to biologically relevant regions, this 517 anchor-guided design reduces unnecessary matrix evaluation and eliminates the need for 518 manually defined buffer zones. Crucially, the constrained alignment focuses on the biologically 519 relevant segment without discarding reads based on unaligned terminal sequence, ensuring the 520 read remains available for visualization and manual review even in cases of complex or 521 ambiguous editing outcomes. The advantages of this architecture are particularly evident in 522 long-read datasets. ONT reads frequently contain adapter remnants, variable overhangs, and 523 length asymmetries relative to the reference amplicon. For instance, in mtDNA editing 524 experiments, where amplicons reached 2–3 kb, anchor-guided semi-global alignment enabled 525 direct processing of long reads without trimming or window redefinition, while maintaining 526 accurate structural reconstruction. 28 Several limitations should be acknowledged. The small-molecule screen was conducted at a 632 single concentration (10 μM) to demonstrate analytical scalability under high-throughput 633 conditions. Although single-dose primary formats are widely used for initial hit triage in 634 screening campaigns ( Butkiewicz et al., 2017 ), this approach does not establish compound 635 potency, specificity, or fully resolve potential cytotoxic effects. While overtly cytotoxic 636 compounds were identified and excluded based on phenotypic assessment, comprehensive 637 viability profiling was beyond the scope of the primary screen. Secondary validation, including 638 dose–response analysis and orthogonal assays, is therefore required to confirm reproducibility, 639 disentangle toxicity from true editing modulation, and establish mechanistic relevance of the 640 identified modulators. 641 Across both Illumina and ONT datasets, cross-platform benchmarking demonstrated strong 642 concordance of editing outcomes. However, ONT sequencing remains subject to intrinsic 643 platform-specific basecalling biases, particularly within homopolymeric regions where indel rates 644 are artificially elevated. These effects reflect characteristics of the sequencing technology rather 645 than the analytical framework itself. Future CleanFinder versions will incorporate optional 646 warning flags for deletions occurring within homopolymeric stretches, especially when 647 overlapping predicted editing windows, to aid interpretation in long-read datasets. Until such 648 safeguards are implemented, results derived from homopolymeric regions should be interpreted 649 with appropriate caution. Recent advances in sequencing technologies further enhance 650 amplicon-based genome editing analyses. Updated short-read platforms such as the Illumina 651 MiSeq i100 improve performance for low-diversity CRISPR libraries, while compact long-read 652 devices and low-cost flow cells such as ONT’s Flongle make targeted sequencing accessible to 653 smaller laboratories. Frameworks such as CleanFinder that support both high-accuracy short 654 reads and cost-efficient long-read datasets are therefore increasingly important for flexible and 655 accessible genome editing analysis. 656

29 The anchor-guided semi-global alignment strategy preserves full-length read continuity and 657 enables robust structural reconstruction. In rare cases, minor labeling inconsistencies may 658 occur in the raw output string representation of complex alleles; however, the full alignment 659 context is retained, allowing manual confirmation of structural integrity when needed. 660 CleanFinder was intentionally developed as a modular, open-source framework to facilitate 661 ongoing extension and community-driven development. Planned enhancements further include 662 support for multi-gRNA analysis within the browser-native implementation and expanded 663 reporting functionality within the command-line interface, including automated generation of 664 publication-quality graphical summaries. 665 Future browser-native versions may incorporate client-side parallelization (e.g., Web Workers) 666 to enable concurrent processing of multiple input files across available CPU cores without 667 altering the deterministic alignment and classification logic. Given the modular pipeline 668 architecture, such parallelization can be implemented without affecting reproducibility or 669 interpretability. 670 Taken together, CleanFinder represents a methodological progression from buffer-based local 671 alignment toward an anchor-guided, semi-global, and modular architecture capable of resolving 672 diverse genome editing outcomes across sequencing platforms and experimental scales. By 673 integrating structural reconstruction, competitive dual-reference scoring for complex edits, k-674 mer–guided acceleration, repair pathway annotation, allele-aware quality control, and scalable 675 deployment modes, the framework provides a flexible and extensible foundation for 676 contemporary genome engineering workflows. 677 678

CleanFinder implementation and Alignment algorithm 864 

865 CleanFinder is implemented as a browser-native single-page application (SPA) using standard 866 web technologies (HTML5, CSS3, and JavaScript). In browser-native modes (standard and 867 Turbo), all sequence processing, including anchor detection, constrained semi-global alignment, 868 genotype classification, and visualization, is executed entirely client-side within the user’s web 869 browser. This architecture preserves data privacy and eliminates the need for server-side 870 computation or dedicated software installation. When gene-based analysis is selected, gene 871 symbols are resolved to genomic coordinates using the Ensembl REST API, and corresponding 872 reference sequences are retrieved programmatically from Ensembl. Alternatively, users may 873 provide custom reference sequences directly. Interactive visualizations are rendered using 874 Chart.js, while gene schematics and alignment views are generated as Scalable Vector 875 Graphics (SVG). Input data consist of FASTQ files generated from targeted amplicon 876 sequencing experiments (Illumina, PacBio, or ONT). Reads are processed iteratively to enable 877 analysis of large datasets within browser environments. 878 Read placement is determined using short reference-derived anchor sequences located at 879 defined positions within the amplicon. Both forward and reverse-complement anchors are 880 evaluated to determine read orientation. Reads lacking valid anchor matches within the allowed 881 mismatch threshold are excluded from downstream alignment. 882 Following anchor localization, reads are aligned using a constrained semi-global (glocal) 883 dynamic programming strategy. Alignment is restricted to the anchor-defined reference window, 884 and terminal gaps are not penalized. This approach preserves full-length structural continuity 885 while tolerating variable read boundaries and large insertions or deletions. By limiting alignment 886 to biologically relevant regions, computational complexity is reduced relative to full-length global 887 alignment. For prime editing experiments, reads are independently aligned against both the 888

Allelic Dropout 927 928 To detect potential hemizygous loss in clonal cell lines, we implemented an Allelic Dropout SNP 929 Analyzer module. The analysis requires three primary inputs: long-read sequencing data 930 (FASTQ), a wild-type reference amplicon sequence (optionally retrieved via the gDNA Finder 931 integrated module), and a user-defined stable anchor sequence (~20 bp). This anchor serves as 932 a conserved genomic landmark, ideally situated distal to the expected editing site, to enable 933 orientation-independent read filtering. The computational pipeline executes a multi-stage 934 workflow: (1) Read Filtering: Raw FASTQ data are scanned for the anchor sequence and its 935 reverse complement to isolate on-target reads. (2) Matrix Construction: A user-defined subset of 936 anchor-positive reads undergoes affine-gap alignment against the reference to construct a 937 positional base-frequency pileup (matrix). (3) SNP Interrogation: The algorithm scans this matrix 938 for heterozygous candidates, defined by a stoichiometric balance (default frequency range: 30– 939 70%) of two dominant alleles and a minimum coverage threshold. Genotype Quantification, Repair Annotation, and Output 949 

950 Following constrained semi-global alignment and genotype assignment, reads are grouped by 951 identical allele structures and quantified to generate genotype frequency distributions. User-952 defined frequency thresholds can be applied to filter low-frequency variants. Deletion and 953 insertion events are extracted directly from aligned allele structures. For indel-based editing, 954 repair-associated features, including microhomology length, insertion duplication, and deletion 955 boundaries relative to the PAM site, are computed to enable RIMA-based repairome 956 classification. Deletions are annotated as PAM-proximal or PAM-distal based on their position 957 relative to the predicted cleavage site, facilitating mechanistic interpretation of repair patterns. In 958 the browser-native implementation, results are rendered as interactive visualizations, including 959 allele tables, frequency plots, and summary charts. These outputs can be downloaded as 960 publication-ready graphics and exported in structured tabular formats (CSV and Excel) for 961 downstream analysis. The full session state, including input parameters and computed results, 962 can be stored as a self-contained HTML file for reproducibility. 963 The Python CLI implements the same analytical workflow and prints structured output to the 964 console while generating tabular result files (including Excel-compatible formats) suitable for 965 batch processing and integration into external pipelines. 966

Integration of Rational Indel Meta-Analysis 2 (RIMA2) and REPAIRome 980 981 CleanFinder integrates repair pathway inference directly into the genotype reconstruction 982 workflow ( Taheri-Ghahfarokhi et al., 2018, EL de Alba · 2025 et al., 2025). Repair annotation is 983 computed from aligned allele structures generated by the constrained semi-global alignment, 984 without requiring external preprocessing or post hoc file conversion. For each aligned allele 985 containing a deletion event, the software identifies the deletion junction within the alignment 986 string and extracts the deleted reference sequence. Microhomology (MH) is then evaluated by 987 comparing the deleted sequence to flanking reference segments immediately upstream and 988 downstream of the deletion boundary. The maximal contiguous sequence identity at the junction 989 is recorded as the MH length. Deletions with microhomology length ≥2 bp are classified as 990 microhomology-mediated end joining (MMEJ), whereas deletions with no detectable 991

42 microhomology (MH = 0) are classified as non-homologous end joining (NHEJ). Single-base 992 deletions are treated as NHEJ events. Deletion events are additionally annotated according to 993 their positional relationship to the predicted cleavage site. Based on the distance between the 994 deletion boundary and the cut site, deletions are categorized as PAM-proximal ( ≤5 bp from the 995 cut site) or PAM-distal (>5 bp). This positional stratification enables inference of locus-996 dependent repair signatures and cleavage architecture effects. Microhomology lengths are 997 aggregated across all reads to generate a microhomology length distribution. In parallel, repair 998 pathway proportions (NHEJ vs MMEJ) are computed by summing allele frequencies according 999 to MH classification. These metrics are visualized in the browser-native implementation as 1000 interactive bar and pie charts and are exported in structured tabular formats in both the web 1001 application and the Python CLI. Insertion events are parsed directly from the aligned allele 1002 strings and quantified as part of the genotype distribution. While insertion duplication status and 1003 templated characteristics can be inferred from alignment context, primary pathway classification 1004 within CleanFinder focuses on deletion-associated microhomology features. This integrated 1005 implementation enables repair pathway inference to be computed natively from reconstructed 1006 alleles and reported alongside genotype distributions, frame status, and editing efficiency 1007 metrics. 1008 1009
