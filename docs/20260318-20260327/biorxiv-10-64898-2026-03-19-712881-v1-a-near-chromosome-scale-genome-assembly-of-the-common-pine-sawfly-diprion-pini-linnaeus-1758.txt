Introduction 38 

The common pine sawfly, Diprion pini (Hymenoptera: Diprionidae ), is a widespread 39 

defoliator of coniferous forests across Europe and Asia, with outbreaks causing extensive 40 

damage to commercially and ecologically important pine forests (Barre et al., 2002; De 41 

Somviele et al., 2004) . However, despite its ecological importance, genomic resources for this 42 

species remain scarce, hindering advances in molecular ecology or pest management. As a 43 

member of the paraphyletic Symphyta (sawflies and woodwasps), D. pini occupies a44 

phylogenetic position that is critical for understanding the early evolution of Hymenoptera, the 45 

insect order that includes both the mainly phytophagous sawflies and the typically parasitoid 46 

or eusocial Apocrita (ants, bees, and wasps). In general, sawflies represent an understudied 47 

lineage within Hymenoptera, which limits comparative genomics across this order such as 48 

identifying ancestral genomic features (He et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025) or reconstructing 49 

the evolution of parasitism (Polaszek and Vilhemsen, 2023) and sociality (Ritter et al., 2026) .50 

Recent efforts to manage D. pini outbreaks have focused on mechanical methods like 51 

handpicking the larvae, as well as the use of chemical insecticides (Sevim and Sevim, 2024) .52 

These methods are labor -intensive and increasingly constrained by environmental 53 

concerns. New genomic tools, facilitated by advances in genome sequencing technologies, can 54 

support and inform pest management. Yet the lack of a high -quality reference genome for D. 55 

pini limits progress in identifying genetic targets for pest control, genomic monitoring of 56 

outbreak source and spread, or exploring adaptations to climate -driven stressors. 57 

Here, we present the first near chromosome -level reference genome assembly and 58 

structural annotation for Diprion pini , generated using PacBio long -read sequencing, 59 

supplemented with and ONT MionION long reads, and 10x linked -read scaffolding. 60 

Additionally, we explore syntenic relationships and gene homology with other Diprionidae 61 

genomes. This resource provides a foundation for advancing genomic -driven pest management 62

Materials and Methods 72 

DNA extraction and sequencing 73 

Diprion pini imagines were reared from cocoons collected from soil searches in 74 

Northwest Poland in autumn 202 1. High molecular weight (HMW) genomic DNA (gDNA) 75 

was isolated following the Chromium ™ Genome Demonstrated Protocol for HMW gDNA 76 

extraction from single insects. For each extraction, the whole insect was ground with a pestle 77 

and digested overnight at 37℃ in lysis buffer (333 mM NaCl, 100 mM EDTA pH 8, 10 mM 78 

Tris HCl pH 8, 0,5% SDS, and 100 μg/ml Proteinase K). After adding 5M NaCl, the mixture 79 

was centrifuged, and the aqueous phase transferred to a new tube. HMW gDNA was 80 

precipitated in ice -cold 100% ethanol and collected by centrifugation. The pellet was air -dried 81 

and dissolved thoroughly in 0.1× TE buffer. The eluted DNA was quantified using a Qubit 3.0 82 

Fluorometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). Quality was assessed via agarose gel 83 

electrophoresis (1.0%) and NanoDrop spectrophotometric analysis (Thermo Fisher Scientific), 84 

with A260/A280 ratios between 1.8 –2.0 and A260/A230 > 2.0. 85 

For preparation of sequencing libraries, the DNA of two individuals was used. PacBio 86 

HiFi long -read libraries were prepared from HMW gDNA of a female and sequenced on the 87

Genome survey , assembly , and quality assessment 100 

Genome size and repetitive content were estimated based on a k -mer approach, in which 101 

the k -mer (k = 21) distribution count was generated by jellyfish v2.3.1 (Marçais and Kingsford, 102 

2011) using the 10x reads. Subsequently, the resulting histogram was analyzed with the 103 

GenomeScope2 web interface  (Vurture et al. 2017, http://genomescope.org/genomescope2.0/)  with 104 

no maximum k -mer coverage (max k -mer coverage = −1). The genome was assembled with 105 

Hifiasm v0.19 (Cheng et al., 2022, 2021) using default parameters and PacBio HiFi and ONT 106 

MinION long reads as input. We used purge_dups v1.2.6 (Guan et al., 2020) to remove 107 

duplicated regions caused by contig overlaps and haplotigs. Potential misassemblies in the 108 

purged genome were resolved using RagTag v2.1.0 “correct” (Alonge et al., 2022) with the 109 

genome of Diprion similis (GCF_021155765.1 ) as a reference due to its taxonomic proximity 110 

(Wutke et al., 2024) . Subsequently, the corrected contigs were placed into scaffolds during 111 

three scaffolding steps: 1) with scaff10x ( https://github.com/wtsi -hpag/Scaff10X ) using the 112

using PacBio HiFi (47× coverage) and ONT MinION (2×) long reads, scaffolded with 10x 185 

linked reads. The final assembly (= Hifiasm’s primary haplotype assembly) spans 268 Mb 186 

(contig N50: 7 Mb; scaffold N50: 18.7 Mb, Fig. 1, Table 1, Supplementary inform ation ), 187

The mitochondrial genome (22.7 kb, Fig. 3) was fully resolved, exhibiting a conserved 212 

gene order typical of Bileteria (Lavrov 2007). With almost 23 kb the mitochondrial genome is 213 

unusually large compared to most other animal mitogenomes, which can be attributed to an 214

authenticity of this region, rather than it being an assembly artifact. Yet , large mitochondrial 216 

genomes might be more the norm than the exception. As Olli et al. (2024) suggested, the control 217 

region length might be underestimated for many organisms due to mapping and assembly 218 

issues using short Illumina reads that do not spam the whole control region. With steadily 219 

improving long -read sequencing technologies, such large mitogenome sizes are being reported 220

Structural and functional genome annotation 227 

Repetitive elements accounted for 27.54% of the genome (Table 1, Suppl emantary Fig. 228 

S4 ), of which most were unclassified repeats (22.59%). Comparative data from hymenopteran 229 

genomes indicate that repeat abundance is variable across the order (Sproul et al., 2023) . While 230 

the genomes of several representatives of the basally branching Symphyta show comparatively 231 

large repeat fractions relative to many apocritan genomes (Sproul et al., 2023) , the pattern is 232 

heterogeneous. However, comparing repeat content across genome assemblies generated with 233 

different technologies and pipelines is delicate, because much of the apparent variation might 234 

be technical rather than biological. Therefore, comprehensive taxon sampling and consistent 235 

repeat annotation pipelines are required to determine whether elevated repeat content is a 236 

general feature of ancestral hymenopteran genomes or the result of lineage -specific TE 237 

dynamics.  The tidk analysis showed that four of the 14 assembled pseudo -chromosomes were 238 

enriched in the telomeric repeat motif on both scaffold ends, and another nine were enriched 239 

on one end of the scaffold. Due to their repetitiveness, assembling telomere s can be 240 

challenging . Nevertheless, the presence of the repeat motif near several scaffold ends support s241 

the near -complete chromosome -level scaffolding of our assembly ( Supplementary Fig. S5 ).242 

Gene prediction with BRAKER2 produced a comprehensive gene set of 26,335 protein -243 

coding genes that further suggests a high quality of our assembly. Of these predicted genes, 244 

12,769 could be functionally annotated with PANNZER2. The comparison of o rtholog s with 245 

other Hymenoptera identified 7,073 shared ortho groups , likely forming the conserved core set 246 

of proteins of the lineage. A further 648 ortho groups containing 2,472 proteins were unique to 247 

D. pini (Fig. 4). While the functions of many proteins remain unknown or relate to general 248 

processes, several carry annotations suggesting roles in interaction with host plants or natural 249 

enemies (e.g., toxin activity, GO:0090729, or modulation of process of another organism, GO: 250 

0035821 , Fig. 5, Supplemantary Fig. S6 ). These annotation s may indicate involvement in 251

Comparative genomics 270 

Whole -genome alignments revealed a high degree of synteny between Diprion pini and 271 

Neodiprion lecontei (Fig. 6, Supplemantary Fig. S7 ), a representative of another genus within 272 

Diprionidae. This strong conservation of syntenic blocks , despite the twofold difference in 273 

haploid chromosome number (n = 7 in Neodiprion vs. n = 14 in Diprion ), suggests that large 274 

chromosomal segments have remained intact during karyotype evolution. Thus, the divergence 275 

in chromosome number likely reflects chromosome fusion or fission events (Robertsonian 276 

changes) rather than large -scale intrachromosomal rearrangement , a pattern reported across 277 

diverse insect lineages (De Vos et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2025; Nakatani and McLysaght, 278 

2019) . The observed synteny with Diprion similis (Fig. 6, Supplemantary Fig. S7) is not 279 

surprising given that the reference genome of that species was used to break misassemblies, 280 

order, and orient scaffolds of the D. pini assembly. D. pini and D. similis show the same 281 

chromosome number and almost identical morphology in their chromosomes (Rousselet et al., 282 

1998) . Based on that and the fact that the D. similis reference genome has been assembled to 283

The D. pini genome presented here represents a high -quality reference , expand ing 291 

genomic resources for sawflies, a group historically under -represented in Hymenoptera 292 

genomics. Given the ecological and economic importance of D. pini as a major defoliator of 293 

pine forests, its genome constitutes a needed resource to develop more effective, targeted, and 294 

sustainable strategies for monitoring, predicting, and managing outbreaks of this important 295 

forest pest . At the same time, it offers opportunities for advancing fundamental entomological 296 

research e.g.,  evolutionary and behavioural ecology of sawflies . Importantly , our  highly 297 

contiguous assembly achieved near -complete chromosome -level scaffolding . This 298 

demonstrat es that the availability of a closely related, high -quality reference genome can 299
