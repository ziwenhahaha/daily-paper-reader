Abstract 60 Cultivated bacterial isolates are essential for elucidating gut microbiota functions, yet 61 reliance on fragmented draft genomes and limited mobile genetic element (MGE) 62 annotation has constrained high-resolution analyses. Here, we present the Cultivated 63 Complete Genome Reference (CCGR), a comprehensive compendium of 1,150 fully 64 circularized human gut bacterial genomes. Overcoming draft limitations, CCGR 65 achieves chromosome-scale resolution and reveals distinct evolutionary strategies 66 shaping gene topological organization in response to bacterial growth. Furthermore, 67 we decode the dynamic landscape of MGEs, demonstrating that broad-host-range 68 phages follow strict positional patterns by integrating at conserved chromosomal 69 hotspots flanked by nutrient transporter loci. Importantly, we provide mechanistic in 70 vivo evidence that accessory genomic elements drive strain-level functional 71 heterogeneity. Specifically, the plasmid-encoded scrK gene in Levilactobacillus brevis 72 involved in fructose catabolism is required to mitigate fructose-diet-exacerbated 73 colitis. Collectively, CCGR establishes a framework linking chromosomal 74 architecture and MGEs to bacterial evolution and host health, enabling precise, 75 strain-resolved functional investigations. 76

Introduction 83 The intricate composition of the gut microbiota and its metabolic activities are 84 fundamental to human health 1,2 . Genomic characterization of gut bacteria serves as a 85 cornerstone for decoding their functional potential, which is important to understand 86 the relationship between host and bacteria 3,4 . Studies of bacterial genomes can reveal 87 strain-level genetic relationships such as phylogenetic diversity, distribution across 88 different cohorts, horizontal gene transfer (HGT) dynamics, and the landscape of 89 mobile genetic elements (MGEs) 5 .90 In recent years, significant advancements in gut microbial genomic databases have 91 expanded our understanding of microbiota-host interactions, with comprehensive 92 resources collectively contributing to the characterization of bacterial diversity, 93 functional gene landscapes, and metabolic potential 6-14 . Metagenomic assembled 94 genomes (MAGs) and culturable draft genomes have enabled analyses of the 95 relationship between human health and many microorganisms, and how probiotics 96 may exert beneficial effects on human health 15-17 . Detailed analyses of microbial 97 pan-genomes have also provided in-depth information on the relationship between 98 microbial evolution and environmental adaptation 18-20 . These studies have not only 99 enriched the catalogs of bacterial genomes but also facilitated research into 100 carbohydrate-active enzymes, secondary metabolites, and probiotic candidates 101 through integrated approaches combining metagenomic assembly, isolate pairing, and 102 preclinical validation. 103 However, reliance on short-read sequencing can lead to fragmentation in the 104 generated draft genomes and the median contig N50 is less than 500 kbp in major 105 databases, which limits analyses of chromosomal organization and MGEs 9 .106 Furthermore, short-read–based draft genomes are often insufficient for resolving 107 repetitive regions in bacterial chromosomes and preserving accurate positional 108 information 21 . For example, terminal inverted repeats (TIRs) that encode biosynthetic 109 gene clusters (BGCs) are frequently misassembled or absent in draft genomes, which 110

Finally, we identified specific phages and 135 plasmids as robust, novel biomarkers across diverse clinical cohorts. Results 141 

A cultivated human gut bacterial genome repository of 1,150 circular genomes 142 To construct a large-scale Cultivated Complete Genome Reference (CCGR), 1,093 143 bacterial isolates were cultivated. Then, the draft genome of each strain was 144 assembled from short-read sequencing data, and the complete genome was assembled 145 using a hybrid approach combining both short-read and long-read data 31 . After 146 genome quality assessment, 966 complete genomes were obtained and 127 147 multi-isolated strains were reassembled into 184 circular metagenome assembled 148 genomes (MAGs). Thus, a total of 1,150 circular genomes were used to construct the 149 CCGR (Fig. 1a, Supplementary Data 1). We subsequently clustered the 1,150 150 genomes into 199 species-level clusters based on 95% average nucleotide identity 151 (ANI). Notably, Bacillota_A represented most of the clusters (146 genomes, 58 152 clusters), and one cluster containing 3 genomes from the genus Collinsella could not 153 be classified at the species-level, indicating previously unidentified species (Fig. 1a, 154 Supplementary Data 2). To further evaluate the contribution of CCGR to bacterial 155 complete genomes, we mapped the CCGR genomes against the human gut-derived 156 1,562 bacterial complete genomes from NCBI RefSeq database. Results showed that 157 complete genomes from 127 bacterial species were underrepresented in NCBI RefSeq 158 database (Fig. 1b), especially for species in three phyla Bacillota_A, Bacillota, and 159 Bacteroidota (Supplementary Fig. 1a). Moreover, CCGR includes a substantial 160 number of high-quality genomes of Bifidobacterium infantis , Bifidobacterium 161 

pseudocatenulatum , Escherichia coli , Phocaeicola dorei , and Bifidobacterium 162 

adolescentis (Supplementary Fig. 1b), which can be used for strain-level comparative 163 genomic analysis. 164

Using the complete genomes, we validated this 181 topological constraint across the five major bacterial phyla in CCGR, confirming that 182 the genomic distribution of highly prevalent genes is non-random, a trend particularly 183 robust in the phylum Actinomycetota (Supplementary Fig. 2). Interestingly, 184 Pseudomonadota genomes frequently exhibited symmetric inversions around the 185 replication origin-terminus axis, a distinctive architectural feature that has been 186 reported in a prior study 33 . Beyond this positional bias, we investigated strand-specific 187 preferences of genes (Supplementary Fig. 3). Actinomycetota and Pseudomonadota 188 exhibited a striking "U-shaped" bimodal profile, with genes strongly polarized to 189 either the leading or lagging strand extremes. In contrast, Bacillota displayed a 190 pervasive unidirectional skew towards the leading strand, whereas Bacteroidota 191 showed a relatively uniform distribution, suggesting relaxed strand constraints 192 (Supplementary Fig. 3). 193 To quantify the structural implications of these strand biases, we calculated the strand 194 switch rate (the frequency of transcriptional orientation changes between adjacent 195 genes) and revealed a marked structural dichotomy across phyla. Pseudomonadota 196 exhibited the highest median strand switch rate, indicating a highly fragmented 197 genomic architecture composed of short transcriptional units. In contrast, Bacillota_A 198 and Bacillota maintained significantly lower switch rates, preserving longer 199 co-directional gene arrays (Fig. 2a, b). This genomic fragmentation was 200 density-dependent, meaning that in regions with a higher gene density, the strand 201 switch rate was lower (Fig. 2c). Regions with the highest gene density harbored 202 significantly longer arrays of co-directional genes compared to low-density regions, 203 indicating that high gene density might impose a topological constraint to select for 204 continuous transcriptional orientations (Fig. 2d). We further dissected these 205 constraints across functional categories. Genes related to translation, ribosomal 206 structure and biogenesis exhibited the most rigid architectural conservation, 207 characterized by the highest leading strand bias and the lowest strand switch rate (Fig. 208 2e, f). 209

9We next explored whether the observed genomic fragmentation is constrained by 210 physical gene length. Globally, a conserved spatial bias where longer genes were 211 preferentially enriched in the replication origin-proximal regions (Fig. 2g). As gene 212 length increased, there were universal increases in leading strand bias and strand 213 switch rate (Fig. 2h, i). However, the stringency of these constraints exhibited sharp 214 phylogenetic divergence. Specifically, the spatial sequestration of long genes near the 215 origin of replication was notably attenuated in Bacillota_A and Bacteroidota, where 216 distance distributions for long and short genes were indistinguishable (Fig. 2j). 217 Similarly, in Pseudomonadota and Bacteroidota, the topological confinement of long 218 genes to the leading strand was effectively uncoupled (Fig. 2k). This topological 219 sorting resulted in their lagging-to-leading gene length ratios being closed to unity, 220 indicating that gene length is decoupled from strand specificity in these phyla 221 (Supplementary Fig. 4). Despite these specific divergences, a unifying architectural 222 feature across these phyla was that long genes consistently displayed higher strand 223 switch rates compared to short genes (Fig. 2l). 224 Finally, to link genomic architecture with life history traits, we correlated architectural 225 metrics with predicted maximal growth rates of genomes. Generally, rapid growth 226 appeared to impose a heavy topological cost, correlating with a paradoxical reduction 227 in leading strand bias near the origin (Fig. 2m), increased strand switch rates (Fig. 2n), 228 and the accumulation of genes in regions proximal to dnaA but located on the lagging 229 strand (Fig. 2o). However, phylogenetic decomposition revealed that this trade-off 230 was driven predominantly by Bacillota and Actinomycetota (Supplementary Fig. 5a-c). 231 In contrast, Pseudomonadota genomes defied these constraints, exhibiting a robust 232 architectural framework where rapid growth was decoupled from topological 233 constraints. Specifically, Pseudomonadota maintained stable origin-proximal bias and 234 displayed a negative correlation between growth and switch rate, thereby preserving 235 architectural order even under high replication pressure. 236

Here, to quantify the advancements in genomic integrity 271 of complete genomes, we compared the counts of multiple genomic metrics between 272 966 complete genomes and paired draft genomes (Fig. 3a, Supplementary Data3). As 273 expected, complete genomes harbored higher counts of bases, coding sequences 274 (CDSs), and genes, exhibiting the most striking improvements in rRNA and tRNA 275 gene counts. Specifically for each genome, the improvement of these parameters by 276 the complete genomes was particularly evident in the genomes of the Bacillota, 277 Bacillota_A, and Pseudomonadota phyla (Fig. 3d). We found that the increase in 278 indicators such as the number of rRNA and tRNA genes was negatively correlated 279 with the GC content and positively correlated with the genome size (Fig. 3e), 280 suggesting that genome closure yields greater improvements in larger and low-GC 281 genomes, which are prone to assembly gaps and gene loss in draft versions. Moreover, 282 the complete genomes led to a marked enhancement in the quality of contig length 283 and gene counts of phages and plasmids (Fig. 3b, c), suggesting complete genomes 284 reveal more refined information regarding the interactions between mobile genetic 285 elements (MGEs) and their host bacteria. 286 We also compared the detailed differences in CDSs (Supplementary Data4) showing 287 that the complete genomes contained a large number of CDSs missing in the drafts 288 (NoM) and CDSs with reduced copy numbers from the draft (NoM). Notably, we 289 found that these genes associated with transformation, transduction, and conjugation 290 and present in complete genomes were largely underrepresented in the draft genomes 291 (Supplementary Fig. 6, Fig. 3d), indicating that complete genomes provided an 292 accurate resolution of intricate HGT within bacterial genomes. 293

Complete genomes resolve phage integration rules and auxiliary metabolic 313 

potential 314 Based on the chromosome-scale resolution of 1,150 complete genomes, we 315 constructed a high-quality phage catalog (CCGRv) comprising 3,046 viral sequences 316 (Supplementary Fig. 7a). This dataset represents a substantial leap in genomic 317 integrity, as 60.09% of phages exhibited significant quality improvements compared 318 to draft-derived assemblies, with the average completeness rising from 39% to 74% 319 (Supplementary Fig. 7b-e, Fig. 4a). A striking example of this resolution is 320 CCGRVR01473, a single complete phage sequence that successfully bridged eight 321 fragmented contigs from the corresponding draft genome into a contiguous scaffold. 322 This refined resolution enabled us to accurately delineate the diversity and integration 323 patterns of gut phages. While phages from Pseudomonadota and Bacteroidota 324 exhibited the highest richness, the phylum Bacillota harbored the majority of novel 325 viral clusters (Supplementary Fig. 7f, Fig. 4b). Furthermore, we observed that while 326 most phages were host-specific, a subset demonstrated potential cross-species 327 transmissibility (Fig. 4c). 328 329 Of note, complete genomes allowed us to decipher the precise integration patterns of 330 these cross-species phages. We focused on Phage Cluster 14, a broad-host-range 331 group widely distributed across distinct species, such as Bacteroides uniformis and 332 

Phocaeicola dorei . Although the absolute genomic coordinates of these prophages 333 varied significantly across different host chromosomes, our synteny analysis revealed 334 that their integrations were governed by strict local genomic context (Fig. 4d). 335 Specifically, the insertion sites of Phage Cluster 14 were consistently flanked by 336 specific nutrient metabolism genes, such as the vitamin B12 transporter Btu B, Hmu U, 337 and Yus V, regardless of the host species (Fig. 4e). Plasmids drive strain-level metabolic heterogeneity and therapeutic efficacy 380 To explore the functional contribution of extrachromosomal elements to host fitness, 381 we constructed a catalog of 1,124 plasmids (CCGRp) from our complete genomes. 382 These plasmids exhibited extensive mobility, with inter-phylum transfer events 383 primarily occurring between Bacteroidota and Bacillota (Supplementary Fig. 9a). 384 These mobile elements are classically recognized as vectors for disseminating 385 antimicrobial resistance, evidenced here by the multi-species clustering of 386 aminoglycoside and tetracycline resistance genes (Supplementary Fig. 9b). We 387 identified five plasmids, each harboring ≥5 antimicrobial resistance genes (ARGs) 388 that were predominantly arranged into discrete gene clusters (Supplementary Fig. 9c). 389 Moreover, we uncovered evidence for putative HGT of ARGs between bacterial 390 chromosomes and plasmids. Mobile genetic elements drive the expansion and strategic trade-offs of host 446 

defense systems 447 Beyond their metabolic contributions, MGEs exert profound selective pressure on 448 host genomes, driving an adaptive "arms race." Leveraging our complete genomes, 449 we systematically mapped the landscape of defense and anti-defense systems 450 (Supplementary Fig. 12a-c). We observed a significant positive correlation between 451 the abundance of defense systems and the density of MGEs (phages and plasmids) 452 within a genome (Supplementary Fig. 12d), confirming that MGE burden is a driver 453 of host immune expansion. Interestingly, this co-evolutionary dynamic exhibits 454 distinct strategies depending on genomic plasticity. In "open" genomes with a low 455 core gene fraction, defense systems expand rapidly to counter MGE influx; conversely, 456 in evolutionarily "conserved" genomes (core gene fraction > 50%), we observed a 457 negative correlation between defense system abundance and genomic conservation 458 (Supplementary Fig. 13e, Supplementary Fig. 14). This suggests a fundamental 459 trade-off where bacteria must balance the fitness costs of maintaining extensive 460 immune arsenals against the benefits of genomic stability. 461 462

Discussion 516 The draft genomes impede our in-depth exploration of the genomic information and 517 biological characteristics of gut microbiota, but complete genome collection for gut 518 bacteria is still lacking. In this study, we have obtained 1,150 circularized bacterial 519 genomes, which made a great contribution to current public databases. The complete 520 genomes provide information for us to investigate the gene placement patterns of 521 bacterial orthologs. Our results showed that the complete genomes significantly 522 increased the number of 16S rRNA genes and tRNA genes and performed much better 523 at plasmid and phage prediction compared to drafts. Moreover, 53% of plasmid 524 sequences derived from the draft genome mapped to the chromosome in the complete 525 genomes, suggesting that plasmid prediction tools may overestimate the number of 526 plasmids in previously fragmented draft genomes. Notably, we found many genes, 527 especially for MGE associated genes, were missing or only presented as a single copy 528 in the draft genomes, implying the CCGR reveal more refined mobile element-host 529 interactions. 530 The location of functional genes and phages on chromosomes is crucial for 531 understanding gene expression and further explaining the infection mechanism of 532 phages. Previous studies have demonstrated that roughly two-thirds of bacterial genes 533 exhibit positional preferences, with genes closer to the replication origin having 534 higher intracellular copy numbers during rapid bacterial growth 32,34 . Our results 535

26 demonstrate that bacterial genomic organization is not governed by a monolithic 536 biophysical law, but by divergent evolutionary strategies. Bacillota relies on rigid 537 topological constraints, strictly sequestering long genes to the leading strand. 538 However, this strategy proves brittle; rapid growth correlates with increased genomic 539 fragmentation, suggesting that strict conflict-avoidance mechanisms become 540 overwhelmed by the mechanics of fast replication. In contrast, Pseudomonadota 541 exhibits architectural robustness. By decoupling gene length from strand specificity 542 and maintaining structural order even during rapid growth, these lineages appear to 543 possess superior conflict-resolution machinery that allows them to manage rather than 544 strictly avoid collisions. 545 546 Our study reveals that phages and plasmids are not merely genomic passengers but 547 potent drivers of disease pathophysiology, acting as robust biomarkers through 548 specific functional contributions. Beyond well-known virulence factors like 549 toxin-antitoxin systems and antibiotic resistance genes, our complete 550 genome-resolved analysis uncovers a deeper layer of metabolic synergy. 551 552 Crucially, we demonstrate that MGEs are primary architects of strain-level metabolic 553 heterogeneity. While excessive fructose intake is a known driver of colitis and 554 metabolic dysregulation, we identified a plasmid-encoded scrK gene in specific L. 555 

brevis strains that confers a scavenging phenotype. This plasmid-dependent adaptation 556 enables the host to rapidly metabolize dietary fructose, thereby mitigating 557 fructose-induced intestinal barrier damage and inflammation 35-41 . This finding 558 exemplifies a critical paradigm: functional differences between strains could be 559 dictate by the dynamic accessory genome rather than the core chromosome. 560 Consequently, probiotic efficacy and pathogenicity may be misattributed in 561 species-level analyses that overlook these mobile traits. 562 563
