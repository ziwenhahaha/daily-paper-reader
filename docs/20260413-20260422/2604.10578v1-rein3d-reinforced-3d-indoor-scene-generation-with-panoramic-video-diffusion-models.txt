Abstract. The growing demand for Embodied AI and VR applications has highlighted the need for synthesizing high-quality 3D indoor scenes from sparse inputs. However, existing approaches struggle to infer mas-sive amounts of missing geometry in large unseen areas while main-taining global consistency, often producing locally plausible but glob-ally inconsistent reconstructions. We present Rein3D, a framework that reconstructs full 360 ° indoor environments by coupling explicit 3D Gaus-sian Splatting (3DGS) with temporally coherent priors from video dif-fusion models. Our approach follows a "restore-and-refine" paradigm: we employ a radial exploration strategy to render imperfect panoramic videos along trajectories starting from the origin, effectively uncover-ing occluded regions from a coarse 3DGS initialization. These sequences are restored by a panoramic video-to-video diffusion model and further enhanced via video super-resolution to synthesize high-fidelity geome-try and textures. Finally, these refined videos serve as pseudo-ground truths to update the global 3D Gaussian field. To bridge the exist-ing data gap for this task, we first introduce PanoV2V-15K, a large-scale dataset of over 15,000 paired clean and degraded panoramic videos tailored for diffusion-based scene restoration. To support this task, we construct PanoV2V-15K, a dataset of over 15K paired clean and de-graded panoramic videos for diffusion-based scene restoration. Exper-iments demonstrate that Rein3D produces photorealistic and globally consistent 3D scenes and significantly improves long-range camera ex-ploration compared with existing baselines. 

Keywords: 3D Indoor Scene Generation · Panoramic Video Diffusion Models · 3D Gaussian Splatting

– We propose Rein3D, a novel framework centered on a V2V diffusion model. It effectively restores missing regions and refines the global 3D scene. 

– Extensive experiments demonstrate our superior performance, particularly in generating large-scale environments and supporting long-range exploration. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 3D Indoor Scene Generation 

Powered by advances in differentiable rendering [19, 28] , 3D generative models have expanded from isolated objects [46, 53] to complex scene synthesis. Early iterative methods employ a “warp-and-refine” paradigm to extend the field of view [6, 14,26,49] but often suffer from geometric drift over long trajectories. To ensure global consistency, subsequent works utilize holistic 360-degree panora-mas [32] or layouts [7] as unified priors, lifting them into 3D Gaussian Splatting for real-time rendering [15, 43, 66]. However, these panorama-centric approaches struggle with significant camera translations due to occlusion. Consequently, re-cent methods [16, 38, 62] use video diffusion models to hallucinate consistent details during movement. In this work, we propose a novel framework that ini-tializes a globally consistent scene from a panorama and leverages video diffusion models to coherently restore and refine scene details during zoom-in exploration. 

2.2 Panoramic Video Diffusion Models 

The evolution of diffusion models [13, 36] revolutionized video synthesis, tran-sitioning from UNet-based architectures with temporal layers [1, 12] to scal-4 D. Wang et al. 

able Diffusion Transformers [31] that master complex spatio-temporal dynam-ics [4, 10, 20, 42, 58]. Although these foundation models excel at perspective con-tent, applying them to 360-degree videos remains challenging due to spherical distortion and boundary discontinuity. Pioneering efforts like 360DVD [44] and VideoPanda [54] employ adapters or multi-view attention to handle spherical geometry. Meanwhile, specific applications focus on animating static panora-mas [22] or expanding narrow field-of-view videos to full immersive views [25,39]. Rencently, ViewPoint [8] introduces a novel map representation combined with pano-perspective attention to ensure spatial continuity, whereas PanoWan [52] employs latitude-aware sampling and rotated denoising to lift powerful DiT back-bones to the panoramic domain. In this work, we propose a video-to-video frame-work that utilizes these advanced DiT priors for panoramic scene restoration. By conditioning on preliminary renderings, our method effectively recovers high-fidelity details in occluded regions while ensuring temporal consistency. 

## 3 PanoV2V-15K Dataset Construction 

3.1 Synthetic Panoramic Video Construction 

To train and evaluate our video diffusion model for panoramic restoration, we construct a large-scale panoramic video dataset follow previous work [7], the dataset consisting of 15,050 distinct indoor scenes, covering diverse room types. The dataset is synthesized from a curated repository of professionally designed indoor environments, enabling high visual fidelity and broad scene diversity. For each room, we first sample valid trajectories on the XY plane, extracting anchor points every 0.5 m. To ensure stable and consistent camera movement, we select the longest linear trajectory segment for each scene. We then place a virtual omnidirectional camera at a fixed height of 1.6,m and record a 360 ◦

panoramic video at 10 FPS, moving from one end of this linear trajectory to the other at a constant speed of 1 m/s. This procedure yields temporally coherent supervision with smooth and physically plausible linear camera motion. The data structure is shown in Fig. 2 Crucially, to construct the conditioning signal for training, we extract the first panoramic view and its corresponding Ground Truth (GT) depth map at the starting endpoint of the recorded trajectory. This single GT depth map is used alongside the first panorama to initialize a coarse 3DGS representation (detailed in Section 3.2). The rendered RGB and alpha (opacity) views from this coarse 3DGS prior, combined with the first panoramic view, serve as the conditioning input to guide the restoration of the subsequent panoramic video. 

3.2 Coarse 3DGS Initialization 

Building upon the conditioning signals extracted at the starting endpoint, we utilize the first RGB panorama and its paired GT depth map to explicitly con-struct a coarse 3DGS geometric prior. Concretely, we lift the omnidirectional Rein3D 5

Specifically, we convert the GT depth map into metric distances via a fixed scale factor, resizing it to match the panorama resolution if necessary. By com-puting the per-pixel viewing rays under the equirectangular projection model, we back-project each pixel using its exact depth to obtain a 3D coordinate. To ensure a stable and renderable 3D prior without requiring iterative opti-mization, we initialize the remaining attributes of the Gaussian primitives un-der a straightforward geometric assumption: each primitive is treated as a fully opaque, isotropic sphere. Consequently, we assign identical values across all three axes for the anisotropic scale si, set the rotation qi to a canonical identity quater-nion, and fix the opacity αi to a constant value representing full opacity. This deterministic initialization yields a lightweight yet effective coarse 3DGS scene, providing reliable geometry-aware renderings — specifically, aligned RGB and alpha panoramic sequences, to condition the downstream video diffusion model.

Video Diffusion. Video diffusion models [2, 4, 5, 20, 42] extend diffusion-based generative modeling from images to videos by learning a distribution over spatiotemporal signals. A video is represented as a sequence of frames 

x ∈ RT ×H×W ×C . In practice, videos are often encoded into a latent space using a pretrained autoencoder, yielding latent videos z ∈ RT ×H×W ×D .Video diffusion defines a forward noising process that gradually perturbs the clean video (or latent) z0 into Gaussian noise: 

q(zt | z0) = N (zt; √αtz0, (1 − αt)I) , (4) where t ∈ { 1, . . . , N } denotes the diffusion timestep and {αt} is a predefined noise schedule. The corresponding reverse process is parameterized by a neural network that iteratively denoises zt to recover z0.Video diffusion models can further be conditioned on auxiliary signals such as text prompts, reference images, or preceding frames, enabling controllable and temporally coherent video generation. 

4.2 Initialization and Camera Exploration Initial Scene Construction. To establish the base environment, we leverage off-the-shelf models capable of handling both text and image inputs. Specifi-cally, given a text prompt, we utilize DiT360 [9] to synthesize a high-resolution panorama, whereas user-provided panoramic images can be directly adopted as input. Subsequently, we employ DA2 [21] to estimate the corresponding depth map. These RGB-D priors are then lifted into a preliminary 3D Gaussian Splat-ting representation, serving as the coarse initialization of the 3D scene. 

Radial Trajectory Generation. To effectively uncover missing regions, we employ a simple yet robust trajectory sampling strategy. Using the panoramic 8 D. Wang et al. 

depth, we first calculate a safe navigation map. Rather than relying on small, lo-calized camera movements around the origin, we formulate a radial search strat-egy consisting of τ uniformly distributed trajectories radiating outward from the scene’s center. We dynamically identify the optimal global orientation for these 

τ directions to maximize the cumulative movement range within the safe navi-gation area. We then render zoom-in RGB-Alpha panoramic videos along these paths. These sequences, which naturally contain visual artifacts and transparent holes, serve as the degraded inputs for our restoration network. 

4.3 Panoramic Video Restoration Network 

To transform imperfect RGB-Alpha renderings into high-fidelity videos, we de-sign a specialized restoration network based on the Wan2.1-1.3B [42] DiT back-bone. We adapt this foundation model to the panoramic domain through a con-ditional injection mechanism and specific spherical optimizations. 

Architecture and Conditioning. Inspired by VACE [18], we utilize a context adapter architecture to effectively handle multi-modal inputs. To ensure global consistency during camera exploration, we explicitly incorporate a Context An-chor mechanism into the conditioning signal. Specifically, the input RGB-Alpha video is processed to distinguish between observed content and missing regions. Let V ∈ RT ×3×H×W denote the RGB video and M ∈ [0 , 1] T ×1×H×W denote the alpha channel. We decompose the input into two complementary streams: 

Vbackground = V ⊙ (1 − M), Vforeground = V ⊙ M (5) These streams are independently encoded by the pre-trained VAE encoder E

and concatenated along the channel dimension to form the video latent features 

Zvideo :

Zvideo = Concat (E(Vbackground ), E(Vforeground ))  (6) Crucially, since our trajectory begins from the scene origin, the starting panoramic view I0 is fully observable (i.e., M0 = 1). We treat I0 as the Context Anchor ,encoding it to obtain Zanchor = E(I0). Finally, we construct the input condition 

Zcondition by prepending the anchor to the video features along the temporal dimension: 

Zcondition = [ Zanchor , Zvideo ] (7) This design ensures that the generation process is explicitly grounded in the initial scene geometry, preventing temporal drift and color inconsistency. 

Spherical Adaptation and Optimization. Standard diffusion models typi-cally treat images as flat, uniform planes, ignoring the inherent geometric dis-tortion of Equirectangular Projection (ERP). Specifically, pixels near the poles are significantly stretched and over-sampled compared to the equator. To cor-rect this, we introduce two complementary mechanisms that align both the noise initialization and the training objective with spherical geometry: (1) Latitude-Aware Sampling. Following PanoWan [52], we align the initial noise with the geometric distortion of the input. Given an input resolution of Rein3D 9

H × W , we define the latitude ϕ for a pixel at vertical coordinate y as ϕ =(0 .5 − y/H ) · π. Since image content at high latitudes is horizontally stretched, we apply a corresponding warp to the noise coordinates: 

x′ = xc + ( x − xc) · cos( ϕ). (8) This operation effectively “stretches” the noise patterns at the poles, ensuring that the noise density matches the visual signal when mapped to the 3D sphere. (2) Latitude-Decay Loss. Complementary to the sampling, we adjust the training objective to account for pixel redundancy. Since polar pixels correspond to a much smaller physical area on the sphere, we reduce their influence on the total loss. We define a spatial weight mask W (ϕ) as: 

W (ϕ) = λ + (1 − λ) cos( ϕ), (9) where λ is a hyper-parameter. This formulation lowers the over-sampled polar regions to match their actual geometric importance, while the constant λ ensures that the model still maintains valid gradients to learn the structure at the poles. 

4.4 3D Scene Refinement 

To enhance visual quality of videos, we first employ FlashVSR [67] to upsample the video sequences, effectively recovering high-frequency details. These high-resolution panoramic frames are then projected into perspective views to serve as pseudo-ground truths. Finally, we fine-tune the 3D Gaussian Splatting against these views. During this optimization, we incorporate robust densification strate-gies and anti-aliasing techniques to effectively suppress geometric artifacts and further refine the scene texture. 

## 5 Experiments 

5.1 Implementation Details 

We implement our Rein3D framework using PyTorch. The video restoration network is initialized from the pre-trained VACE-1.3B [18] checkpoint. We fine-tune the model on our proposed dataset using the AdamW [24] optimizer with a constant learning rate of 5 × 10 −5. During training, the input panoramic video sequences are fixed to a length of 41 frames, with a spatial resolution of 448 ×896 .The total batch size is set to 32, and the training proceeds for 5,000 steps on 4 NVIDIA H200 (141GB) GPUs. For the final 3D scene refinement stage, we utilize the efficient gsplat li-brary [59]. We optimize the 3D Gaussian Splatting for 15,000 steps against the upsampled pseudo-ground truths. ## 6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduced Rein3D, a novel framework for high-fidelity 3D indoor scene generation that synergizes the explicit geometry of 3D Gaussian Splatting with the generative consistency of video diffusion models. By formulat-ing scene completion as a panoramic video restoration task, we effectively resolve severe occlusions and geometric ambiguities inherent in single-view reconstruc-tion. To bridge the data gap, we constructed a large-scale dataset specifically tailored for panoramic video inpainting, enabling robust learning of indoor scene priors. Experimental results demonstrate that our cyclic "restore-refine" strategy Rein3D 15 

produces photorealistic, 360-degree consistent representations that significantly outperform state-of-the-art baselines, particularly in supporting long-range cam-era exploration. Future work will focus on supporting detailed semantic editing and dynamic environment synthesis.
