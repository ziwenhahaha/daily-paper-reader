## Abstract 

Precision-critical manipulation requires both global trajectory orga-nization and local execution correction, yet most vision-language-action (VLA) policies generate actions within a single unified space.

This monolithic formulation forces macro-level transport and micro-level refinement to be optimized under the same objective, causing large motions to dominate learning while suppressing small but failure-critical corrective signals. In contrast, human manipulation is structured by global movement planning together with continu-ous local adjustment during execution. Motivated by this principle, we propose AnchorRefine, a hierarchical framework that factorizes VLA action modeling into trajectory anchor and residual refine-ment. ## 1 Introduction 

Vision-language-action (VLA) models have emerged as a promising paradigm for general-purpose robotic manipulation [ 4 , 6, 8, 25 , 44 ]. Their progress has been driven by large-scale embodied datasets [ 7,35 , 40 ] and pretrained vision-language backbones [ 2, 3, 24 ]. Repre-sentative systems such as OpenVLA [ 25 ] and 𝜋 0 [4 ] demonstrate that a single policy can map visual observations and language in-structions to robot actions across diverse tasks and embodiments. Despite these advances, a critical limitation remains in precision-critical manipulation. Current VLA systems often fail not because they misinterpret global motion intent, but because they lack suf-ficient local correction near execution boundaries, such as pose deviation before grasping, contact misalignment, or mistimed grip-per closure [ 26 ]. Although these errors are numerically small, they often determine whether a task succeeds or fails. We argue that this limitation arises from an action-scale mismatch 

in existing VLA policies. Long-horizon manipulation requires solv-ing two coupled but distinct subproblems: trajectory anchoring ,which captures globally coherent motion structure consistent with task semantics, and execution refinement , which models local correc-tive adjustment during contact-sensitive phases. The former gov-erns approach, transport, and coarse alignment, whereas the latter determines whether grasping, releasing, or fine repositioning can be executed reliably. Existing methods, however, optimize both within a unified action-generation objective. As a result, large-amplitude arm motions dominate learning, while small but success-critical corrective signals remain insufficiently modeled. This view is supported by the three observations in Fig. 1. As illus-trated in Fig. 1(a), human goal-directed reaching naturally follows a coarse-to-fine process, progressing from fast transport to interme-diate correction and final fine adjustment [ 13 , 14 , 36 ], suggesting that separating global motion formation from local correction is a natural principle for dexterous control. Consistent with this struc-ture, Fig. 1(b) shows that residual actions around a coarse anchor exhibit substantially smaller norms and lower covariance than raw geometric actions, indicating a more compact and learnable target space. Moreover, the failure analysis in Fig. 1(c) shows that over 88% of LIBERO manipulation failures involve gripper-related er-rors, suggesting that reliable task execution depends critically on accurate correction near contact-sensitive phases. Motivated by these observations, we propose AnchorRefine , a hierarchical VLA framework that factorizes action modeling into trajectory anchoring and residual refinement. AnchorRefine con-tains two functionally distinct components: a Trajectory Anchor Planner , which captures task-level motion structure, and a resid-ual refine module , which models execution-level deviations to improve geometric precision and contact accuracy. This design en-ables global motion organization and local corrective behavior to be learned in a more structured manner, thereby reducing interference between coarse transport and precision-critical adjustment. Beyond arm motion, we further address heterogeneity within the action space. Gripper control is more sensitive to decision bound-aries and more discrete in nature than continuous arm kinematics. Treating it as ordinary regression can blur the distinction between open and closed states and weaken correction at grasping and release phases. We therefore introduce a decision-aware gripper re-finement mechanism that adaptively modulates correction strength according to the reliability of the anchor-phase gripper prediction. This mechanism enables the model to better distinguish continuous motion refinement from discrete interaction correction. We validate AnchorRefine by integrating it into both direct-regression and iterative-denoising VLA backbones, and we eval-uate it on LIBERO-LONG [ 29 ], CALVIN ABC →D [ 34 ], and real-robot benchmarks. Across all settings, AnchorRefine consistently improves manipulation performance. These results support the cen-tral claim of this work: explicit decomposition of action modeling into global trajectory anchoring and local residual refinement is an effective principle for precision-critical robotic manipulation. Our contributions are summarized as follows: 

• We identify action-scale mismatch as a key limitation of existing VLA policies. Global trajectory organization and local precision correction are entangled within a unified generation objective, which causes small but success-critical corrective signals to be insufficiently modeled. 

• We propose AnchorRefine, a hierarchical VLA framework that factorizes action modeling into trajectory anchoring and resid-ual refinement, enabling coordinated modeling of global motion structure and local execution correction. 

• We introduce a decision-aware gripper refinement mechanism to better capture the heterogeneity of robotic action spaces, in AnchorRefine: Trajectory Anchoring and Residual Refinement Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY

## 2 Related Work 2.1 Vision-Language-Action Models 

Vision-language-action (VLA) models unify visual perception, lan-guage conditioning, and robotic control within a single policy framework. Recent systems, such as RT-2 [ 6], OpenVLA [ 25 ], and GR-1 [ 44 ], have demonstrated strong multi-task manipulation capa-bility through large-scale pretraining and transformer-based archi-tectures. From the perspective of action generation, existing VLA methods mainly follow two paradigms: autoregressive or direct regression-based prediction [ 16 , 22 , 33 , 39 , 42 , 49 ], and iterative denoising over action chunks [ 21 , 30 , 37 , 41 , 43 ]. Representative denoising-based methods include Diffusion Policy [ 11 ], 𝜋 0 [4 ], and X-VLA [ 51 ], which combine pretrained multimodal backbones with diffusion-style or flow-matching action generators. Despite their architectural differences, these methods generally predict the full action sequence as a unified target in a homogeneous action space. Our work departs from this formulation by explic-itly decomposing action modeling into trajectory anchoring and residual refinement, with the goal of improving precision-critical manipulation. 

## 2.2 Coarse-to-Fine and Residual Refinement for Manipulation 

Coarse-to-fine decomposition and residual correction are widely used principles in robotic manipulation. Hierarchical approaches introduce structure at different levels of abstraction: SuSIE [ 5] pre-dicts visual subgoals for a low-level policy, HDP [ 31 ] separates end-effector planning from trajectory generation, and related meth-ods impose intermediate structure through keyframes or trajectory traces [ 15 , 17 , 27 , 28 , 45 , 48 , 52 ]. Another line of work uses residual learning [ 19 ] to improve control [ 10 , 12 , 18 , 46 , 53 ]. Residual RL [ 23 ]and residual policy learning [ 38 ] model control as the combination of a base policy and a learned corrective term, and later studies extend this idea to imitation learning and sim-to-real [1, 12]. AnchorRefine is related to these directions, but differs in both setting and formulation. Rather than introducing hierarchy across planning phases or temporal abstraction levels, it factorizes action modeling within the same prediction horizon by separating anchor and refinement signals over the same action chunk. In addition, unlike conventional residual control, which corrects an external controller or policy, AnchorRefine jointly learns the anchor and residual branches within a unified imitation learning framework for VLA models. 

## 3 Methodology 

We present AnchorRefine , a language-conditioned vision-language-action (VLA) policy that factorizes action generation into trajectory anchoring and residual refinement . Rather than modeling an en-tire action chunk within a single monolithic target space, Anchor-Refine decomposes manipulation control into three coordinated components: trajectory-level anchor prediction, execution-level continuous correction, and decision-aware gripper adjustment. This factorization reflects the heterogeneous structure of robotic ma-nipulation, where global transport, local geometric correction, and discrete gripper switching serve distinct functional roles and exhibit different optimization characteristics.

## 3.3 Trajectory Anchor Planner

Reconstruction-style training tends to prioritize large-scale trans-port structure before fine corrective details, causing this branch to first absorb trajectory-level regularities such as reaching direction, gross transport motion, and coarse approach geometry. Moreover, because the anchor branch is trained independently without a re-finement pathway in Phase 1, it must account for the full action prediction within a single branch, which further biases it toward the dominant motion structure most directly inferable from task semantics and scene configuration. Empirical evidence supports this behavior: the residual between the trained anchor output and the ground truth exhibits a substantially smaller norm and lower variance than the original action target (Fig. 1(b)), indicating that the anchor absorbs the majority of the transport component. Two-phase training. AnchorRefine adopts a sequential two-phase training strategy, as illustrated in Fig. 2.

## 4 Experiments 

We evaluate AnchorRefine on standard long-horizon VLA bench-marks and further examine its behavior through mechanistic anal-ysis, ablation studies, and real-world validation. We first assess whether AnchorRefine consistently improves representative back-bone policies on LIBERO-Long [ 29 ] and CALVIN ABC-D [ 34 ] (Sec-tions 4.1 and 4.2). We then investigate the underlying mechanisms through quantitative analysis (Section 4.3) and evaluate the contri-bution of each key component through controlled ablations (Sec-tion 4.4). Finally, we complement the simulation results with real-world experiments on a physical robot (Section 4.5). 

## 4.1 Experimental Setup 

Backbone policies and training protocol. To examine whether the proposed anchor–refine decomposition generalizes across different policy formulations, we instantiate AnchorRefine on top of two representative VLA backbones: GR-1 [ 44 ], a regression-based pol-icy, and X-VLA [ 51 ], a diffusion-based policy. This setting enables evaluation across distinct action-modeling paradigms rather than within a single architectural family. For both backbones, we adopt the two-phase training strategy described in Section 3.6. Anchor-Refine is initialized from the corresponding benchmark-specific backbone and trained to maintain a clear separation between anchor generation and residual refinement, yielding a matched compari-son with the underlying backbone while preserving the intended coarse-to-fine action structure.

## 4.3 Mechanistic Analysis 

Beyond final task success, we further examine whether Anchor-Refine reshapes the learning problem in the intended manner. We analyse its behaviour on LIBERO from three perspectives: correc-tion directionality, optimization dynamics, and target-space com-pactness. 

4.3.1 Correction directionality analysis. To examine whether re-finement truly corrects anchor-level errors , we conduct an episode-level transition analysis over 10 LIBERO-Long tasks ( 20 rollouts per task, 200 total). For each episode, we compare the anchor-only prediction with the full anchor-plus-refinement pipeline and focus on two transitions: Fail →Success , where refinement recovers an episode that the anchor alone fails on, and Success →Fail , where refinement turns a previously successful anchor episode into a failure. As shown in Fig. 3, Fail →Success transitions occur more often than Success →Fail transitions overall, and this trend appears across most tasks rather than in a single case. This pattern indicates that refinement acts mainly as a directional error-correction mechanism, more often recovering incorrect anchor trajectories than disrupting correct ones. Consistent with the baseline failure breakdown in Fig. 1(c), we further find that gripper-related errors are reduced by approximately 26%, suggesting that AnchorRefine mitigates the contact-sensitive bottleneck highlighted in our initial motivation rather than merely shifting outcomes across episodes. Qualitative examples of such gripper correction are provided in the Appendix. 

4.3.2 Training dynamics. We compare the training loss of the monolithic baseline with the refinement-phase objective of An-chorRefine on LIBERO-Long. As shown in Fig. 3, the refinement objective decreases more rapidly from the early phase of training and stabilizes at a lower level. Although the two losses are defined over different prediction targets and are therefore not directly comparable in absolute value, the faster convergence of the refinement objective suggests that, once a coarse anchor is available, the remaining correction problem is easier to fit than predicting the full action in a single phase. This observation is consistent with our hypothesis that anchoring turns the original action prediction problem into a more localized and learnable residual correction task. 

4.3.3 Residual target compactness. Anchor-induced residualization also yields a substantially more compact target space. As illustrated in the teaser (Fig. 1(b)), we analyse action statistics on LIBERO-Long by comparing the original ground-truth action chunks with the residual targets defined relative to detached anchor predictions generated by GR-1. Specifically, we measure the mean action norm and the trace of the empirical covariance matrix over the collected targets. Compared with the raw action space, the residual target exhibits both a much smaller mean norm and a lower covariance trace, indicating that the refinement branch operates in a more localized and concentrated correction space. This result is consis-tent with the intended functional separation in AnchorRefine: the anchor captures the dominant transport structure, while refinement focuses on the remaining execution-level deviations.

Second, we keep the same two-phase setup but replace the residual target in Phase 2 with direct final-action prediction. This variant yields 73.6%, again substantially worse than AnchorRefine. These results suggest that the gain does not come from increased capacity or the two-phase procedure alone, but from learning refinement in the anchor-relative residual space. 

## 4.5 Real-World Evaluation 

We evaluate AnchorRefine in real-world settings using a LeRobot SO101 robotic arm [ 9]. For physical deployment, we instantiate AnchorRefine on the X-VLA backbone. We study four language-conditioned manipulation tasks: Pick up the black cube , Push pen into holder , Stack blocks , and Open the drawer , which cover free-space transport, insertion-style interaction, object stacking, and articulated-object manipulation, thus spanning a representative range of control difficulties and interaction patterns. For each task, we collect 50 training trajectories, all obtained as expert demonstrations through human teleoperation. During evaluation, each method is tested over 20 trials per task under identical conditions. We compare AnchorRefine with three repre-sentative baselines: ACT [ 50 ], 𝜋 0, and X-VLA. As shown in Fig. 4, AnchorRefine achieves the highest average success rate in real-world evaluation, outperforming all baselines overall. At the task level, it performs best on Pick up the cube , matches the strongest AnchorRefine: Trajectory Anchoring and Residual Refinement Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY 

baseline on Push pen into holder , and shows clear gains on Stack blocks and Open the drawer .The improvement is particularly evident on tasks that demand higher terminal precision and exhibit greater sensitivity to contact. In particular, Stack blocks and Open the drawer require accurate local alignment near the final phase of interaction, where even small pose or timing errors can lead to failure. The stronger performance of AnchorRefine on these tasks is consistent with the intended role of the method: the anchor phase produces a usable coarse motion trajectory, and the refinement phase corrects local residual errors that matter most near contact and task completion, where precise adjustment is often decisive for successful execution. Overall, the real-world results further corroborate the findings from the simulation benchmarks. By decomposing action genera-tion into trajectory anchoring and refinement, the proposed frame-work improves execution precision not only in simulated rollouts but also in real-world scenarios, thereby demonstrating effective transfer beyond simulation and reinforcing the practical value of the proposed formulation. 

## 5 Conclusion 

This work presents AnchorRefine, a structured action-generation framework for VLA models that decomposes action prediction into trajectory anchoring and residual refinement. By separating coarse motion formation from fine-grained execution correction, the framework offers a more effective formulation for modeling precision-critical manipulation. Experiments on simulation bench-marks and real-robot tasks demonstrate that AnchorRefine con-sistently improves the performance of both regression-based and diffusion-based backbones. The current framework remains limited by a non-adaptive refinement formulation and limited evaluation of generalization under open-world settings. Future work will investi-gate adaptive refinement strategies and conduct broader evaluation on unseen tasks.

## A Appendix A.1 Benchmark and Task Details 

A.1.1 CALVIN Benchmark. We evaluate AnchorRefine on CALVIN, a standard long-horizon language-conditioned manipulation bench-mark in which a policy executes temporally extended task composi-tions from visual observations and language instructions. CALVIN contains 34 tasks spanning skills from pick-and-place to articulated object manipulation, and includes four environments with a Franka Panda arm. We adopt the ABC →D evaluation protocol, where policies are trained on demonstrations from environments A, B, and C and evaluated in a zero-shot manner on environment D. The evalua-tion set contains 1,000 instruction chains, each consisting of five consecutive tasks. For CALVIN, baseline results are taken from the official benchmark. 

A.1.2 LIBERO Benchmark. We further evaluate AnchorRefine on LIBERO, a benchmark for lifelong robot manipulation learning. LIBERO contains four task suites: LIBERO-Spatial, LIBERO-Object, LIBERO-Goal, and LIBERO-Long. The first three mainly evaluate the transfer of declarative and procedural knowledge, whereas LIBERO-Long focuses on more challenging long-horizon tasks with diverse object interactions. We use LIBERO-Long as the main benchmark because it provides the most demanding setting for evaluating whether the proposed coarse-to-fine action decomposition improves both global trajectory formation and local execution correction. 

A.1.3 Real-World Setting. To complement the simulation results, we conduct real-world experiments on four manipulation tasks with different levels of contact sensitivity, terminal precision, and trajectory-control difficulty. Experiments are performed on the LeRobot SO101 platform. Visual observations are provided by a fixed third-person RGB camera and a wrist-mounted camera. At test time, the policy takes image observations, the task instruction, and robot states as input, and outputs an action trajectory for execution. Each task is evaluated over 20 trials with a binary success crite-rion defined by the final task outcome. Success rate is reported as the fraction of successful trials. Unless otherwise noted, all methods use the same camera setup, instruction format, and rollout budget. 

Pick up the cube. The robot grasps a black cube from the tabletop and places it into a box. Success requires correct grasping and final placement inside the box. 

Push pen into holder. The robot establishes stable contact with a black pen and pushes it into the holder. Success requires the pen to end inside the holder. 

Stack blocks. The robot picks up the blue block and places it on top of the yellow block. Success requires the blue block to remain stably stacked for at least 5 seconds. 

Open the drawer. The robot reaches the drawer handle or front edge and pulls the drawer open. Success requires the drawer to reach the target extent and remain open at the end of the episode.

X-VLA -based instantiation, the anchor branch retains the original denoising-based action-generation mechanism and serves as the coarse trajectory generator for the refinement phase. The anchor phase is initialized from the released checkpoint of the corresponding backbone. Its objective is to produce globally plausible trajectories that not only capture the dominant motion pattern of the task, but also provide a reliable structural prior for subsequent refinement. 

A.3.2 Refine-Phase Training. In the second phase, the anchor branch— including the vision encoder, state encoder, and anchor transformer— is frozen, and only the refinement module is optimized to predict a residual correction on top of the coarse trajectory. The action latent tokens are initialized from the anchor latent tokens and jointly optimized with the refinement module. For GR-1, these tokens correspond to the query tokens used for action prediction; for X-VLA, they correspond to the noisy action tokens refined during denoising. Conditioned on the observation context and the coarse action prediction, the refinement phase learns to correct local execution errors, especially in contact-rich and terminal interaction stages where small deviations can directly lead to task failure. This two-phase scheme decouples global trajectory formation from local execution correction and encourages learning in a lower-variance residual space, thereby making the correction objective more fo-cused and easier to optimize.

A.4.2 Gripper Error Around the Transition Step. Figure 6 illus-trates gripper-level correction around a critical interaction mo-ment. The horizontal axis is centered at the gripper transition step, and the curves show the distance between the predicted gripper position and the ground-truth gripper position before and after refinement. The shaded region marks the contact-sensitive neigh-borhood around the transition. In contrast, after refinement, the error at the same critical moment is pulled back below the tolerance threshold. This result suggests that many anchor failures arise from local mis-alignment at the gripper-switching stage rather than from globally incorrect motion. Therefore, AnchorRefine mainly acts as a targeted local cor-rection mechanism around failure-prone steps. By restoring the gripper error to a feasible range near the critical interaction mo-ment, the proposed gripper-refinement design effectively improves contact reliability and terminal execution accuracy.
