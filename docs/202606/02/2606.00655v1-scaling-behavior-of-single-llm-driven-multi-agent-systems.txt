Abstract 

The burgeoning field of LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) promises to tackle com-plex tasks through collaborative intelligence, yet fundamental questions regarding their scal-ing behavior and intrinsic collective dynamics remain underexplored. This paper systemat-ically investigates how the performance of a homogeneous MAS evolves as the number of agents increases, isolating the variable of col-laboration from model or knowledge hetero-geneity. We propose the Sequential Iterative Multi-Agent System (SIMAS) framework, a minimalist architecture centered on sequential inter-agent communication, to clearly observe scaling effects. Through extensive experiments across diverse tasks and model scales, we es-tablish that MAS performance does not scale monotonically with agent count but follows a pattern of diminishing returns, governed by a trade-off between collaborative synergy and co-ordination overhead. Our findings reveal that effective MAS requires a sufficiently capable base LLM, that task type critically modulates the optimal agent count, and that collective in-telligence is an emergent property contingent on strategic interaction design rather than a guaranteed outcome of agent plurality. The performance degradation stems coordination overhead rather than merely long-context fail-ure, and the scaling tendency generalizes across interaction architectures like structured debate topologies. This work provides a foundational understanding of MAS scaling laws, offering practical guidance for designing efficient col-laborative systems and challenging the prevail-ing assumption that more agents invariably lead to better performance.

In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in text

Though individual agent has exhibited excellent problem-solving ability in expansive fields with performance enhanced—for instance, through Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompt-ing (Wei et al., 2022) to elicit step-by-step reason-ing, or by enabling models to leverage external APIs via frameworks like Toolformer (Schick et al., 2023), however, many real-world challenges, such as sophisticated software development or multi-faceted problem-solving, inherently require col-laborative efforts. This necessity has driven the emergence of LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems (MAS), a field that has rapidly evolved from early exploratory frameworks to complex systems (Xi et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2025), where multiple agents interact to achieve common goals. In MAS, collaboration enables agents to share knowledge, assign specialized roles, and in-tegrate their complementary strengths, thereby tackling tasks beyond the scope of any single agent—ranging from handling extensive contexts (Zhang et al., 2025) to complex social simulations (Qian et al., 2023). This vision is supported by a growing series of frameworks: AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023) facilitates customizable agent conversations, CAMEL (Li et al., 2023) explores role-playing for idea exploration, and MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2023) assigns standardized workflows to simulate a soft-ware company. These systems often deploy agents as "reasoning experts" or "fact-checkers" to boost collective output. However, despite these engineer-ing advances and recent efforts to probe the robust-ness of agent communication (He et al., 2025), a fundamental scientific understanding of MAS col-lective behavior remains under-explored. 2.1 Architectures for Multi-Agent Collaboration 

The foundation of MAS lies in defining how agents interact to extend the capabilities of individual LLM. Early research focused on unstructured com-municative frameworks. For instance, CAMEL (Li et al., 2023) introduced "inception prompting" to facilitate autonomous role-playing, demonstrat-ing that distinct personas can guide solution explo-ration. Building on this, AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023) provided a flexible infrastructure for dynamic con-versations between agents, while MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2023) incorporated Standard Operating Pro-cedures (SOPs) with rigid role assignments to streamline complex workflows like software gen-eration. Similarly, AgentVerse (Chen et al., 2023) and the recent Agent-Pro (Zhang et al., 2024) in-troduce iterative optimization mechanisms, where agents engage in "reflect-and-refine" loops to cor-rect errors dynamically. Despite the diversity of existing multi-agent ar-chitectures, they all fundamentally rely on a core collaborative process involving iterative commu-nication, shared contextual memory, and sequen-tial reasoning (Xi et al., 2025, 2023; Guo et al., 2024). Advanced mechanisms such as dynamic reflection are merely engineering scaffolds to mit-igate the inherent friction of this core, meaning all such systems belong to the same interactive paradigm (Talebirad and Nadiri, 2023). By design-ing SIMAS as a minimalist system that isolates this core (Xi et al., 2025), we reveal that the trade-off between collaborative synergy and coordination overhead is a universal property of all multi-agent systems, challenging the assumption that adding more agents always improves performance and un-derscoring the importance of architectural design. 

2.2 Scaling Behavior in Multi-Agent Systems 

Recently, the community has begun to formally investigate the scaling laws and inherent limita-tions of LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems (MAS). A notable concurrent work (Qian et al., 2025) ex-plores scaling multi-agent collaboration by struc-turing agents into a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) topology, termed MacNet. By enforcing localized information passing they successfully mitigate con-text explosion and observe a logistic growth pattern as the system scales to hundreds or thousands of agents. Conversely, other recent studies have begun to critically examine the inherent vulnerabilities of MAS as they scale. (Flint et al., 2025) investigate group size effects, revealing that expanding agent counts can paradoxically exacerbate collective mis-alignment and consensus failure. Similarly, (Cemri et al., 2025) provide a comprehensive taxonomy of why MAS fail, highlighting critical issues such as reasoning fragmentation, compounding hallucina-tions, and cascading errors during complex inter-actions. Complementary to the coordination over-head perspective, a concurrent work by (Shehata and Li, 2026) reveals an orthogonal failure mode: the algorithmic “Bystander Effect” in MAS, where simulated social pressure triggers cognitive loafing: agents abandon independent reasoning to sycophan-tically comply with swarm consensus even when internally deriving correct answers. Together, these works establish that multi-agent collaboration faces both coordination and social-psychological chal-lenges, with effective MAS requiring architectural safeguards against both. While these studies offer crucial insights, they leave a critical gap in understanding the raw, un-mitigated scaling dynamics of MAS. Our work bridges this gap by introducing the SIMAS frame-work as a deliberate, minimalist control group. By intentionally stripping away complex topologies, information-filtering mechanisms, and structured aggregation, SIMAS utilizes pure sequential com-munication with progressive history accumulation to empirically quantify the tension collaborative synergy and coordination overhead to demonstrate the scaling behavior of unstructured multi-agent dialogue.

3.3 Setting of Single Agent 

In this experiment, each agent ai ∈ A was driven by LLM and assigned descriptions, personalities, beliefs, and strengths, with the descriptions set as "the i-th assistent in the group chat" and therefore not considered as influencing factors. All agents are powered by the same LLM, sharing identical model parameters and knowledge base. To intro-duce diversity in reasoning styles, each agent is assigned a unique profile before the discussion be-gins. The profile consists of three attributes: personal-ity, core beliefs, and expertise . Personality is a gen-eral disposition (e.g., "Skeptical" or "Aggressive") that guides the tone and focus of their reasoning, 

expertise is a specific domain of knowledge (e.g., "Data Interpretation" or "Logical Reasoning") they are instructed to prioritize, and core beliefs are a set of principles (e.g., "Precision outweighs speed") that influence their approach to problem-solving. These attributes are automatically generated by the language model for each agent to simulate a group of diverse experts. The specific configura-tions and formal definitions of agent attributes are detailed in Appendix A.

off is most critical. Regarding agent configurations, we established a baseline condition where agents possess personality traits, expertise, and beliefs. For performance evaluation, we employ three complementary benchmarks, each targeting dis-tinct cognitive capabilities: The Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU) dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021), covering 57 di-verse subjects across STEM, humanities, and social sciences, to assess general knowledge and broad reasoning capabilities. From MMLU, we select 6 representative disci-plines (see Table 3) and sample 17 questions each to balance efficiency with robustness. Each experi-mental configuration is repeated three times, with results averaged to ensure stability. We employ two widely-used, open-source model families: Llama-3.1 (AI, 2024) and Qwen2.5 (Yang et al., 2024). To understand the impact of model scale, we use versions with 8B/7B and 70B/72B parameters from each family, and due to computa-tional cost and inference speed constraints, we did not evaluate larger models beyond 70B parameters. These models provide a strong baseline for general-purpose reasoning, allowing for a clear analysis of multi-agent scaling effects with balance of capabil-ities and generation speed. To isolate the contribution of each attribute, we compare this baseline against four alternative con-figurations: one lacking all three attributes, and three configurations each removing exactly one of the three attributes. This structured ablation en-ables us to analyze how each factor contributes to system performance and stability. For these abla-tion studies, we specifically use the Llama-3.1-70B model with agent number n ∈ { 2, 4, 6, 8}, chosen to represent key points in the scaling range while reducing experimental overhead. To address the potential confound between agent count and context length, we conduct a con-trolled token-padding experiment (Section 4.3) where total input length is fixed across all agent counts using neutral filler tokens. To assess cross-architectural generality, we implement a parallel AutoGen GroupChat debate baseline (Section 4.4) with the same model–dataset combinations.

If a MAS is sufficiently driven, the per-formance will scale with diminishing re-turns, following an inverted-U relation-ship with agent count. 

For sufficiently driven MAS, performance initially improves due to collaborative syn-ergy—diverse perspectives and mutual error cor-rection—but peaks at an optimal n∗. Beyond this point, coordination overhead (information re-dundancy, conflicting reasoning paths) dominates and causes decline. Increasing n in SIMAS also lengthens the total context, and to decouple these factors, we pad con-versation history with neutral filler tokens to a fixed total length. 

Finding 4 

Performance degradation is causally driven by coordination overhead rather than long-context failure. 

As shown in Figure 6, the inverted-U pattern remains clearly visible for Qwen2.5-72B across three MMLU subjects. Finding 6 

For reasoning tasks, the primary failure mode of minimalist MAS is the fragmen-tation of coherent thought. 

On reasoning-intensive benchmarks such as ab-stract algebra (Figure. 20a) and AIME 2025 (Fig-ure. 8), CoT consistently matches or significantly surpasses the best SIMAS configuration. This failure mode stems from the frag-mentation of coherent reasoning chains across se-quential agent turns: without a dedicated mecha-nism to synthesize fragments into a unified line of reasoning or to correct accumulating errors, the col-lective output becomes disjointed and drifts from the correct solution path. On open-ended generation and coding tasks (Figures 20c, 9), results are mixed and model-dependent: SIMAS achieves marginal advantages in some settings but shows inconsistent bene-fits. Finding 7 

The value of multi-agent collaboration is not automatic but is engineered through specific interaction architectures. 

The frequent underperformance of SIMAS ver-sus CoT clarifies that collective intelligence is an emergent property of interaction architecture , not a guaranteed outcome of agent plurality. As sum-marized in Table 2, frameworks like AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023) and Multi-Agent Debate (Liang et al., 2023) incorporating structured workflows and criti-cal evaluation loops surpass single-agent CoT on challenging benchmarks (e.g., AIME 2024: 16.7– 20.0% vs. 16.7% Naive-CoT with Llama-3.1-70B), while SIMAS, lacking such mechanisms, incurs overhead without reliably capturing synergistic ben-efits. This, together with our AutoGen replication (Section 4.4), implies that the pursuit of collective intelligence must be architectural, not merely nu-merical. 

6 Conclusion 

This work systematically demonstrates that LLM-based Multi-Agent System performance does not 8scale linearly with agent count but exhibits a pat-tern of diminishing returns. Effective collaboration first requires a sufficiently capable base LLM. The optimal number of agents is a critical design pa-rameter, heavily dependent on task type and model architecture, balancing synergy against overhead. The performance degradations derived from col-laboration overhead generalizes across interaction architectures. Crucially, collective intelligence is not an automatic outcome of adding agents but an emergent property contingent on deliberate interac-tion design. Without architectural support for syn-thesis and refinement, multi-agent dialogue risks inefficiency. Future MAS development must there-fore prioritize designing adaptive, task-aware col-laboration protocols over simply increasing agent plurality. 

Limitations 

This study, while providing foundational insights into MAS scaling behavior, has several limitations that point to future research directions. First, our focus on homogeneous agents using a single LLM isolates collaboration effects but does not capture the heterogeneous model and tool use common in practical systems, where complementarity might alter scaling dynamics. Second, our minimalist SIMAS architecture, while clarifying first prin-ciples, omits sophisticated coordination mecha-nisms (e.g., voting, dynamic workflows) that could mitigate the overhead we observed. While our AutoGen comparison (Section 4.4) confirms the inverted-U law’s cross-architectural generality, ex-ploring a broader range of topologies (DAG-based, hierarchical) and their interaction with model capa-bility remains an open direction. Third, although our token-padding experiment (Section 4.3) con-firms that coordination overhead rather than long-context failure drives the inverted-U pattern, we evaluated only output accuracy; deeper psycholin-guistic analysis of agent dialogues—quantifying semantic diversity, conversational dynamics, and belief divergence—could further characterize the micro-level mechanisms of collaboration break-down. Fourth, our evaluation primarily uses closed-book QA benchmarks, which may not fully capture collaborative benefits in longitudinal, creative, or tool-augmented tasks. Finally, scaling was tested only up to 8 agents; the dynamics of much larger collectives and their potential for novel emergent phenomena remain unexplored. 

Ethical Concerns 

This study focuses on the algorithmic and archi-tectural principles of multi-agent scaling, utilizing exclusively publicly available and widely-adopted benchmark datasets (e.g., MMLU, AIME, E-KAR). No sensitive personal data, simulated social dy-namics, or real-world decision-making scenarios are involved. The Sequential Iterative Multi-Agent System (SIMAS) framework is designed as a mini-malist research tool to investigate fundamental col-laboration dynamics, explicitly excluding applica-tions in opinion manipulation, autonomous action with real-world consequences, or the generation of deceptive content. All experimental interactions are confined to closed, controlled environments for problem-solving, and no user privacy information is stored in our codebase or logs; only aggregated performance metrics are reported. During the re-search process, AI assistants (including DeepSeek-R1) were employed as tools to aid in specific auxil-iary tasks such as brainstorming initial ideas, gen-erating code for experimental pipelines, polishing textual descriptions, and creating visualizations. We explicitly state that all core research ideas, ex-perimental design, data analysis, interpretation of results, and scientific conclusions were originated, critically evaluated, and decisively finalized by the human authors. The AI tools served strictly as supportive instruments, and every piece of their output was rigorously reviewed, validated, and of-ten substantially revised by the authors to ensure correctness and alignment with the research objec-tives. The primary ethical considerations—such as potential reasoning bias, coherence fragmenta-tion, or the amplification of base model errors—are emergent properties stemming from the underly-ing LLM capabilities and the chosen task prompts, rather than inherent flaws of the SIMAS architec-ture itself. Should the principles explored here inform the design of future MAS deployed in open or user-facing contexts, rigorous additional risk as-sessment and governance mechanisms would be mandatory, adhering strictly to established ethical guidelines in AI research and development.

A Formal Definitions for MAS 

Definition 3 (System Output) . For an input ques-tion q ∈ Q , the system output is defined as: 

o = fM(q) = Π output (hn,T , a 1)

where hn,T ∈ H represents the complete conversa-tion history after T rounds of discussion. 

Definition 4 (Agent Configuration) . Each agent 

ai ∈ A is configured as a quadruple: 

ai = ⟨LLM , d i, p i, b i, e i⟩

where LLM represents the underlying language model (shared across all agents), di ∈ D denotes the description, pi ∈ P denotes the personality trait, bi ∈ B denotes the core beliefs, ei ∈ E de-notes the expertise, with D, P, B, E representing the respective attribute spaces. 

Property 3 (Model Homogeneity) . All agents share the same underlying language model: 

∀ai, a j ∈ A , LLM (ai) = LLM (aj )

Property 4 (Configuration Heterogeneity) . Agents achieve diversity through distinct configuration pa-rameters: 

∀ai, a j ∈ A , i ̸ = j ⇒ (di, p i, b i)̸ = ( dj , p j , b j )

B Framework of SIMAS 

B.1 Problem Provider 

The Problem Provider module loads datasets (e.g., MMLU, E-KAR), preprocesses questions to ensure consistency, and normalizes answers by mapping responses to standardized formats (e.g., options A/B/C/D). 

B.2 Agent Generator 

The Agent Generator takes as inputs the disci-pline type of the problem and the number of agents 

N , and outputs N dynamically generated agents. These agents are created by invoking the LLM API with tailored prompts to assign unique roles and personalities. 

B.3 Group Chat Engine 

The Group Chat Engine executes the main work-flow of SIMAS, managing multi-round discussions. It monitors for timeouts or errors and implements a retry mechanism with a maximum of 100 retries (max_retries=100 ). 

B.4 Evaluator 

The Evaluator compares the final group chat an-swer with the ground truth and records auxiliary metrics, such as confidence scores, to assess re-sponse quality. The framework leverages the LLM’s API for text generation and processing, offering the follow-ing key functionalities: It supports Agent Gener-ation by automatically creating agents with dis-tinct personalities and expertise based on specified quantities and role requirements. Its Group Chat Management orchestrates communication among agents, ensuring sequential contributions and main-taining discussion history for context-aware re-sponses. The Answer Evaluation functionality extracts the final answer from the last agent’s re-sponse after group chat completion and compares it with the ground truth to compute accuracy. The framework supports seamless switching be-tween different language models, enabling compar-ative experiments across models. This flexibility ensures robust evaluation of multi-agent system performance under varying configurations. 

C Mechanisms of U-Shaped Recovery 

A notable deviation from the canonical inverted-U pattern occurs in Philosophy with Llama3.1-70B, where accuracy exhibits a U-shaped curve: declin-ing from the single-agent baseline through inter-mediate agent counts before partially recovering at n=8 . Qualitative analysis of agent dialogues on an MMLU item concerning Aristotle’s concept of incontinence (knowing the wrong and doing it anyway) reveals a three-phase mechanism. In Phase 1 ( n=2 –3), dialogue remains focused and efficient: one agent dominates with correct rea-11 soning while coordination overhead is minimal. In Phase 2 ( n=4 –5), social meta-discourse (apologies, acknowledgments, redundant praise) proliferates and divergent minority interpretations emerge—for instance, conflating akrasia with the distinct con-cept of enkrateia . This fragments the reasoning chain and causes the accuracy dip. In Phase 3 (n=8 ), the sheer volume of majority agents reit-erating the correct pre-training-consistent answer statistically suppresses earlier noise, producing an apparent recovery. This recovery, however, consti-tutes pseudo-stability rather than genuine collabo-rative intelligence, as it depends on the model’s pre-training bias toward the consensual answer rather than on structured synthesis of diverse per-spectives. Qwen2.5-72B does not exhibit the U-shape, attributable to its lower semantic diversity and stronger long-context robustness. This case reveals a key distinction: error-propagating tasks (mathematics, formal logic), where a single incorrect step irreversibly derails the solution, exhibit classic inverted-U patterns with steep post-peak declines; consensus-recoverable tasks (interpretive philosophy, fact retrieval with high prior agreement), where majority voting over redundant correct responses can mask mid-scale co-ordination failures, may exhibit U-shaped recovery contingent on model-specific semantic diversity.
