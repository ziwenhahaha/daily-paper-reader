# ABSTRACT 

We investigate the application of MAP-Elites (a well-known quality diversity algorithm) to design levels for First-Person Shooter (FPS) games. We consider two well-known map representations (All-Black and Grid-Graph) and introduce two novel representations (Point-Line and Spatial-Layout) that improve the characterization of FPS maps. We define a series of metrics to describe maps’ topological properties (which solely depend on maps’ layout), and emergent properties (which must be evaluated through actual gameplay). We perform an in-depth analysis to identify the most suitable features to guide MAP-Elites illumination process. We apply MAP-Elites with Sliding Boundaries (MESB) to evolve populations of FPS maps. Our results show that the new representations can generate maps with higher diversity and quality than the representations previously used for evolving FPS maps. 

Keywords Quality Diversity · Map-Elites · Procedural Content Generation · Level Design · Map Design · First-Person Shooter 

# 1 Introduction 

Search-based procedural content generation has been applied to evolve maps for FPS games using traditional [ 1 , 2, 3, 4]and interactive evolutionary computation [ 5 ]. All these approaches focus on one specific aspect of level design, such as the average gameplay time between kills [ 1], gameplay balance [ 2 , 3], enabling fleeing behavior [ 4 ], or adherence to a set of design guidelines [ 5]. These approaches generate maps that maximize one specific objective and usually explore a limited design space [5]. In this paper, we explore applying quality diversity methods, specifically MAP-Elites [ 6], to evolve maps for multiplayer FPS games. MAP-Elites is an evolutionary algorithm for exploring and optimizing diverse, high-performing solutions across an illuminated search space defined by a pair of features. Individuals are characterized by a fitness that the system must maximize and an archive of elite solutions that the algorithm maintains. The solutions are stored in bins identified by the values of the features the system uses to illuminate the search space. In particular, we apply MAP-Elites with Sliding Boundaries (MESB) [ 7 ], which maintains an archive of adaptive bins. We consider two well-known map representations (All-Black and Grid-Graph) [ 1 ] and introduce two new representations (Point-Line and Spatial-Layout) we specifically created to enrich the characterization of FPS maps. We define several metrics to describe maps’ topological properties, computed from the maps’ layout, and emergent properties, evaluated through actual gameplay. We performed an extensive analysis of the available features and focused on the ones that, in the literature, are regarded as the most interesting ones for FPS map design [ 8, 1, 9]. In particular, we focus on three topological features: the walkable area and the map symmetry ( maxSymmetry ), which provide high-level descriptions of the map topology [ 8 , 1]; and the map eccentricity ( averageEccentricity ), which was specifically introduced for graph-based representations of FPS maps [ 9]. # 2 Related Work 

Search-based Procedural Content Generation (SB-PCG) [ 14 ] applies a generate-and-test approach to procedural content generation. Candidates are first generated, then evaluated using a fitness function, and new candidates are created based on the result of the evaluation. SB-PCG encompasses several methods of genetic and evolutionary computation and it has been applied to several game genres including first-person shooters. 

2.1 SB-PCG for FPS Games 

Cardamone et al. [ 1] were the first to apply search-based techniques to generate playable FPS maps for the “deathmatch" game mode of Cube 2: Sauerbraten [ 15 ]. In their work, they introduced four genotypes ( All-White , All-Black , Grid 

and Random-Digger ) and the mapping functions to generate the corresponding maps for Cube 2 . Candidate maps were evaluated by simulating a 10 minutes match with four bots; fitness was computed as the average time between the bot starts fighting until the time it is killed by an opponent. In [ 1], the authors argued that interesting maps should have features allowing for long fights to happen, such as escape routes and well-placed resources. Lanzi et al. [ 2] focused on evolving maps that allowed for balanced 1-vs-1 "deathmatch" matches between bots with different skill levels and play style (e.g., aggressive, defensive). They also used Cube 2 , the All-Black genotype [ 1 ], and evaluated maps through simulation of matches involving different types of bots. Fitness function was computed as the entropy of the score (number of kills) ratio distribution since the higher the entropy, the more balanced the score distribution, and therefore the more balanced the match. Bhojan and Wong [ 16 ] focused on faster evolution methods to evolve maps online for the “Capture the Flag" game mode in a game they developed to test their method. Bhojan and Wong [ 16 ] aimed at fast evolution and therefore did not use simulations but defined a fitness which was the sum of measured map features such as, connectivity, forced collision points, flag fairness, overall flag fairness [ 16 ]. They generated maps by placing tiles that were either an indoor area, an outdoor area, or inaccessible, following a rule of adjacency with pre-existing tiles. The maps were then cleaned of artifacts and scanned with a Flood Fill algorithm [ 17 ] to identify regions that would then be connected with doors where needed. The maps were finally decorated with strategic points such as spawn points, flag locations and covers. Ølsted et al. [ 5 ] moved to evolving maps for the game FPSEvolver focusing on the “Bomb Defusal" game mode, similar to that of Counter-Strike . They defined a set of guidelines ( the good engagement ) and designed a genotype that could create layouts that adhere to these concepts, avoiding dead ends and ensuring arenas. They applied iterative evolutionary computation to evolve maps with human subjects that played the maps and then were asked to leave simple binary (thumb-up or thumb-down) feedback which was used to guide the evolution [ 5]. Cachia et al. [ 3 ] evolved maps that occupied more than one floor in Cube 2 with a genome that combined different representations for the first and the second floor—the former as a set of corridors and connected rooms identifying passable tiles, the latter using a Random-Digger [ 1 ]. Fitness was based on simulations with artificial agents, on the available space for navigation, and on the presence of specific level patterns. Loiacono et al. [ 4] took an approach similar to [ 1 ], leveraging the same All-Black representation to evolve maps that foster fleeing behavior. Fitness was computed as the number of times during a match that a bot lost track of an enemy it is currently fighting. They showed that their approach could generate maps that enabled fleeing behavior in bots that are designed to always attack while creating maps that significantly reduce pace [4]. 

2.2 Map-Elites for Games 

MAP-Elites has been used to generate content for a variety of game genres such as, platformers, “Zelda-like" adventures, puzzles, and card games. 

Platformer Games. Khalifa et al. [ 18 ] introduced Constrained MAP-Elites to evolve a variety of playable Super Mario Bros. levels and compared different simulation approaches to generate them [ 18 ]. Warriar et al. [ 19 ] implemented 

PlayMapper , a variation on the MAP-Elites algorithm, that is able to generate Super Mario Bros. levels of different sizes and grants a significant amount of control over their generation. Fontaine et al. [ 20 ] applied a set of MAP-Elites methods to illuminate the latent space of a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) assessing the algorithms’ performance on the task of generating scenes for Super Mario Bros. with specific features. 2Procedural Generation of First Person Shooter Maps using Map-Elites 

Adventure Games. Alvarez et al. [ 21 ] introduced Interactive Constrained MAP-Elites to evolve dungeon rooms in 

Evolutionary Dungeon Designer (EDD), a mixed-initiative co-creativity tool. They combined EDD with Feasible-Infeasible 2-Population (FI2Pop) genetic algorithm [ 22 ] to create an interactive version of Constrained MAP-Elites 

in which users guided the search by selecting the features to explore and gave feedback on the generated rooms [ 21 ]. Charity et al. [ 23 ] searched for levels with specific mechanics in the GVG-AI Framework ’s games [ 24 ], which include a “Zelda-like" game [ 23 ]. González-Duque et al. [ 25 ] applied Intelligent Trial-and-Error (a form of Bayesian Optimization that uses MAP-Elites to generate the priors) to perform Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment , producing levels of the desired difficulty for a “Zelda-like" game. Viana et al. [ 26 ] applied MAP-Elites to generate levels with locked doors missions and enemy placement, with high quality and diversity . 

Puzzle Games. Charity et al. [ 27 ] developed Baba is Y’all , a mixed-initiative version of Baba is You where players design levels in collaboration with the machine. The game allows players to edit Baba is You levels, similar to Super Mario Maker , with the added goal of filling the archive of the underlying MAP-Elites algorithm, where levels are placed based on the combination of mechanics (rules) that are activated when solving them. The system can propose various starting levels for the user to edit based on what is already in the archive and what’s currently missing to help players fill the archive [27]. 

Card Games. MAP-Elites has also been used to generate competitive card decks for Hearthstone . Fontaine et al. [ 7] noted that MAP-Elites uniformly divides the behavior space, however this can lead to a mismatch with the true distribution of the behavior space, which would result in a less effective illumination. Moreover, they argue that knowing a priori distribution can be hard or even impossible. To solve these issues, they propose MAP-Elites with Sliding Boundaries (MESB), a variation of MAP-Elites where boundaries between cells are not placed uniformly based on the value of the features, but instead are dynamically placed at certain percentage marks of the distribution. A remap frequency is specified, meaning that the boundaries are periodically recalculated, allowing for a good estimation of the distribution of the search space. [7] 

Other Genres. Khalifa et al. [ 28 ] have applied MAP-Elites to generate levels for bullet hell games. Gravina et al. [ 29 ]applied Constrained Surprise Search to generate weapons for Unreal Tournament III first person shooter. Quality Diversity methods have yet to be applied to the generation of levels for FPS games, which is the goal of our research. 

# 3 Project Arena 

Project Arena [ 11 ] is an open-source platform for research in first-person shooter games developed using the Unity game engine. It has a modular architecture structured in a series of managers, bots, and visualizations that can be easily customized, thanks to the many available Unity extensions. 

Managers. The Game Manager handles the main lifecycle, including setup, game modes, matches, map management, and generation. Project Arena has three game modes: Duel , implementing the classic deathmatch available in all FPSs; 

Target Rush , with increasingly difficult waves of attacking enemies that the player must eliminate; and Target Hunt , in which the player must search and eliminate enemies within a time limit. The Map Manager handles the load/save of existing maps as well as the generation of maps. It currently provides three classical map generation methods based on cellular automata, random digger, and binary space partition [ 30 ]. The Experiment Manager lets users organize a series of experiments in a collection of studies, each one consisting of a set of cases (defined by a set of game modes, maps, and bots) and an evaluation procedure (round-robin by default); all the information generated during the experiments are logged; designers can also include an optional survey if the experiments involve human subjects. 

Bots. The framework provides several bots that can be customized based on their (i) skill level, expressed as a percentage between 0% (rookie) and 100% (expert); (ii) fighting strategy, which depends on their weapon (e.g., a sniper rifle needs a completely different tactics than a shotgun or grenade launcher). 

# 4 FPS Map Representations 

In our study we considered four representations of maps for first-person-shooters: All-Black [1], Grid-Graph [11 ]; 

Point-Line and Spatial-Layout that we specifically designed for using with MAP-Elites. 

All-Black [1] carves walkable spaces from a map that is initially filled with walls. Maps are lists of triplets representing 

rooms and corridors . Rooms are defined by the coordinates of their left-bottom corner, height, and width; corridors are 3Procedural Generation of First Person Shooter Maps using Map-Elites defined by the coordinates of their left-bottom corner and length, which encodes whether they are horizontal (positive length) or vertical (negative). This representation has issues with locality (small mutations in the genome can cause major changes in the map phenotype) and redundancy (different genomes can produce identical maps). Furthermore, the resulting maps often contain several dead ends and confusing features [5]. 

Grid-Graph is inspired by the dungeon generation in Rogue [ 31 ], the father of the rogue-like game genre. It starts with a map full of walls (like All-Black ) that it partitions into an R × C grid (fixed to 3 × 3 in Rogue [ 31 ]); each grid position can contain at most one room, defined by its position within the cell, its width, and height. Rooms in adjacent cells can be connected by corridors, which are encoded as a boolean value for each couple of rooms horizontally and vertically adjacent. This approach is more compact and less redundant than All-Black however the number of layouts depends on the grid size and it is generally more limited than All-Black .

All-Black has known limitations [ 5 ] and Grid-Graph allows for a limited number of topologies which depends on the predefined grid size. Accordingly, we introduced two representations we specifically designed for quality diversity search algorithms by solving some of the limitations of All-Black while allowing for a richer and much diverse search space. 

Point-Line genome is a sequence of P tuples ⟨p1, p 2, s 1, s 2, c ⟩ each representing a pair of rooms connected by an L-shaped corridor; p1 and p2 are the rooms’ positions; s1 and s2 are their sizes; c represents the orientation of the L-shaped corridor. Point-Line improves locality over All-Black by explicitly connecting rooms with corridors. It eliminates the issue of dead ends of All-Black [ 5 ] and it is more compact since one point represents both rooms’ and corridors’ coordinates. All-Black rarely generates long corridors since these are built as a sequence of short corridors. In contrast, by defining corridors using their start and end positions, Point-Line gives long and short corridors an equal chance making it more likely to explore a wider variety of designs than All-Black . However, it does not solve the issue of redundancy. 

Spatial-Layout is inspired to the work of [ 32 ]. The genome consists of (i) a list of R axis-aligned rectangular rooms defined by their width and height; (ii) a list of L line segments defined by two points; (iii) the minimum separation parameter s which determines the minimum distance between rooms’ borders in the genome. Spatial-Layout does not include room positions since these are determined using a Satisfiability Modulo Theories (SMT) solver, implemented using Python’s Z3 library, based on the following constraints: rooms are inside the maps boundaries; rooms do not overlap; rooms are close enough to the lines. Once room positions are determined, corridors are positioned by computing the minimum spanning tree from the Delaunay triangulation of the rooms, which ensures connectivity. Mutation changes rooms and lines parameters; crossover swaps rooms and lines between two parent genomes. Whitehead [ 32 ] tackled dungeon designs where an SMT solver would suitably represent a linear layout with a start, an end, and some optional areas. FPS maps have more complex topologies that entail loops, arenas, and alternate routes [ 33 ]. To improve map complexity and navigability, we extended the approach of [ 32 ] with a heuristic to add corridors between rooms intersected by the genome’s lines, if they were not already connected. Note that, SMT solver is not deterministic and the same genome can produce more topologies, which might lead to poor locality but more diversity. Furthermore, Spatial-Layout genomes may not allow a feasible solution and thus might be discarded. 

# 5 FPS Map Features 

We defined several features to describe maps based on their topology (46 features) and on the gameplay they enable (23 features). In this section, we focus on the most relevant features that we also discuss in the experimental section; we refer the interested reader to [10] for the complete list of 69 features. 

Topological Features describe the map’s structure. They solely depend on the map’s layout and are extracted from the phenotype without the need for simulations. First, we apply a technique for region decomposition based on the line segment Voronoi diagrams to extract a graph representation of the map’s topology. Then, we compute several metrics such as the number of loops, the average distance between rooms, the number of alternative paths, etc. [ 10 ]. We also apply a grid-based method to extract more advanced features such as the area , computed as the number of tiles that are walkable compared to the total number of tiles in the map, or the map’s visibility matrix, describing for each tile the number of tiles that are visible from it. We gain insights on the average visibility, the number of local maxima, their average distance and the maximum visibility. Finally, we compute high-level map properties like the map symmetry as the percentage of tiles that are symmetrical counterpart with respect to an axis ( xSymmetry and ySymmetry ) and the maximum symmetry that the map has ( maxSymmetry computed as the max of xSymmetry and ySymmetry ). Another 4Procedural Generation of First Person Shooter Maps using Map-Elites example is the averageEccentricity of the rooms’ eccentricity that is computed, for each room, as the maximum distance between the room and all other rooms. 

Emergent Features are computed from actual gameplay sessions which, in our study, are simulated using bots implementing a variety of fighting strategies. They include metrics such as pace, average fight time, target loss rate, kill difference, etc. [ 10 ]. Pace measures the frequency of combat engagements, normalized between 0 and 1. # 6 Experimental Results 

We performed a series of experiments to evaluate the quality and diversity of the maps evolved using MAP-Elites and the four representations of FPS maps (Section 4). At first, we performed a series of simulations with random maps to collect data about the several features we defined (Section 5) and focused on the ones that, according to the literature, are the most relevant for FPS map design [ 8 , 1, 9]. In this section, we present the result of our analysis on the four most important features ( area , MaxSymmetry , pace , and averageEccentricity ) and refer the reader to [10] for an analysis of the other features we considered. We grouped the four features into two pairs: area and MaxSymmetry , which capture only topological characteristics of the maps; pace and averageEccentricity , which combine gameplay and topological characteristics. Next, we applied MAP-Elites with Sliding Boundaries (MESB) [ 7] to evolve highly diverse, high quality, FPS maps which could enable a balanced gameplay. 

6.1 Fitness 

Previous research in the evolution of FPS maps used fitness function either based on topological [ 16 , 5] or emergent gameplay properties [ 1, 2 , 3 , 4]. In our experiments, we defined a fitness inspired to the latter approaches [ 1 , 2, 3, 4]that enables a balanced gameplay in a Duel (i.e., 1-vs-1 deathmatch ) game mode independently of the skill levels and the equipment of the players involved. We selected two bots with very different skill levels (15% and 85%) equipped with two weapons that require completely different fighting tactics: (i) a sniper rifle, suited for long distance combat and more effective in environments with large visibility areas, (ii) a shotgun, effective only in close combat scenarios. 6.2 Design of Experiments 

We applied MAP-Elites with Sliding Boundaries (MESB) [ 7 ] using an archive of 10 bins for each feature, for a total of 100 maximum solutions. Each run starts with 20 random individuals and lasts for 400 iterations using 10 emitters ; thus, each iteration generates 10 new individuals. Fitness is computed as the average entropy over five matches (Section 6.1). After a map is evaluated using five matches, the illuminating features are computed for each match, averaged, and the resulting values are used to update the corresponding MESB archive with the new individual. The experiments were performed using the PyRibs library [ 12 ] for quality diversity optimization which is built on a highly modular conceptual QD framework and provides access to all the most important algorithms and statistics.

# 7 Conclusions 

We investigated the application of MAP-Elites with Sliding Boundaries (MESB) to the evolution of maps for First-Person Shooter (FPS) games. We introduced two novel representations (Point-Line and Spatial-Layout) we designed to tackle the limitations of existing well-known representations of FPS maps [ 1, 2, 5]. We defined several metrics to describe the maps’ topological and emergent properties. We selected the features that, according to the literature, are considered the most relevant for FPS map design [ 8 , 1, 9]. We combined the selected features into two pairs (area-maxSymmetry and pace-averageEccentricity ) that we used to illuminate MAP-Elites. We presented the results of two sets of experiments aimed at evolving balanced FPS maps using different pairs of features to illuminate the search space ( area-maxSymmetry and pace-averageEccentricity ). The results show that the combination of quality diversity and our representations (Point-Line and Spatial-Layout) generate the best trade-off between balance and design, unlike previous optimization algorithms and representations [ 1, 2, 4 ]. Our representations generate cleaner maps that enable several gameplay tactics, either with long loops and long corridors to achieve low-paced gameplay or very centralized maps with a large central room to achieve high-paced gameplay. Our study is limited in that we used bots using only two weapons enabling long-distance and close combat fighting tactics, which, however, implement the most typical behaviors of human players [ 33 , 1 ]. Traditional search methods tend to rapidly focus on interesting (high-performing) maps that users can enjoy playing [ 1, 2, 5]. In contrast, for its nature, quality diversity methods explore a much wider range of maps, including the ones that, although enabling balanced gameplay, would be dull or uninteresting to play for human subjects.
