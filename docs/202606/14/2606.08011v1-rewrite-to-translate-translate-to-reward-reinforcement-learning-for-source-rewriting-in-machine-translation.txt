## Abstract 

Although directly prompting off-the-shelf Large Language Models (LLMs) to generate meaning-preserving source rewrites can effec-tively enhance Machine Translation (MT) qual-ity, doing so requires manually tuning prompts for different MT models. In this work, we propose RLSR (Reinforcement Learning for 

Source Rewriting), a novel RL-based frame-work for training a source rewriting model with-out tuning prompts for each MT model. RLSR optimizes the rewriting model by directly us-ing the improvement in downstream translation quality yielded by each rewritten source as the reward. Extensive experiments across six MT models and 16 language pairs demonstrate that our 4B rewriting models trained via RLSR sig-nificantly outperform the no-rewriting baseline and existing same-scale prompt-based rewrit-ing baselines, while achieving competitive per-formance against prompt-based baselines based on the 235B LLM. 

## 1 Introduction 

Source rewriting, sometimes referred to as pre-editing, involves modifying the source text before feeding it into a Machine Translation (MT) model to improve translation quality. Particularly when the internal workings of the MT model are inac-cessible, source rewriting serves as an effective approach to enhancing translation quality (Pym, 1988; Reuther, 2003; Seretan et al., 2014; Miyata and Fujita, 2021; Ki and Carpuat, 2025). With the rise of Large Language Models (LLMs), leveraging specific prompts to guide LLMs to perform rewrit-ing has proven to be an effective strategy (Koretaka et al., 2025; Ki and Carpuat, 2025). However, the prompt design for these LLM-based rewriting methods is highly heuristic and heavily relies on empirical trial-and-error. This ad-hoc nature leads to a critical issue: the effectiveness of a given rewriting strategy is often inconsistent Original

For instance, a prompt strategy that works well for certain MT models may prove entirely inef-fective for others. Consequently, this necessitates manually tuning rewriting prompts individually for each specific MT model, a process that is both time-consuming and resource-intensive. Can we discover a universal rewriting method that performs consistently well across diverse MT models with-out requiring manual prompt tuning? To answer this, we shift the rewriting paradigm to automated optimization via a reinforcement learning (RL)-based approach, namely RL for Source Rewriting ( RLSR ; see Figure 1). Since the goal of source rewriting is to enhance translation quality, we directly optimize the rewriting model toward this objective: we use the difference in au-tomatic metric scores between translations of the rewritten source and original source as the reward, and train the model with an on-policy RL algo-rithm. We evaluated RLSR against various baseline methods on six MT models across all 16 lan-guage pairs in the WMT2025 General MT Shared Task (Kocmi et al., 2025). Experimental results demonstrate that our 4B rewriting models trained via RLSR significantly outperform both the no-rewriting baseline and existing rewriting methods of the same scale, while achieving competitive performance against existing methods based on the 235B LLM. Our further analysis reveals that, compared to rewriting models trained via super-vised fine-tuning (SFT), RL-trained models gener-ate more diverse and creative rewrites, effectively avoiding degeneration into merely copying the orig-inal source text. In summary, our approach pro-vides a streamlined method for source rewriting that adapts seamlessly to diverse MT models and significantly improves translation quality. 

## 2 Related Work 

As source rewriting is a well-studied subfield of MT, various strategies have been evaluated since the era of statistical MT (Shirai et al., 1993; Mi-tamura and Nyberg; Yoshimi and Sata, 1999). These strategies include, but are not limited to, word reordering (Xia and McCord, 2004; Li et al., 2007; Hoshino et al., 2015), paraphrasing (Callison-Burch et al., 2006; Mirkin et al., 2009; Marton et al., 2009; Ki and Carpuat, 2025), simplification (Šta-jner and Popovic, 2016; Štajner and Popovi´ c, 2019; Mehta et al., 2020; Ki and Carpuat, 2025), and conversion to translationese (Mehta et al., 2020). However, some studies have questioned the actual effectiveness of certain rewriting strategies (Zhu, 2015; Du and Way, 2017). Furthermore, other works have conducted detailed analyses to inves-tigate which specific aspects of MT outputs are affected by source rewriting (Miyata and Fujita, 2021; Yamaguchi et al., 2024), as well as under what conditions such rewriting is appropriate and effective (Miyata and Fujita, 2017). With LLMs demonstrating exceptional text gen-eration capabilities (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023), recent works have begun exploring their potential in source rewriting. Ki and Carpuat (2025) investigated the efficacy of three distinct prompt strategies. Similarly, Koretaka et al. (2025) proposed an approach that uses feedback from a segment-level reference-free evaluation model during inference, enabling the LLM to iteratively rewrite the source text. However, these methods exhibit significant limitations: they either heavily rely on heuristic manual prompt tuning, or suffer from computationally expensive inference (primar-ily due to the repeated execution of both the rewrit-ing model and the MT model). In contrast, our pro-posed RLSR framework fundamentally eliminates the need for manual prompt tuning. Moreover, RLSR is efficient at inference time, as it requires running the rewriting model and the downstream MT model only once. 

## 3 Methodology 

3.1 Preliminary: Source Rewriting 

Source rewriting aims to improve the translation quality of a given MT model not by changing the model itself, but by generating an alternative, meaning-preserving source text designed to yield a higher-quality translation. Formally, let s denote a source text and r de-note its reference translation. Let M be a fixed MT model that maps a source text to a translation, and let Rθ be a source rewriting model parame-terized by θ. Given s, the rewriting model gener-ates a rewritten source text ˜s = Rθ(s). The MT model then produces translations of both the origi-nal source M (s) and the rewritten source M (˜ s).Let Q(s, t, r ) be an automatic evaluation func-tion that measures the quality of a model translation 

t of the source s against the reference translation 

r, where higher values indicate better translation quality. To quantify the utility of a rewrite, we measure the marginal improvement it brings to the final translation. We define this improvement as the reward R:

R(s, ˜s, r ) = Q(s, M (˜ s), r )−Q(s, M (s), r ). (1) The goal of source rewriting is to find a rewriting model Rθ that achieves a positive reward ( R > 0). When the rewriting model is an LLM with-out task-specific rewriting training, its behavior is controlled through natural-language prompts that implicitly attempt to maximize this reward (Ki and Carpuat, 2025; Koretaka et al., 2025). However, rewriting quality depends heavily on the prompt, the LLM, and the downstream MT model—a prompt that helps one MT model may harm another—so prompt-based rewriting often requires careful per-model tuning. 23.2 Proposed Method: RLSR 

To overcome the limitations of prompt-based rewriting, we aim to directly optimize the rewriting model to maximize the reward R(s, ˜s, r ) defined in Eq. 1. However, optimizing this objective via stan-dard SFT is intractable due to the non-differentiable nature of the target MT model M , the evaluation metric Q, and the discrete text generation process of ˜s. Since we cannot backpropagate gradients through the MT model or the evaluation metric, a natural approach is to formulate this optimiza-tion as an RL problem. Driven by this motivation, we propose RLSR , which directly leverages im-provements in downstream translation quality as the reward. The RLSR framework starts with an initial rewriting model, denoted as the reference pol-icy Rref (for instance, an instruction-following LLM equipped with a rewriting prompt). During training, for a given source text s from a dataset 

D = {(s, r )}, the current rewriting model Rθ (act-ing as the policy) samples a rewrite ˜s ∼ Rθ(·| s).Next, the fixed target MT model M translates both 

s and ˜s. Models We employed two categories of LLMs as our target MT models ( M ): general LLMs, including Gemma3 27B ( gemma-3-27b-it ) (Team et al., 2025), Gemma4 31B ( gemma-4-31B-it 2), Qwen3 30B (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 )(Yang et al., 2025), and Qwen3 4B (Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 ) (Yang et al., 2025); and translation-optimized models, including Translategemma 27B ( translategemma-27b-it )(Finkelstein et al., 2026) and our custom Qwen3 4B SFT-RL. 3 For the source rewriting models, we used Qwen3 4B and Qwen3 235B (Yang et al., 2025). Performance Degradation in SFT The evalua-tion results, presented in Table 2, demonstrate that 5the SFT-trained models consistently underperform their RL-trained counterparts across all metrics, and paradoxically, even perform worse than the no-rewriting baseline. 

The “Copying” Degeneration To understand this degradation, we measured how often each model copies the source verbatim (i.e., outputs 

˜s = s). SFT-trained models degenerate into copy-ing in 89.74% and 75.66% of cases for Gemma3 27B and Qwen3 30B, respectively, whereas the RL-trained models do so in only 6.33% and 8.09% of cases. This behavior is not simply inherited from unchanged training pairs: a statistical check re-veals that ˜s∗ = s instances constitute only 10.10% and 13.98% of the Gemma3 and Qwen3 training sets. A more plausible explanation lies in the sur-face form of the supervised targets: most reward-improving rewrites differ from the source by only a few words or by splitting a long sentence, so the target ˜s∗ shares the vast majority of its tokens with 

s. Under the token-level cross-entropy objective of SFT, collapsing into a “copying” strategy thus becomes a shortcut that minimizes the loss across almost the entire training set—an easily accessible local optimum. Conversely, in the RL framework, merely copying the original source yields zero re-ward, thereby forcing the policy to explore more diverse and creative rewriting strategies to effec-tively maximize translation quality. 

Filtering Unchanged Sources To probe whether removing these unchanged pairs forces the SFT model to learn meaningful strategies, we trained another model on a filtered dataset (“SFT (filtered)” in Table 2) that strictly excludes all ˜s = s instances. However, performance deteriorated further. We at-tribute this to the SFT objective assigning equal weight to every token: as noted above, reward-improving rewrites alter only a small fraction of the source, so the few tokens that drive the im-provement are vastly outnumbered by those carried over unchanged, and the equally weighted SFT loss cannot single them out within these highly simi-lar sequences. RL, by contrast, must differentiate trajectories with nearly identical tokens but vastly different rewards, letting it focus on the crucial modifications. In summary, RL provides a far more effective paradigm for training rewriting models than SFT. While advanced SFT techniques, such as identi-fying critical tokens and assigning them larger weights in the training objective (Zhang et al., 2025), might narrow this gap, we leave the explo-ration of such methods to future work. 

5.2 Does a Trained Rewriting Model Generalize to Other MT Models? 

As described in Section 3.2, each rewriting model is trained for a specific MT model M . Training a ded-icated rewriting model for every MT model, how-ever, is relatively costly: in our experiments, a sin-gle RLSR run takes roughly 60 GPU-hours. This raises a natural question: can a rewriting model trained via RLSR be reused across multiple MT models without per-model retraining? 

To investigate this, we conduct two experiments using Gemma3 27B and Qwen3 30B as the MT models. We deliberately select these two models because they are built upon different LLM families, and we therefore expect them to exhibit distinct preferences for rewritten sources. 

Experiment 1: Cross-Application We take the rewriting models that were independently trained for Gemma3 27B and Qwen3 30B, and we swap them: the rewriting model optimized for one MT model is directly used (without any further training) to rewrite sources for the other MT model.

RLSR refrains from rewriting an already-adequate source Example 1 in Table 5 contrasts the four methods on a single source that is already fluent and readily translatable. The three prompt-based methods nonetheless transform it, because their prompts instruct the LLM to simplify, para-phrase, or rewrite in every case. Simplification and Easy Translate stay faithful but flatten the au-thor’s deliberately loaded diction, replacing “in-fantilised diaper king” with the blander “child-ish . . . king” and unpacking the compound “Diet Coke-summoning button” . Paraphrase drifts fur-ther still: it drops the original “diaper king” image and fabricates content absent from the source— “a whimsical touch that evokes the indulgence of a pampered figure, almost like a grown-up version of a child’s fantasy” —a fluent but unsupported elabo-ration, which the MT model then translates as if it were genuine. This tendency to rewrite indiscrimi-nately helps explain why, despite using the 235B LLM, Paraphrase falls below the no-rewriting base-line on almost all MT models in Table 1. ## 6 Conclusions and Future Work 

In this work, we introduced RLSR, a framework that trains a source rewriting model by directly using the improvement in downstream transla-tion quality of the rewritten source as the reward, thereby eliminating the need for heuristic prompt tuning. Across six MT models and all 16 language pairs of the WMT2025 General MT Shared Task, our 4B RLSR models significantly and consistently outperform both the no-rewriting baseline and ex-isting same-scale prompt-based rewriting methods, while remaining competitive with prompt-based rewriting baselines based on the 235B LLM. We see two promising directions for future work. First, since RLSR is considerably more expensive to train than SFT, developing more efficient training methods could substantially reduce this cost. One concrete avenue, building on our analysis in Sec-tion 5.1, is to improve the SFT objective—for ex-ample, by up-weighting the few critical tokens that drive reward improvements (Zhang et al., 2025)— so that it approaches the effectiveness of RLSR at a fraction of the training cost. Second, the cross-MT adaptability of RLSR-trained models warrants a more systematic study, including jointly training 8a single rewriting model that serves a larger and more diverse set of MT models. 

## Limitations 

First, our approach assumes that the automatic met-rics we use faithfully reflect improvements in trans-lation quality. However, because the RLSR reward is computed directly from xCOMET, the learned metrics (xCOMET and MetricX) may be biased in favor of RLSR-rewritten sources. We mitigate this concern by additionally evaluating with GEMBA, an LLM-prompt-based, reference-free metric that operates on fundamentally different principles; the consistent gains under GEMBA indicate that the improvements are not merely metric overfitting. A more definitive solution would be human evalua-tion, which we did not conduct due to its substantial cost. Second, our experiments are limited in scope. While we cover six MT models and 16 language pairs, validating RLSR on a broader range of MT models, domains, and language pairs would further strengthen our conclusions. Finally, RLSR training is considerably more ex-pensive than standard SFT, since it requires repeat-edly running the downstream MT model and the evaluation metric to compute rewards. Under tight compute budgets, our method may be impractical to apply.

## D Implementation Details of Training Rewriting Models via SFT 

The source and reference texts used for SFT are identical to those used for RLSR (Section 4.1). To construct the supervised target for each source, we prompted Qwen3 4B with the “Easy Translate” prompt (Appendix A) to generate N = 16 distinct rewriting candidates. For each target MT model, we then selected the candidate ˜s∗ that yielded the largest xCOMET-XL improvement over the orig-inal source (i.e., the candidate with the highest reward R defined in Eq. 1); when no candidate improved upon the original source, we used the original source itself as the target ( ˜s∗ = s). We fine-tuned Qwen3 4B on the resulting (s, ˜s∗) pairs for a maximum of 3 epochs with an effective batch size of 512 (accounting for gradient accumulation and multi-GPU parallelism). We used a learning rate of 2 × 10 −5 with a cosine decay scheduler and a warmup ratio of 0.05, and adopted the 8-bit AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2019; Dettmers et al.). ## F Training Rewriting Models via DPO 

Motivation As discussed in Section 5.1, the ad-vantage of RL over SFT for training rewriting mod-els may arise because RL forces the policy to dis-criminate between rewrites that are nearly identical in surface form yet receive markedly different re-wards. Intuitively, the learning objective of Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) shares this property: it trains the model to separate a preferred (chosen) output from a rejected one, and such pairs typically differ only subtly. Mo-tivated by this similarity, we investigate whether DPO can serve as an effective alternative for train-ing rewriting models.

Summary Overall, DPO provides a more effec-tive training signal than SFT but a substantially less reliable one than RLSR. We attribute this gap to the distinction between off-policy and on-policy learn-ing. RLSR is on-policy: it continually re-samples 15 rewrites from the current policy and obtains fresh reward feedback, allowing it to explore the rewrit-ing space and correct its own errors throughout training. DPO, by contrast, is trained on a fixed set of preference pairs collected once from the refer-ence model; this static dataset may cover the rewrit-ing space only sparsely, leaving the policy unable to discover better rewriting strategies that on-policy exploration reaches—and, in the worst case (Trans-lategemma 27B), even steering it toward rewrites that actively harm translation quality. 16
