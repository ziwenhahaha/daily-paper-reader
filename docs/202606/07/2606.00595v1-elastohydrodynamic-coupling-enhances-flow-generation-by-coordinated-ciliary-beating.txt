# Elastohydrodynamic coupling enhances flow generation by coordinated ciliary beating 

Shota Nakano, 1 Shinji Deguchi, 1 and Daiki Matsunaga 1, ∗

> 1

Graduate School of Engineering Science, The University of Osaka, Toyonaka 560-8531, Japan 

(Dated: June 2, 2026) Ciliary arrays pump fluid at low Reynolds number through non-reciprocal beating and phase co-ordination between neighbouring cilia. Previous studies have often found antiplectic metachronal waves to be more effective than symplectic waves in enhancing transport, and have proposed several physically intuitive explanations for this preference. What remains incomplete is a predictive analyt-ical understanding of how hydrodynamic coupling and beat geometry determine the flow-maximising phase difference. Here, we address this problem in two steps: we first use reinforcement learning to identify flow-maximising coordination in a bead–spring cilia model, and then introduce an an-alytically tractable reduced model, termed the tilted-slider model, to analyse the weak-coupling limit. Reinforcement learning identifies antiplectic coordination as the flow-maximising state in linear arrays, and further analysis shows that the nearest-neighbour phase difference accounts for most of the flow enhancement. We then use the tilted-slider model to show that a shift of the time-averaged position opposite to the effective-stroke direction enhances fluid transport through its coupling with the elastic restoring force. The reduced model further reveals that changes in beat geometry can shift the optimum from antiplectic to symplectic coordination. These results identify a simple elastohydrodynamic mechanism underlying flow-maximising metachronal coordination. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Motile cilia are slender, actively beating organelles that generate fluid motion in biological processes ranging from mucociliary clearance and cerebrospinal-fluid transport to feeding, pumping and locomotion in microorganisms [1–4]. At the scale of individual ciliary beats, the surrounding fluid is typically governed by Stokes flow, where time-reversible reciprocal actuation cannot, by itself, produce net locomotion, as captured by the scallop theorem [5, 6]. A single motile cilium generates pumping by executing a non-reciprocal beat, usually consisting of an effective stroke away from the surface and a recovery stroke closer to the surface [7, 8]. In ciliary arrays, an additional level of organisation arises when neighbouring cilia beat with a phase difference, forming metachronal waves [9–11]. Waves propagating in the direction of the effective stroke are termed symplectic, whereas those propagating in the opposite direction are termed antiplectic [12]. The pumping performance of such arrays is thus characterised by a cycle-averaged flow rate that depends on both the individual beat and the phase relation between neighbouring cilia. Previous studies have provided important insights into metachronal coordination in autonomous or self-sustained ciliary systems. Early reduced models represented ciliary dynamics using hydrodynamically coupled oscillators or rotors. These studies identified several ingredients that allow fluid-mediated interactions to generate synchronisation and phase locking, including beat geometry [13], oscillator compliance [14], and generic phase-response conditions [15]. Extensions to chains and carpets further demonstrated travelling metachronal waves and collective phase dynamics in larger arrays [16, 17]. Building on these reduced descriptions, recent theories have connected local ciliary mechanics to large-scale phase structures: beat and array geometry can select stable wavevectors [18], multiscale biophysical models describe discrete metachronal-wave states [19], and large-scale carpet models link spontaneous phase coordination to fluid pumping [20]. Wave characteristics can also be shaped by elasticity and surface effects [21], while near-field hydrodynamic interactions can select the travelling-wave direction [22]. Together, these studies have substantially clarified how metachronal waves emerge, persist and acquire their collective phase structure in self-organised ciliary systems. When the beat kinematics and phase relation are prescribed or externally controlled, the cycle-averaged flow rate can be examined directly as a function of phase coordination. Computational studies in a range of ciliary flow settings have repeatedly identified antiplectic coordination as advantageous for transport or swimming performance. In ciliary carpets, antiplectic coordination enhances transport and mixing compared with symplectic coordination [23]. The same trend has been reported in models of mucociliary transport in a two-phase flow [24–26], as well as for ciliated swimmers [27, 28]. In this context, the antiplectic advantage has often been rationalised through local interactions between neighbouring cilia during the beat cycle. Gauger et al. [29] associated this advantage with a cilium undergoing its recovery stroke close to a neighbour, which hinders the flow generated during recovery and thereby increases net transport. Dong et al. [30] reported a similar effective–recovery asymmetry, with antiplectic waves reducing flow blocking during the effective stroke but increasing it during the recovery stroke. More broadly, this asymmetry has been interpreted in terms of shielding or direct obstruction between neighbouring cilia: the phase relation determines whether the effective- or recovery-stroke flow is preferentially obstructed [31–34]. Taken together, these studies establish the phase relation between neighbouring cilia as a key variable controlling pumping when the individual beat is prescribed. The first question addressed here is how hydrodynamic coupling and beat geometry determine the phase relation that maximises the cycle-averaged flow rate. This question builds on previous interpretations based on near-field shielding, direct obstruction and empirical tip-spacing arguments, which provide physically transparent explanations of why antiplectic waves often outperform symplectic waves. However, these local-in-time descriptions do not directly yield a cycle-averaged analytical criterion for predicting the flow-maximising phase difference, its dependence on inter-cilium spacing, or its variation with beat geometry. The second question is therefore under what conditions this criterion selects antiplectic coordination, and whether this preference can be weakened or reversed as the beat geometry is changed. To address these questions, we combine reinforcement-learning optimisation of a torque-driven cilia model with an analytically tractable reduced model. Reinforcement learning has recently been used to discover and analyse actuation strategies for low-Reynolds-number microswimmers [35–37]. The resulting optimised motions identify the phase-dependent flow enhancement and motivate a tilted-slider model, in which each cilium is represented by a bead constrained to move reciprocally along a tilted line near a no-slip wall. This reduced model retains the excursion geometry relative to the wall and the wall-mediated hydrodynamic coupling needed to analyse phase-dependent pumping explicitly. A weak-coupling perturbation analysis then yields an analytical expression for the cycle-averaged flow rate and identifies a shift in the time-averaged ciliary position opposite to the effective-stroke direction, hereafter referred to as the negative shift, as the leading mechanism for phase-dependent flow enhancement. The same theory gives an analytical criterion for flow-maximising phase selection by linking this negative shift to the balance between wall-mediated horizontal and vertical flow responses, thereby explaining how spacing and beat geometry alter the preferred phase relation, including a possible shift towards symplectic coordination in the tilted-slider model.

> IV. MECHANISM OF FLOW ENHANCEMENT

In the previous section, reinforcement learning showed that antiplectic coordination is preferred over the explored parameter range of cilia number, cilia spacing, and bending stiffness. Although reinforcement learning can search for the flow-maximising coordination under each condition, it does not by itself reveal the underlying physics of the obtained solution. This makes it difficult to assess the generality of the obtained coordination and, hence, to understand how the optimal coordination varies across parameter space, including the transition between symplectic and antiplectic coordination. To complement this limitation, we now introduce a reduced tilted-slider model in which each cilium is represented by a bead constrained to move along a tilted straight line near a no-slip wall. This model is inspired by rower-type minimal models for hydrodynamically coupled cilia, which have proved useful for describing synchronisation and metachronal coordination [17, 49, 50]. In contrast to these rower-type models, which employ geometric switching between stroke states, the present model is driven by prescribed phase-lagged forcing together with an elastic restoring response. The tilt angle serves as a reduced descriptor of the mean beating orientation relevant to wall-bounded pumping, while the detailed effective-recovery effective–recovery stroke asymmetry is ignored. In this section, we use this reduced model to reveal analytically how the flow-maximising coordination depends on system parameters.  

> A. Reduced tilted-slider model

The reduced model considered here consists of two spherical beads of radius a, separated by a distance ℓ along the x-direction. Each bead is constrained to move along a straight slider axis in the xz -plane, and the slider axis is obtained by rotating ex by an angle ϕ ∈ (−π/ 2, π/ 2) about the positive y-axis, as shown in figure 5. The unit vector along the slider axis is es = (cos ϕ, 0, − sin ϕ) and the perpendicular unit vector is es, ⊥ = (sin ϕ, 0, cos ϕ). The position of i-th bead is written as ri = ci + sies (i = 1 , 2), where si(t) is the scalar displacement along the slider axis, and c1 = −ℓex/2 + hez and c2 = ℓex/2 + hez denote the centres of the two slider axes, located at a distance h away 10 from the wall. The system is driven by the active forces defined as 

f1(t) = fA cos( ωt + δ), f2(t) = fA cos( ωt ), (15) where fA is the forcing amplitude, ω is the driving angular frequency, and δ is the imposed phase difference. Each bead is attached to its reference position by a linear restoring spring of stiffness kr . The total force on the i-th bead is therefore written as

The reduced model is then characterised by three dimensionless parameters: the bead radius a∗ = a/h , the inter-slider spacing ℓ∗ = ℓ/h , and the spring stiffness

Note that, as expected, a single slider cannot produce a net flow because of time-reversal symmetry [5, 6]. Although each individual unit does not break time-reversal symmetry, a collection of sliders can still generate net flow by breaking this symmetry collectively [51, 52]. In the standard convention, also used in the previous section, symplectic and antiplectic coordination are distinguished by comparing the direction of the effective stroke with that of phase propagation. Since an isolated slider is reciprocal and has no intrinsic effective stroke, we define the coordination type based on the direction of phase propagation relative to the resulting fluid transport direction. The model is studied using two complementary approaches: analytical and numerical methods. We first briefly describe the numerical method in the next subsection, while the asymptotic solution is derived using a regular perturbation method in the following section.  

> 1. Numerical method

In the numerical analysis, the slider dynamics are solved using the force (16) and the mobility relation (6). The slider velocity ˙ si = es · vi is obtained from the mobility relation together with the constraint es, ⊥ · vi = 0 where vi is the bead velocity. The position si is updated using a forward Euler scheme with time step ∆ t∗ = 4 .0×10 −5. We sweep the phase difference δ ∈ [−π, π ] and obtain the maximised flow rate Q∗

The tilted-slider model shows that the optimal coordination corresponds to the phase lag that induces a negative shift in the time-averaged position, which explains why the optimal coordination depends on the beat geometry ϕ.We finally examine whether the negative shift in the time-averaged position is also observed in the two cilia system analysed in § III C. As seen in the tilted-slider model, the flow rate is negatively correlated with the horizontal shift, and larger flow rates are expected when the time-averaged position is shifted in the negative direction. The two cilia prefer antiplectic coordination, ∆ < 0, and this preference is consistent with the prediction of the tilted-slider model for relatively small beating angles ϕ. Figure 8(b) compares the flow-maximising phase difference ∆ with the phase difference ∆ ⟨x∗⟩ that minimises ⟨x∗⟩ over all simulated conditions, showing again that the negative shift provides a useful criterion for predicting the flow-maximising coordination. In Appendix D, we briefly examine how the optimal coordination is altered by externally modifying the beating orbit with a background simple shear flow, and show that the resulting phase-selection trend is consistent with the prediction of the tilted-slider model. The above analysis shows that collective flow enhancement is associated with the negative shift induced by hydro-dynamic coupling with neighbouring cilia. This negative shift provides a useful signature of enhanced transport, but it does not by itself generate the enhancement. Rather, the flow enhancement arises through elastohydrodynamic coupling, in which the negative shift modifies the elastic response of the cilia and thereby produces larger net flow.  

> V. CONCLUSIONS

We have investigated how hydrodynamic coupling and beat geometry determine the phase difference between neighbouring cilia that maximises the cycle-averaged flow rate in wall-bounded Stokes flow. We addressed this problem by first using reinforcement learning to optimise the cilia model without prescribing either the beat pattern or the phase relation. Guided by the resulting optimised motions, we then developed a reduced tilted-slider model that allows a weak-coupling perturbation analysis of phase-dependent flow enhancement. In the cilia model, reinforcement learning optimisation of a single cilium produced a non-reciprocal beating motion with distinct effective and recovery strokes. For larger arrays, the optimisation selected antiplectic metachronal coordination as the flow-maximising state, with approximately uniform nearest-neighbour phase differences. For two cilia, the phase-sweep analysis reproduced most of the enhancement obtained by direct reinforcement learning optimisation. These results show that the imposed phase difference accounts for most of the flow enhancement, while allowing the actuation of the two cilia to be optimised independently provides only a smaller additional contribution. The tilted-slider model isolates the mechanism behind this phase dependence. In this model, each cilium is repre-sented by a bead constrained to move reciprocally along a tilted line near a no-slip wall, with the tilt parametrising the excursion geometry relative to the wall. A weak-coupling perturbation analysis yields an analytical expression for the cycle-averaged flow rate. The analysis shows that an imposed phase difference produces a shift in the time-averaged ciliary position opposite to the effective-stroke direction. This negative shift enters the leading elastic contribution to the flow-rate expression, and therefore provides the main contribution to the phase-dependent change in the cycle-averaged flow rate. The same analysis shows that the flow-maximising phase difference is determined by the balance between the wall-mediated horizontal and vertical flow responses. This explains why small tilts favour antiplectic coordination whereas larger tilts can move the optimum towards the symplectic side in the tilted-slider model. These findings complement previous interpretations based on near-field shielding, direct obstruction and instanta-neous spacing between neighbouring cilia by providing a cycle-averaged interpretation of the same physical asymmetry. In particular, the empirical tip-spacing metric proposed by Dong et al. [30] predicts larger flow when neighbouring tips are farther apart during the power stroke and closer during the recovery stroke. The present analysis identifies the hydrodynamic mechanism behind this rule: close approach during the recovery stroke strengthens the interaction that drives the negative shift, whereas the forward shift during the effective stroke is weaker because the cilia are farther apart. The antiplectic optimum is therefore selected not by the wave direction alone, but by whether the phase relation produces this negative shift. The weak-coupling theory makes this interpretation predictive. It gives an analytical criterion for flow-maximising phase selection by linking the negative shift to the balance between wall-mediated horizontal and vertical flow re-sponses. This criterion explains both how spacing shifts the optimal phase difference and how beat geometry can weaken or reverse the antiplectic preference. It also suggests a design principle for artificial ciliary pumps: the phase difference, spacing and beat geometry should be chosen to generate the negative shift that enhances the cycle-averaged flow rate. In biological or self-sustained ciliary systems, the same shift is accessible from kinematic imaging and can therefore provide a link between metachronal-wave coordination and pumping function when direct flow-rate mea-surements are difficult. Thus, the present study identifies the negative shift as a mechanistic and observable quantity linking metachronal phasing to flow generation in wall-bounded Stokes flow. 18

Appendix D: Geometry-dependent phase selection under an imposed shear flow 

The tilted-slider results in figure 6 show that the flow-maximising phase difference depends not only on the inter-cilium spacing but also on the effective beat geometry. As a complementary check in the cilium model, we deliberately 22 modify the realised single-cilium beat by imposing a simple shear flow. In dimensional variables, the imposed back-ground flow is u∞ = ˙ γz ex. Using the cilium length scale L and the velocity scale τmax /(μL ), we write the imposed flow as u∞∗ = ˙ γ∗z∗ex, with ˙ γ∗ = μL 2 ˙γ/τ max . The imposed shear flow is introduced solely to perturb the realised beat geometry in a controlled manner, rather than to model ciliary pumping in an externally sheared environment. For each value of ˙ γ∗, an isolated cilium was first optimised by reinforcement learning under the imposed shear flow. The resulting distal-bead trajectories are shown in figure 9(b). As ˙ γ∗ is decreased from 0 .02 to negative values, the distal-bead path is lifted away from the wall and becomes closer to a wall-parallel stroke, whereas the trajectory at ˙γ∗ = 0 .02 is more strongly tilted. The corresponding torque sequence was then used in the pairwise phase-sweep calculation described in §III C, with the same value of ˙γ∗ retained during the pair calculation, as illustrated in figure 9(a). Figure 9(c) shows that changing ˙ γ∗, and hence the realised beat geometry, alters the spacing dependence of the flow-maximising phase difference ∆ /π . For trajectories closer to a wall-parallel stroke, ∆ is more negative at short spacing and shifts towards less negative values as ℓ∗ increases. For more strongly tilted trajectories, this trend is reversed: ∆ is closer to zero at short spacing and shifts towards more negative values with increasing ℓ∗. At large 

ℓ∗, the curves approach a similar negative phase difference. The same reversal appears in the numerical phase sweeps of the tilted-slider model in figure 9(d), where the inclination angle ϕ is varied. Although ˙ γ∗ in the cilium model and ϕ in the tilted-slider model are not directly comparable, both parameters modify the effective beat geometry. This agreement in the direction of the spacing-dependent shift provides a complementary consistency check that the geometry-dependent phase selection found in the tilted-slider model is also present in the cilium model.
