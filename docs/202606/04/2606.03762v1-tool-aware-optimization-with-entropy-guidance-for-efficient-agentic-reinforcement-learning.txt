# Tool-Aware Optimization with Entropy Guidance for Efficient Agentic Reinforcement Learning 

Hongye Cao , Nuo Yan , Haoyuan Deng , Ziwei Wang , Tianpei Yang , Jing Huo ,Yuyao Zhang, and Yang Gao , Senior Member, IEEE, 

Abstract —Agentic reinforcement learning (RL) equips large language models (LLMs) with tool-use capabilities that substan-tially improve reasoning on complex tasks. However, integrating external tools often destabilizes training: over-reliance on tools can induce input distribution shift, while overly conservative tool use limits effective exploration. To address this issue, we propose a unified framework TAO-RL that couples tool-aware trajectory filtering with entropy-guided exploration for efficient policy optimization. Specifically, at the data level, TAO-RL filters rollout trajectories along two criteria: discarding those where all tool invocations fail to execute, and removing those where all rollouts are either correct or incorrect, as both cases yield degenerate advantage estimates that contribute no discriminative learning signal. This joint filtering retains data that are both tool-capable and informative, establishing a high-quality training distribution. At the algorithmic level, we introduce a tool-aware entropy-guided bonus that reshapes the advantage function at post-tool-call tokens, encouraging the policy to explore more diverse reasoning paths at critical decision points. These two com-ponents are mutually reinforcing: trajectory filtering establishes a clean and informative training foundation, while entropy-guided exploration drives stronger reasoning behaviors at critical tool-interaction junctures. Extensive experiments on 7 challenging reasoning benchmarks across 3 model scales demonstrate the superiority of TAO-RL over existing methods. 

Index Terms —Agentic reinforcement learning, tool-aware, tra-jectory filtering, reasoning, entropy 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# AGENTIC reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful post-training paradigm for improving the reasoning ability of large language models (LLMs) [1]–[7]. By enabling models to interact with external tools, such as code interpreters and search engines, LLMs have shown clear gains on complex multi-step reasoning tasks [8]–[13]. However, these gains come with a central challenge: once tools are integrated into the training loop, policy optimization often becomes substantially less stable [14]–[16]. This instability stems from how tool interaction reshapes model inputs. A. Agentic RL 

Agentic RL extends LLMs from passive text generation to active environmental interaction, requiring multi-turn decision-making and precise tool-use strategies [14], [20]–[24]. Recent advancements have transitioned from heuristic-based prompt engineering to end-to-end policy optimization via environmen-tal feedback. On the architectural side, frameworks such as VERLTOOL [3] and ARTIST [2] focus on the co-design of distributed RL systems and agentic reasoning traces. Similarly, TORL [4] and ReTool [11] investigate the scalability of integrating code execution into the training loop to improve sample efficiency. On the algorithmic side, the focus shifts to stabilize the training of tool-integrated reasoning. For instance, SimpleTIR [17] mitigates gradient instability by filtering void turns, while GRPO-RoC [19] addresses environment noise through a resample-on-correct strategy. However, existing methods do not comprehensively account for the effects introduced by tool interaction. Data curation approaches filter trajectories based on format or output qual-ity, but ignore whether tool executions actually succeeded or produced valid environmental feedback that is critical in agentic settings where failed tool calls inject pure noise into the training distribution. Token-level sampling identifies high-value decision points based on model-internal signals alone, without considering how successful tool outputs reshape the informational value of subsequent reasoning steps. Our work addresses both gaps within a unified framework: at the data level, we apply tool-aware trajectory filtering that jointly considers tool execution validity and answer discriminabil-ity; at the algorithmic level, we introduce an entropy-guided advantage reshaping mechanism that concentrates exploration incentives specifically at post-tool-call tokens. 

B. Entropy-Based Exploration in RL 

Entropy-based exploration prevents entropy collapse, the rapid degradation of generation diversity frequently observed in DAPO [25], standard PPO [26] and GRPO [27]. To counter this, methods like integrating entropy into advantage functions encourage exploratory reasoning [28]–[32] and selectively penalize high-covariance tokens to halt entropy decline [33], and others restrict policy updates to pivotal high-entropy minority tokens based on the ”80/20 rule” [34], or dynamically scale learning signals via entropy-based advantage reweight-ing [35]. In agentic RL, ARPO [10] leverages characteristic entropy spikes following tool invocations to balance rollout sampling, while AEPO [8] extends this by penalizing consec-utive high-entropy steps to prevent over-branching. Despite these advances, existing entropy-based methods apply explo-ration incentives without accounting for the structural role of tool interaction. TAO-RL addresses both gaps by coupling tool-aware trajectory filtering with entropy-guided advantage reshaping explicitly localized to high-entropy post-tool-call tokens, achieving precise and stable exploration at the most consequential agentic decision points. IV. A PPROACH 

As illustrated in Fig. 2, TAO-RL consists of two core components: tool-aware trajectory filtering and an entropy-guided exploration mechanism. First, we generate model roll-outs across the training dataset and apply two complementary filtering criteria to curate a high-quality training distribu-tion. Following this, we execute token-level optimization by identifying high-entropy tokens following tool executions and applying an entropy-guided bonus to encourage exploration at these critical decision points. 

A. Tool-Aware Trajectory Filtering Not all rollout trajectories provide valid or informative learning signals for training . Trajectories where tool inter-actions consistently fail inject only noise into training, while JOURNAL OF L ATEX CLASS FILES, VOL. 14, NO. 8, AUGUST 2021 4

those where the model either answers correctly or incorrectly on every rollout yield degenerate advantage estimates that contribute no discriminative gradient information. TAO-RL 

filters rollout trajectories along two complementary criteria, retaining only those that are both tool-capable and informative contrastive signals. For each query x, we sample G rollout trajectories 

{oi}Gi=1 ∼ πθold (· | x) with corresponding binary rewards 

{ri}Gi=1 ∈ { 0, 1}G. We then apply the following two criteria. 

Criterion 1: Tool Execution Validity. In agentic RL, the value of a trajectory depends not only on whether the final answer is correct, but also on whether the intermediate tool interactions provide meaningful environmental feedback. A trajectory that produces an incorrect answer while all of its tool invocations fail with execution errors is doubly uninformative: it neither guides the policy toward correct reasoning nor provides valid grounding from external tool outputs. The entire reasoning chain in such a trajectory is constructed without reliable environmental signal, making it indistinguishable from pure hallucination and harmful to stable policy learning. In-cluding such trajectories in training risks reinforcing erroneous reasoning patterns that happen to invoke tools in syntactically valid but semantically meaningless ways. We therefore discard trajectory oi if: 

ri = 0 ∧ S tool (oi) = 0 ,

where Stool (oi) denotes the number of successful tool in-vocations in trajectory oi. This criterion specifically targets the intersection of answer incorrectness and tool failure, pre-serving incorrect trajectories that nonetheless contain at least one successful tool execution, as these still carry informative signals about how tool feedback shapes reasoning.

Empirical Analysis. To investigate the impact of different data processing strategies on training performance, we compare TAO-RL against two baselines. The first is SimpleTIR, which only filters trajectories based on format validity. The second is a curriculum-based variant, where instead of discarding degenerate queries via pass rate filtering, we organize all training data in a progressive easy-to-hard order based on task difficulty, allowing us to directly assess whether difficulty-aware data organization is a viable alternative to our answer discriminability filtering. As shown in Fig. 3, TAO-RL achieves the best average accuracy on AIME25, outperforming both baselines. In terms of training stability, TAO-RL exhibits the smallest gradient norm fluctuations during training, while both baselines suffer from larger oscillations. These results demonstrate that neither format-based filtering alone nor progressive difficulty ordering can adequately handle the degenerate training signals introduced by tool interaction, validating the effectiveness of our joint tool execution validity and answer discriminability filtering strategy. B. Entropy-Guided Exploration 

While tool-aware trajectory filtering establishes a clean and informative training foundation, it does not explicitly encourage exploration within the retained trajectories. Existing methods address exploration through uniform entropy regular-ization across all token positions, which is ill-suited to agentic settings: it introduces unnecessary noise at inconsequential po-sitions while failing to concentrate sufficient incentives at the critical junctures where tool execution results are interpreted. Our key insight is that exploration should be concentrated at tokens following successful tool executions, where predictive uncertainty is highest and the downstream consequence of the policy’s choice is critical. Experimental Setup 1) Benchmarks and Implementation Details: To compre-hensively validate the effectiveness of TAO-RL across dif-ferent model scales and data regimes, we conduct training under two settings: we train Qwen2.5-1.5B on the Math3-5 dataset from SimpleRL [37], and scale up to Qwen3-4B-Base and Qwen2.5-7B trained on the larger DAPO-MATH-17K dataset [25], ensuring thorough evaluation across both model capacity and training data diversity. The rollout size M is 16 ,temperature factor is 1.0, and learning rate η is 1e−6. For fair comparisons, we reproduce all baselines and conduct training under the same hyperparameters in the VeRL platform [38]. We select GRPO as our basic post-training algorithm. We conduct extensive validation on seven benchmarks, including AIME 2025&2024 [39], AMC 2023 [40], MATH500 [41], OlympiadBench [42], Hmmt Feb-25 [43], and Minerva [44]. We compare against TIR, SimpleTIR [17], and AEPO [8] as our baselines. More details of the experimental setup and benchmarks are provided in Appendix C. 

2) Metrics: We assess the reasoning ability boundaries using the Pass@K : represents at least one of K sampled model outputs passes verification, and assess the average performance using the Avg@K : denotes the average accuracy over K eval-uations, Len@K : average response length over K evaluations per benchmark. We vary K across experiments to ensure the statistical reliability of our evaluation results. For agentic behavior, we introduce two complementary metrics: Tool Call ,which quantifies the average number of tool invocations per response, capturing the degree to which the model leverages external computation; and Valid Code , which measures the proportion of tool calls that produce syntactically correct and executable code, reflecting the quality of the model’s code generation and tool-use proficiency. Box Rate measures the proportion of responses that correctly follow the required answer format. Code Line measures the number of lines of code generated per rollout.

In this work, we present TAO-RL , a unified framework for efficient agentic reinforcement learning that addresses the fundamental tension between training stability and effective exploration introduced by tool integration. TAO-RL operates jointly at two levels: at the data level, tool-aware trajectory filtering removes degenerate rollouts by jointly considering tool execution validity and answer discriminability; at the algorithmic level, entropy-guided advantage reshaping con-centrates exploration incentives at high entropy post-tool-call tokens. Extensive experiments across challenging reasoning benchmarks and three model scales consistently demonstrate that TAO-RL outperforms existing agentic RL methods in both reasoning performance and training stability, while also cultivating deeper and more structured tool-use behaviors as evidenced by substantial gains in response length, code lines, and tool invocation quality. 

Limitation and Future Work: TAO-RL has several limita-tions worth noting. The hyperparameters governing the entropy bonus and the gating proportion require tuning. Also, TAO-RL 

considers only code interpreter as the external tool, which represents a relatively constrained form of tool interaction. Real-world agentic scenarios involve more diverse and compo-sitional tool ecosystems, such as search engines, databases, and multi-step calls. APPENDIX 

A. Broader Impact 

TAO-RL addresses a fundamental challenge in agentic reinforcement learning: achieving stable and effective policy optimization in the presence of tool interaction. By coupling tool-aware trajectory filtering with entropy-guided exploration, 

TAO-RL provides a principled framework for improving reasoning capabilities while maintaining training stability. This has promising implications for developing more capable and reliable agentic systems across diverse domains, from mathematical problem-solving to code generation and complex multi-step decision-making tasks. The unified framework that jointly addresses data quality and algorithmic exploration offers a generalizable paradigm extending beyond the specific setting of tool-integrated reasoning. The principle of filtering degenerate training signals before applying targeted exploration incentives is broadly applicable to any RL setting where noisy or uninformative rollouts risk destabilizing policy optimization. Similarly, the idea of concentrating exploration at structurally critical positions, rather than applying uniform incentives across all tokens, provides a general design principle for more efficient policy gradient methods in long-horizon sequential decision-making. From a societal perspective, more stable and efficient agentic RL training reduces the computational resources required to develop capable reasoning models, lowering the barrier to research and deployment of LLM-based agents. More reliable tool-use behavior also improves the trustworthiness of agentic systems in real-world applications, where erratic tool invocations can have unintended downstream consequences. We hope the insights from TAO-RL inspire further research into structured, tool-aware training paradigms for the broader agentic AI community.

Reasoning Performance. On all three benchmarks of AIME24, AIME25, and MATH500, TAO-RL consistently outperforms TIR in both Avg@16 and Pass@16 throughout training. While TIR exhibits erratic fluctuations and fails to maintain a stable improvement trajectory, TAO-RL achieves steady and monotonic performance gains, ultimately reaching substantially higher accuracy on both average and peak metrics. The gap is particularly pronounced on the harder AIME benchmarks, where TIR’s instability leads to severe performance degradation in later training stages. 

Training Dynamics. The training curves reveal a stark contrast in optimization behavior. TIR exhibits large and irregular gradient norm fluctuations throughout training, reflecting the destabilizing effect of unfiltered tool interactions on policy updates. In contrast, TAO-RL maintains consistently lower and more stable gradient norms, confirming that tool-aware trajectory filtering effectively suppresses noisy gradient signals from degenerate rollouts. 

Tool-Use Quality. The response length and code lines curves further highlight the depth of tool engagement cultivated by 

TAO-RL . While TIR produces shorter responses with fewer lines of code, TAO-RL generates substantially longer and more elaborate reasoning trajectories throughout training, reflecting deeper and more structured tool-use behavior. Most notably, the valid code rate of TIR remains markedly lower and more volatile compared to TAO-RL , which maintains a near-ceiling valid code rate throughout training. This confirms that without filtering tool-execution-failed trajectories, TIR consistently produces malformed tool interactions that contaminate the training distribution. Taken together, these results demonstrate that simply incorporating tools into the training loop without principled data filtering and targeted exploration incentives leads to unstable optimization, shallow tool engagement, and degraded reasoning performance, all of which TAO-RL systematically addresses through its two complementary components. 

2) Comparison of Curriculum Learning: To further investigate whether preserving data quantity through structured data organization can serve as a viable alternative to our quality-driven filtering strategy, we compare TAO-RL against a curriculum learning variant that organizes the full training data in a progressive easy-to-hard order based on task difficulty. Unlike our filtering approach, this variant retains all trajectories, including degenerate ones, aiming to stabilize training through difficulty-aware data ordering rather than explicit data removal. As shown in Fig. 11, the curriculum-based approach does yield a moderate stabilization effect compared to vanilla TIR: both gradient norm fluctuations and response length variability are somewhat reduced, confirming that progressive data organization provides a degree of training stability. However, the improvement is limited precisely because degenerate trajectories, those with failed tool executions or uninformative advantage estimates, remain in the training distribution and continue to inject noisy gradient signals throughout training. In contrast, TAO-RL achieves consistently higher Avg@16 on both AIME24 and AIME25, along with substantially lower and more stable gradient norms and greater response lengths throughout training. These results demonstrate that explicitly removing degenerate rollouts is fundamentally more effective than reorganizing them, as the root cause of instability lies in the quality of individual trajectories rather than their ordering. Structured data organization can partially mitigate the symptoms of tool-induced instability, but only principled trajectory filtering can address the underlying noise at its source.
