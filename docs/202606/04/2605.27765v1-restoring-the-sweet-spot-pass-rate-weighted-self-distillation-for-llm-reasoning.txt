ABSTRACT 

Self-Distillation Policy Optimization (SDPO) provides dense token-level credit assignment for rein-forcement learning with large language models by leveraging the model’s own feedback-conditioned predictions as a self-teacher. Unlike GRPO, however, whose group-relative advantage naturally con-centrates learning on a sweet spot of intermediate-difficulty questions, SDPO’s KL-based advantage lacks an implicit notion of difficulty awareness. We analyze this gap through the lens of GRPO’s advantage normalization. Extending the learnability framework to normalized rewards, we show that normalization absorbs the variance term p(1 − p),equalizing leading-order learnability across questions and leaving pp(1 − p) as the sole residual scaling factor in the per-question gradient. This analysis yields a simple prescription: weight each question’s SDPO loss by [ˆ p(1 − ˆp)] 1/2, resulting in SC-SDPO, a scale-consistent variant of SDPO. The proposed weights are obtained as a zero-cost byproduct of on-policy rollouts with batch-adaptive normalization, inducing an implicit curriculum that dynamically tracks the model’s evolving com-petence. Experiments on scientific reasoning and tool-use benchmarks demonstrate that SC-SDPO consistently improves over SDPO, yielding gains of +3.2/+4.3 (mean@16/maj@16) on Qwen3-8B and +1.8/+3.0 on OLMo-3-7B, while preserving stable training dynamics throughout optimization. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly post-trained with reinforcement learning in verifiable domains such as code and mathematics [Guo et al., 2025, Jaech et al., 2024, Team et al., 2025, Olmo et al., 2025]. The dominant approach, reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR), trains models using sparse binary outcome rewards. More recently, on-policy distillation (OPD) has emerged as a complementary paradigm that provides dense, token-level supervision from a teacher model, achieving strong performance on reasoning-intensive tasks [DeepSeek-AI, 2026, Xiao et al., 2026, Yang et al., 2025, Zeng et al., 2026]. Despite its effectiveness, RLVR suffers from a fundamental credit-assignment bottleneck: a single scalar reward per attempt indicates whether the model was wrong, but not where or why [Hübotter et al., 2026]. Self-Distillation Policy Optimization [SDPO; Hübotter et al., 2026] offers an elegant solution to this bottleneck. By con-ditioning the same model on privileged information—such as runtime errors or previously discovered solutions—SDPO constructs a self-teacher that provides dense, logit-level supervision without requiring an external model. This mecha-nism converts tokenized feedback into fine-grained credit assignment and substantially outperforms Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [Guo et al., 2025, Shao et al., 2024]. However, while SDPO resolves GRPO’s credit-assignment bottleneck, it also sacrifices a useful property that GRPO possesses by construction. In GRPO, the group-relative advantage naturally concentrates learning on a sweet spot of intermediate-difficulty questions, where the model sometimes succeeds and sometimes fails. By contrast, SDPO’s KL-based distillation loss does not implicitly encode difficulty awareness: its gradient budget is largely insensitive to pass rate, diluting the learning signal precisely where self-distillation is most informative.

Prior work has formalized the role of difficulty in RLVR. Bae et al. [2026] show, under raw binary rewards, that policy improvement is lower-bounded by the reward variance p(1 − p). However, this analysis does not account for GRPO’s advantage normalization. We extend their framework to the normalized setting and show that normalization absorbs the 

p(1 − p) factor, equalizing leading-order learnability across questions. The residual per-question gradient magnitude instead scales as pp(1 − p) rather than p(1 − p) (section 4). This analysis directly motivates SC-SDPO, which restores the sweet spot to SDPO by scaling each question’s loss contribution by w = [ˆ p(1 − ˆp)] 1/2. The weighting automatically suppresses all-correct and all-incorrect groups while peaking at ˆp = 0 .5. The weights are recomputed from the current policy’s rollouts at every training step using batch-adaptive normalization, forming an implicit curriculum that tracks the model’s evolving competence at zero additional rollout cost. We evaluate SC-SDPO on scientific reasoning and tool-use tasks. Our main findings are: • SC-SDPO improves over SDPO by +3.2/+4.3 (mean@16/maj@16) on Qwen3-8B and +1.8/+3.0 on OLMo-3-7B across five tasks, demonstrating that difficulty-aware weighting and dense credit assignment are comple-mentary. • The α = 1 /2 exponent consistently outperforms α = 1 , supporting the scale-consistent argument. • Batch-adaptive weighting outperforms static single-pass schemes while incurring zero additional rollout cost. 

2 Related Work 

On-policy self-distillation. On-policy distillation trains a student model on its own generations to match a teacher’s distribution [Agarwal et al., 2024, Gu et al., 2024, Lu and Lab, 2025, Song and Zheng, 2026]. When no external teacher is available, self-distillation methods condition the model on privileged in-context information to construct a self-teacher. Representative approaches include SDPO [Hübotter et al., 2026], SDFT [Shenfeld et al., 2026], OPSD [Zhao et al., 2026], OPSDC [Sang et al., 2026], and G-OPD [Yang et al., 2026a]. Several works further improve sample routing within this paradigm: SCOPE [Zheng et al., 2026] assesses signal reliability to adaptively filter noisy teacher corrections and reinforce diverse reasoning paths, HDPO [Ding, 2026] tackles sparse rewards on excessively hard tasks in GRPO by injecting privileged information to construct high-quality distillation targets, SRPO [Li et al., 2026] routes samples based on correctness, applying token-level distillation to errors and GRPO to successes, TRACE [Wang et al., 2026] introduces a token-level routing framework that adaptively splits the training objective between self-distillation and GRPO, RLSD [Yang et al., 2026b] addresses privileged information leakage by decoupling the update direction (determined by environment reward) from the update magnitude (modulated by a per-token teacher-student likelihood ratio), and SD-ZERO [He et al., 2026] takes a two-phase approach, first training the model to revise its own outputs conditioned on binary rewards, then distilling the revision capability back into the generator via on-policy KL minimization. 

Difficulty-aware training. DAPO [Yu et al., 2026] discards homogeneous rollout groups in RLVR, and Bae et al. [2026] provide a theoretical foundation under raw binary rewards by showing that policy improvement is lower-bounded by the reward variance p(1 − p). GRPO-LEAD [Zhang and Zuo, 2025] and MathForge [Dai et al., 2026] reweight advantages by the difficulty within the RLVR framework. In the on-policy distillation setting, PACED [Xu et al., 2026] derives a Beta-kernel weight w(p) = pα(1 − p)β from the signal-to-noise ratio structure of distillation gradients, and demonstrates its effectiveness in both teacher-forced distillation and on-policy self-distillation. Our work differs from PACED in two key respects: we adopt α = β = 1 /2 rather than α = β = 1 , justified by section 4; and we compute weights on-policy at every step as a free byproduct of the existing rollout loop, whereas PACED estimates pass rates in a separate offline rollout pass.

6 Experiment 

6.1 Experimental Setting Models and training. Following Hübotter et al. [2026], we conduct experiments on Qwen3-8B [Yang et al., 2025] and OLmo3-7B-Instruct [Olmo et al., 2025], both in non-thinking mode. Qwen3-8B serves as our primary model; OLmo3-7B-Instruct is used to verify generalization across model families. For OLMo-3-7B, we report only the core comparison between SDPO and SC-SDPO ( α = 0 .5) due to computational constraints; the full ablation suite (PACED, Hard Filter, SRPO, α = 1 ) is conducted exclusively on Qwen3-8B. All runs are trained for 200 steps on 2 NVIDIA H200 GPUs using the verl framework [Sheng et al., 2024]. We report results in terms of training steps rather than wall-clock time (as in Hübotter et al., 2026, Li et al., 2026), since step-based evaluation is independent of hardware configuration and ensures reproducibility across different compute environments. 

Data. We evaluate on two task families for which neither model has been explicitly fine-tuned: (1) Science Q&A 

(Chemistry, Physics, Biology, Materials Science), using the undergraduate-level reasoning subsets (L3) from SciKnow-Eval [Feng et al., 2024]; and (2) Tool Use , mapping tool-API specifications and user requests to correct tool calls, using ToolAlpaca [Tang et al., 2023]. We adopt the same train-test splits as Hübotter et al. [2026]. Prompt templates are provided in section A.7. 

Methods. We compare 8 configurations: (1) GRPO : the standard group-relative policy optimization baseline, using the same optimization setup as Hübotter et al. [2026]; (2) GRPO w/o Norm : GRPO without the standard-deviation normalization in the advantage, using raw centered rewards ri − ¯r directly; (3) SDPO : the unweighted baseline; (4) 

SDPO w/ PACED : pass-rate weights wj = ˆ pj (1 − ˆpj ) estimated in a single offline rollout pass before training and frozen throughout, following Xu et al. [2026]; (5) SDPO w/ Hard Filter : a binary filtering strategy that retains only questions with 0.2 ≤ ˆpj ≤ 0.8 and discards the rest, recomputed on-policy at each step; (6) SRPO [Li et al., 2026]: uses the sample-level routing and dynamic-weighted SDPO mechanisms proposed in the original paper with default hyperparameters; all other settings (learning rate, batch size, number of rollouts, etc.) are kept consistent with SDPO and the other methods in this work; (7) SC-SDPO ( α=1 ): batch-adaptive variance-based weighting wj = ˆ pj (1 − ˆpj ) with on-policy recomputation and normalization at every step; (8) SC-SDPO ( α=0 .5): our proposed method, batch-adaptive scale-consistent weighting wj = [ˆ pj (1 − ˆpj )] 1/2 with on-policy recomputation and normalization at every step. All other hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, number of rollouts, EMA rate) are held constant across configurations to isolate the effect of the weighting scheme. Details of implementation and hyperparameter are given in section A.2.

SC-SDPO achieves the best overall performance. On Qwen3-8B, SC-SDPO ( α=0 .5) achieves the highest average accuracy on both mean@16 (74.8%) and maj@16 (76.9%), outperforming the SDPO baseline by +3.2 and +4.3 points, respectively. The improvement is consistent across tasks, with particularly large gains on Physics (+5.8/+6.4) and Biology (+5.0/+6.6), where the pass rate distribution offers the broadest intermediate zone for difficulty-aware weighting to exploit (Figure 5a). 

Dense credit assignment is essential. Both GRPO variants substantially underperform all SDPO-based methods, confirming that SDPO’s dense, token-level credit assignment provides a fundamental advantage over GRPO’s uniform per-token signal. Notably, removing the advantage normalization (GRPO w/o Norm) further degrades performance, consistent with our theoretical analysis in section 4: normalization equalizes learnability across questions and is a key component of GRPO’s implicit difficulty awareness. These results underscore that SC-SDPO’s gains come from combining both ingredients—dense credit and difficulty modulation—rather than from either alone. 

Scale-consistent weighting outperforms variance-based weighting. Comparing the two exponents, α=0 .5 consis-tently outperforms α=1 across all tasks, with an average gap of +3.5 on mean@16 and +5.2 on maj@16. This validates our theoretical argument (section 4): under GRPO’s normalized rewards, the leading-order learnability is equalized across questions, and the residual per-question gradient magnitude scales as pp(1 − p); α=1 over-suppresses this signal by an additional factor of pp(1 − p) ≤ 0.5.(Figure 3, Right). 

Static weighting and hard filtering underperform. PACED, which applies variance-based weights ( α=1 ) frozen at initialization, is the weakest method—falling 3.4/3.1 points below the unweighted SDPO baseline on average. This degradation is attributable to both the squared-scale suppression of α=1 and the staleness of frozen weights, which become increasingly misaligned with the model’s evolving competence (section A.6). Hard filtering (retaining 

0.2 ≤ ˆp ≤ 0.8) performs closer to SDPO but still trails SC-SDPO by 4.1/5.0 points on average, confirming that soft, continuous weighting extracts more signal than binary thresholding. 

Comparison with SRPO. SRPO achieves competitive results, ranking second on maj@16 (74.5%) and obtaining the best scores on Tool Use (71.8/73.3) and Materials maj@16 (80.3). However, SRPO’s sample-level routing mechanism introduces substantially higher gradient variance (Figure 3, Right) and training instability compared to SC-SDPO, which achieves a higher average with smoother optimization dynamics. 6.3 Training Dynamics Analysis 

Figure 3 presents three complementary views of the training dynamics. Per-dataset breakdowns and extended analysis are provided in section A.6. 

Pass rate progression (Left). All methods exhibit rising mean pass rates as training progresses, reflecting the model’s growing competence. SC-SDPO ( α=0 .5) achieves the highest mean pass rate in the later stages of training, indicating that concentrating gradient budget on the sweet spot accelerates overall skill acquisition. ToolUse is a notable exception where pass rates remain low across all methods due to the polarized difficulty distribution (cf. Figure 5a in the Appendix). 

8A PREPRINT - M AY 28, 2026 

Sweet spot retention (Middle). SRPO maintains the highest fraction of intermediate-pass-rate samples throughout training, which is expected given its routing design that preserves mixed-outcome groups. However, as shown in Table 1, this does not translate into the best average accuracy, suggesting that a large sweet spot is necessary but not sufficient—optimization stability and signal quality matter equally. Among non-routing methods, SC-SDPO ( α=0 .5)retains the highest intermediate fraction in early training (steps 1–50), when the learning frontier is broadest and difficulty-aware weighting has the greatest impact. As more questions are mastered, the fraction naturally declines across all methods; SC-SDPO converges smoothly, consistent with the implicit curriculum effect of our batch-adaptive design. 

Gradient stability (Right). SC-SDPO ( α=0 .5) maintains a gradient norm profile closely aligned with the unweighted SDPO baseline, confirming that scale-consistent weighting reshapes the gradient distribution across questions without disrupting the overall optimization scale. This stability follows directly from the batch-adaptive normalization (Eq. 16), which preserves unit-mean weights. In contrast, PACED produces large gradient spikes in early training due to two compounding factors: its global normalization allows batch-level weight sums to fluctuate, and its frozen weights become misaligned with the model’s evolving competence. SRPO exhibits persistently unstable gradients, suggesting that its sample-level routing mechanism introduces inconsistent gradient directions across steps. The variance-based weighting ( α=1 ) drives the gradient norm well below the baseline, starving the optimizer of learning signal, while the hard filter yields similarly suppressed gradients due to its reduced effective batch size. 

7 Conclusion 

We identified a fundamental gap between SDPO and GRPO: SDPO provides dense token-level credit assignment but lacks question-level difficulty awareness, while GRPO possesses the opposite strengths. By extending the learnability analysis of Bae et al. [2026] to GRPO’s normalized rewards, we showed that advantage normalization absorbs the 

p(1 − p) factor from the raw-reward bound, leaving pp(1 − p) as the natural per-question gradient scaling. This analysis directly prescribed SC-SDPO, which restores difficulty awareness to SDPO through a single scalar weight per question. Experiments on scientific reasoning and tool-use tasks confirmed that SC-SDPO consistently outperforms SDPO and all baselines, with α = 1 /2 validating the scale-consistent scaling predicted by our theory. 

8 Limitations 

Dependence on pass-rate granularity. With G = 8 rollouts per question, the empirical pass rate ˆp takes only nine discrete values {0, 18 , . . . , 1}. The Beta-type weight [ˆ p(1 − ˆp)] 1/2 smooths over this discretization, but fine-grained difficulty distinctions may be lost. Increasing G improves resolution at the cost of additional generation compute. 

Binary reward assumption. Our theoretical justification for α = 1 /2 relies on the Bernoulli structure of binary rewards. In domains with continuous or partial-credit rewards, the relationship between reward variance and gradient signal strength may differ, and the optimal exponent could change accordingly. 

Task scope. We evaluate on scientific reasoning and tool-use tasks with verifiable answers. Extending pass-rate weighting to open-ended generation tasks (e.g., creative writing, summarization) would require a proxy competence signal such as reward-model scores, introducing additional noise and calibration challenges. 

Model scale. Our experiments are conducted on 7–8B parameter models. While the method is architecture-agnostic, its effectiveness on substantially larger or smaller models remains to be validated. Following the scaling findings of SDPO [Hübotter et al., 2026], we expect the benefit to grow with model scale, as stronger in-context learners produce more reliable self-teacher signals, but this hypothesis requires empirical confirmation. 

Cross-model ablation coverage. Our full ablation suite is conducted only on Qwen3-8B. OLMo-3-7B is limited to the core SDPO vs. SC-SDPO comparison, which suffices to confirm cross-family generalization of the proposed weighting but leaves open whether the relative ranking among baselines transfers across architectures. Extending the complete ablation to additional model families is a natural direction for future work.

Tool Use Prompt Template 

Your task is to answer the user’s question using available tools. You have access to the following tools: Name: {tool_name} Description: {tool_description} Documentation: {method_name}: {method_description} Parameters: {parameters_json} Output: {output_description} [. . . additional tools . . . ] Use the following format: Thought: you should always think about what to do Action: the action to take, should be one of the tool names. Action Input: the input to the action, must be in JSON format. All of the action input must be realistic and from the user. Begin! Question: {user_question} 

The expected response follows the Thought / Action / Action Input format. The verifier checks two conditions jointly: (1) the predicted action names match the ground-truth action names (multiset equality), and (2) the merged JSON parameters across all Action Input blocks exactly match the ground-truth parameters. Both conditions must hold for a reward of 1 (correct); otherwise the reward is 0 (incorrect).
