## Abstract 

Rewriting source text with large language mod-els (LLMs) before translation has been shown to improve machine translation (MT) quality. However, we find that prompt-based rewrit-ing can degrade translation quality rather than improve it, particularly when smaller LLMs, such as 4B-parameter models, are used. We argue that this limitation stems from the diffi-culty of controlling rewriting behavior through natural-language prompts alone: a rewrite is useful only if it improves downstream transla-tion, yet existing prompt-based methods do not explicitly optimize for this signal. To address this issue, we propose RLSR (Reinforcement 

Learning for Source Rewriting), a reinforce-ment learning framework that trains the rewrit-ing model with a reward based on the down-stream translation-quality improvement pro-duced by each rewrite. Experiments across six MT systems and 16 language pairs show that our 4B RLSR-trained rewriting models signifi-cantly outperform both the no-rewriting base-line and prompt-based rewriting baselines at the same model scale, while remaining compet-itive with baselines that use a 235B LLM. 

## 1 Introduction 

Source rewriting, sometimes referred to as pre-editing, modifies source text before it is passed to a machine translation (MT) model in order to improve translation quality. When the internal workings of an MT model are inaccessible, source rewriting provides a practical way to improve trans-lation quality (Pym, 1988; Reuther, 2003; Sere-tan et al., 2014; Miyata and Fujita, 2021; Ki and Carpuat, 2025). Recent work has shown that large language models (LLMs) can be prompted to pro-duce effective source rewrites (Koretaka et al., 2025; Ki and Carpuat, 2025). However, we find that prompt-based rewrit-ing is unreliable: rather than improving transla-tion quality, it often degrades it, especially for Original

A prompt can express only an in-tended behavior, such as “simplify the text” or “make it easier to translate,” whereas the practical utility of a rewrite depends on whether it leads the downstream MT model to produce a better trans-lation. Prompt-based methods neither observe this signal nor optimize for it; they instead rely on the assumption that following the instruction will im-prove the translation. This raises a central question: 

how can we obtain a rewriting model that reliably improves downstream translation quality, rather than relying on prompts that only indirectly steer the rewrite? 

To address this question, we optimize the rewrit-ing model directly for the objective of interest, rather than using prompts as an indirect steering mechanism. We call this approach reinforcement learning (RL) for Source Rewriting ( RLSR ; see Figure 1). Because source rewriting aims to im-1

We evaluate RLSR against a range of baselines on six MT models across all 16 language pairs in the WMT2025 General MT Shared Task (Kocmi et al., 2025). The results show that our 4B RLSR-trained rewriting models significantly outperform both the no-rewriting baseline and prompt-based rewriting baselines at the same model scale, while remaining competitive with baselines that use a 235B LLM. Further analysis shows that, compared with rewriting models trained via supervised fine-tuning (SFT), RL-trained models generate more diverse rewrites and are less prone to degenerating into simple copies of the original source text. 

## 2 Related Work 

Source rewriting is a well-studied area of MT re-search, with various strategies evaluated since the statistical MT era (Shirai et al., 1993; Mitamura and Nyberg; Yoshimi and Sata, 1999). These strate-gies include word reordering (Xia and McCord, 2004; Li et al., 2007; Hoshino et al., 2015), para-phrasing (Callison-Burch et al., 2006; Mirkin et al., 2009; Marton et al., 2009; Ki and Carpuat, 2025), simplification (Štajner and Popovic, 2016; Štajner and Popovi´ c, 2019; Mehta et al., 2020; Ki and Carpuat, 2025), and conversion to translationese (Mehta et al., 2020). However, some studies have questioned the practical effectiveness of certain rewriting strategies (Zhu, 2015; Du and Way, 2017). Other work has analyzed which aspects of MT out-puts are affected by source rewriting (Miyata and Fujita, 2021; Yamaguchi et al., 2024), as well as the conditions under which rewriting is appropriate and effective (Miyata and Fujita, 2017). Given the strong text-generation capabilities of LLMs (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023), recent work has explored their potential for source rewriting. Ki and Carpuat (2025) investigated the effectiveness of three prompt strategies. Similarly, Koretaka et al. (2025) proposed an approach that uses feedback from a segment-level, reference-free evaluation model at inference time, enabling the LLM to iteratively rewrite the source text. However, these methods share a key limitation: they control the rewriting model only indirectly ,through hand-crafted prompts (and, for Koretaka et al. (2025), prompt-guided iterative refinement), without updating the model’s parameters to opti-mize downstream translation quality. Their effec-tiveness therefore hinges on how well an off-the-shelf LLM follows the prompt, a fragile depen-dence that, as we show, breaks down for smaller models. The inference-time approach of Koretaka et al. (2025) also incurs a high inference cost, since both the rewriting and MT models must be run repeatedly. In contrast, RLSR optimizes the rewrit-ing model’s parameters directly for downstream translation quality and remains efficient at infer-ence time, requiring only a single pass through the rewriting model and the downstream MT model. 

## 3 Methodology 

3.1 Preliminaries: Source Rewriting 

Source rewriting aims to improve the translation quality of a given MT model by generating an alter-native, meaning-preserving source text that is more likely to yield a high-quality translation, while leav-ing the MT model itself unchanged. Formally, let 

s denote a source text and r denote its reference translation. Let M be a fixed MT model that maps source texts to translations, and let Rθ be a source rewriting model parameterized by θ. Given s, the rewriting model generates a rewritten source text 

˜s = Rθ(s). The MT model then produces transla-tions for both the original source, M (s), and the rewritten source, M (˜ s).Let Q(s, t, r ) be an automatic evaluation func-tion that scores a model translation t of source s

against the reference translation r, with higher val-ues indicating better translation quality. To quantify the utility of a rewrite, we measure the marginal improvement it yields in the final translation. We define this improvement as the reward R:

R(s, ˜s, r ) = Q(s, M (˜ s), r )−Q(s, M (s), r ). (1) The goal of source rewriting is to learn a rewriting model Rθ that tends to obtain positive reward ( R >

0). When the rewriting model is an LLM with-out task-specific rewriting training, its behavior is controlled only by natural-language prompts intended to elicit high-reward rewrites (Ki and Carpuat, 2025; Koretaka et al., 2025). This con-trol is purely indirect: the prompt expresses an instruction, but the reward R is never observed dur-ing generation, so the model receives no feedback 2on whether a given rewrite actually helps. Conse-quently, prompt-based rewriting is unreliable and, as shown in Section 4.2, can even yield negative re-ward ( R < 0), especially for smaller LLMs, whose limited instruction-following ability makes them more prone to producing harmful rewrites. 

3.2 Proposed Method: RLSR 

To overcome this indirect control, we optimize the rewriting model directly to maximize the reward 

R(s, ˜s, r ) defined in Eq. 1. However, standard SFT cannot directly optimize this objective, because the target MT model M , the evaluation metric Q,and the discrete generation process for ˜s are non-differentiable. Since gradients cannot be backprop-agated through the MT model or the evaluation metric, a natural approach is to formulate this opti-mization as an RL problem. We therefore propose 

RLSR , which uses improvements in downstream translation quality directly as rewards. RLSR starts from an initial rewriting model, which we denote as the reference policy Rref (e.g., an instruction-following LLM given a rewriting prompt). During training, given a source text s

sampled from a dataset D = {(s, r )}, the current rewriting model Rθ acts as the policy and samples a rewrite ˜s ∼ Rθ(·| s). The fixed target MT model 

M then translates both s and ˜s. Finally, both trans-lations are evaluated with the metric Q to compute the reward R(s, ˜s, r ).To discourage reward hacking (e.g., generating nonsensical text that exploits metric vulnerabili-ties), we anchor the model to the reference policy 

Rref using a Kullback–Leibler (KL) penalty. The adjusted total reward Rtotal is defined as:

## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup Training Datasets We constructed the RLSR training data from the WMT2019–2024 News/Gen-eral MT Shared Task test sets (Barrault et al., 2019, 2020; Akhbardeh et al., 2021; Kocmi et al., 2022, 2023, 2024). The resulting dataset comprises 21K samples across 24 language pairs. We randomly selected 5% of the data for validation and used the remainder for training. 

Models Our target MT models (M ) come from two categories of LLMs. The first category consists of general-purpose LLMs: Gemma3 27B ( gemma-3-27b-it ) (Team et al., 2025), Gemma4 31B (gemma-4-31B-it ), 1

Qwen3 30B (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 )(Yang et al., 2025), and Qwen3 4B (Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 ) (Yang et al., 2025). The second category consists of translation-optimized models: Translategemma 27B (translategemma-27b-it ) (Finkelstein et al., 2026) and an in-house Qwen3 4B SFT-RL model. 2 For the source rewriting models, we used Qwen3 4B and Qwen3 235B (Yang et al., 2025). We focused on the Qwen3 family because a preliminary study with the Gemma4 family showed that prompt-based rewriting did not yield consistent improvements (Appendix G). Because of computational constraints, we trained RLSR only with Qwen3 4B. The prompts used for the MT and rewriting models are provided in Appendix A. We used greedy decoding for all MT and rewriting models. We used xCOMET-XL 

(Guerreiro et al., 2024) as the MT metric Q during RLSR training because it correlates strongly with human evaluation (Freitag et al., 2023, 2024) while remaining computationally feasible.

Evaluation We conducted our evaluation on the complete test set of the WMT2025 General MT Shared Task (Kocmi et al., 2025), which cov-ers 16 language pairs. For evaluation, we used two learned, reference-based metrics, xCOMET (xCOMET-XXL ) (Guerreiro et al., 2024) and Met-ricX ( MetricX-24-XXL ) (Juraska et al., 2024), as well as GEMBA ( GEMBA-MQM ) (Kocmi and Feder-mann, 2023), a prompt-based, reference-free LLM metric based on Gemini 3.0 Flash (Team et al., 2023). We selected these metrics because they correlate strongly with human evaluation (Freitag et al., 2023, 2024). However, because a learned metric ( xCOMET-XL ) was used explicitly as the re-ward for optimizing the rewriting model during RLSR training, there is a risk of “metric bias” (Ko-vacs et al., 2024): other learned metrics may also favor translations generated from RLSR-rewritten sources. To obtain a more independent check, we also evaluated with GEMBA, which operates on fundamentally different principles from the learned metrics. We performed paired bootstrap testing (Koehn, 2004) to compare RLSR with the prompt-based rewriting baselines that use the same base model (Qwen3 4B), as well as with the no-rewriting baseline. Full implementation details of RLSR are pro-vided in Appendix B.

By contrast, in the RL framework, merely copy-ing the original source yields zero reward, which forces the policy to explore more varied and tar-geted rewriting strategies that improve translation quality. 

Filtering Unchanged Sources To test whether removing these unchanged pairs would force the SFT model to learn meaningful strategies, we trained another model on a filtered dataset (“SFT (filtered)” in Table 2) that excludes all ˜s = s in-stances. However, performance deteriorated further. We attribute this to the equal weighting of all to-kens in the SFT objective. As noted above, reward-improving rewrites alter only a small fraction of the source, so the few tokens that drive the improve-ment are vastly outnumbered by tokens carried over unchanged. The equally weighted SFT loss cannot isolate these crucial tokens within highly similar sequences. RL, by contrast, is trained to differen-tiate trajectories with nearly identical tokens but substantially different rewards, allowing it to focus on the crucial modifications. In summary, RL provides a more effective paradigm for training rewriting models than SFT. As a middle ground, we also explored Direct Pref-erence Optimization (DPO), which, like RL, learns to discriminate between similar rewrites of differ-ing quality. DPO improves over SFT but remains markedly less stable than RLSR (Appendix F). Ad-vanced SFT techniques, such as identifying critical tokens and assigning them larger weights in the training objective (Zhang et al., 2025), might nar-row this gap; we leave this direction to future work. 

5.2 Does a Trained Rewriting Model Generalize to Other MT Models? 

As described in Section 3.2, each rewriting model is trained for a specific MT model M . Training a ded-icated rewriting model for each MT model, how-ever, is relatively costly: in our experiments, a sin-gle RLSR run takes roughly 60 GPU-hours. This raises a natural question: can a rewriting model trained via RLSR be reused across multiple MT models without per-model retraining? 

To investigate this, we conducted two experi-ments using Gemma3 27B and Qwen3 30B as the MT models. We selected these two models because they are built on different LLM families and may therefore exhibit distinct preferences for rewritten sources.

RLSR therefore avoids two failure modes: the “copying” degeneration of SFT (Section 5.1), which leaves 75 –90% of sources un-changed, and the indiscriminate full rewriting of prompt-based methods. 

Case Study 1: RLSR refrains from rewriting an already-adequate source Example 1 in Table 5 contrasts the four methods on a source sentence that is already fluent and readily translatable. Sim-plification and Easy Translate remain faithful to the source but flatten the author’s deliberately loaded diction, replacing “infantilised diaper king” with the blander “childish . . . king” and unpacking the compound “Diet Coke-summoning button” . The Paraphrase baseline drifts even further: it drops the original “diaper king” image and fabricates content that is absent from the source, namely “a whimsical touch that evokes the indulgence of a pampered figure, almost like a grown-up version of a child’s fantasy” . The MT model then translates this unsupported elaboration as if it were genuine. This tendency to rewrite indiscriminately helps ex-plain why, despite using a 235B LLM, Paraphrase falls below the no-rewriting baseline on almost all MT models in Table 1. RLSR, in contrast, leaves the sentence largely intact, editing only the British spelling infantilised → infantilized and preserving the meaning.

## 6 Conclusions and Future Work 

In this work, we introduced RLSR, a framework that trains a source rewriting model using each rewrite’s improvement in downstream translation quality as the reward. This replaces the indirect control of natural-language prompts with direct optimization of the task objective. Across six MT models and all 16 language pairs of the WMT2025 General MT Shared Task, our 4B RLSR models significantly and consistently outperform both the no-rewriting baseline and prompt-based rewriting methods at the same model scale, while remaining competitive with baselines based on a 235B LLM. We identify two promising directions for future work. First, RLSR is considerably more expensive to train than SFT, so developing more efficient train-ing methods could substantially reduce this cost. One concrete avenue, building on our analysis in Section 5.1, is to improve the SFT objective, for ex-ample by up-weighting the few critical tokens that drive reward improvements (Zhang et al., 2025), so that it approaches the effectiveness of RLSR at a fraction of the training cost. Second, the cross-MT adaptability of RLSR-trained models warrants a more systematic study, including jointly training a single rewriting model that serves a larger and more diverse set of MT models. 

## Limitations 

First, our approach assumes that the automatic met-rics we use faithfully reflect improvements in trans-lation quality. However, because the RLSR reward is computed directly from xCOMET, the learned metrics (xCOMET and MetricX) may be biased in favor of RLSR-rewritten sources. We mitigate 8this concern by also evaluating with GEMBA, a prompt-based, reference-free LLM metric that op-erates on fundamentally different principles. The consistent gains under GEMBA suggest that the improvements are not merely metric overfitting. A more definitive solution would be human eval-uation, which we did not conduct because of its substantial cost. Second, our experiments are limited in scope. Although we cover six MT models and 16 language pairs, evaluating RLSR on a broader range of MT models, domains, and language pairs would further strengthen our conclusions. Finally, RLSR is costly to train and to scale across MT models. Training is considerably more expensive than standard SFT, since it requires re-peatedly running the downstream MT model and the evaluation metric to compute rewards. This cost is further compounded by the fact that each rewrit-ing model is optimized for a specific MT model (Section 3.2): adapting RLSR to a new MT model in principle calls for a separate training run, which takes roughly 60 GPU-hours on NVIDIA H100 GPUs in our setup. As a result, RLSR may be impractical when compute budgets are tight. Our analysis in Section 5.2 does suggest that RLSR-trained models exhibit a degree of cross-MT gen-eralization, which could alleviate this per-model cost; however, owing to our limited compute, we examined this property on only two MT models, and a broader validation across more diverse MT models remains an important direction for future work.

## B Implementation Details of RLSR 

Our training codebase was built on ms-swift 

(Zhao et al., 2024). RLSR training was conducted on several servers, each equipped with 4 × NVIDIA H100 GPUs. ## D Implementation Details of Training Rewriting Models via SFT 

The source–reference pairs used for SFT are the same as those used for RLSR (Section 4.1). To construct a supervised target for each source, we prompted Qwen3 4B with the “Easy Translate” prompt (Appendix A) to generate N = 16 distinct rewriting candidates. For each target MT model, we then selected the candidate ˜s∗ that yielded the largest xCOMET-XL improvement over the orig-inal source (i.e., the candidate with the highest reward R defined in Eq. 1); when no candidate improved upon the original source, we used the original source itself as the target ( ˜s∗ = s). We fine-tuned Qwen3 4B on the resulting (s, ˜s∗) pairs for at most three epochs with an effective batch size of 512 (accounting for gradient accumulation and multi-GPU parallelism). We used a learning rate of 2 × 10 −5 with a cosine decay scheduler and a warmup ratio of 0.05, and adopted the 8-bit AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2019; Dettmers et al.). The “SFT (filtered)” variant in Table 2 uses the same configuration but excludes all unchanged pairs ( ˜s∗ = s) from the training set. 

## E Computing Source Similarity 

The “Src. sim.” column of Table 4 measures how much of the source a rewrite preserves. We use the character-level similarity ratio from Python’s

## F Training Rewriting Models via DPO 

Motivation As discussed in Section 5.1, the ad-vantage of RL over SFT for training rewriting mod-els may arise because RL forces the policy to dis-criminate between rewrites that are nearly identical in surface form yet receive markedly different re-wards. The Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) objective (Rafailov et al., 2023) shares this prop-erty: it trains the model to separate a preferred (chosen) output from a rejected one, and such pairs typically differ only subtly. Motivated by this simi-larity, we investigate whether DPO can serve as an effective alternative for training rewriting models.

Summary Overall, DPO provides a more effec-tive training signal than SFT but a substantially less reliable one than RLSR. We attribute this gap to the distinction between off-policy and on-policy learning. RLSR is on-policy: it repeatedly sam-ples rewrites from the current policy and obtains fresh reward feedback, allowing it to explore the rewriting space and correct its errors throughout training. DPO, by contrast, is trained on a fixed set of preference pairs collected once from the ref-erence model. This static dataset may cover the rewriting space only sparsely, leaving the policy unable to discover better rewriting strategies that on-policy exploration can reach. In the worst case (Translategemma 27B), it can even steer the model toward rewrites that actively harm translation qual-ity. 

## G Exploring the Gemma4 Family as the Rewriting Model 

As noted in Section 4.1, the experiments in the main text use the Qwen3 family for the rewrit-ing model when studying the effectiveness of the prompt-based rewriting baselines and RLSR. Here, we also explore the effectiveness of the Gemma4 family, a popular family of open-weight LLMs, as rewriting models. As a first step, we evaluated the prompt-based rewriting baselines. We used two model sizes as rewriting models: Gemma4 4B ( gemma-4-E4B-it 4) and Gemma4 31B ( gemma-4-31B-it ), the largest model in the Gemma4 family. To assess the effectiveness of the Gemma4 family as rewriting models at a lower ex-perimental cost, we restricted these experiments to two downstream MT models, Gemma3 27B and
