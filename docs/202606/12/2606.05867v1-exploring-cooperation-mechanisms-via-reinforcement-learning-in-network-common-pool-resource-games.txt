Abstract 

Sustaining cooperation in resource-constrained populations requires allocation mechanisms that balance individual incentives, resource sustainability, and distributional fairness. This paper proposes a network common-pool resource game in which individuals are embedded in complex networks, participate in multiple overlapping local resource pools, and face endogenous resource constraints during strategy evolution. Within this framework, we first examine two representative allocation mechanisms, equal allocation and proportional allocation. The results show that equal allocation produces fair but ine ffi cient outcomes by weakening contribution incentives, whereas proportional alloca-tion can temporarily promote cooperation but amplifies accumulated advantages and leads to severe inequality. To overcome these limitations, we develop a graph neural network-based reinforcement learning framework in which a learned social planner allocates local pool resources without directly controlling individual strategies. Simulation results under four representative network topologies show that the learned planner sustains higher cooperation levels and average accumulated resources, and reduces inequality compared with the baselines. Furthermore, we interpret the learned policy and distill it into two simpler mechanisms: a resource-dependent mixture mechanism for regular networks and a degree-conditioned mixture mechanism for heterogeneous networks. These mechanisms reveal that eff ective allocation should adapt to both local resource states and structural positions, providing an interpretable route from reinforcement learning policy search to mechanism design in networked resource-sharing systems. 

Keywords: Cooperation evolution, Deep reinforcement learning, Network common-pool resource game, Interpretable allocation mechanism 

1. Introduction 

Cooperation in resource-constrained populations is a fundamental problem in evolutionary game theory, social governance, and socioeconomic systems [1–5]. Many collective systems depend on the repeated allocation, use, and replenishment of shared resources, such as public funds, ecological stocks, organizational resources, platform bene-fits, and public goods. A central di ffi culty in these systems is that resource recipients often face a tension between private incentives and collective sustainability [6–8]. Once resources are allocated, each participant may either recip-rocate by contributing back to the collective system or selfishly retain the benefits without bearing the corresponding obligation. In repeated interactions, such behaviour may be individually advantageous in the short run, but if it be-comes widespread, the common resource base will gradually decline, future allocation capacity will be weakened, and collective welfare will eventually deteriorate [9–11]. This dilemma appears in many practical contexts, includ-ing welfare and taxation systems, environmental governance, collaborative organizations, and digital platforms where shared resources must be continuously maintained by the participants themselves [12, 13]. Hence, the key challenge

for mechanism design is to construct allocation rules that can simultaneously preserve cooperation incentives, sustain resource regeneration, and avoid excessive inequality among participants. A large body of research has studied how cooperation can be promoted in social dilemmas and networked popu-lations. Classical mechanisms include voluntary participation [14–19], reputation and punishment [20–25], prosocial exclusion [26, 27], network reciprocity [28–30] and so on [31, 32]. These mechanisms provide di ff erent ways to align individual incentives with collective welfare. For example, the option of voluntary participation allows individ-uals to mitigate risk by adopting a loner strategy rather than a defection strategy when social pressure is too high, thereby promoting cooperation. Reciprocity and reputation encourage individuals to maintain cooperative behaviour through future rewards or social evaluation, while punishment and exclusion deter free-riding by imposing costs on uncooperative participants. In structured populations, network reciprocity further shows that local interactions and spatial clustering can support the persistence of cooperation. These studies have established important theoretical foundations for understanding how external rules, institutional arrangements, and interaction structures can promote cooperation. With the development of reinforcement learning, especially deep reinforcement learning, researchers have in-creasingly used learning-based agents to model adaptive decision making in repeated games and social dilemmas. In this perspective, individuals are treated as adaptive agents that update their strategies according to their own experi-ences, payo ff s, and observations of the environment [33–40]. Compared with fixed behavioural rules or analytically prescribed update dynamics, reinforcement-learning agents can capture richer forms of bounded rationality and strate-gic adaptation. For example, Kopacz et al. [41] evaluated cooperative–competitive dynamics using deep Q-learning, showing how deep reinforcement learning can be used to study adaptive strategic behaviour in environments involving both cooperation and competition. Lee and Weng et al. [42] introduced a granular Q-learning adaptation mechanism in the multi-agent Prisoner’s Dilemma and showed that a refined learning process can improve collective welfare. Zhu et al. [43] incorporated second-order reputation into the Q-learning update process and found that reputation-based learning can promote the evolution of trust in structured populations. Zou and Huang et al. [44] studied cooperation dynamics on hypergraphs with punishment and Q-learning, illustrating how learning rules can be combined with ex-ternal incentive mechanisms in higher-order interaction structures. More recently, Weng and Lee et al. [45] further proposed a Q-learning-driven adaptive rewiring mechanism for cooperative control in heterogeneous networks, high-lighting the role of learning-based structural adaptation in promoting cooperation. These studies shift the focus from static strategy rules to adaptive individual learning, showing that cooperation can emerge or collapse depending on how agents perceive information, evaluate long-term returns, and update their behavioural strategies. Beyond modelling individual adaptive behaviour, reinforcement learning has been further used as a computational tool to search for cooperation-promoting strategies and to assist the design of cooperation-promoting mechanisms. Recent work has shown that learning-based methods can explore strategy spaces that are di ffi cult to characterize manually. For example, Su et al. [46] proposed a multi-agent reinforcement learning framework for iterated and evo-lutionary games and discovered a memory-two bilateral reciprocity strategy that promotes cooperation and improves social welfare in evolving populations. In parallel, recent behavioural experiments have emphasized that networked cooperation is strongly a ff ected by the degree of social networking agency. Jia et al. [47] showed that allowing in-dividuals to act di ff erently toward di ff erent neighbours enhances cooperation, trust, and fairness in several economic games, suggesting that local network structure and neighbour-specific interactions are central to prosocial outcomes. At the mechanism level, reinforcement learning can also be used to train an external social planner or mechanism designer whose task is to improve collective outcomes such as cooperation, welfare, inclusion, and fairness. Early work on network engineering showed that autonomous agents can increase cooperation in human groups by shaping social connections [48]. More recently, McKee et al. [49] used deep reinforcement learning to train a social planner that recommends network rewiring actions and found that the learned planner promotes cooperation not by simply isolating defectors, but by adopting a more conciliatory strategy that places them in small cooperative neighbour-hoods. In common-pool resource settings, Koster et al. [50] used deep reinforcement learning to design a dynamic resource allocation mechanism for human participants, showing that the learned planner can outperform equal and proportional baselines by conditioning its allocation policy on available resources and by balancing surplus, equality, and inclusion. More generally, these studies belong to the emerging direction of deep mechanism design, in which deep learning and reinforcement learning are used to search for social and economic policies that are di ffi cult to derive analytically [51]. Building on these advances, this study investigates how adaptive resource-allocation mechanisms can promote 2cooperation in complex networked common-pool resource systems. Existing studies have shown that reinforcement learning can help discover cooperation-promoting strategies, engineer social networks, and design dynamic alloca-tion mechanisms. However, resource allocation in structured populations remains less understood when individuals are embedded in complex networks, participate in multiple overlapping local resource pools, and face endogenous resource constraints that a ff ect their future cooperative capacity. This setting is closer to many real collective systems, where resource exchange, contribution incentives, and social connections are locally organized rather than globally uniform. To address this problem, we propose a network common-pool resource game with endogenous resource con-straints and adaptive allocation mechanisms. In the proposed model, each node represents both an individual agent and a local resource pool. Individuals choose whether to cooperate or defect according to evolutionary dynamics, while cooperators contribute resources to the pools associated with themselves and their neighbours. The resources in each pool then grow subject to a cooperation-dependent capacity constraint and are allocated back to the corresponding lo-cal group. Importantly, individuals possess endogenous resource stocks, and those with insu ffi cient resources cannot choose cooperation, which captures the e ff ect of the poverty trap in resource-constrained systems. This formulation explicitly couples strategy evolution, resource accumulation, local interaction structure, and allocation mechanisms. Within this framework, we first examine two representative allocation mechanisms: equal allocation and pro-portional allocation. These two baselines correspond to two basic principles in real-world socioeconomic systems, namely uniform equal redistribution and contribution-based proportional redistribution. The comparison shows that each principle has an inherent limitation. Equal allocation can reduce inequality, but it weakens contribution incen-tives and may lead to cooperation collapse. Proportional allocation can stimulate cooperation in the short run, but it amplifies accumulated advantages and may produce severe inequality. These contrasting outcomes suggest that a sus-tainable allocation mechanism should not be fixed as purely equal or purely proportional. Instead, it should adaptively balance incentive provision, resource preservation, and inequality control. To search for such an adaptive mechanism, we introduce a graph neural network-based reinforcement learning framework in which the learning agent acts as a social planner. The planner does not directly determine whether individuals cooperate or defect. Rather, it observes the evolving network state and determines how the resources of each local pool should be allocated among agents in the corresponding ego network. This design preserves the evolutionary nature of individual strategy updates while allowing the allocation mechanism to adapt to both local resource conditions and network structure. To further un-derstand the learned policy, we approximate it using simpler and more interpretable mixture mechanisms based on three allocation components: equal allocation, proportional allocation, and self-allocation. On regular networks, the learned policy can be approximated by a resource-dependent mixture mechanism, denoted by M1, whose allocation weights change with the ego-network resource state. On heterogeneous networks, however, resource information alone is insu ffi cient because nodes with di ff erent degrees occupy di ff erent structural positions and participate in dif-ferent numbers of local pools. We therefore construct a degree-conditioned mixture mechanism, denoted by M2, in which the allocation weights depend on both local resources and node degree. Overall, this paper shows that e ff ective resource redistribution in networked common-pool systems requires a state-dependent and structure-aware balance between equality, incentives, and self-preservation. Purely equal redis-tribution may protect fairness but can undermine the willingness to contribute, whereas purely contribution-based redistribution may reward e ff ort but can lock structurally disadvantaged individuals into poverty traps and concentrate resources around already advantaged nodes. The extracted mixture mechanisms provide a more nuanced and instruc-tive allocation principle. For low-degree or structurally peripheral nodes, preserving part of the local resource for self-development can help maintain their future cooperative capacity. For middle-degree nodes, contribution-based allocation plays a dominant role in sustaining active participation and reciprocal cooperation. For high-degree or hub-like nodes, proportional reward remains important, but equal redistribution should still be partly retained to prevent excessive resource concentration and to share the benefits generated around central local pools. These findings suggest that sustainable allocation mechanisms in social and economic systems should not rely on a single redistribution prin-ciple. Instead, they should adapt to both individual resource conditions and structural positions, thereby promoting cooperation, maintaining collective resources, and controlling inequality in complex networked populations.

3.2. Reinforcement learning framework 

To learn an adaptive resource allocation mechanism, we introduce a graph neural network-based reinforcement learning (GNN-RL) framework, as illustrated in Fig. 3. Di ff erent from traditional evolutionary game models in which 8the allocation mechanism is manually specified, the proposed framework acts as a social planner, learning an allocation mechanism from repeated interactions with the network common-pool resource environment. The GNN-RL agent does not directly determine whether individuals cooperate or defect. Instead, it acts as a social planner. At each time step, every agent first chooses their strategies according to the evolutionary game process described in Section 2. Cooperators contribute resources to local pools, and the GNN-RL agent then determines how the expanded pool resources should be allocated back to the agents in each local group, as shown in Fig. 1(b). The framework consists of three main modules: an observation graph module, an actor network, and a critic network. At each time step, the current game system state is represented as the environment state and we represent the current game state as a graph tensor representation ( u, V, E). The global feature vector u ∈ R4 contains graph-level summary statistics, including the fraction of cooperators fc, the mean accumulated resource ¯ R(t), the mean resource pool value ¯ G(t), and the Gini coe ffi cient. The node feature matrix V ∈ RN×4 contains, for each node i, its resource pool 

Gi(t), accumulated resource Ri(t), degree di, and the contribution resource ei(t). The edge feature tensor E ∈ RN×N×1

records the connectivity information used by the model. Specifically, each entry indicates whether node j is included in the feasible ego-network of node i.The actor network is responsible for generating allocation actions. It first encodes the global structural and state information of the network using a two-layer GraphNet backbone. Then, for each focal node i, which defines a local resource pool over its ego-network, a shared ego-network allocation head is applied to the subgraph consisting of node i and its neighbours. Based on the learned local representations, the actor produces allocation scores for all feasible participants in the pool, and a softmax operation normalizes these scores into allocation weights. Repeating this process for all focal nodes yields the final allocation action over all local pools. This design matches the local interaction structure of the common-pool resource game. Since each resource pool is defined within a local neighbourhood, the ego-network allocation head enables pool-level decision making while sharing parameters across the entire graph. The critic network evaluates the quality of the allocation actions generated by the actor. It takes the current graph state and the allocation action as input, and estimates the expected long-term return. To improve training stability and reduce overestimation bias, we adopt a twin-critic structure. Two critic networks independently estimate the action value, and the smaller value is used during policy evaluation. This design follows the common practice in actor–critic algorithms for continuous control problems. 

3.3. Training design 

Training the social planner in the proposed evolutionary game is inherently challenging because the environment is highly non-stationary. Individual agents continuously update their strategies during an episode, and the planner’s allocation decisions influence not only the immediate state but also the subsequent evolutionary trajectory of the system. In particular, poor allocation decisions in the early stage of an episode may drive the population into a low-cooperation regime that is di ffi cult to recover from later. To limit this long-horizon instability and improve optimization tractability, we use relatively short episodes of 200 steps. This choice is also supported by preliminary simulations, in which representative heuristic baselines such as Equal and Proportional allocation already exhibit stable convergence behaviour within this horizon. To improve training stability and robustness, we train the planner over multiple graph topologies rather than a single fixed interaction structure. Specifically, the training distribution comprises four topology families, including regular networks, ER networks, BA networks, and WS networks. For each topology, we construct a fixed graph bank using predetermined random seeds. During training, graph instances are sampled from these fixed banks. This design increases structural diversity while avoiding the excessive variance that would arise if entirely new graphs were regenerated at every reset. As a result, the planner is exposed to heterogeneous network structures while the optimization process remains reproducible and more stable. The actor architecture is further designed to match the local interaction pattern of the common-pool resource game. After global message passing over the entire graph, the final policy head is implemented as a shared ego-network allocation head, which is applied repeatedly to each focal node and its local neighbourhood. In this way, each focal node defines a local resource pool, and the actor produces a row-wise allocation distribution over the corresponding local participant set. In other words, a social planner applies the same allocation mechanism to the ego network of every node in the entire graph. For the optimization objective, we use a resource-oriented planner reward. At each step, the planner receives a scaled reward proportional to the population-mean resource level in the next state, denoted by λR ¯R(t + 1). The training objective is to maximize the cumulative return over the episode. This reward design is motivated by the observation that persistently high resource levels can only be maintained when the population remains in a highly cooperative regime. Therefore, although the reward is defined in terms of system-wide resources, it also serves as an indirect objective for sustaining cooperation over time. We adopt the standard TD3 update scheme, including delayed policy updates and soft target-network updates. The detailed network architecture and training hyperparameters are provided in Appendix A. Overall, the proposed GNN-RL framework learns an adaptive allocation mechanism over structured populations. By combining graph neural networks with reinforcement learning, the model can exploit local topological information while optimizing long-term collective outcomes.

5. Conclusions 

This study investigated how adaptive allocation mechanisms can promote cooperation in resource-constrained networked populations. We proposed a network common-pool resource game in which individuals are embedded in complex networks, participate in overlapping local resource pools, and face endogenous resource constraints during strategy evolution. This framework captures the coupling among cooperation, resource accumulation, local interaction structure, and allocation rules. Our results show that simple hand-designed allocation mechanisms face inherent limitations. Equal allocation produces fair but ine ffi cient outcomes, because it weakens contribution incentives and may lead to cooperation col-lapse. Proportional allocation can temporarily stimulate cooperation, but it amplifies accumulated advantages and may push resource-poor individuals into poverty traps. By contrast, the proposed GNN-RL social planner learns adaptive allocation policies that achieve more balanced outcomes across di ff erent network topologies, sustaining higher co-operation levels, maintaining larger average accumulated resources, and avoiding the severe inequality induced by purely proportional allocation. More importantly, the learned policy reveals interpretable principles for resource shar-ing. The extracted mixture mechanisms show that e ff ective allocation should not be fixed, but should depend on both the local resource state and the structural role of the resource pool. On regular networks, the learned policy can be approximated by a resource-dependent mixture mechanism. On heterogeneous networks, the degree-conditioned mix-ture mechanism further shows that peripheral nodes, intermediate-degree nodes, and hub-like nodes require di ff erent combinations of protection, contribution-based reward, and redistribution. These findings suggest that sustainable cooperation can benefit from allocation mechanisms that are sensitive to both local scarcity and network position. This point is further supported by the steady-state analysis in the Appendix, which shows that even under persistent global cooperation and equal local redistribution, heterogeneous networks can still induce nonzero resource inequality through degree heterogeneity. These results provide implications for public goods provision, ecological resource management, platform gover-nance, and organizational incentive design. A mechanism that appears fair in the short term may fail if it removes incentives to contribute, while a mechanism that strongly rewards contribution may become unsustainable if it al-lows early advantages to accumulate without correction. In this sense, the learned and extracted mechanisms o ff er a computational perspective on how artificial intelligence can assist the design of adaptive and interpretable governance rules. Future research may further examine the theoretical stability of the extracted mechanisms, extend the frame-work to larger and dynamically changing networks, and incorporate richer behavioural assumptions. In particular, since individual strategy updates in the present model are governed by a fixed Fermi rule, future work could introduce human participants or human-behaviour models to evaluate whether the learned allocation mechanisms remain e ff ec-tive under more realistic decision-making processes [47, 49, 50]. More broadly, when AI systems are deployed as social planners in the real world, their intervention principles should be evaluated not only by aggregate performance, but also by transparency, interpretability, and alignment with human welfare [52–54].

Appendix A. Implementation details of the GNN-RL planner 

This appendix provides additional implementation details of the proposed GNN-RL planner. The planner is de-signed to act as an external social planner that observes the current networked resource state and determines how the allocable resources of each local pool should be distributed. Its implementation consists of two main parts: a graph-based actor network that outputs local allocation weights and twin graph-based critic networks that estimate the action value of the allocation policy. The detailed network architecture is summarized in Table A.1. The actor first encodes the global network state through stacked GraphNet blocks and then applies a shared ego-network allocation head to each focal node. This design allows the same local allocation rule to be repeatedly applied to di ff erent resource pools while still conditioning the decision on the local neighborhood structure and node-level resource states. The critic networks use a similar graph-based state representation but are independently parameterized from the actor, so that policy evaluation and policy improvement remain separated during training. The training hyperparameters are reported in Table A.2. We use the TD3-style training scheme with twin critics, delayed actor updates, and soft target-network updates. These components are used to reduce overestimation bias and improve training stability in the non-stationary evolutionary environment. During training, each episode contains 200 evolutionary steps, while the evaluation experiments in the main text use longer simulation horizons to examine whether the learned allocation mechanism can sustain cooperation over extended dynamics. The social planner’s reward at each step is defined as the next-step average accumulated resource. At each training step, after the planner determines the allocation action and the environment evolves to the next state, the planner receives a scaled next-step average-resource reward: 

rplanner (t) = λR ¯R(t + 1) . (A.1) Here, λR > 0 is a constant reward-scaling coe ffi cient used to adjust the numerical magnitude of the reward during training. It should be noted that the reward does not explicitly include the cooperation level or the Gini coe ffi cient. Therefore, the improvements in cooperation and fairness reported in the main text should be interpreted as emergent consequences of the learned allocation policy rather than as directly optimized objectives. Also, due to the properties of network dynamics, achieving a high level of cumulative resources requires a low Gini coe ffi cient and a high level of cooperation.
