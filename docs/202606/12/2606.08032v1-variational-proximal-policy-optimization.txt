## Abstract 

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback via Proximal Policy Optimization often suffers from policy mode collapse, brittle exploration loops, and distribution drift. This paper introduces Variational Proximal Policy Optimization ( VP 2 O ), a particle-based variational inference framework that maps policy optimization to Stein Variational Gradient Descent within a Mixture-of-Experts architecture. By leveraging functional kernels over localized expert prototypes alongside an expert orthogonalization loss, VP 2 O introduces a geometry-based proximal-control mechanism that can reduce reliance on fixed clipping or KL schedules. Our results on a 33B/4B sparse Mixture-of-Experts model show several improvements across complex reasoning benchmarks, establishing a +179 ELO gain on Codeforces and a 32 % reduction in token count on AIME mathematical reasoning tasks. 

## 1 Introduction 

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for aligning AI systems with human preferences, enabling applications ranging from dialogue generation, code completion, to mathematical reasoning. However, RLHF pipelines built on Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) [Schulman et al., 2017] face persistent limitations. First, policies often collapse to a narrow set of high-reward behaviors, sacrificing the diversity needed to capture the full spectrum of human preferences [Casper et al., 2023]. Second, exploration remains inefficient, particularly in sparse or noisy reward landscapes [Wang et al., 2024a]. Third, policies can overfit to misspecified reward models, leading to reward hacking or distributional drift [Amodei et al., 2016, Gao et al., 2023]. While recent advances in alignment like GRPO [Shao et al., 2024] improve stability through gradient penalties, they lack mechanisms for principled exploration and diversity-aware optimization. Our approach addresses these challenges by integrating Stein Variational Policy Gradient ( SVPG ) [Liu et al., 2017] with PPO, casting policy optimization in RLHF as variational inference over a population of policies. We refer to our approach as Variational Proximal Policy Optimization (or VP 2 O). We instantiate VP 2 O within a Mixture-of-Experts ( MoE ) architecture, combining it specifically with GRPO, where each expert acts as a distinct policy particle in the variational ensemble. 1 VP 2 O uses Stein-style transport fields to promote policy diversity and to define geometry-aware proximal controls, providing an alternative to fixed clipping and KL schedules commonly used in PPO and variants such as GRPO. To further promote structural diversity and prevent expert collapse, we combine the Stein update with an expert-specialization objective [Guo et al., 2026], favoring orthogonal expert representations while reducing routing uniformity and functional overlap within the policy ensemble. The key intuition behind VP 2 O is that PPO/GRPO-style RLHF can be viewed through the lens of KL-regularized reward maximization, which induces an optimal policy distribution p∗ [Korbak et al., 2022, Rafailov et al., 2023]. VP 2 O uses this target distribution as the object of a Stein variational update. More intuitively, rather than treating proximal control as a scalar clipping rule, VP 2 Odefines a functional geometry over expert prototypes and transports multiple expert particles toward the high-reward regions of p∗ while preserving diversity. Finally, we unify posterior sampling and policy constraints into a single framework, VP 2 O, enabling policies that are diverse, uncertainty-aware, and aligned with human preferences. 

## 2 Preliminaries 

Standard PPO/GRPO-style RLHF optimize policies through clipped or regularized objectives that, while effective, face persistent limitations [Ahmadian et al., 2024]: (i) they often converge to single modes, sacrificing diversity; (ii) their exploration relies on heuristics ( e.g. entropy bonuses); and (iii) their trust regions are enforced via ad-hoc constraints ( e.g. clipping or fixed DKL penalties) or heuristics which suffer from high-variance importance sampling [Thomas et al., 2015, Mahmood et al., 2014]. Token-level importance ratios can become in particular a major source of variance and instability during RL training [Zheng et al., 2023, 2025]: ratios spike under actor-learner drift, destabilizing training and complicating credit assignment across long sequences; clipping becomes a hand-tuned band-aid that distorts gradients and requires per-task tuning, refresh timing turns ad-hoc; and replay gets fragile. For large language models, this materializes as amplified variance, which accumulates over long sequences, wasted rollouts or over-refreshed actors, and brittle convergence. As demonstrated below, the PPO/GRPO’s objective can be reformulated as minimizing a DKL (πθ ∥p∗)

between a target policy πθ and the optimal policy distribution p∗. This recasting reveals a deeper connection: PPO/GRPO ’s heuristic constraints are approximations of a principled variational prob-lem , where trust region constraints can be replaced with geometric controls to stabilize optimization, reduce reliance on IS, and provide data-driven refresh criteria instead of arbitrary refresh schedules.

For RLHF, the model architecture that lends itself the most to this optimization is the mixture of experts .32.3 Mixture of Experts 

To realize the variational inference framework described in Section 2.2, we require a parameterized distribution that can maintain multiple “modes” or particles of behavior. We leverage the Sparsely-Gated Mixture of Experts ( MoE ) architecture [Hinton et al., 2015, Shazeer et al., 2017], reinterpreting its modular structure as a discrete ensemble of policy particles. In a standard Transformer-based MoE, the dense Feed-Forward Network (FFN) layers are replaced by N independent expert blocks. For any given input token x, a trainable router ϕ(·) selects a sparse subset of K experts to process the information. While MoE is traditionally used for computational efficiency—increasing model capacity without a proportional increase in FLOPs—we utilize it here for its functional modularity. 

Expert Specialization and Particle Identity. Each expert i within an MoE layer acts as a functional “particle” in the Stein discrepancy framework. Rather than viewing the MoE as a single monolithic model, we treat the individual experts as distinct policy components πθi that collectively approximate the optimal policy distribution p∗. The MoE architecture provides two critical properties for our approach: (i) conditional computation and (ii) structural diversity . For (i), by activating only a subset of Top-K experts, the model naturally creates high-density regions of the policy space, which we can then regularize using Stein forces. For (ii), the separation of experts allows us to apply the repulsive force (5) directly to the expert parameters or their output projections, preventing the “mode collapse” common in standard RLHF where the model converges to a single, narrow set of high-reward tokens. 

Routing as Variational Assignment. The MoE gating function ω(x) = softmax (ϕ(x)) serves as a local assignment mechanism. In our formulation, the router’s role is not just load balancing, but also distributional coverage. By analyzing the co-activation patterns of these experts, we can define a kernel K that measures the similarity between policy particles in the functional space Hd. This allows the Stein update to move “similar” experts toward high-reward regions (via attraction) while pushing “redundant” experts toward unexplored areas of the preference landscape (via repulsion). One of the primary benefits of MoE architectures is their efficiency [Shazeer et al., 2017, Fedus et al., 2021, Jacobs et al., 1991]. Although, the number of experts N in an MoE layer can be relatively large, only a subset of the experts are selected to process each token [Fedus et al., 2021]. This means that only a small portion of the MoE model parameters— given by the experts selected at each layer —are active when processing a given token. Precisely, given a token representation x, each MoE layer maintains a routing structure ϕ(·) to sparsely select the experts to activate. Such a structure can take one of two forms: a soft-routing or hard-routing . Formally, soft-routing applies a linear transformation to the token vector x, forming a vector of size N , the number of experts in the layer, followed by a softmax to induce a probability distribution over the set of all experts. ## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup Model. We evaluate VP 2 O on a 33B/4B-parameter sparse MoE model initialized from a pre-trained checkpoint with N = 20 experts per feed-forward layer and Top-K routing. The same architecture and initialization are shared with the baseline model to isolate the effect of the training objective. 

Training details. Advantages are computed via group-relative policy optimization [Shao et al., 2024]. Optimization uses AdamW [Loshchilov and Hutter, 2017] ( β1 = 0 .9, β 2 = 0 .95 ) with a cosine learning-rate schedule. The Stein kernel temperature is set to τ = T0/N and the repulsion coefficient to λrep = 0 .1. The orthogonality-loss weight follows Guo et al. [2026]’s experimental settings with 

β = 0 .01 . We train at two generation lengths (8K and 16K tokens) to assess sensitivity to context. Checkpoints are saved at regular step (after every 80 training steps) intervals throughout training. 

Baseline. Our primary comparison is a GRPO-trained MoE model trained under identical compute, data, and architectural conditions. This is the strongest known PPO-variant for our setting. 

Benchmarks. We evaluate across the followong five categories: • Mathematical reasoning: AIME 2024 [Zhang and Math-AI, 2024] and AIME 2025 [Zhang and Math-AI, 2025], competition-level problem solving. • Scientific reasoning: GPQA Diamond [Rein et al., 2023], graduate-level science QA. • Knowledge: MMLU [Wang et al., 2024b], a multi-task language understanding benchmark. • Code generation: Codeforces ELO and Pass@1, measuring competitive-programming ability. • Instruction following: IFBench [Pyatkin et al., 2025] and IFEval [Zhou et al., 2023], evaluated under both loose and strict protocols. Metrics are averaged across all evaluation checkpoints. 

4.2 Main Results 

Table 1 reports the average score of each method across training checkpoints and generation lenghts. VP 2 O delivers consistent gains across all benchmark categories, with improvements that generally 

increase from 8K to 16K, indicating that the variational framework scales with generation length. • Mathematical reasoning. VP 2 O improves AIME 2024 and AIME 2025 by +2.6 and +2.8

percentage points (pp) under 8K. In the 16K setting, AIME 2024 converges around 2,000 steps 7earlier than the baseline while retaining a +1.6 pp lead on average, consistent with the Stein driving force pooling gradient information across expert particles. While the gains seem modest, the early convergence shows that VP 2 O reaches strong AIME 2024 performance earlier than the baseline. • Scientific and knowledge reasoning. GPQA Diamond shows near-parity at 8K generation length (−0.3 pp) but a clear advantage at 16K generation length ( +1.8 pp). MMLU-Pro improves modestly but consistently ( +0.4 and +1.1 pp) across both 8K and 16K. These results suggest that expert diversity benefits harder multi-step scientific tasks most under longer generation budgets. • Code generation. The largest gain appears on Codeforces at 16K generation length where the result is the most striking. VP 2 O surpasses the baseline by +179 ELO and +3.6 Pass@1 points, while 8K shows near-parity. The gap emerges because longer generation windows allow essentially the Stein repulsive force to push experts toward structurally distinct solution strategies, which is particularly valuable in competitive programming where multiple correct approaches exist. • Instruction following. 4.3 Training Dynamics 

Figures 1 and 2 show training trajectories for the six academic benchmarks. Three patterns stand out. 1. Stable advantage from early training. On every benchmark where VP 2 O leads, the gap is established within the first few thousand steps and does not erode, consistent with the geometric trust regions (Section 2.1) providing well-calibrated, data-driven step-size control from the start. 2. Faster convergence at 16K. On AIME 2024, the baseline requires roughly ∼2,000 additional steps to reach the performance level VP 2 O achieves at step ∼4,000 (see Figure 2, top left). Although, the baseline eventually approaches the VP 2 O performance level in late training, the reduced training steps suggests a convergence- speed benefit rather than only a final-score benefit. 3. Baseline degradation at 8K. On AIME 2024 and Codeforces the baseline shows a declining trend in late training while VP 2 O remains stable. This behavior is consistent with reward over-optimization or reward-mode collapse, two forms of reward hacking that the Stein repulsive force can help mitigate. A stronger diagnosis would require, however, additional measurements such as router entropy, expert utilization, expert-overlap scores, and reward-model score trajectories.

4.5 Discussion When does VP 2O help most? VP 2 O appears most helpful in three regimes: longer generation budgets, tasks that admit multiple solution strategies, and early-to-mid training phases where expert diversity has not yet collapsed. The strongest evidence appears at 16K on Codeforces and instruction following, where VP 2 O improves both quality and solution-token efficiency. GPQA at 8K is the main exception: VP 2 O trails by 0.3 pp, but the 16K result recovers to a +1.8 pp advantage. 

Computational overhead. The Stein transport field φ(θi) requires one additional forward pass per expert to compute the prototype update and co-activation scores, adding approximately 5–8% wall-10 clock overhead relative to our GRPO baseline in this implementation. The geometric trust-region check is O(N D out ) per step and is negligible relative to the model forward/backward pass. 

Limitations. Our evaluation is limited to a single model family and scale. Whether the variational benefits persist at larger scales (e.g. 70B+) or with more experts ( N ≫ 20 ) is an important open question. The current work also does not report on routing diagnostics such as router entropy, expert utilization, expert-overlap measures, KL drift, or ESS trajectories. Future work should test whether the same benefits hold across different model families, expert counts, and reward/data mixtures. 

## 5 Conclusion 

We presented VP 2 O, a variational proximal policy optimization framework that views regularized alignment optimization, such as PPO/GRPO, as particle-based variational inference within sparse Mixture-of-Experts architectures. By using functional kernels defined over low-dimensional expert prototypes, VP 2 O introduces geometry-aware proximal controls that complement or partially replace fixed clipping and KL divergence schedules. Empirical evaluations on a single 33B/4B sparse MoE model show consistent improvements on several mathematical reasoning, code-generation, and instruction-following benchmarks, with the strongest gains appearing at longer generation lengths. These results suggest that preserving functional diversity across expert parameters is a promising direction for stabilizing long-form reinforcement-learning trajectories in large language models, while broader validation across model families, scales, reward and data mixtures remains future work.
