# Automatically Differentiable Nonlinear Tensor Networks (ADNTNs) for Exponential Compression of Deep Neural Networks 

Andrzej Cichocki Michał Więtczak June 2, 2026 

Abstract 

Large neural networks often work well precisely because they contain more parameters than the immediate task appears to require. That surplus can make training easier and can improve expressivity, but it also creates a practical problem: at deployment time the same parameters must be stored, moved through memory, and evaluated under tight latency and energy budgets. This paper asks whether selected dense, convolutional, and attention layers can be stored not as large tables of numbers, but as the output of much smaller differentiable tensor network generators. We study Automatically Differentiable Nonlinear Tensor Networks (ADNTNs), a family of structured weight generators whose compact core tensors are trained end-to-end by reverse-mode automatic differentiation (AD). The approach can be viewed as a natural extension of low-rank adaptation and tensor factorisation: instead of using one low-rank matrix update, an ADNTN builds a large weight tensor through a hierarchy of small cores, nonlinear activations, and optional lateral mixing tensors. The paper focuses on three architectures: Tree Tensor Networks (TTNs), augmented TTNs (aTTNs) with boundary disentanglers, and Multi-scale Entanglement Renormalisation Ansätze (MERA). Our main mathematical contribution is a unified forward-adjoint calculus for these generators. For each architecture we state the forward contraction, the pre-activation adjoints, and the contracted-environment gradients of the core tensors. These formulae make transparent what an AD system computes when loss.backward() is called. The formulation supports nonlinear activations, task-aware objectives, batching, and hardware-aware execution schedules. At the same time, the paper keeps a clear distinction between 

differentiating a contraction program and making contraction free: AD does not remove the cost of large intermediates, poor contraction orders, or exact contraction of general loopy tensor networks. The key modelling idea is simple. The layer used by the neural network may remain linear in its input, for example y =̂ WΘx, while the generator that produces ̂ WΘ is nonlinear in its small trainable cores. This nonlinearity relaxes the fixed multilinear rank restrictions of classical tensor networks and allows the generated weights to live on richer low-dimensional manifolds. We also standardise notation and initialisation across the architectures, because the variance of the latent tensor, expansion cores, and disentanglers strongly affects AD stability in deep hierarchical generators. Preliminary simulations on AlexNet and VGG-16 layers show per-layer compression ratios from roughly 2,000 × to 77 ,000 × in the studied settings, with accuracy often matching the dense baseline and, in several VGG-16 cases, improving it. These results are encouraging rather than final: they suggest that ADNTNs are a promising, mathematically structured, and hardware-aware route toward much smaller neural networks, provided that optimisation, contraction schedules, and deployment kernels are designed together.

# 1 Introduction 

Modern state-of-the-art DNNs and LLMs often contain hundreds of millions or billions of parameters. They are trained on large distributed hardware clusters and then deployed in latency-sensitive applications such as image classification, clustering, recognition, medical screening, interactive dialogue, search, and mobile assistance. This scale is beneficial for expressivity, optimisation landscape, and emergent capabilities, but it creates practical obstacles at inference time: a large model requires high memory capacity, large memory bandwidth, expensive accelerators, and non-negligible energy per query. A substantial empirical literature shows that many trained networks are functionally redundant: parameters can often be pruned, quantised, factorised, shared, or distilled with limited loss of accuracy in the moderate-compression regime (Blalock et al., 2020; Ganesh et al., 2020). Tensor decompositions provide a structured way to exploit this redundancy because they replace a dense tensor by a network of smaller factors whose parameter count can scale linearly (or near-linearly) in the number of tensor modes rather than exponentially (Kolda and Bader, 2009; Cichocki et al., 2016; Oseledets, 2011). Recent work on automatically differentiable tensor networks for neural-network compression further suggests that deep tensor-network generators can represent large neural weights with very few trainable degrees of freedom (Qing et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2023). 

1.1 Motivation and Compression Objectives 

Model compression is a multi-objective design problem that seeks to balance several competing goals simultaneously. 

• Memory reduction: reduce the number of stored parameters and the bytes per parameter. 

• Latency reduction: reduce wall-clock inference time, not merely the formal parameter count. 

• Energy and cost reduction: reduce accelerator memory traffic, arithmetic intensity, and operational cost. 

• Accuracy preservation: maintain the predictive performance of the original model on the target task. 

• Behavioural fidelity: preserve the teacher (dense) model’s output distribution, calibration, intermediate representations, and safety-relevant behaviour. 

• Deployability: produce structures that can be executed efficiently by available libraries and hardware. Although all of these objectives are desirable, they can be mutually contradictory. Unstructured sparsity may reduce the parameter count but often fail to accelerate inference on dense hardware. Very aggressive quantisation may reduce memory bandwidth but damage rare capabilities. Domain-specific compression may improve a specialised benchmark while weakening general-purpose behaviour. Compression is therefore best understood as a Pareto frontier,

The principal classical compression families are the following. 

• Pruning: removing individual weights, channels, heads, neurons, filters, or blocks according to a saliency criterion (Blalock et al., 2020). 

• Quantisation: replacing 32-bit or 16-bit floating-point parameters and activations by lower-precision formats, commonly 8-bit, 4-bit, or mixed precision (Dettmers et al., 2023; Frantar et al., 2023). 

• Knowledge distillation: training a compact student to imitate the logits, probabilities, hidden states, attention maps, or behaviour of a larger (dense) pre-trained teacher (Hinton et al., 2015; Ganesh et al., 2020). 

• Parameter-efficient adaptation: freezing a pre-trained model and learning a small struc-tured update, as in LoRA, where ∆W = BA is low-rank (Hu et al., 2022). 

• Low-rank and tensor-network factorisation: approximating a weight matrix by W ≈

AB with a small inner dimension, or approximating a higher-order tensor Tensor Train (TT/MPS), Tensor Ring (TR), Matrix Product Operator (MPO), Tucker, Canonical Polyadic Decomposition (CPD), Tree Tensor Network (TTN), and MERA or related decompositions (Phan et al., 2020; Gusak et al., 2019; Qing et al., 2025; Gu et al., 2025; Javanmard et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2023). LoRA illustrates the conceptual transition from dense retraining to structured low-dimensional adaptation. Tensor-network compression generalises this idea: instead of a single rank-r matrix update, a large weight matrix or convolutional kernel is represented as the contraction of many small core tensors. The principal examples are the TT/TR, MPO, CPD, Tucker, TTN, and MERA (Cichocki et al., 2016, 2017; Oseledets, 2011; Kolda and Bader, 2009; Zhao et al., 2016; Vidal, 2007). 

1.3 From ADTN to ADNTN: Key Definitions 

Definition 1 (ADTN) . An Automatically Differentiable Tensor Network (ADTN) is a tensor network implemented as a differentiable computation graph so that its core tensors can be optimised by automatic differentiation (AD), with optional ReLU nonlinearities (Liao et al., 2019; Liao and Liu, 2021; Qing et al., 2025). 

Definition 2 (ADNTN) . An Automatically Differentiable Nonlinear Tensor Network (ADNTN) is an ADTN in which nonlinear activation functions, normalisation, gates, or other optimised differentiable, learnable, nonlinearities are inserted between tensor contractions. 

In a classical tensor network, all contractions are multilinear in the cores. In an ADNTN, the contractions remain differentiable but are no longer multilinear, because activations such as ELU or GELU are interleaved with the contraction layers (Hendrycks and Gimpel, 2016; Clevert et al., 2015). This modification is essential when small-radix ( d = 2 ) cores are used to generate expressive neural weights, as discussed in Section 4. 

1.4 Main Contributions 

The main contributions of this paper are as follows. 1. We formulate ADNTNs as hierarchical nonlinear tensor-network generators for dense, convo-lutional, and attention-layer weights. 32. We give explicit reverse-mode AD equations and training algorithms for the three architectures studied in detail: TTN, aTTN, and MERA. 3. We clarify why nonlinear generation can be useful even when the generated layer is used linearly, while avoiding the overclaim that a fixed tiny-bond generator is universal for all dense matrices. 4. We synchronise the notation for generated tensors, target tensors, adjoints, bond dimensions, physical radix, and layer indices, and we introduce a global initialisation convention for stable AD training. 5. We report proof-of-concept simulations on AlexNet and VGG-16 layers, including parameter counts, compression ratios, and validation accuracy changes relative to dense baselines.

# 3 Hierarchical Nonlinear Tensor Networks for DNN Compres-sion 

We consider three top-down decoders: the Tree Tensor Network (TTN), the augmented TTN (aTTN), and the Multi-scale Entanglement Renormalisation Ansatz (MERA). In all cases, a small latent/root tensor is expanded through local core tensors into a high-order tensor which is finally reshaped into the weight matrix or convolutional kernel required by the neural network layer. A multilinear TTN, also known in numerical multilinear algebra as a hierarchical Tucker-type model, recursively expands a parent index into two child indices. The topology is loop-free: cutting an internal bond separates the tree into two subtrees. This makes TTNs simple to batch and easy to train, but it can create boundary artefacts between neighbouring leaves that are separated early in the tree. Nonlinear TTNs retain the same sparse connectivity but apply an 6activation after each layerwise expansion, allowing the generator to represent richer families of weights than a purely multilinear tree.

V (ℓ,k ). ELU-type activations are applied after hidden expansion layers, while the final layer is left linear so that the generated tensor can be reshaped into an ordinary linear or convolutional weight. The aTTN augments a TTN by adding one or more layers of local two-site disentanglers near the physical boundary. These lateral tensors mix neighbouring branches before the final generated tensor is read out. The goal is to reduce cross-branch discontinuities at modest additional parameter cost. MERA pushes this idea further by inserting disentanglers at every scale, thereby creating overlapping causal cones and stronger multi-scale mixing (Vidal, 2007; Evenbly and Vidal, 2014).

3.1 Quantised Local Cores Nonlinearly Interconnected 

A central design principle in ADNTN compression is to keep all local dimensions small. For a homogeneous binary architecture we often use physical radix d = 2 and bond dimension χ = 2 .The canonical core shapes are chain/branching core: G ∈ Rχ×d×χ, (7) binary expansion core: V ∈ Rχ×χ×χ, (8) two-site disentangler: U ∈ Rχ×χ×χ×χ, (9) leaf map: P ∈ Rχ×d. (10) For a binary tree with Q leaves and bounded χ, a representative parameter count is 

¯PTTN ≈ (Q − 1) χ3 + Qχd, (11) whereas a dense order-Q target tensor has dQ entries. Thus, for fixed d and χ, the nominal parameter compression grows exponentially with Q. This is a parameter-count statement; accuracy, latency, and energy gains must still be verified empirically and hardware-specifically. The binary radix d = 2 is attractive because it gives extreme compression, uniform tensor legs, and power-of-two memory layouts. Larger d or χ can be introduced adaptively when validation loss indicates that the bottleneck is too restrictive. 

3.2 Practical Architectural Choices 

Mode ordering. Mode ordering is a modelling choice, not a cosmetic detail. For matrices, paired indices (oq, i q) often preserve local input-output structure. For convolutional kernels, spatial modes and channel modes should usually be treated differently because the spatial modes are small while channels dominate the parameter count. • The execution graph maps data to predictions, for example y =̂ WΘx or Y =̂ KΘ ⋆ X.This graph may remain linear in the input.

6.3 Forward-Mode and Reverse-Mode AD 

AD has two complementary modes (Baydin et al., 2018). 

• Forward mode propagates tangents. Given a direction ˙θ, it computes ˙L = ∇θL · ˙θ.

• Reverse mode propagates adjoints. Starting with ¯L = 1 , it computes all parameter adjoints by repeated use of Eq. (38). Forward mode is efficient when the number of inputs is small; reverse mode is efficient when the number of scalar outputs is small. Since training normally uses one scalar loss and many parameters, reverse mode is the natural choice. Modern systems such as TensorFlow, PyTorch, and JAX provide reverse-mode AD, checkpointing, compilation, batching, mixed precision, and custom gradients (Abadi et al., 2016; Paszke et al., 2019; Bradbury et al., 2018). 

6.4 Advantages over Numerical and Symbolic Differentiation 

Compared with finite differences and symbolic differentiation, AD is attractive because it 

• avoids finite-difference truncation and cancellation errors; 

• avoids symbolic expression blow-up by differentiating the executed program; and 

• composes cleanly through arbitrary differentiable modules, including tensor contractions and downstream neural-network losses. Recent analyses in scientific machine learning also emphasise that replacing AD by finite differences can alter optimisation dynamics, not only derivative accuracy (Chen et al., 2025). 14 6.5 Differentiable Programming 

Differentiable programming treats whole programs as trainable maps. Tensor network contrac-tions, iterative solvers, spectral routines, and neural-network layers can be combined in one differentiable objective, provided that the implemented primitives have valid local backward rules (Liao et al., 2019; Liao and Liu, 2021). For ADNTNs this means that the same code can support Frobenius reconstruction, end-to-end cross-entropy, distillation, quantisation-aware fine-tuning, and hardware-aware penalties without deriving a new optimisation algorithm for each loss.

7.5 Advantages of AD vs. Classical Algorithms (SVD, DMRG, HOSVD) 

Nonlinear generators. SVD, HOSVD, ALS, and standard DMRG updates exploit multilinear-ity. AD only requires differentiable primitives, so it can train ELU/GELU/Swish/SIREN/rational ADNTNs without deriving a new environment equation for each activation. 

Task-aware losses. Classical decompositions most naturally minimise Frobenius reconstruction error. AD can optimise cross-entropy, KL distillation, calibration, robustness, quantisation, or hardware penalties directly. 

Correct treatment of contraction complexity. AD does not make exact contraction of arbitrary loopy tensor networks easy. It computes exact gradients of the contraction program that is actually executed. For TTNs this program is naturally tree-structured. For aTTN and MERA, the chosen open-boundary expansion-mixing schedule and bounded local dimensions keep intermediates manageable in the studied architectures. More general PEPS-like loopy networks would still require approximate contraction, sampling, or careful contraction-order optimisation (Ran et al., 2020). 

7.6 Training Pipeline 

A robust ADNTN training schedule is as follows. 1. Select layers. Compress the largest linear, convolutional, or attention projection layers first. 2. Tensorise. Choose radix d, mode ordering, latent order Qbot , bond dimension χ, and topology. 3. Initialise consistently. Use the global policy in Section 3.5: He/Kaiming scaling for hidden nonlinear cores, Xavier/Glorot scaling for final linear cores, identity-plus-noise for disentanglers, and optional TT-SVD/Tucker warm starts. 4. Block-wise fitting. Fit one block or group at a time by reconstruction, input-output matching, or task loss. 5. End-to-end fine-tuning. Insert generated weights into the model and optimise the composite loss in Eq. (35). 19 6. Adaptive growth. Increase χ, d, or selected disentangler layers where validation loss or gradient saliency indicates underfitting. 7. Quantisation-aware training. Add fake quantisation or quantisation penalties after the floating-point model has stabilised. 8. Prune, cache, and export. Remove small gates, fold constants, cache generated weights when appropriate, and export fused kernels.

8.4 Optimising DNN Inference Latency: SRAM Implementation and Caching Strategies for Nonlinear Tensor Networks 

Extreme parameter compression does not automatically imply latency reduction. If a generated dense weight is reconstructed on every inference call, the generator can dominate runtime. A deployment-oriented ADNTN implementation therefore has two modes. 

• Cached-weight mode: generate ̂ WΘ once, cache it, and run the original dense/convolutional kernel. This is simplest and usually fastest when memory allows. 

• On-the-fly mode: evaluate the tensor generator inside a fused kernel. This is attractive when weights are too large to store or when inputs allow activation reuse, but it requires careful compilation. 

SRAM/cache residency. The core tensors in Table 3 occupy from hundreds of bytes to a few tens of kilobytes depending on precision. Such parameter sets can often reside in L1/L2 cache, 22 GPU shared memory, or microcontroller SRAM/TCM. In a custom CUDA kernel, cores can be loaded into __shared__ memory at kernel start; on edge devices, cores can be allocated in tightly coupled memory where available. This reduces memory traffic, but the actual latency gain must be measured on the target hardware. 

Activation reuse. For structured inputs with repeated local patterns, the first one or two tree levels may repeatedly receive identical sub-vectors. A small look-up table can cache post-activation values of the form σ(Core × InputSubvector ). This is most useful for sparse, padded, or repeated inputs; it should be disabled when cache lookups exceed saved arithmetic. 

Graph compilation and fusion. Dozens of small contractions can be slower than one dense kernel if executed as separate framework operations. JAX jit , PyTorch compile , XLA, and Triton-style custom kernels can fuse contractions, activations, and reshapes so that intermedi-ates remain in registers or shared memory (Bradbury et al., 2018; Paszke et al., 2019). The implementation target is a small number of fused kernels per compressed layer, not a Python loop over individual cores. 

Branch-level parallelism. In TTN and aTTN hidden layers, all branches at the same depth are independent. They should be mapped to parallel threads, warps, or vector lanes. MERA adds lateral mixing, but contractions within a scale still expose useful parallelism. In preliminary cached-weight implementations, this approach produced inference latencies in the range of 12 –18 

ms/image; these numbers should be treated as hardware- and implementation-dependent rather than universal. 

Figure 6: Deployment pipeline for turning extreme parameter compression into practical inference speed. A large dense layer is replaced by a compact nonlinear TTN/MERA generator whose cores can be pinned in cache or SRAM. Low latency then depends on implementation choices–cached weights when memory allows, fused on-the-fly generation when memory is the bottleneck, and branch-level parallelism to avoid many small sequential kernel launches. The most realistic near-term deployment strategy is optimistic but pragmatic: train ADNTNs end-to-end, cache generated weights when memory allows, and use fused on-the-fly generation only for layers or devices where the memory savings outweigh the additional arithmetic. 

# 9 Conclusions and Future Directions 

9.1 Summary of Contributions 

This paper has developed ADNTNs as compact, differentiable generators of neural network weights. The central idea is to separate two roles that are often implicitly fused: the layer used 23 during inference can remain an ordinary linear, convolutional, or attention projection, while the parameters of that layer are produced by a much smaller nonlinear tensor-network program. This separation is useful because it preserves familiar neural-network operations while moving the storage and optimisation burden into a structured low-dimensional generator. 

Theoretical. We made explicit the cut-rank restrictions that govern purely multilinear tensor networks and explained why inserting nonlinear activations between contraction layers changes the picture. The claim is deliberately cautious: a fixed χ = 2 generator is not a universal representation of all dense matrices. Nevertheless, nonlinear generators can explore weight families that are much richer than the corresponding multilinear tensor networks, and this matches the practical hypothesis that many trained DNN layers occupy structured, highly compressible regions of parameter space. 

Architectural. We described TTN, aTTN, and MERA decoders using the same notation for state tensors, pre-activations, expansion cores, disentanglers, and generated weights. TTN gives the simplest and most economical hierarchy; aTTN adds a targeted boundary-mixing mechanism; MERA gives the strongest multi-scale mixing, at the price of more permutations and larger contraction constants. The revised initialisation policy also makes these architectures easier to compare because core variance, latent scale, and disentangler perturbations are handled in a single consistent way. 

Algorithmic. We derived forward-adjoint equations for the studied generators. These for-mulae show that reverse-mode AD computes pre-activation adjoints, parent-state adjoints, and contracted-environment gradients for every trainable core. This is valuable not because AD makes tensor contractions costless, but because it lets nonlinearities, task-aware losses, batching, modern optimisers, and hardware-aware schedules be handled inside one differentiable program. 

Empirical. The preliminary CIFAR-10 experiments on AlexNet and VGG-16 are encouraging. They show compression ratios from roughly 2,000 × to 77 ,000 × in the studied layers, with several VGG-16 settings matching or improving the dense baseline. These results should be read as proof of concept rather than a final benchmark: they indicate that the ADNTN manifold can act as a useful implicit regulariser, but broader validation is still needed. 

9.2 Limitations 

• Optimisation remains non-convex. AD supplies accurate gradients of the executed program, not global optima. Initialisation, activation scaling, optimiser choice, and learning-rate schedules materially affect the result. 

• Variance control matters. Deep generators with many small cores are sensitive to the initial scale of latent tensors, expansion cores, and disentanglers. The global initialisation convention proposed here should therefore be treated as part of the model specification, not as an implementation afterthought. 

• Contraction and materialisation costs remain real. If a full generated weight is materialised at every training or inference step, memory and runtime can dominate. Sampling, caching, checkpointing, and careful contraction ordering are important. 

• Latency is hardware-dependent. Parameter compression does not automatically imply wall-clock speedup. Practical acceleration requires fused kernels, cache/SRAM residency, and hardware-specific measurement. 24 • The evidence is preliminary. The reported simulations are promising but limited. Larger datasets, transformer blocks, LLM projections, ablations, and reproducible implementations are important next steps. 

9.3 Future Directions 

ADNTNs are best understood as trainable weight generators rather than fixed post-hoc decom-positions. That viewpoint opens several constructive research directions. 

• Adaptive topology search. Learn mode ordering, tree connectivity, bond dimensions, and disentangler placement jointly with the core tensors. 

• Faithful compression. Combine cross-entropy with logit distillation, feature matching, at-tention transfer, calibration losses, and safety-relevant behavioural tests so that the compressed model preserves more than top-1 accuracy. 

• Hardware-aware co-design. Optimise contraction order, precision, caching strategy, quantisation format, and kernel fusion with hardware-in-the-loop feedback. 

• Dynamic growth and pruning. Grow χ, d, or selected disentangler layers only where validation loss or gradient saliency indicates underfitting, and prune gates or cores where sparsity penalties indicate redundancy. 

• Operator learning. Use encoder-decoder TTN/MERA topologies as compact nonlinear operators for images, signals, and scientific-computing surrogates. 

• Quantum and neuromorphic links. MERA-like causal structures connect naturally to quantum circuits and specialised accelerators; as hardware evolves, such generators may become even more attractive. The overall message is optimistic but pragmatic. The transition from classical tensor algorithms (SVD, HOSVD, DMRG) to automatic differentiation is not a software convenience — it is a mathematical and conceptual necessity for deep nonlinear generators. ADNTNs do not remove the need for careful mathematics, stable initialisation, or hardware-aware engineering. What they do offer is a coherent way to combine tensor-network structure with modern differentiable programming. If future experiments confirm the early compression and generalisation trends reported here, ADNTNs could become a credible path toward neural networks that are far smaller, easier to deploy, and still expressive enough for demanding tasks.
