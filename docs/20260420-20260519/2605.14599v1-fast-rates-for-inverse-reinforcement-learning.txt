# Fast Rates for Inverse Reinforcement Learning

## Abstract 

We establish novel structural and statistical results for entropy-regularized min-max inverse reinforcement learning (Min-Max-IRL) with linear reward classes in finite-horizon MDPs with Borel state and action spaces. On the structural side, we show that maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) and Min-Max-IRL are equivalent at the population level, and at the empirical level under deterministic dynamics. On the statistical side, exploiting pseudo-self-concordance of the Min-Max-IRL loss, we prove that both the trajectory-level KL divergence and the squared parameter error in the Hessian norm decay at the fast rate O(nвҲ’1), where 

n is the number of expert trajectories. Our guarantees apply under misspecification and require no exploration assumptions. We further extend reward-identifiability results to general Borel spaces and derive novel results on the derivatives of the soft-optimal value function with respect to reward parameters. 

## 1 Introduction 

In many sequential decision-making problems, including robotic manipulation or language model alignment, the reward function is unknown, difficult to specify, or too sparse to be practical, and it is easier to demon-strate desired behavior. This motivates the imitation learning setting, where the goal is to infer a policy from a dataset of expert demonstrations. The most direct approach to imitation learning is behavioral cloning (BC), which fits a policy to the data by supervised learning without requiring access to the environ-ment. However, BC is often associated with compounding errors, as it only imitates the expertвҖҷs actions on the support of the expert data and is agnostic to the MDP dynamics. Inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) instead recovers a reward that induces an optimal policy that imitates the expertвҖҷs behavior within the MDP. By parameterizing rewards rather than policies, IRL can encode structural priorsвҖ“such as linearity in features, sparsity, or stability (Kalman, 1964; Pfrommer et al., 2025)вҖ“without restrictive assumptions on the dynamics. As expert demonstrations are costly, a central question in imitation learning is how many expert trajectories n

are required to imitate the expert well in a given metric. This is a classical statistical estimation problem. For BC, the statistical picture is fairly complete: Foster et al. (2024) show that maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) ensures that the trajectory-level squared Hellinger distance between the expert and the imitation policy decays as O(nвҲ’1) for well-specified and sufficiently learnable policy classes. Furthermore, they show that this Hellinger bound yields optimal suboptimality rates that scale only with the maximum return and an action-variance term, with no additional dependence on the horizon T . This contrasts with BC under other losses, such as the 0/1 loss, for which Ross & Bagnell (2010) show that a linear horizon dependence is unavoidable. Given that IRL has access to the MDP while BC does not, one might expect strictly stronger statistical guarantees. Existing IRL results, however, typically give slow O(nвҲ’1/2) rates with quadratic horizon depen-dence T 2 (Syed & Schapire, 2007), require strong exploration assumptions for reward parameter recovery (Schlaginhaufen & Kamgarpour, 2024), and are largely confined to finite state-action spaces. Furthermore, the role of entropy regularizationвҖ“central to modern IRL algorithms (Ziebart et al., 2010; Ho & Ermon, 1

> arXiv:2605.14599v1 [cs.LG] 14 May 2026

2016; Garg et al., 2021) and to reward identifiability (Cao et al., 2021; Schlaginhaufen & Kamgarpour, 2023; 2024)вҖ“is poorly understood from a statistical perspective. In this paper, we close this gap. We establish a connection between MLE BC and entropy-regularized IRL, and show that the latter achieves fast O(nвҲ’1) rates for the trajectory-level KL divergence and parameter estimation. Our upper bounds apply under misspecification, require no exploration assumptions, and can yield horizon-independent rates in specific settings. Furthermore, we extend existing results on reward identifiability from the tabular setting to Borel state and action spaces, and establish novel results on the derivatives of the optimal value with respect to the reward. 

Contributions Our main contributions can be summarized as follows: 1. Structural equivalences In Section 3, we show that MLE and (entropy-regularized) Min-Max-IRL are equivalent at the population level (Theorem 3.1). At the empirical level, the equivalence holds under deterministic dynamics and under stochastic dynamics, an additional correction term arises due to randomness of the state transition. From an optimization perspective, Min-Max-IRL is a convex optimization problem, while MLE-IRL is non-convex in general. 2. Fast rates for Min-Max-IRL In Section 4, we derive novel statistical guarantees for Min-Max-IRL. In particular, we establish pseudo self-concordance (Ostrovskii & Bach, 2021) of the Min-Max-IRL loss to prove that both the trajectory-level KL divergence and the squared parameter error (in a Hessian norm) decay as O(nвҲ’1) in the number of expert demonstrations n (Theorem 4.3). Our results apply in misspecified settings and we make no exploration assumptions. 3. Additional technical contributions In Appendix A, we derive several novel technical results that may be of interest in their own right. In particular, Proposition A.6 and Corollary A.16 extend existing results on reward identifiability to general Borel spaces, and Lemma A.13 derives the first three derivatives of the optimal value function with respect to reward parameters вҖ“ a result that is, to the best of our knowledge, novel even in the tabular setting.
