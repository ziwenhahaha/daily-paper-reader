# Lyapunov-Certified Direct Switching Theory for Q-Learning 

Donghwan Lee Department of Electrical Engineering, Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology (KAIST), Daejeon 34141, South Korea 

donghwan@kaist.ac.kr 

Abstract 

Q-learning is one of the most fundamental algorithms in reinforcement learning. We analyze constant-stepsize Q-learning through a direct stochastic switching system representation. The key ob-servation is that the Bellman maximization error can be represented exactly by a stochastic policy. Therefore, the Q-learning error admits a switched linear conditional-mean recursion with martingale-difference noise. The intrinsic drift rate is the joint spectral radius (JSR) of the direct switching family, which can be strictly smaller than the standard row-sum rate. Using this representation, we derive a finite-time final-iterate bound via a JSR-induced Lyapunov function and then give a computable quadratic-certificate version. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Q-learning [1] is a foundational reinforcement learning (RL) [2] algorithm for solving dis-counted Markov decision processes (MDPs) when the transition kernel is unknown. Classical con-vergence results establish asymptotic convergence under stochastic approximation stepsizes [3]вҖ“ [5]. More recent work has focused on finite-time guarantees for synchronous and asynchronous Q-learning under either independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) or Markovian observation models [6]вҖ“[12]. This paper focuses on the i.i.d. observation model and a constant-stepsize recursion. The same direct stochastic-policy switching idea can be extended to Markovian observation models and 

> arXiv:2604.19569v1 [cs.LG] 21 Apr 2026 2

diminishing stepsizes. We do not pursue these extensions here because our goal is to present the core concept in the clearest possible form. A complementary viewpoint is to regard Q-learning as a discrete-time stochastic switching system [13], [14]. This perspective was developed in [15], [16] and was later used to obtain a final-iterate convergence bound for constant-stepsize Q-learning under an i.i.d. observation model [17]. In that formulation, the Q-learning error is represented as a stochastic affine switching system [13], [14]. The affine term appears because the greedy policy induced by the current iterate need not be optimal. To handle this term, the previous analysis [15]вҖ“[17] uses upper and lower comparison systems, which leads to an exponential convergence rate, called the row-sum rate in this paper, and an additional comparison-induced transient term. The purpose of this paper is to show that this affine term can be removed directly. The main idea is a stochastic-policy representation of the Bellman maximization error. For any current Q-function, the difference between its induced value function and the optimal value function lies, state by state, in the convex hull of the corresponding action-wise Q-errors. Therefore, this value-function error can be represented exactly by averaging the Q-error under a suitable stochastic policy. Substituting this representation into the i.i.d. Q-learning recursion gives a direct switched linear description of the conditional mean error without the affine term. The martingale-difference noise remains the same as in the original stochastic recursion, but the deterministic drift no longer contains an affine term. This direct representation is the central object of the paper. The direct representation also identifies the relevant exponential rate. Instead of being tied to the standard row-sum convergence rate, the deterministic drift is governed by the joint spectral radius (JSR) of the direct switching family [18], [19]. We denote this direct JSR rate by ПҒdir  

> Оұ

.The row-sum rate remains a valid upper bound, but it can be conservative when the sampling distribution is nonuniform or when the transition kernel mixes the state-action coordinates in a favorable way. The first contribution of this paper is a finite-time final-iterate bound based directly on the direct JSR. For any certified rate above ПҒdir

The second contribution is a computable quadratic-certificate version of the direct switching bound. When the direct switching family admits a common quadratic Lyapunov function (CQLF) 3

certificate, the proof becomes simpler because the martingale cross term vanishes in the quadratic expansion. The resulting final-iterate bound has a clean exponential transient governed by the certified direct switching rate and a constant-stepsize noise floor of the usual order. II. R ELATED WORK 

Finite-time analysis of Q-learning has a long history. Early bounds were obtained for indirect algorithms and Q-learning variants [6], [7], while later works developed sharper guarantees under synchronous, asynchronous, constant-stepsize, and Markovian sampling settings [8]вҖ“[12]. These analyses typically exploit the contraction structure of the Bellman operator or stochastic approximation arguments. The switching-system approach to RL was introduced in [15] and further developed in [16]. The final-iterate bound in [17] is particularly relevant to the present paper. It studies constant-stepsize i.i.d. Q-learning and obtains a final-iterate bound using upper and lower comparison systems. The present work keeps the same i.i.d. observation model but replaces the comparison-system argument with a direct stochastic policy switching representation of the original error dynamics. The exact stochastic policy representation used here is closely related to switching system and geometric analyses of value iteration and Q-value iteration [20], [21]. In those settings, the Bellman maximization can be absorbed into a stochastic policy, yielding a switched linear representation of the error. Our contribution is to apply this representation to the conditional mean drift of asynchronous i.i.d. Q-learning and to use it to derive finite-time stochastic final-iterate bounds. The mathematical tools used in this paper are connected to the stability theory of switched systems [13], [14] and JSR analysis [18], [19]. CQLFs give computable sufficient certificates for exponential stability, but they are known to be conservative in general. Approximation and complexity results for Lyapunov certificates and the JSR can be found in [22]вҖ“[24]. These results motivate our distinction between quadratic certificates, JSR-induced Lyapunov functions, and weighted norm certificates. 4

B. Switching-system viewpoint 

We recall the switching-system viewpoint used in [17]. A discrete-time switched affine system has the form 

xk+1 = AПғk xk + bПғk + Оҫk,

where Пғk вҲҲ { 1, 2, . . . , M } is a switching signal, AПғk is selected from a prescribed family of matrices H := {A1, A 2, . . . , A M }, bПғk is a mode-dependent affine term, and Оҫk is a disturbance or martingale noise. When bПғk = 0 , the deterministic part reduces to a switched linear system, 

xk+1 = AПғk xk.

The worst-case exponential rate of the switched linear family is characterized by the JSR [18], [19]. This perspective is useful for Q-learning because the greedy policy induced by the current iterate naturally plays the role of the switching signal. In the final-iterate analysis of [17], constant-stepsize Q-learning under an independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) observation model is represented as a stochastic affine switching system. The affine term arises from the fact that the greedy policy associated with Qk need not be an optimal policy. The present paper starts from the same switching-system viewpoint, but later replaces this affine representation by a direct switched linear representation obtained through a stochastic-policy linearization of the Bellman maximization error.
