# ABSTRACT 

We present an approach for enhancing non-playable characters (NPCs) in games by combining large language models (LLMs) with computer vision to provide contextual awareness of their surroundings. Conventional NPCs typically rely on pre-scripted dialogue and lack spatial understanding, which limits their responsiveness to player actions and reduces overall immersion. Our method addresses these limitations by capturing panoramic images of an NPC’s environment and applying semantic segmentation to identify objects and their spatial positions. The extracted information is used to generate a structured JSON representation of the environment, combining object locations derived from segmentation with additional scene graph data within the NPC’s bounding sphere, encoded as directional vectors. This representation is provided as input to the LLM, enabling NPCs to incorporate spatial knowledge into player interactions. As a result, NPCs can dynamically reference nearby objects, landmarks, and environmental features, leading to more believable and engaging gameplay. We describe the technical implementation of the system and evaluate it in two stages. First, an expert interview was conducted to gather feedback and identify areas for improvement. After integrating these refinements, a user study was performed, showing that participants preferred the context-aware NPCs over a non-context-aware baseline, confirming the effectiveness of the proposed approach. 

Keywords Context-Aware NPCs · Immersive Gameplay · Environmental Perception · Semantic Perception 

# 1 Introduction 

Well designed non-playable characters (NPCs) in video games play a central role in player engagement and narrative immersion. These characters make immersion deeper and the virtual worlds more believable by deepening the storyline and providing players with a sense of agency through interactive dialogue. However, despite remarkable advancements in game technology, game design, and artificial inteligence (AI) for games, NPC interactions frequently remain constrained by pre-scripted dialogue trees and predictable behavior patterns. Such static systems, while functional in specific contexts, fail to fully leverage the dynamic and richly detailed environments that modern game worlds now offer. This disconnect between NPCs and their surroundings often disrupts immersion. For instance, players may encounter NPCs oblivious to environmental changes, such as weather, dynamic events, or even the consequences of the player’s actions. These shortcomings can make the game world feel artificial and, in some cases, diminish the overall gaming experience Isbister [2022]. While NPC AI has undergone notable progress—particularly in terms of adaptive behavior and learning systems that respond to player actions Laird and VanLent [2001], Laird [2001]—most NPCs still lack the ability to interpret and integrate real-time environmental context into their interactions. This limitation underscores a significant gap in the development of interactive and immersive game worlds.

The increasing complexity of game environments makes this challenge even harder. Modern games now feature open worlds teeming with dynamic elements such as real-time weather systems, detailed object physics, and non-linear storylines that unfold differently depending on player decisions. These advancements set the stage for heightened player expectations: NPCs should no longer function merely as reactive agents tied to scripted scenarios but as proactive entities capable of perceiving, understanding, and responding to the environment in ways that enhance realism and narrative cohesion. For instance, an NPC that comments on a nearby event, such as the presence of a strange object in a village or the arrival of reinforcements during a battle, adds depth to the game world and makes the interaction feel meaningful. This evolution toward contextually aware NPCs aligns with broader trends in AI and natural language processing. The emergence of large language models (LLMs) offers new opportunities for transforming NPC dialogue systems. Unlike traditional rule-based methods, LLMs excel at generating human-like responses and can be adapted to incorporate a wide range of contextual inputs, including semantic information about the game environment Cox and Ooi [2023]. However, realizing the potential of these models requires novel approaches to bridge the gap between visual data and textual reasoning. Panoramic images, for example, can capture the richness and spatial dynamics of a NPC’s surroundings, providing an essential input for generating context-aware dialogue. By processing these images through semantic segmentation and object recognition techniques, it becomes possible to distill actionable insights about the environment into a format that LLMs can interpret and use. This paper argues that empowering NPCs with environmental context is not merely an incremental improvement but a paradigm shift in game design. It enables a new class of NPCs capable of engaging players on a deeper level by dynamically reflecting the world around them. Such capabilities have implications beyond games, offering insights into human-computer interaction, virtual training simulations, and the development of immersive storytelling experiences. By integrating LLMs with panoramic imaging and advanced computer vision techniques, we present a framework that bridges the gap between static dialogue systems and the next generation of contextually aware NPCs. In this paper, we propose a novel system that enhances the contextual awareness of NPCs, offering the following key contributions: • Adding environmental awareness to NPCs through panoramic image analysis: We utilize semantic segmentation on panoramic images to extract environmental data, translating complex visual inputs into structured information that can be effectively processed and understood by LLM. • Development of context-sensitive NPCs: By integrating environmental context, our system enables NPCs to reference nearby objects, landmarks, and spatial relationships during their interactions with players. This approach introduces dynamic, environment-aware conversations that surpass traditional static dialogue trees or basic LLM-based chatbot behaviors. • Enhancing realism in NPC behavior: Our work bridges the gap between the realism of game worlds and the responsiveness of NPC interactions, empowering NPCs to “perceive” and interpret their surroundings. This capability lays the groundwork for more immersive, contextually relevant dialogue systems in gaming and beyond. In the remainder of the paper, we first present related work in Section 2, followed by the presentation of our method in Section 3. Next, we present the results in Section 4 and discussion in Section 5. In Section 6, we present the conclusions and the possible future work. 

# 2 Related Work 

Enhancing NPCs with more realistic, adaptive, and believable behavior has long been a central topic in game AI and interactive narrative research. While significant progress has been made in adaptive behavior modeling, procedural dialogue, and narrative generation, relatively few approaches incorporate real-time perception of the game environment into NPC reasoning and interaction. This section reviews relevant research on NPC dialogue systems, adaptive game AI, environmental perception, context-aware interaction, and recent uses of LLMs and Computer Vision (CV) in games, situating our work within these domains. 

2.1 NPC Dialogue Systems 

Traditional NPC dialogue systems rely predominantly on pre-defined branching dialogue trees, offering limited flexibility and variation based on player choices. Early work in computational narrative, such as the story understanding model by Black and Wilensky Black and Bower [1980], focused on dynamic story generation but remained purely narrative-driven, with no awareness of the physical game environment. 2Empowering NPCs with Environmental Context Using LLMs TECHNICAL REPORT 

A major step toward more dynamic interaction was Façade , developed by Mateas and Stern Mateas and Stern [2003], which enabled NPCs to respond to player actions within an emergent narrative framework. Despite this advancement, the NPCs remained bound to narrative logic and internal state, lacking any explicit perception or understanding of the surrounding environment. Feng et al. Feng and Tan [2016] further explored autonomous behavior learning for NPCs, yet environmental cues were not incorporated into the decision-making process. Feng Feng [2014] presents a comprehensive investigation into NPC behavior modeling through imitative learning, online adaptation, and affective modeling. His work examines how NPCs can learn behavior patterns from players, adapt through interaction and feedback in dynamic settings, and integrate emotion to create more believable characters. While this research substantially advances autonomy and affective behavior, the proposed models operate primarily on internal state and interaction feedback, without explicitly incorporating perception or structured representations of the surrounding game environment. Basart Basart i Bosch [2022] proposes a narrative-centric approach emphasizing thematic coherence and responsiveness over traditional Aristotelian narrative structures. Although this approach dynamically adapts narratives to player input, environmental perception remains outside its scope. 

2.2 Adaptive NPCs and Game AI 

Adaptive game AI has investigated methods enabling NPCs to adjust behavior based on player actions and internal states. Laird’s anticipatory NPCs Laird [2001] demonstrated how characters could predict player behavior and adapt accordingly, marking an important milestone in adaptive behavior design. However, adaptation was driven by internal models rather than perception of the physical game world. Yannakakis and Togelius Yannakakis and Togelius [2011] explored experience-driven procedural content generation (PCG), allowing game environments and NPC behavior to co-evolve based on player interaction. Similarly, search-based PCG approaches Togelius et al. [2010] generate environments tailored to player preferences. While these methods enhance adaptivity and variability, they do not enable NPCs to explicitly perceive or reason about their surroundings during interaction. More recently, Csepregi Csepregi [2021] demonstrated that context-aware LLM-based NPCs can improve player engagement through more dynamic dialogue. Vidrih and Mayahi Vidrih and Mayahi [2023] highlight similar benefits of generative AI in creating personalized experiences outside gaming. These works underline the potential of LLMs but primarily focus on dialogue generation rather than grounding interactions in real-time environmental perception. 

2.3 Environmental Awareness in Game AI 

Environmental perception has been extensively studied in robotics and autonomous systems. Thrun et al. Thrun et al. [2006] demonstrated how real-time sensor data enables autonomous navigation in complex environments. Despite their success, such perception techniques have been only sparsely applied to NPCs in games, particularly for conversational interaction. Advances in CV have enabled fine-grained scene understanding. Long et al. Long et al. [2015] introduced fully convolutional networks for semantic segmentation, allowing pixel-level scene interpretation. Kirillov et al. Kirillov et al. [2019] further introduced panoptic segmentation, combining semantic and instance segmentation to capture object identities and spatial layout. These developments provide a foundation for enabling NPCs to visually perceive their surroundings. Work on spatial reasoning in navigation, such as Marza et al. Marza et al. [2022], shows that reinforcement-learning agents equipped with auxiliary perception tasks can develop strong spatial understanding without explicit maps. This emphasis on perception and spatial reasoning aligns closely with our goal of enabling NPCs to interpret and reference their environment during interaction. 

2.4 Context-Aware NPC Interaction 

Context-aware computing was introduced by Schilit et al. Schilit et al. [1994] as a paradigm in which systems adapt behavior based on situational context, such as location or nearby objects. While widely adopted in mobile and ubiquitous computing, its application to NPCs interaction remains limited. Most game NPC systems still lack mechanisms for grounding dialogue in explicit environmental context. Our work addresses this gap by integrating panoramic vision and semantic segmentation to provide NPCs with structured, real-time representations of their surroundings, enabling spatially grounded interaction. 3Empowering NPCs with Environmental Context Using LLMs TECHNICAL REPORT 

2.5 Use of LLMs in Games and Game Development 

The rapid adoption of LLMs has significantly influenced game development and game AI. Gallotta et al. Gallotta et al. [2024] present a comprehensive survey and research roadmap for LLMs in games, covering narrative generation, player modeling, mixed-initiative design, and tooling. Sweetser Sweetser [2024] provides a preliminary scoping review highlighting emerging design patterns and open challenges related to agency, authorship, and ethics. Yang et al. Yang et al. [2025] offer an updated scoping review focused on GPT-based models between 2020 and 2024, identifying strong emphasis on dialogue and prototyping, but limited exploration of grounding LLMs in real-time game-world perception. Beyond surveys, individual applications span game generation Hu et al. [2024], procedural narrative Buongiorno et al. [2024], multiplayer role-playing dialogue Koomen [2023], emergent storytelling Peng et al. [2024], user perception of generated dialogue Akoury et al. [2023], and collaborative gameplay with LLM-driven NPCs Rao et al. [2024]. While these approaches integrate LLMs into gameplay or development pipelines, they largely treat the game world symbolically or implicitly, without explicit environmental grounding. 

2.6 Use of Computer Vision Approaches in Games 

Game engines have been used both as platforms for CV research, such as UnrealCV Qiu and Yuille [2016], and as sources of synthetic training data Krähenbühl [2018]. Conversely, CV techniques have been employed to augment gameplay, including visual perception systems for NPCs Mahmoud et al. [2014], behavior analysis Fink et al. [2007], and automated control or cheating Nhu et al. [2023]. Erdelyi Erdelyi [2019] explored the use of CV for playing existing games. Despite this diversity of applications, the use of CV for improving NPCs environmental awareness in conversational contexts remains largely unexplored. 

2.7 Image Segmentation 

A key enabling technology for our work is the Segment Anything Model (SAM) Kirillov et al. [2023], developed by Meta. SAM enables zero-shot segmentation of unseen objects and is supported by one of the largest segmentation datasets to date. Its flexibility has led to numerous derivative tools Xiahao Xu [2024]. We evaluated several SAM-based approaches, including Semantic Segment Anything Chen et al. [2023] and the Label Anything Pipeline Yu et al. [2023]. Ultimately, we selected the Recognize Anything Model (RAM) Zhang et al. [2023], which supports open-set recognition with high accuracy and efficient inference. RAM builds upon earlier SAM-derived systems such as Grounded SAM Ren et al. [2024], Tag2Text Huang et al. [2023], and BLIP Li et al. [2022]. Its ability to generate reliable object annotations makes it well suited for extracting structured environmental context to support NPCs interaction. 

# 3 Method 

The proposed approach was developed within the Unreal Engine 5 (UE5) 1 developed by Epic Games Inc. with custom-built Blueprints and methods and is built on top of a LLM-based NPC, which has the context of its story defined either during training or with a Supporting Prompt defining its context. Such NPC can be placed anywhere in the game Scene 

but does not have any information on where it is and what is within its vicinity. During the game-building process, our plugin is initiated to provide the NPC with information about its surroundings using the data obtained with semantic segmentation of panoramic image captured at its location, a list of objects inside a bounding sphere with their directions, and a supporting prompt guiding the LLM to stay focused on the given job. 3.3 Panoramic Image Capture 

In order to generate information used as inputs for the LLM-based NPC, we first need to gather data from the scene where the NPC is located. The first step is image capturing. We put a camera at eye-level of the NPC which takes a panoramic image composed of four images, each covering 90 ◦ of its view ( front , left , right , and behind ). We make sure the capturing process ignores objects and characters it does not or should not see ( e.g., portions of NPC’s own body). The obtained images are then fed as inputs for the next step: semantic object segmentation. An example of such an image set is shown in Figure 2. 

3.4 Semantic Object Segmentation using RAM++ 

The images from the previous step are fed into the modified Recognize Anything Model (RAM++) Zhang et al. [2023]. RAM++ is an open-set image tagging model effectively leveraging multi-grained text supervision and is used in the key stage of our method. We use four separate images instead of one continuous panoramic image to improve model performance since the model was trained with limited field-of-view images and not panoramic ones. We exploited this to include simple directions based on camera direction for the generated object tags. Generated tags were formatted in a JSON file format, with the tags being grouped into arrays left , right , front , and behind . The model is executed through a single Python script from within the UE5. It needs a pre-trained dataset accessible locally. An example of the resulting JSON file is presented below.     

> {"left": ["cabinet", "pottery", "closet", ...], "in-front": ["barrel", "basement", ...], "right": ["altar", "basement", "candle", ...], "behind": ["altar", "candle", ...] }

3.5 Radial Object Selection 

Concurrently to System initialization step, the system provides another flavor of context information from within the engine. We achieve this by using a component bounding sphere, which returns any object the sphere collides with within a given radius. The radius was empirically set to 10 meters. This stage is usable only when proper asset naming regulations exist within the game development environment. If the names of objects are meaningless, it is better to omit this step in the method. However, when objects are given proper names (or tags), this method can prove helpful, as it provides more precise directional data for each object and a better sense for plurality in the case of more than one instance of the same object. The directional vectors point to the object from the position of the NPC. An example of some of the data generated by this method is presented below.

We try to provide a cohesive, immersive, and relatively detailed conversation experience. # 5 Discussion 

We evaluated the results twice, first with an interview with an expert, and once we addressed the concerns he pointed out, and then evaluated the system also with a comparative user study. 

5.1 Expert Evaluation of the System 

The expert was from the game technology field with more than 10 years of experience. In an interview, we went through the structure of the system and explained how individual stages are implemented, what is the intended use of the system and how it can be integrated into the existing game development pipeline. The expert agreed that the system is meaningful, as is its implementation. He also supported our design decisions and system structure. Next, we went through the control test inputs and outputs and agreed that they are meaningful and that the system works adequately well. The augmentations of objects’ backstories are believable and make sense. The described object locations reflected their locations in the scene. He mentioned that the responses could clearly benefit from more detailed object descriptions which would contain more than just names, but he agreed that that would increase the prompt sizes considerably, making the response times longer. Also, the preprocessing would be considerably harder; either the user (game developer) would need to add those object descriptions manually, or an adequate object recognition model should be used that would also return the object properties, which would again be more resource-demanding. Next, we went through the results of Test 2 . The expert identified that the system could benefit from additional relational data between the objects. In the input, only the general directions of the objects are defined (in front, left, right, and behind), but the system does not know where one object that is in a specific direction ( e.g., left) is in relation to the other objects in the same direction. To some degree, this is solved within our system if you use the Radial Object Selection 

stage. There, the system could get the relations between objects from directional vectors. However, this information does not include precise the depth due to the distance to object origin only. This is one of the aspects we want to address in the future. In the responses of Test 3 , where only the Supporting Prompt is used, there are many mix-ups and hallucinations from the LLM. A system that would only contain such a stage is hardly usable for games with graphics but could still be used in text-based adventure games. In such cases, the memory concept would need to be implemented so that the system would not make up things from one step to the next. We still included this in the user study for validation. By examining the responses from Test 4 , where Supporting Prompt and Radial Object Selection data are available to the system, one can see that there are several mix-ups since the object descriptors in the scene graph are not always adequate. The expert commented that even though the idea of directional vectors is good, the system might work better if the spatial directions were quantized into discrete world directions. Supporting Prompt only, performs the worst. During the interview, we also identified a few system limitations, such as detailed object descriptions and a lack of spatial relations between the objects.

The participants of the user study were mostly young people in their 20s who, on average, obtained a Bachelor’s Degree in Computer Science. While there was more negative feedback in freeform feedback, the results, on average, were favorable. The limitations identified from the user feedback were in the length and detail of answers. Most participants would prefer shorter answers. # 6 Conclusion 

The presented LLM-based NPC system leverages the automatically extracted information of surroundings in the scene from the rendered view and scene graph and uses a Supporting Prompt for initializing an NPC at a specific location in the game. The results of the user study and the interview with the expert provided us with several directions to pursue as future directions for this research. Some of these include looking into how much memory context is lost during the conversations, the effect of spatial direction quantization on performance, bringing object depth information into the system with object depth rendering, and implementing an octree to see if it could be used to improve the acquisition of spatial relations between objects in the scene. We believe that the system presents a good contribution to deep-learning-supported game development where existing technologies can be repurposed for novel tasks.
