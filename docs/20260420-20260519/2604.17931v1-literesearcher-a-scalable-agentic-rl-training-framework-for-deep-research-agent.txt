## Abstract 

Reinforcement Learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful training paradigm for LLM-based agents. However, scaling agentic RL for deep research re-mains constrained by two coupled challenges: hand-crafted synthetic data fails to elicit genuine real-world search capabilities, and real-world search dependency during RL training introduces instability and prohibitive cost, which limits the scalability of Agentic RL. LiteResearcher is a training framework that makes Agentic RL scalable: by constructing a lite virtual world that mirrors real-world search dynamics, we enable a continuously improving training recipe that empowers a tiny search agent to outperform large-scale open-source and commercial models (e.g., Tongyi DeepResearch and Claude-4.5 Sonnet). ## 1 Introduction 

Recent advances in reinforcement learning (RL) on reasoning models (Liu et al., 2025b; Guo et al., 2025; Team et al., 2025a) have demonstrated that continuously scaling model capabilities is achievable within stable training environments and with data of appropriate difficulty. However, when transitioning to agentic RL, where reasoning must be integrated with tool use, prior efforts have struggled to achieve similar sustained improvements. We identify the primary bottleneck as the inherent noise and instability of the real-world environment. In the domain of Coding Agents (Wei et al., 2025b; Jain et al., 2025), this challenge has been effectively addressed through the use of isolated sandboxes configured to simulate production environments while filtering out infrastructure-level noise, thereby enabling scalable training. However, existing works in the domain of Deep Research have yet to realize such scalable RL training. Online RL frameworks (Zheng et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025d) interact directly with the live internet, introducing significant training variance and prohibitive costs that limit continuous capability growth. Local retrieval-based systems (Jin et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025c; Sun et al., 2025) are typically confined to a narrow corpus, making it impossible to replicate the complex search dynamics of the diverse internet. Meanwhile, current real-web graph-based data synthesis methods (Jin et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a; Gao et al., 2025) rely on over-engineered reasoning structures, focusing on handcrafted logic while ignoring the diversity of atomic search techniques required for deep research, such as cross-verification and enumeration. We argue that scaling agentic RL for Deep Research requires a virtual world that mirrors the structure of the live internet but is isolated in execution: the twin architecture ensures that policies optimized in the virtual environment can generalize to the open web, while isolation shields the training process from the high variance and stochasticity of environmental noise. In this paper, we present LiteResearcher , an Agentic RL training framework designed to construct a virtual world that mirrors real-world web architecture . We follow these steps to realize the scaling potential of agentic RL: (1) Synthesize real search data; (2) Simulate search environment; (3) Achieve sustained improvements through curriculum RL. The foundation of this virtual world is generating realistic training tasks. To capture the diversity of information seeking in real-world user queries, we prioritize the scale of information sources in our virtual world. We find that by scaling up information sources, even a simple-but-effective data synthesis can yield a realistic distribution of training tasks. Crucially, handling these synthetic tasks requires a corresponding execution environment. We simulate a realistic search environment by enriching our corpus with these tasks. For every synthetic task, we perform a targeted expansion of the local webpage corpus by searching and fetching related real-world webpages, which then construct the local search and browse tool. This ensures that for any given training task, the local search and browse tools provide search dynamics analogous to the open web, allowing the agent to obtain real-web atom capabilities in a controlled environment. Even with synthetic tasks and tools, training can still saturate if the model gets trapped at a certain difficulty threshold, correctly solving easy tasks while completely failing complex ones. We apply a difficulty-aware curriculum RL which progressively enhances both task difficulty and context length across training stages. At each stage, we only keep the synthetic tasks that the current model can partially solve, discarding both trivially easy and impossibly hard instances, thereby providing consistent training signal for improvement. Our tiny search agent LiteResearcher-4B achieves 71.3% on GAIA (Mialon et al., 2023) and 78.0% on Xbench (Chen et al., 2025), surpassing open-source agents up to 8 × larger such as Tongyi DeepResearch 30B (70.9%/75.0%) and WebSailor 30B (53.2%/53.3%), while matching or exceeding proprietary systems including Claude-4.5-Sonnet (71.2%/66.0%) and GLM-4.6 (71.9%/70.0%). These results demonstrate that scalable RL can yield a powerful on-device deep research agent. 2Preprint. Under review. 

## 2 Related Work 

Reinforcement Learning for Reasoning The pursuit of advanced reasoning in LLMs initially relied on inference-time heuristics such as Chain-of-Thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022) and Tree of Thoughts (Yao et al., 2023). A paradigm shift occurred with the demonstration that RL can internalize reasoning capabilities directly into model weights. DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025) showed that RL with verifiable rewards (RLVR) is sufficient to unlock emergent reasoning behaviors such as self-verification and backtracking, and subsequent work has rapidly advanced optimization (Yu et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025b), reward design (Cui et al., 2025), and scaling (Hu et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025), establishing RLVR as a general-purpose post-training paradigm. However, all these methods operate as closed-world reasoners: they rely solely on parametric knowledge and lack the ability to interact with external dynamic environments—a critical limitation for tasks requiring real-time information retrieval. 

Agentic Deep Research Models Recent research has pivoted towards Agentic Deep Research, where agents interact with external search environments through iterative query formulation and page navigation. This line of work can be grouped by environment design. Online real-world RL systems (Zheng et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025d;a) train directly on the live web, offering realistic interactions but incurring high cost and non-deterministic reward signals. Local retrieval-based systems (Jin et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025c; Gao et al., 2025) construct environments from Wikipedia or similar corpora, providing fast, deterministic interactions but remaining confined to narrow, homogeneous domains. Simulated systems such as ZeroSearch (Sun et al., 2025) replace search engines by training an LLM to mimic search engine outputs, but sacrifice page-level fidelity. Recent lightweight explorations such as AgentCPM-Explore (Chen et al., 2026) further show the capability of small deep-research agents, but still fail to realize scalable RL training. In contrast, our framework builds a local environment with real-world data and a search engine, enabling scalable RL training. 

## 3 Preliminaries: Agentic Framework 

We model deep research as a sequential decision-making process under the ReAct frame-work (Yao et al., 2022). Given an initial user query q, the agent interacts with the environment over multiple steps by alternately generating reasoning thoughts, executing actions, and receiving observations. The history up to step t is denoted by 

Ht = ( q, τ1, a1, o1, . . . , τt, at, ot),where τi, ai, and oi denote the thought, action, and environmental observation at step i,respectively. At each step, the agent first generates a reasoning thought τt conditioned on the preceding history, and then selects an executable action at conditioned on both Ht−1

and τt:

τt ∼ πθ (· | H t−1), at ∼ πθ (· | H t−1, τt).

Action Space. The action space A consists of two primitive actions: • Search (q′): submits a natural-language query q′ to the search engine and returns a ranked list of retrieved snippets and URLs. • Browse (u, q′): visits a selected URL u and returns a query-conditioned summary of the page content. After collecting sufficient information, the agent terminates environment interaction and generates a final response to the original user query q.

To construct a dataset with both capability diversity and scalability , rather than relying on complex, handcrafted generation logic, we first scale up the underlying information sources to comprehensively cover the diverse search patterns of the real world. Subsequently, we filter this vast information pool into solvable, high-quality tasks. Specifically: 

QA generation from Seed Corpus The data construction process begins with a high-quality Seed Corpus including Wikipedia and BBC News articles. We then prompt an LLM with the template in Appendix A.1 to process this raw content and extract structured question-answer (QA) pairs that target key factual entities within the content. These ini-tial QA pairs serve as the foundational “seed tasks” that trigger the subsequent iterative expansion of the local environment. 

Information Source Masking To prevent the agent from relying on trivial shortcuts, we implement a source masking mechanism. After a QA pair is generated, we deliberately delete its original information source from the local corpus. This forces the agent to navigate the expanded corpus to discover alternative, non-trivial search pathways, naturally eliciting the complex atomic search capabilities detailed in Table 1. 

Strict Quality and Difficulty Filtering To guarantee the quality and solvability of the dataset, the candidate QA pairs undergo a rigorous LLM-based evaluation guided by a 7-point rubric (Appendix A.2). As detailed in Appendix A.2, this rubric contains dimensions such as independence, ambiguity, answer verifiability, etc. A data point is retained only if it strictly satisfies all seven criteria. Furthermore, prior to each training session, we apply an additional difficulty filter to ensure the tasks are appropriately calibrated to the agent’s 5Preprint. Under review. current capability (see Section 4.3). This final distillation process provides a diverse and realistic data distribution for subsequent training. 

Continuously Expanding Corpus For each filtered QA pair, we utilize a search engine to fetch related webpages from the live internet. These newly retrieved pages are continuously appended to the Enriched Corpus . This iterative expansion creates a corpus covering the vast and diverse information distribution of the real world. 

4.2 Stable Local Tool Environment 

Leveraging the enriched corpus from Section 4.1 ( ∼32M pages, 1M+ domains), we build a fully local tool environment that replaces online interaction during RL training. Unlike standard RAG pipelines that split each page into many chunks, we adopt page-level indexing : each webpage is represented by a single vector (title + summary). This keeps the index small enough to serve hundreds of concurrent rollouts at low latency, which would be infeasible with a chunk-level index that is ∼10 × larger. During training, the system sustains hundreds of concurrent tool requests, accumulating to 73.2M calls over the full RL run (Section 5). The corpus is served through two purpose-built services (infrastructure details in Appendix D): • Local Search Engine : We adopt BGE-M3 (Chen et al., 2024) for hybrid retrieval, which produces both dense and learned sparse embeddings in a single forward pass. The two signals are fused at query time through the built-in hybrid search of Milvus (Wang et al., 2021). All embeddings are stored on disk and accessed via DiskANN (Jayaram Subra-manya et al., 2019) with mmap, achieving ∼0.15 s/query ( ∼10 × faster than online search engine). • Local Browse Tool : Full webpage content is stored as Markdown in PostgreSQL, keyed by URL and tuned for 1,000 concurrent connections; pages are returned directly upon request at ∼0.17 s/page ( ∼46 × faster than Jina Reader). 

4.3 Difficulty-Aware Curriculum Learning 

A fundamental challenge in Agentic RL is training saturation, where the reward curve converges because the model becomes trapped at a difficulty threshold, correctly solving easy tasks while completely failing complex ones, resulting in no meaningful learning gradient. 

Difficulty-Aware Data Filtering To address training saturation, before each RL stage, we evaluate the difficulty of training queries. For each query, we generate K = 8 rollouts (pass@8) and strictly retain only those where the number of correct responses c satisfies 1 ≤ c ≤ 7. Queries with c = 8 are discarded as trivial (yielding zero useful gradient), while those with c = 0 are deemed impossible or excessively noisy.

## 5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate LiteResearcher-4B on a suite of complex reasoning and agentic search bench-marks, including GAIA (Mialon et al., 2023), BrowseComp (Wei et al., 2025a), HLE (Phan et al., 2025), Frames (Krishna et al., 2025), WebWalker (Wu et al., 2025b), Seal-0 (Pham et al., 2026), and Xbench-DeepSearch-2505 (Chen et al., 2025). For BrowseComp, we randomly sampled 400 examples for evaluation. Evaluation is conducted with online APIs (Serper for search and Jina for browsing), following the same tool setup as prior work (Team et al., 2025c). We compare against leading closed-source foundation models, representative open-source deep-research agents, and AgentCPM-Explore (Chen et al., 2026) as a concurrent lightweight baseline. All experiments are initialized from Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507 (Yang et al., 2025). We first perform a cold-start SFT stage to teach the model basic tool-use capabilities such as query formulation, search invocation, and page browsing (details in Appendix B). The RL stage generates rollouts entirely within our local environment, with a global batch size of 128 (on-policy update), generating 8 rollouts per query. The learning rate is fixed at 1 × 10 −6.The maximum response length is initialized at 32K tokens in the first stage and expanded by 16K tokens in each subsequent stage. Our loss function uses neither a KL divergence penalty nor an entropy regularization term.

A 4B model trained locally approaches commercial-level performance. These results suggest that the data–environment bottleneck is at least as important as model scale for strong agentic search performance. 

Local training matches or exceeds online training. AgentCPM-Explore-4B (Chen et al., 2026), a concurrent 4B model trained on the live internet, is outperformed by LiteResearcher on all 8 benchmarks (e.g., GAIA 71.3% vs. 63.9%, Xbench 78.0% vs. 70.0%). AgentCPM-Explore further reports that online environment instability is a major source of reward noise, limiting its RL gains to only +3.8% over the SFT baseline on GAIA. Our deterministic local environment eliminates such noise, sustaining over 700 stable RL steps with monotonic improvements. 

Context length and BrowseComp. BrowseComp requires exceptionally deep browsing chains, often exceeding 20 pages per query. For a 4B model, this frequently exhausts the 128K context window, causing performance degradation (20.3%/28.4% without memory, Table 9). A memory mechanism that summarizes prior tool interactions at 64K mitigates this, improving results to 27.5%/32.5%. This gap highlights long-context utilization as a key limitation of small-scale agents, and we report both configurations for transparency.

## 6 Conclusions 

In this paper, we presented LiteResearcher , an agentic RL training framework that resolves the critical bottlenecks of data diversity and environment stability by constructing an isolated virtual world. Crucially, our work yields three key insights for the future of deep research agents. First, we demonstrate that Agentic RL can drive sustained capability improvement . The training saturation observed in prior works is due to reliance on high-variance live environments or a narrow local corpus, rather than a limitation of the RL paradigm itself. Second, we reveal that a simple but scalable synthetic data pipeline ,driven by the continuous expansion of real-world information sources, is significantly more effective than over-engineered, handcrafted reasoning structures. Finally, trained entirely locally at zero marginal cost, our compact LiteResearcher-4B achieves 71.3% on GAIA and 78.0% on Xbench-DS. By matching or exceeding commercial models, our results suggest that scalable RL training, beyond model scale alone, is a critical enabler for strong deep research capabilities . A.3 Multi-hop QA Synthesis 

In addition to the single-hop QA extraction pipeline described in Section 4.1, we employ a separate backward graph-based multi-hop QA synthesis method. The core idea is to first build a knowledge graph grounded in real web evidence, then sample a connected subgraph, and finally generate a question backward from the graph structure so that answer-ing it naturally requires multi-hop reasoning along the graph’s edges. The full pipeline is summarized in Table 5. 

Phase Step Operation 1. Knowledge Graph ( ≤ Nmax ) Input Seed entity e0; hyperparams Nmax = 8, Kfeat = 2, Kent = 2

Search Query Serper API for entity ei; LLM selects reliable sources; crawl pages 

Extract LLM extracts Kfeat factual features per entity from crawled evidence 

Discover LLM identifies ≤ Kent new concrete entities + directed relations; exclude generic concepts (e.g., “Programs of Study”) and media sources (e.g., “Wikipedia”) 

Expand Add new entities to graph G; repeat Search →Discover until |V| ≥ Nmax 

> 2. Subgraph Sampling

Init Pick random node v0 ∈ G as BFS root 

Grow Greedily add candidate with most edges to subgraph + perturbation Uniform (0, 0.5 )

Format Anonymize entity IDs; truncate to ≤3 features per entity; output 6-node subgraph 

> 3. Backward QA Gen

Select Strong LLM picks a target entity as the answer 

Constrain Convert each edge → relationship constraint with vague references (e.g., “a late antique writer”) 

Augment Optionally add minimal entity constraints from features (only if needed for uniqueness) 

Compose Integrate all constraints into a natural-language multi-hop question 

Output QA pair requiring 3–5 hop reasoning; information-minimal design prevents trivial solving 

Table 5: Multi-hop QA synthesis pipeline. Three phases: (1) iteratively build a knowledge graph from web evidence, (2) sample a connectivity-biased subgraph, (3) generate a back-ward question that requires traversing the subgraph to answer.

C.4 Trajectory Behavior During RL 

After SFT, the model acquires the basic search workflow and achieves a clear performance gain over the base model. However, we observe that among the incorrectly answered queries, a dominant failure mode is repetitive action loops —the model repeatedly issues the same search query or revisits the same URL without making progress, consuming the token budget on redundant interactions rather than exploring alternative strategies. These repetitive trajectories inflate both the response length and the number of turns without contributing useful information. Figure 7 tracks four metrics during RL training that jointly show how the model eliminates this failure mode while improving task performance. The mean reward steadily increases from ∼0.42 to ∼0.70 over 420 steps (Figure 7a), confirm-ing that the agent solves progressively more queries correctly. The remaining three metrics 21 Preprint. Under review. reveal the mechanism behind this improvement: as RL suppresses the repetitive-action loops, the mean response length drops from ∼18K to ∼12K tokens (Figure 7b), the mean number of interaction turns decreases from ∼30 to ∼24 (Figure 7c), and the length clip ratio—the fraction of trajectories truncated for exceeding the context window—falls from 

∼0.28 to ∼0.02 (Figure 7d). All three reductions reflect the same underlying cause: the model learns to stop repeating futile actions, resulting in shorter, more purposeful search trajectories.
