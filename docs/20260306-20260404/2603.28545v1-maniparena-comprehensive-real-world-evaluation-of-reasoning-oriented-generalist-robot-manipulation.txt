Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models and world models have recently emerged as promising paradigms for general-purpose robotic intelligence, yet their progress is hindered by the lack of reliable evaluation protocols that reflect real-world deployment. Existing benchmarks are largely simulator-centric, which provide controllability but fail to capture the reality gap caused by perception noise, complex contact dynamics, hardware constraints, and system latency. Moreover, fragmented real-world evaluations across different robot platforms prevent fair and reproducible comparison. To address these challenges, we introduce ManipArena, a standardized evaluation framework designed to bridge simulation and real-world execution. ManipArena comprises 20 diverse tasks across 10,812 expert trajectories emphasizing reasoning-oriented manipulation tasks requiring semantic and spatial reasoning, supports multi-level generalization through controlled out-of-distribution settings, and incorporates long-horizon mobile manipulation beyond tabletop scenarios. The framework further provides rich sensory diagnostics, including low-level motor signals, and synchronized real-to-sim environments constructed via high-quality 3D scanning. Together, these features enable fair, realistic, and reproducible evaluation for both VLA and world model approaches, providing a scalable foundation for diagnosing and advancing embodied intelligence systems.

# 1 Introduction 

The pursuit of scaling learning and robotics to achieve true generalization has placed Vision-Language-Action (VLA) ( 2, 5, 7) and world models ( 11 ) at the forefront of modern artificial intelligence. As pivotal paradigms for general-purpose robotic intelligence, these models have demonstrated promising results across diverse domains, including manipulation, mobile manipulation, and long-horizon tasks. However, the continued progress of these models critically depends on the availability of reliable and realistic evaluation protocols. Currently, the community faces a significant bottleneck: the lack of a widely adopted, fair, and realistic evaluation framework that can accurately reflect a model’s performance during real-world deployment. The core limitation of existing evaluation paradigms lies in the profound conflict between simulator-centric assessment and real-world capability. Current evaluations are highly concentrated within simulators such as RLBench ( 6), VLABench ( 12 ) and Libero ( 8). While these environments offer essential controllability and reproducibility, they fail to account for the “reality gap” characterized by perception noise, complex contact dynamics, system latency, and hardware constraints. Consequently, success within a simulation environment cannot reliably predict performance in the physical world. Furthermore, current real-world evaluations remain fragmented; researchers often utilize disparate robot platforms and environments, making horizontal comparisons inherently unfair and results difficult to replicate. This diagnostic gap underscores the urgent need for a standardized evaluation protocol that bridges the divide between virtual training and physical execution.

To address these challenges, we introduce ManipArena , an evaluation framework built upon five core design principles to enable standardized, reasoning-heavy, and reproducible assessment for both VLA and world model approaches. 1. Reasoning-Oriented: ManipArena prioritizes reasoning-oriented manipulation by incorporating complex spatial constraints, multi-stage bimanual operations, and semantic understanding that go beyond simple motor skills, requiring models to demonstrate genuine execution reasoning or semantic reasoning rather than trajectory memorization. 2. Multi-Level Generalization: It facilitates controlled multi-level generalization through a green-screen enclosed environment with fixed lighting, systematic diversity guides for data collection, and layered out-of-distribution testing across object materials, appearances, and spatial configurations. 3. Mobile Manipulation: Beyond tabletop tasks, ManipArena incorporates long-horizon mobile manipulation requiring navigation, spatial memory, and sustained whole-body control across an extended workspace, providing comprehensive coverage of real-world operational scenarios. 4. Rich Sensory Diagnostics: The framework provides rich and open sensory signals, including low-level motor currents and joint velocities beyond standard joint states, allowing developers to perform granular force-aware action modeling and diagnostic analysis. 5. Real-to-Sim Synchronization: By utilizing high-quality 3D scanning, we provide synchronized real and simulated environments (Real2Sim), constructing physically consistent simulation counterparts to enable scalable evaluation and sim-to-real diagnostics without sacrificing physical realism. 

# 2 Benchmark Design 

2.1 Competition Format and Evaluation Protocol 

ManipArena adopts a server-side inference architecture that fundamentally differs from existing real-robot benchmarks. Rather than requiring participants to deploy models on specific hardware, participants expose a single HTTP endpoint that accepts observation data (camera images and proprioception) and returns action commands. The organizers’ infrastructure handles all robot control, data collection, and scoring. Crucially, ManipArena enforces a one-model-for-all-tasks rule: each participant submits a single model endpoint that is evaluated across all tasks. This is a deliberate design choice that tests genuine reasoning and generalization ability rather than task-specific overfitting—a participant cannot train 15 separate specialists, but must build one unified policy that handles pouring water, sorting headphones, inserting wires, and pressing buttons alike. This mirrors the real-world desideratum of a general-purpose manipulation system and creates a fundamentally different optimization pressure from benchmarks that allow per-task models. From the 2Figure 1 Representative examples from each task category. 

participant’s perspective, this also simplifies participation : one only needs to train and serve a single model, while the organizers handle evaluation across all tasks on all robots. This architecture offers several additional advantages: 

• Low barrier to entry : No specialized hardware required from participants. 

• Reproducibility : All trials run on identical hardware under identical conditions. 

• Fair comparison : No advantage from hardware optimization or latency. 

• IP protection : Model weights and code are never shared with organizers. Each task is evaluated over 10 trials . Each trial is scored on a 0–10 scale based on sub-task completion, giving a maximum of 100 points per task and a total of 1,500 points across all 15 evaluated tabletop tasks. 

2.2 Task Design 

ManipArena comprises 20 tasks organized into three categories: Execution Reasoning , Semantic Reasoning , and 

Mobile Manipulation . A deliberate design principle is that ManipArena contains no trivial tasks , i.e. , every task requires reasoning, differing only in where the reasoning bottleneck lies. This distinguishes ManipArena from benchmarks that include simple pick-and-place tasks as baselines; here, even the most straightforward tasks demand precise visuomotor integration under object and spatial variation. The overview of the ManipArena benchmark is presented in Figure 3. Tasks were selected to satisfy four criteria: (1) coverage of diverse manipulation primitives (grasping, insertion, pouring, arranging, sorting); (2) coverage of diverse object types (rigid, articulated, deformable, liquid); (3) a clear, unambiguous success criterion amenable to sub-task scoring; and (4) sufficient difficulty that no current model achieves ceiling performance, ensuring the benchmark remains discriminative. > 2.2.1 Execution Reasoning Tasks (10 tasks)

The challenge of this type of tasks lies in how to execute—grasp strategy, force control, and bimanual coordination. These tasks test whether a model can translate a known goal into precise, adaptive motor behavior across varied object instances and spatial configurations.  

> 2.2.2 Semantic Reasoning Tasks (5 tasks)

The challenge of this type of tasks lies in what to do—understanding object categories, attributes, and relations before acting. A model that pattern-matches to training object appearances will fail when presented with novel instances of the same semantic category. These tasks create a clean diagnostic: if a model fails, it is because it cannot resolve the semantic ambiguity, not because it lacks motor skill.  

> 2.2.3 Mobile Manipulation Tasks (5 tasks)

Mobile tasks require long-horizon whole-body control across an extended workspace, demanding navigation, spatial memory, and sustained task execution over 2–3 minutes. They are on average 4.3 × longer than tabletop tasks (2,878 vs. 665 avg frames at 20 fps) and account for 60.6% of total dataset frames despite comprising only 26.7% of trajectories. This length difference makes them particularly challenging for current VLA architectures with fixed context windows. 

2.3 Controlled Evaluation Environment 

A fundamental challenge in real-robot benchmarks is attribution : when a model fails on a new trial, is it because the object changed, the layout changed, or simply because the background lighting shifted? Most existing benchmarks evaluate in open environments where all these factors vary simultaneously, making it impossible to attribute performance differences to specific generalization axes. A second challenge is 

> reproducibility

: real-world lab environments change over time—furniture is moved, lighting conditions shift with weather and time of day, and clutter accumulates on work surfaces—making it difficult to reproduce evaluation conditions weeks or months later, let alone across different sites. ManipArena addresses both challenges by conducting all evaluations inside a green-screen enclosed environ-ment —a self-contained booth with uniform chroma-key walls and ceiling. This design provides three key properties: 4Figure 3 The overall statistics of ManipArena including the average trajectories of each task, the episode length, the sub-task count and other statistics.  

> Variable isolation.

By fixing the background to a uniform surface, we eliminate uncontrolled visual variation. Performance differences across trials can only arise from object-level or spatial-level changes that we explicitly design, transforming the benchmark from a black-box leaderboard into a controlled experiment where generalization is measurable and attributable.  

> Controlled illumination.

The enclosure uses fixed artificial lighting with constant color temperature and intensity, shielding the workspace from external light sources (windows, overhead lab lights, seasonal daylight variation). This eliminates a confound that plagues open-environment benchmarks: apparent performance drops caused not by policy failure but by shadow shifts, specular highlights, or white-balance changes that alter the visual appearance of objects and surfaces.  

> Reproducibility and portability.

Because the booth is self-contained, the entire evaluation environment can be  

> relocated or replicated

without affecting visual conditions. Moving the setup to a different room, building, or institution preserves identical background and lighting—the only requirement is re-placing the task objects according to the diversity guide. This also enables longitudinal consistency : evaluations conducted weeks apart produce visually identical conditions, a property that open labs cannot guarantee. 2.4 Systematic Diversity Design 

Within the controlled visual environment established by the green screen, we systematically inject diversity into the training data so that high scores must come from genuine generalization, not memorization . Unlike benchmarks where training data diversity arises incidentally from natural collection, ManipArena treats diversity as a designed experimental variable . Each task is accompanied by a diversity guide —a structured specification that defines the exact object variants, color sets, spatial configurations, and (for semantic tasks) compositional permutations to be covered. Trained operators collect approximately 500 trajectories per task (10,812 total across 20 tasks, ∼188 hours) following these guides, with each diversity dimension kept approximately uniformly distributed (within 10–15% of equal distribution) to prevent models from exploiting frequency biases. A critical design constraint is the strict separation between training and test distributions : OOD test objects are never present in the training data. For example, put_spoon_to_bowl trains on stainless steel and children’s spoons but tests on a black plastic spoon; sort_headphone trains on wired, over-ear, and bluetooth types but tests on a neckband headphone. This separation ensures that models cannot achieve high OOD scores through memorization—they must genuinely generalize. We organize training diversity into three hierarchical levels, each targeting a different generalization axis. 2.5 Stratified OOD Evaluation Design 

The 10 evaluation trials per task are not randomly configured. They follow a stratified structure with increasing generalization difficulty: trials T1–T4 test in-domain competence with training-distribution objects in varied positions; T5–T8 introduce visual shifts —appearance changes ( e.g ., different shape or color) within the training distribution; and T9–T10 present semantic OOD objects unseen during training. For example, in 

put_spoon_to_bowl , T1–T4 use a stainless steel spoon, T5–T8 use a children’s spoon (different shape), and T9–T10 use a black plastic spoon (new material and color). This structure enables a complete generalization profile from a single evaluation session—one can directly compare a model’s performance across three generalization levels and compute the degradation curve without running separate experiments. 

Semantic distance determines OOD difficulty. Not all OOD is equally difficult. We deliberately include OOD objects at varying semantic distances from the training distribution—from near-OOD ( e.g ., goggles for the glasses task, preserving grasp strategy) to far-OOD ( e.g ., neckband headphones, requiring fundamentally different recognition). This allows us to test the hypothesis that OOD generalization difficulty is determined by semantic distance, not merely by the binary in/out-of-distribution distinction. 

Compounding OOD factors. Several tasks are designed so that OOD trials change multiple factors simultaneously, testing whether generalization degrades gracefully or catastrophically . For example, in sort_headphone ,T7–8 change only the headphone type (bluetooth, still black)—single-factor OOD; T9–10 change both type (neckband) and color (white)—compound OOD. Similarly, in put_spoon_to_bowl , OOD trials change both material (plastic) and color (black) simultaneously. This design enables disentangling the individual and combined effects of OOD factors on model performance (see Section 4 for quantitative analysis). 

Configuration-level generalization. For the remaining 8 tasks where T9–T10 do not introduce new object types, the 10 trials systematically cover the spatial and compositional configuration space within the training distribution. For example, pick_items_into_basket varies basket position (right → center → left) across 8trials, and press_button_in_order permutes color sequences. These trials test whether models are robust to layout and semantic composition changes—a prerequisite for reliable real-world deployment even without OOD objects. Detailed per-trial specifications are provided in Appendix C.  

> Controlled generalization through three pillars.

The green-screen environment, systematic diversity design, and stratified OOD evaluation form an integrated framework for controlled generalization measurement . The green screen eliminates uncontrolled visual confounders, ensuring that performance differences are attributable to the variables we design. The diversity guides ensure that training data covers a sufficiently broad distribution, so that OOD evaluation tests genuine extrapolation rather than interpolation. The stratified trial structure then measures generalization at multiple difficulty levels within a single evaluation session. Together, these three pillars make ManipArena not merely a task collection with a leaderboard, but a controlled experimental platform for diagnosing where and why generalization succeeds or fails.

2.7 Sensor Data and Action Space 

ManipArena provides 56-dimensional state and action per frame for tabletop tasks (62D for mobile tasks), richer than standard LeRobot format. Each 56D vector comprises: end-effector poses (position, rotation, gripper = 7 D × 2 arms = 14 D), joint positions ( 7D × 2 = 14 D), joint velocities ( 7D × 2 = 14 D ), and joint currents ( 7D × 2 = 14 D, where the last dimension of each arm is the gripper current). Mobile tasks add 6 extra dimensions: head rotation (2D), lifting column height (1D), and base velocity in x, y, θ (3D). The full data collection records 112D per frame (follow + master arms); the open-source release provides the follow-arm subset for direct policy training (see Appendix D). The inclusion of motor current and joint velocity data is a deliberate design choice. Motor currents serve as a torque proxy—critical for contact-rich tasks such as pour_water (sensing liquid weight), insert_wireline 

(detecting insertion contact), and arrange_cup (avoiding excessive force). Joint velocities capture dynamic motion profiles that complement static position information. Current VLA and world model baselines do not utilize these signals, and we provide them to encourage research on force-aware and dynamics-aware policies. Three camera views (face, left wrist, right wrist) provide complementary perspectives: the face camera captures the global workspace, while wrist cameras provide close-up views critical for precision tasks. 

2.8 Real2Sim 

To enable scalable offline evaluation and sim-to-real transfer research, we construct digitally twinned simulation environments that are geometrically and visually consistent with the physical evaluation setup (Figure 8).  

> Simulation platform.

The simulation environment is built on IsaacLab and IsaacLab-Arena , providing high-fidelity physics simulation with GPU-accelerated parallel environments. This enables large-scale policy evaluation and reinforcement learning experiments that would be prohibitively expensive on physical hardware.  

> Scene reconstruction.

The physical workspace—including the green-screen enclosure, tables, shelves, and fixtures—is reconstructed using 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) , capturing both geometry and appearance with photorealistic fidelity. This ensures that the visual observations in simulation closely match those from the real cameras. 10 Figure 8 Real2Sim environment construction. The physical workspace is reconstructed via 3D Gaussian Splatting, task objects are generated with Hunyuan3D, and the simulation runs on IsaacLab with trajectory-level alignment to real-robot data.  

> Object generation.

Task objects (cups, spoons, headphones, etc.) are generated using Hunyuan3D , producing high-quality 3D assets with accurate geometry and texture. These assets are imported into the simulation environment with appropriate physical properties (mass, friction, collision meshes).  

> Trajectory alignment.

The simulation data is directly aligned with real-robot trajectories: we replay the recorded real-robot joint trajectories in simulation, producing paired real-sim observation sequences. This tight alignment enables precise sim-to-real gap analysis—researchers can compare model behavior on identical trajectories in both domains to isolate the sources of transfer failure. 

2.9 Evaluation Scoring 

Each task is decomposed into ordered sub-tasks, and evaluation uses partial credit scoring : a trial that completes 7 out of 10 sub-tasks receives a score of 7, not 0. This provides richer signal than binary success/failure—it reveals where in the task pipeline a model fails, not just whether it fails.  

> Scoring examples.

Consider pour_water_from_bottle (5 sub-tasks): (1) grasp the bottle, (2) lift the bottle, (3) move to pouring position above the cup, (4) tilt and pour water, (5) return the bottle upright. A model that grasps and lifts the bottle but fails to pour receives 3/10 rather than 0—clearly distinguishing it from a model that cannot even grasp. Similarly, put_items_into_drawer has 10 sub-tasks spanning opening the drawer, picking each item, placing it inside, and closing the drawer; partial credit reveals whether failures occur at the drawer-manipulation stage or the object-grasping stage. Sub-task complexity varies dramatically across tasks: press_button_in_order has 5 sub-tasks (avg 18.4 s per trial), while put_clothes_in_hamper has 14 sub-tasks (avg 193.6 s). Each trial is scored out of 10, each task out of 100 (10 trials), and the overall competition score sums to 1,500 across 15 evaluated tabletop tasks. The combination of sub-task scoring and stratified trial structure produces a rich diagnostic matrix : for each task, we obtain a 10-trial × N -sub-task score grid, segmented into three generalization levels (in-domain, visual shift, semantic OOD). This enables fine-grained analysis—for instance, one can determine that a model consistently fails at sub-task 4 (pouring) across all OOD trials but succeeds at grasping, pinpointing the generalization bottleneck to force control rather than object recognition.

3.2 Per-Task Statistics 

Table 3 summarizes the dataset statistics per task category. Per-task breakdowns are provided in Appendix A. 12 The three categories exhibit markedly different episode length profiles. Semantic reasoning tasks have the shortest episodes (avg 25.0 s) despite requiring higher cognitive complexity—the manipulation itself is simple once the semantic ambiguity is resolved. Mobile tasks generate dispropor-tionately large data volumes per trajectory, and their long-horizon structure challenges VLA architectures with fixed context windows. 

3.3 Data Format and Open Release 

All data is released in LeRobot v2.1 format , a standardized structure for robot learning datasets. Each task is packaged as an independent dataset with the following structure: 

• meta/info.json — dataset metadata (episode count, feature dimensions, fps, splits). 

• meta/episodes.jsonl — per-episode metadata (language instruction, episode length). 

• meta/tasks.jsonl — task descriptions in natural language. 

• data/chunk-{id}/episode_{id}.parquet — proprioceptive state and action data (56D per frame). 

• videos/chunk-{id}/{camera}/episode_{id}.mp4 — compressed video streams for each of the three camera views. The open release includes all 20 tasks (15 tabletop + 5 mobile; 10,812 episodes total) in LeRobot v2.1 format. Tabletop tasks use 56D state/action vectors; mobile tasks use 62D (56D + 6D for head rotation, lifting height, and base velocity). The action and state vectors are each 56-dimensional, organized as follows for the bimanual follow arms: 

• End-effector poses (14D): Cartesian position (3D), rotation (3D), and gripper state (1D) for each arm. 

• Joint positions (14D): 7-DOF per arm (6 arm joints + 1 gripper joint). 

• Joint velocities (14D): Joint angular velocity per joint—captures dynamic motion profiles. 

• Joint currents (14D): Motor torque proxy per joint (last dimension per arm is gripper current)—critical for force-sensitive tasks. For mobile manipulation tasks, 6 additional dimensions are provided: head rotation (2D), lifting column height (1D), and base velocity ( x, y, θ; 3D), yielding 62D per frame. This modular layout allows participants to select task-relevant subsets ( e.g ., end-effector-only control with 14D, or full joint-level control with 56D/62D). The LeRobot format ensures direct compatibility with popular VLA training frameworks, allowing participants to load data with minimal preprocessing.

> DreamZero (World Action Model).

DreamZero ( 11 ) takes a fundamentally different approach: it learns a joint video-action generative model via autoregressive video diffusion. > Inference latency.

The two paradigms exhibit drastically different computational costs. This latency difference reflects the inherently higher computational cost of autoregressive video generation compared to direct action prediction.

4.3 Single-Task vs. Multi-Task VLA 

Comparing π0.5-Single (626.3) with OneModel (640.5) on the same architecture reveals a nuanced trade-off between cross-task transfer and task-specific knowledge retention.  

> 4.3.1 Multi-Task Gains: Semantic Recognition Transfer

OneModel substantially outperforms Single on tasks requiring object-level semantic understanding : sort_-headphone (73 vs. 35, +109%), pair_up_items (51.5 vs. 21, +145%), and pick_fruits (94 vs. 90). As shown in Figure 10(b), this advantage concentrates on semantic reasoning tasks (OneModel avg 58.9 vs. Single avg 50.6), while execution tasks show comparable performance (34.6 vs. 37.3). The common thread is that these tasks require distinguishing object categories—headphone types, matching pairs, fruits versus non-fruits—and multi-task training exposes the model to a broader variety of objects across tasks, building richer shared visual representations that transfer to categorical recognition.  

> 4.3.2 Multi-Task Costs: Procedural Knowledge Forgetting

Conversely, OneModel suffers severe degradation on tasks that require task-specific procedural reasoning :

press_button_in_order (13 vs. 48, −73%) and put_items_into_drawer (7 vs. 34, −79%). These tasks share a distinctive structure: the model must read a visual instruction on the table (a color-sequence card for 

press_button ; the three-drawer category mapping for put_items_drawer ) and execute a specific procedure conditioned on that instruction. Single-task fine-tuning can memorize these task-specific input-output mappings, but multi-task training dilutes them across 15 tasks. Notably, on press_button , OneModel scores exactly 1 point on 8 out of 10 trials—suggesting that it has lost the procedural policy entirely and defaults to a minimal behavior, while Single at least attempts the correct sequence and occasionally succeeds (T1=10, T10=10).  

> 4.3.3 Summary

Multi-task training produces a clear trade-off: it enhances cross-task visual recognition (beneficial for semantic reasoning) at the cost of task-specific procedural memory (detrimental for instruction-conditioned sequential tasks). This finding has practical implications for competition participants: a unified model benefits from diverse visual co-training but may require task-specific adaptation mechanisms to retain procedural knowledge. 15 4.4 VLA vs. World Action Model 

We compare OneModel (the unified VLA representing ManipArena’s competition format) against DreamZero (the world action model) to diagnose the capability boundaries of the two paradigms. Their cross-task correlation is low ( r = 0 .43 ), confirming fundamentally different learned strategies. We analyze their complementary profiles along four dimensions.  

> 4.4.1 Coarse-Grained Success, Fine-Grained Failure

DreamZero’s performance reveals a consistent pattern: it can execute coarse pick-and-place operations but fails at fine-grained manipulation in later sub-task stages. On pick_items_into_basket , which requires only picking objects and dropping them into a basket, DreamZero achieves 97.8/100 with 90% SR—far exceeding OneModel’s 37/100. Yet on tasks requiring precision in the final sub-steps, the roles reverse: 

• insert_wireline : DreamZero scores exactly 3 on 8 out of 10 trials. Given the scoring rubric (left-hand pick cable: 3pts → right-hand receive: 3pts → insert: 3pts → retract: 1pt), the fixed score of 3 indicates completion of only the first sub-step . OneModel scores similarly overall (21/100) but with more varied per-trial patterns. 

• sort_headphone : DreamZero achieves 9/10 on 6 trials, consistently missing only arm retraction (1pt)—likely because retraction produces minimal visual change in the video prediction objective. OneModel achieves full 10/10 on 7 trials, demonstrating that VLA’s direct action supervision captures even low-visual-signal behaviors. 

• put_glasses : DreamZero scores 0% SR (37/100) while OneModel achieves 70% SR (87/100). The bottleneck is the final bimanual shelf-placement step—coarse planning suffices for pickup but not for coordinated dual-arm precision. This contrast is striking: on the same robot with the same tasks, OneModel excels where precision matters (put_glasses : 87) while DreamZero dominates where coarse planning suffices ( pick_items : 97.8). World models learn effective coarse-grained planning through video prediction—gross object movements produce large visual signals—but lack the fine-grained motor control that VLA models achieve through direct action supervision.  

> 4.4.2 Semantic Understanding Gap

OneModel achieves a semantic-task average of 58.9 versus DreamZero’s 32.7, but this aggregate gap masks a more nuanced pattern across individual tasks. On tasks where the language instruction fully specifies the task objective— sort_headphone (“put headphones into the basket”) and pick_fruits (“pick fruits into the basket”)—DreamZero scores 66 and 45 respectively, remaining competitive with VLA models. The semantic challenge in these tasks reduces to object-level recognition, and the manipulation itself is straightforward pick-and-place. However, on tasks where the language instruction only provides a high-level goal and the model must extract the concrete action plan from visual cues in the scene— press_button_in_order (read a printed color-sequence card to determine which buttons to press) and pair_up_items (infer matching rules from the displayed object arrangement)—DreamZero scores only 3 and 13.5, while OneModel achieves 13 and 51.5. This pattern suggests that the performance gap depends on whether the task objective is fully specified by the language instruction or must be additionally inferred from visual context. When language alone suffices to define the goal, world models remain competitive; when the model must ground scene-level visual information into a concrete action sequence, VLA models show a clearer advantage—though neither paradigm demonstrates robust performance across all semantic task types.

ManipArena’s sub-task scoring reveals critical distinctions that binary success/failure metrics would obscure. Under a binary criterion, all three models appear similar—each achieves SR >50% on only 1–2 tasks. However, partial credit reveals qualitatively different failure modes:

> A.2.3 press_button_in_order Goal:

> Sub-task scoring (10 pts):

Press 1st button (3) → Press 2nd button (3) → Press 3rd button (3) → Retract arms (1).  

> Training colors:

Pink, yellow, blue, green (24 possible permutations of 3 from 4).

Orange, purple (unseen colors).

> OOD objects:

Glove 4–5, sock 3 (unseen patterns).   

> A.2.5 pick_fruits_into_basket Goal:

Identify fruits on the table (among distractors like bread and lettuce) and place only the fruits into a basket.  

> Sub-task scoring (10 pts, variable):

When 3 fruits: place each (3 pts each) + retract (1). When 2 fruits: place each (4 pts each) + retract (2). Penalty: −1 per non-fruit item placed.  

> Training objects:

Apple, pear, banana, grape; distractors: bread, lettuce; basket 1/2.  

> OOD objects:

Orange, cherry; distractor: small toy doll; basket 3.

# B Training Data Diversity Specifications 

Each task is accompanied by a diversity guide specifying the exact object variants, spatial configurations, and compositional permutations to be covered during data collection. Table 5 summarizes all diversity dimensions.  

> Collection protocol.

For each task, trained operators follow the diversity guide to collect trajectories (range: 451–645 per task, 10,812 total). Each diversity dimension is kept approximately uniformly distributed (within 10–15% of equal frequency) to prevent models from exploiting distribution biases. Data collection spanned 28 days across 5 robot units, with quality checks to filter failed demonstrations. 

# C OOD Evaluation Specifications 

ManipArena’s evaluation probes generalization along multiple dimensions. For 7 of the 15 tabletop tasks, T9–T10 introduce objects never seen during training (true object-level OOD). For the remaining 8 tasks, T9–T10 vary spatial or compositional configurations within the training distribution (configuration-level generalization). Table 6 provides the complete design. 26 Semantic distance design. For the 7 tasks with true OOD, objects are chosen at varying semantic distances from the training distribution: 

• Near-OOD: Safety goggles (for put_glasses) — similar overall shape, same grasp strategy applicable. 

• Medium-OOD: Bluetooth earbuds (for sort_headphone) — different form factor, same functional category; sunglasses (for put_glasses) — similar shape, different folding. 

• Far-OOD: Neckband headphones (for sort_headphone) — radically different form + white color = compound shift; black plastic spoon (for put_spoon) — both material and color changed simultaneously.
