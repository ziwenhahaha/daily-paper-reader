Abstract 

Zero-shot vision-and-language navigation in continuous environments (VLN-CE) remains challenging for modern vision-language mod-els (VLMs). Although these models encode useful semantic priors, their open-ended rea-soning does not directly translate into stable long-horizon embodied execution. We argue that the key bottleneck is not missing knowl-edge alone, but missing an execution structure for organizing instruction following, perceptual grounding, temporal progress, and stage veri-fication. We propose EmergeNav, a zero-shot framework that formulates continuous VLN as structured embodied inference. EmergeNav combines a Plan–Solve–Transition hierarchy for stage-structured execution, GIPE for goal-conditioned perceptual extraction, contrastive dual-memory reasoning for progress grounding, and role-separated Dual-FOV sensing for time-aligned local control and boundary verification. On VLN-CE, EmergeNav achieves strong zero-shot performance using only open-source VLM backbones and no task-specific training, ex-plicit maps, graph search, or waypoint predic-tors, reaching 30.00 SR with Qwen3-VL-8B and 37.00 SR with Qwen3-VL-32B. These re-sults suggest that explicit execution structure is a key ingredient for turning VLM priors into stable embodied navigation behavior. 

1 Introduction 

Vision-and-Language Navigation (VLN) requires an embodied agent to follow natural-language in-structions in a visually grounded 3D environment [1 , 2]. In continuous environments (VLN-CE), this problem becomes substantially harder because the agent must perform fine-grained control under par-tial observability over long horizons, without privi-leged graph structure, oracle waypoints, or perfect localization [ 3]. Although recent vision-language models (VLMs) show strong zero-shot reasoning ability, directly applying them as VLN-CE policies remains unreliable, often leading to long-horizon drift, multi-view attention diffusion, and hard-to-recover execution errors [3, 4]. We argue that the key bottleneck in zero-shot VLN-CE is not missing navigation knowledge itself, but missing a structure that can reliably translate such knowledge into embodied con-trol. Modern VLMs already encode useful priors about landmarks, spatial relations, action seman-tics, and instruction-grounded scene understand-ing, yet these priors do not naturally yield stable long-horizon behavior. Existing zero-shot meth-ods either expand perceptual coverage through panoramic or dense multi-view input, or reduce cost through more direct observation-to-action gen-eration. However, neither direction fully resolves the same issue: the model still lacks an explicit mechanism for organizing execution, grounding progress, and verifying transitions. From this per-spective, the central challenge is not how to in-ject more knowledge, but how to structure existing VLM competence into reliable embodied behavior [5, 6, 4, 7]. A reliable zero-shot VLN-CE framework should therefore decompose long-horizon instructions into executable stages, ground perception in task-relevant evidence, track progress over time, and verify transitions explicitly. In short, zero-shot nav-igation requires not only semantic competence, but also an execution structure for planning, grounding, and verification. Based on this view, we propose EmergeNav , a zero-shot framework that formulates continuous VLN as structured embodied inference. Emer-geNav follows a Plan–Solve–Transition hierar-chy: Plan decomposes instructions into anchor-grounded subgoals, Solve generates local actions under the current visual and temporal context, and Transition verifies stage completion before advanc-ing. This turns long-horizon navigation from mono-lithic reasoning into stage-structured execution. To support this process under partial observabil-1

2 Related Work 

Our work relates to zero-shot VLN-CE, structured agent inference, long-horizon memory, and effi-cient perception. We review prior work from a com-mon perspective: how to organize existing VLM capabilities into stable embodied behavior without task-specific training. 

2.1 Zero-shot and Foundation-Model-based VLN-CE 

VLN was originally studied in discrete settings such as R2R, RxR, whereas VLN-CE extends the problem to continuous environments without pre-defined topology, oracle short-horizon actions, or idealized localization [ 1 , 3]. Recent work has ex-plored whether large language or vision-language models can serve as zero-shot navigation agents. Open-Nav studies open-source LLMs for con-tinuous instruction following [ 4 ]; DreamNav in-troduces trajectory-level imagination for longer-horizon planning [8]; and InstructNav emphasizes systematic instruction following in unseen envi-ronments [ 9]. While these methods demonstrate the promise of foundation models for navigation, they often rely on auxiliary maps, value structures, multi-stage planning, or task-specific stabilization. These results suggest that the difficulty lies not only in model capability, but also in how zero-shot execution is organized in continuous environments. EmergeNav focuses on this execution structure. We retain a pure VLM-policy setting with minimal external modules, and emphasize structured scaf-folding rather than explicit maps, graph search, or separate waypoint predictors. 

2.2 Structured Agent Paradigms for Embodied Control 

Recent work on disembodied LLM agents shows that reliability can often be improved through struc-tured inference rather than task-specific training. ReAct interleaves reasoning and acting [ 10 ], Plan-and-Solve emphasizes explicit decomposition [ 11 ], Reflexion introduces verbal feedback and memory [12 ], and ReWOO decouples reasoning from obser-vation usage to improve efficiency and robustness [13 ]. Related ideas have also been transferred to in-teractive settings, such as language-mediated feed-back in Inner Monologue and iterative prompting with memory in Voyager [ 14 , 15 ]. Taken together, these studies suggest that pretrained models be-come more reliable when inference is organized through intermediate states, staged execution, and structured feedback. This perspective is directly relevant to zero-shot VLN-CE, where long-horizon control also requires decomposition, progress tracking, and selective use 2of observations, loosely echoing the stage-based and intermittent nature of human wayfinding. EmergeNav follows the same high-level princi-ple of structured inference, but adapts it to zero-shot VLN-CE through navigation-specific staged execution, explicit transition checking, and struc-tured memory. 

2.3 Memory Mechanisms for VLN and Embodied Navigation 

Recent work has explored memory for long-horizon navigation in many forms, including ex-plicit maps, topological structures, retrieval sys-tems, serialized observation histories, and reusable spatial representations such as semantic maps or grid-based memory [ 3, 16 , 17 , 18 , 19 ]. In zero-shot VLN-CE, structured spatial priors such as value maps have also been used to guide online decisions [7]. These studies show that memory is valuable not merely for storing more history, but for stabilizing progress over time, similar to how human wayfind-ing relies on both recent motion context and persis-tent landmark cues. EmergeNav departs from explicit map building and instead uses memory as a contrastive progress-grounding mechanism . STM maintains recent mo-tion tendencies and local action continuity, while LTM stores sparse but persistent evidence of veri-fied progress, allowing the agent to compare short-term dynamics with longer-term progress state and infer whether it is advancing, revisiting, or stalling. 

2.4 Selective Perception and Panoramic Trade-offs in VLN-CE 

Recent zero-shot VLN-CE work reveals a recur-ring trade-off in perceptual design. Panoramic or multi-view pipelines improve environmental cov-erage and support waypoint generation or down-stream action selection [ 16 , 4, 5 ], but they also introduce latency, redundancy, and attention dif-fusion. By contrast, panoramic-free designs such as Fast-SmartWay reduce per-step perceptual cost by using only forward-facing triplet views with uncertainty-aware reasoning [ 6 ]. More broadly, richer visual input does not necessarily yield bet-ter decisions, especially for smaller open-source VLMs. This suggests that the key issue is not simply how much perception to use, but how to allocate perception according to decision need, echoing the selective and forward-centered nature of human visual guidance during navigation. EmergeNav adopts this view through two cou-pled designs. GIPE treats perception as goal-conditioned evidence extraction rather than ex-haustive visual description, and role-separated Dual-FOV sensing assigns forward views to high-frequency local control while reserving panoramic observations for low-frequency self-localization and stage confirmation. This reframes the panoramic versus non-panoramic trade-off as a problem of role-aligned perceptual scheduling. 

3 Method 

3.1 Method Overview 

EmergeNav formulates zero-shot continuous VLN as stage-structured embodied inference . Rather than mapping observations directly to actions, it maintains an explicit execution process defined by an active subgoal, goal-conditioned perceptual evi-dence, and dual-memory progress state. Navigation proceeds through a Plan–Solve–Transition (PST) 

hierarchy: Plan decomposes the instruction into anchor-grounded subgoals, Solve performs local control under the current subgoal, and Transition 

verifies stage completion before advancing. Four components jointly realize this framework. 

PST provides the execution scaffold. GIPE ex-tracts compact task-relevant evidence from multi-view observations. Contrastive dual-memory reasoning grounds progress through short-term motion continuity and long-term verified anchors. 

Role-separated Dual-FOV sensing aligns per-ceptual input with decision time scale by using forward-centered views for continuous control and panoramic views for boundary verification. To-gether, they organize VLM reasoning into a unified structured inference loop for reliable zero-shot em-bodied navigation.

3.3 Plan–Solve–Transition Hierarchy Plan–Solve–Transition (PST) is the execution scaffold of EmergeNav. Its purpose is to turn long-horizon navigation from flat free-form execution into stage-wise embodied control with explicit ad-vancement conditions. Instead of requiring the model to implicitly track global task structure, lo-cal motion state, and switching conditions at the same time, PST separates instruction decomposi-tion, local solving, and stage transition into distinct roles. 

Plan. The planning stage converts the original in-struction into a sequence of short anchor-grounded subtasks. The key principle is semantic preserva-tion rather than aggressive rewriting: the planner keeps the original anchor phrases and their order, while removing only purely orientation-only ex-pressions when they do not define stage semantics. As a result, each subgoal gk corresponds to one in-terpretable anchor-state transition, such as leaving a region, passing an object, or stopping near a final anchor. This provides the downstream policy with a stable stage variable for long-horizon execution. 

Solve. Given the active subgoal gk, the solve stage performs local control using only forward-centered observations and memory. It follows a short ReAct-style loop within the current subgoal, repeatedly reasoning over local evidence, selecting a candidate heading, executing a short action bun-dle, and updating STM with the resulting trajectory-like interaction record. At the end of each local rollout, the solve stage produces a factual summary of recent motion and anchor presentation. This summary is used only as auxiliary evidence for transition auditing and does not itself determine stage advancement. 

Transition. The transition stage is a separate control module whose sole responsibility is to de-cide whether the current subgoal has been com-pleted. It does not generate navigation motion or revise the plan. Instead, it receives panoramic ob-servations, the current and next subgoals, the latest LTM state, and the rollout summary from the solve stage, and then performs a conservative boundary audit to output either continue or switch . This role separation is central to PST: solve may indi-cate progress, but transition alone can advance the stage index. PST improves both controllability and auditabil-ity. Controllability comes from reducing long-horizon navigation to repeated local execution un-der an explicit active stage. Auditability comes from making subgoal completion a discrete, in-spectable decision supported by panoramic evi-dence and memory, rather than an implicit side effect of local action generation. In this way, PST places global checking where it is most informa-tive: at candidate subgoal boundaries rather than at every control step. 5Figure 3: GIPE interface in EmergeNav. In the solve stage, GIPE extracts compact local evidence from the active subgoal, forward-centered triplet views, and mem-ory context to support action generation. In the transi-tion stage, GIPE extracts boundary-level evidence from the current and next subgoals, panoramic observations, long-term memory, and rollout summary to support stage verification and Continue/Switch decisions. 

3.4 Goal-Conditioned Information-Guided Perceptual Extraction Goal-Conditioned Information-Guided Percep-tual Extraction (GIPE) is the perceptual inter-face of EmergeNav. Rather than serving as a stan-dalone perception network, GIPE is implemented as a prompt-level structured evidence protocol in-side both the solve and transition modules. Its role is to convert raw multi-view observations into com-pact, task-aligned evidence that retains only the information needed for the current decision. The motivation is straightforward: richer visual input does not automatically produce better control. In continuous VLN, directly exposing a VLM to many images often increases redundancy, diffuses attention, and weakens temporal consistency, espe-cially for smaller open-source models. GIPE ad-dresses this by enforcing selective extraction rather than unconstrained scene description. Instead of de-scribing everything visible, the model is required to retain only evidence relevant to the active subgoal and current decision. GIPE operates differently in solve and transition because the two stages serve different control roles. In the solve stage, GIPE processes the local triplet together with STM and LTM to extract subgoal-relevant evidence for local action generation, in-cluding the current anchor transition, the memory-grounded agent-anchor relation, local traversabil-ity and obstacle structure, and the action implica-tions of the selected heading. As a result, the solve module reasons over a compact goal-conditioned evidence schema rather than unconstrained visual narration. In the transition stage, GIPE operates over panoramic views and stage-level context to sup-port boundary verification rather than local mo-tion selection. Here the extracted evidence cap-tures whether instruction-defined anchors are visi-ble, whether the current stage boundary has been reached, whether the next subgoal is actionable, and whether the change from LTM to the current observation indicates genuine advancement rather than rotation-only drift. In this way, panoramic sensing becomes a verification signal rather than a per-step action input. A key property of GIPE is that it imposes explicit grounding constraints. Across both solve and tran-sition, evidence must be supported by visible pixels or explicitly provided memory. Ambiguous cues may be marked as uncertain, but they cannot serve as decisive support; likewise, instruction-defined anchors must be preserved faithfully rather than rewritten into looser semantic abstractions. This prevents the structured interface from degenerating into unconstrained chain-of-thought narration. Although GIPE is realized through prompting rather than architectural modification, it is not merely a formatting choice: it serves as the ex-plicit perceptual control mechanism that makes structured embodied inference feasible under multi-view partial observability.

3.6 Role-Separated Dual-FOV Sensing 

EmergeNav adopts a role-separated sensing sched-ule rather than a uniform observation policy. The central idea is that different navigation decisions require different spatial coverage and should there-fore consume different visual inputs. For continuous action generation, the solve stage uses three forward-centered views with camera headings at −30 ◦, 0◦, and +30 ◦, each rendered with a horizontal field of view of 72 ◦. This pro-duces a forward-centered observation band with substantial overlap and an effective union that cov-ers the frontal sector most relevant to immediate heading selection. The design is inspired by a sim-ple property of human visual guidance: locomotion is primarily controlled through forward-centered visual input, while the most effective processing is concentrated near fixation rather than uniformly across the full visual field. In this sense, our triplet-view design should be understood not as a strict physiological model, but as a navigation-oriented approximation of forward-centered functional vi-sion. This design is sufficient for local control because the solve stage only needs to compare immedi-ately relevant candidate headings while keeping perceptual redundancy low. Instead of invoking panoramic sensing at every step, the agent follows a short ReAct-style local loop under the active subgoal, using the triplet views to reason about local traversability, heading choice, and the short-horizon consequences of recent actions. For subgoal transition auditing, EmergeNav switches to panoramic sensing at six headings sep-arated by 60 ◦. This broader field of view is not used to generate every local action. Instead, it is reserved for low-frequency verification after a local solve rollout, when the system must decide whether the current subgoal boundary has actually been reached and whether the next subgoal has be-come actionable. In this sense, panoramic input is scheduled for boundary confirmation , not for continuous control. More broadly, role-separated Dual-FOV sensing instantiates a simple principle: information allo-cation should follow decision time scale. High-frequency local control benefits from direction-ally focused, low-redundancy input, whereas low-frequency stage verification benefits from broader coverage that supports self-localization, came-from reasoning, and next-stage readiness checks. By separating these two regimes, EmergeNav avoids the inefficiency of all-panorama control while also avoiding the brittleness of purely forward-view nav-igation with no global verification. Role-separated Dual-FOV sensing is therefore not merely an observation-engineering detail. It is the perceptual scheduling mechanism that makes PST operational under partial observability. With-out it, the transition module would lack the global evidence needed for conservative switching, while the solve module would be overloaded by unnec-essary panoramic redundancy. With it, Emerge-Nav realizes a navigation process best described as 

continuous local advancement plus low-frequency boundary confirmation .

4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate EmergeNav on the Habitat-based VLN-CE benchmark, following the 100-episode evaluation protocol used in prior zero-shot work for direct comparison [ 4 ]. This setting requires the agent to follow free-form navigation instructions in continuous 3D environments without access to privileged graph topology, oracle waypoints, or task-specific training. 8All ablation experiments are conducted with Qwen3-VL-8B on a server equipped with 2×

NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPUs. To examine scalabil-ity to a stronger open-source backbone, we addi-tionally evaluate the full EmergeNav framework with Qwen3-VL-32B on 2× NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs. Unless otherwise specified, all methods are evaluated in a fully zero-shot manner, without fine-tuning, explicit map construction, graph search, or learned waypoint prediction. We report trajectory length (TL), navigation er-ror (NE), oracle success rate (OSR), success rate (SR), and success weighted by path length (SPL) in the main comparison, and additionally report nDTW, SDTW, number of steps, distance-to-goal, and collision rate in ablation studies. These met-rics jointly reflect task completion, navigation effi-ciency, and long-horizon execution quality. 

4.2 Comparison with Baselines 

Table 1 compares EmergeNav with prior super-vised and zero-shot methods under the VLN-CE setting. To contextualize the quantitative results, Table 2 further summarizes representative differ-ences in input modality, policy backbone, external spatial priors, waypoint prediction, and sensing regime. In contrast to prior panoramic or frontal-view baselines, EmergeNav adopts a role-separated sensing design: forward-centered triplet views for high-frequency local control and low-frequency panoramic observations for boundary verification. With Qwen3-VL-8B as the policy backbone, EmergeNav achieves 30.0 SR and 21.3 SPL, outper-forming strong zero-shot baselines such as Smart-Way (29.0 SR, 22.46 SPL) and substantially ex-ceeding frontal-view Fast-SmartWay in SR (27.75 vs. 30.0). More importantly, EmergeNav does so while retaining a pure RGB-only VLM-policy de-sign without explicit spatial priors or waypoint pre-dictors, as shown in Table 2. This supports our main argument that explicit execution structure can compensate for the limited stability of smaller open-source VLMs in continuous navigation. Scaling the backbone from Qwen3-VL-8B to Qwen3-VL-32B further improves SR from 30.0 to 37.0 and OSR from 48.0 to 58.0, while reduc-ing NE from 8.38 to 7.60. The stronger backbone therefore yields more reliable stage completion un-der the same structured inference framework. No-tably, the gain is achieved without modifying the execution scaffold, suggesting that EmergeNav is compatible with stronger open-source VLMs while preserving the same policy design. A useful way to interpret these results is through the contrast between final success and path effi-ciency. EmergeNav achieves strong OSR and SR, indicating robust recovery and stage completion, but its SPL remains slightly below the strongest zero-shot baseline. This pattern is consistent with our qualitative observation: under smaller back-bones, the agent can still make short locally plau-sible deviations before re-anchoring to the cor-rect subgoal. In other words, EmergeNav already improves whether the agent eventually succeeds, while leaving room to further improve how directly it reaches the goal. 

4.3 Ablation Study 

We next analyze the contributions of GIPE and dual memory. Table 3 compares the full 8B system with three ablated variants: removing GIPE, removing memory, and removing both GIPE and memory. Removing GIPE causes a large drop in task suc-cess: SR decreases from 30.0 to 12.0, OSR from 48.0 to 32.0, and SPL from 21.3 to 6.5. This shows that structured perceptual extraction is critical for stable zero-shot action selection. Without GIPE, the model receives richer but less organized visual context, which weakens subgoal-aligned evidence selection and leads to poorer local decisions. Removing memory leads to a different failure mode. Although SR only drops to 17.0, the tra-jectory becomes substantially less efficient: the average number of steps increases from 145.9 to 219.8, path length from 19.50 to 30.36, and colli-sion rate from 0.273 to 0.306. This indicates that dual memory mainly contributes long-horizon sta-bility and progress control rather than immediate perceptual quality. Without memory grounding, the agent is more prone to over-exploration, repeated adjustment, and inefficient recovery. When both GIPE and memory are removed, per-formance remains far below the full model across all metrics. Compared with the single-component ablations, this result suggests that perceptual fil-tering and temporal grounding are complementary rather than interchangeable. GIPE improves what 

evidence the agent attends to, whereas dual mem-ory improves how that evidence is interpreted rela-tive to accumulated progress. 4.4 Analysis and Discussion 

Taken together, the results support three conclu-sions. First, explicit execution structure matters: even a relatively small open-source VLM can achieve competitive zero-shot continuous naviga-tion when instruction following is organized into stage-structured execution rather than left as un-constrained action generation. Second, perceptual control and progress grounding play different but complementary roles: GIPE mainly improves deci-sion relevance, whereas dual memory mainly im-proves long-horizon stability and efficiency. Third, EmergeNav’s gains are not reducible to a single heuristic, but arise from the interaction of stage-structured execution, goal-conditioned perceptual extraction, progress-anchored memory, and role-separated sensing. 

5 Conclusion 

We presented EmergeNav , a zero-shot framework for vision-and-language navigation in continuous environments that formulates long-horizon naviga-tion as structured embodied inference . Our cen-tral claim is that the main challenge of zero-shot VLN-CE lies not only in model capacity or missing semantic knowledge, but in the absence of an execu-tion structure that can organize VLM reasoning into stable embodied behavior. To address this, Emerge-Nav combines stage-structured execution through Plan–Solve–Transition, goal-conditioned percep-tual extraction through GIPE, contrastive dual-memory progress grounding, and role-separated Dual-FOV sensing for boundary-aware verifica-tion. Experiments on VLN-CE show that this struc-tured inference perspective substantially improves zero-shot navigation with open-source VLMs. In particular, EmergeNav achieves strong perfor-mance under a role-separated sensing design with-out task-specific training, explicit maps, graph search, or waypoint predictors, demonstrating that even relatively small open-source VLMs can be-10 Variant SR OSR SPL nDTW SDTW Steps Dist.
