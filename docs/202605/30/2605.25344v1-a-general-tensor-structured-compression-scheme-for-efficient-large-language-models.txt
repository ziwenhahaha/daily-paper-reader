Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are dominated by dense linear transformations, whose storage, mem-ory and computational overheads hinder efficient adaptation and deployment while masking the functional impacts of structural simplification. Here we present Tensor Mixture (MixT), a general tensor-structured compression scheme that replaces targeted dense linear layers with natively exe-cutable mixtures of tensor operators. Operating directly on generic linear projections instead of model-specific components, MixT is potentially applicable across Transformer-based LLMs and other dense neural mappings. We evaluate MixT on Qwen3-8B and LLaMA2-7B under a unified recov-ery protocol, identifying a broad compressible regime in which MMLU accuracy is largely preserved before an abrupt transition at model-specific boundaries. This transition coincides with coordinated shifts in output entropy, prediction entropy and inter-layer geometry. At the LLaMA2-7B transition boundary, MixT reduces full-model parameters by 47.5%, inference FLOPs by 37.1%, training FLOPs by 52.1% and peak inference memory by 60.4%, demonstrating its practical potential for lower-cost LLM compression. 

LLMs have achieved impressive performance, but dense parameterizations drive rapid growth in storage, memory and computation [1–3]. This has motivated strong interest in efficient and struc-tured model designs that reduce cost while pre-serving capability, including parameter-efficient adaptation [4], quantization [5–9], pruning and structural compression [10–14], and layer-level modification or removal [15, 16]. Recent stud-ies suggest that tolerance to structural sim-plification is depth dependent: some layers or substructures can be altered with little loss, whereas others remain tightly coupled to model function [15–18]. Complementary evidence from neural-language alignment studies also points to depth-organized functional hierarchy, reinforcing the view that layer roles are non-uniform across 1

However, existing approaches are usually judged by final accuracy–efficiency trade-offs [20], which offers limited insight into how performance evolves along a controlled com-pression path within Transformer blocks [21]. More broadly, for models dominated by dense and weakly structured parameterizations, a framework is still needed that makes structural complex-ity explicit and allows compression depth to be scanned in a controlled and comparable way. In quantum many-body physics and multilin-ear algebra, tensor methods provide a principled way to manage the accuracy–efficiency trade-off by combining compact representations with explicit internal structure and systematic control over order, mode size and connectivity [22–24]. These properties enable efficient representations of otherwise intractable systems while retaining interpretability of how global behaviour emerges from local interactions [22, 24]. A similar chal-lenge appears in large-scale machine learning, where dense parameterizations obscure the inter-nal organisation of model interactions. This paral-lel suggests that tensor-structured representations are a natural way to inject explicit structure into neural computations. While tensorized approaches have been explored for compression, reparameterization, embedding compression and efficient adapta-tion in neural networks and NLP [25–30], many tensorized parameterizations for LLMs recon-struct dense weights or do not fully preserve structured execution in the forward computation path [31]. As a result, their impact on runtime computational and memory efficiency can remain constrained, limiting the potential practical benefits of tensor-based compression techniques. In this work, we introduce Tensor Mix-ture (MixT), a tensor-structured framework that replaces standard dense linear transformations in Transformer blocks with directly executable tensor operators, making structural complexity explicit and controllable (Figure 1). Figure 1 sum-marises the MixT construction, including block-level replacement of Transformer linear maps, 

NT -controlled operator scaling and a dataset-specific empirical scaling comparison between dense Qwen3 base models and recovered MixT 2descendants of Qwen3-8B. MixT rewrites dense mappings as mixtures of coupled local tensor components, enabling structural complexity to be controlled along two axes: local tensor structure and replacement depth. Because MixT operates on dense linear projections rather than model-specific modules, it is potentially applicable to a broad class of Transformer-based LLMs; here we evaluate this property on two representative 7B/8B-scale model families. By retaining struc-tured operators in the forward path, MixT pro-vides a route to lower storage, computational cost and memory usage. We apply MixT to progressive structural sim-plification in Qwen3-8B [32] and LLaMA2-7B [33], treating compression depth NB as an explicit con-trol variable. In both settings, the models show a broad region in which substantial structural reduction causes little performance loss, followed by an abrupt transition beyond a model-specific boundary. This transition is accompanied by coor-dinated shifts in output-distribution statistics and internal representations, including abrupt changes in answer entropy, prediction entropy and inter-layer geometry under prompting. These results suggest that structural simplification in the tested LLMs can exhibit critical-like behaviour: perfor-mance declines smoothly up to a threshold, and then degrades sharply. MixT therefore provides a controlled framework for locating benign simplifi-cation regimes and probing transition boundaries along the efficiency–performance trade-off. 

# Results 

## Abrupt transition boundary under progressive back-to-front MixT compression 

Figure 2 shows that the tested LLMs admit sub-stantial structural compression under MixT, while performance on Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU) [34] remains nearly unchanged across a wide range of compression depths NB . This indicates a broad regime in which a large fraction of each model can be structurally simplified with little MMLU loss. However, this stability breaks down abruptly once the transition boundary (denoted ˜NB ) is crossed. For Qwen3-8B, the abrupt transition occurs at approximately 

˜NB ≃ 11 , whereas for LLaMA2-7B it occurs at 

˜NB ≃ 17 . Beyond these boundaries, replacing only one or two additional blocks is sufficient to induce large degradation, in some cases reducing accuracy close to random-guessing level. The transition is therefore abrupt rather than gradual. Within this sweep, the transition bound-ary provides the strongest structural simplifica-tion before the sharp performance drop. Taking LLaMA2-7B with the number of tensor branches 

NT = 4 as an example, a representative dense out-put projection is reduced from 16 .78 M parameters to about 1.05 M (6.25% of the original). This yields an estimated whole-model size of 3.54B parame-ters, corresponding to a 47.5% reduction from the 6.74B dense baseline (52.5% remaining). The transition boundary is relatively stable within each tested model. Varying the internal complexity of the MixT operator, for example by changing the number of tensor branches ( NT ), has only a minor effect on ˜NB . Even under aggressive operator-level compression above 98% ( NT = 5 

for a single linear map), the transition threshold in Qwen3-8B remains near ˜NB ≃ 11 . The per-formance curve nevertheless becomes more irreg-ular, with increased variability emerging in the pre-transition regime ( NB < ˜NB ). The inset of Fig. 2( b) shows that compression behaviour is strongly direction dependent. When the same procedure is applied from earlier to later layers, performance deteriorates immediately and monotonically, without an extended stable regime or a comparable abrupt-transition threshold. This asymmetry indicates that tolerance to structural simplification is not uniform across depth, with earlier layers exhibiting substantially greater sen-sitivity, consistent with previous reports of depth-dependent functional specialization in LLMs [15, 16, 29].

## Output-distribution statistics 

We next ask whether the abrupt transition reflects only a drop in benchmark accuracy or also a change in output behaviour. Figure 3 shows that both occur on MMLU. For both Qwen3-8B and LLaMA2-7B, crossing the transition boundary is accompanied by an abrupt accuracy drop and a sharp change in output-distribution statistics. Figure 3a,b first shows a clear change in output entropy. Before ˜NB , accuracy remains rel-atively stable and OE, defined in Eq. 3, stays low, indicating that the model usually concen-trates probability mass on a small subset of answer options for each question. Once ˜NB is crossed, OE rises abruptly while accuracy falls. The tran-sition boundary therefore marks a sudden loss of per-question decisiveness, rather than a gradual weakening of performance alone. Prediction entropy behaves differently. OE is defined from the full four-way answer distribution on each MMLU question, whereas PE, defined in Eq. 4, is computed from the distribution of final predicted labels over the full evaluation set. In both sweeps, PE is high before the boundary and drops after it. This means that the post-transition model becomes not only more uncertain on indi-vidual questions, but also more concentrated in its final label usage across the dataset. We developed MixT as a tensor-structured com-pression framework for dense linear layers and used it to study how performance changes under progressive back-to-front structuring for LLMs. Across both Qwen3-8B and LLaMA2-7B, the same overall pattern emerges on MMLU: per-formance remains stable over a broad range of compression depths and then drops sharply once the number of compressed blocks NB crosses a model-dependent transition boundary. In this sense, MixT does more than provide a compressed alternative to dense layers. It turns compression depth into an explicit structural coordinate and makes failure onset measurable along a controlled compression path. This framework also shows practical potential. MixT yields substantial reductions in parame-ter count, storage footprint, training and infer-ence complexity, and memory usage in the tested setting. These reductions are not limited to severely degraded regimes, but already appear where MMLU performance remains near the pre-transition level. MixT therefore provides a use-ful compromise between structural simplicity and retained capability. Equally importantly, these gains rely on retaining the structured operator directly in the execution path rather than using tensorization only as a compressed parameteriza-tion (e.g., [31]). The compression path is explicit, tunable and comparable across depths, making it possible to relate resource reduction to the point at which performance ceases to remain stable. The abrupt transition observed during pro-gressive MixT simplification exhibits character-istics of a critical-like transition. This shift is marked by abrupt performance degradation and coordinated alterations in output statistics and inter-layer geometry. These findings suggest that the tested models undergo functional reorganisa-tion from a stable, well-structured regime to a sharply degraded state. This behaviour provides a controlled setting for studying how structural sim-plification affects functional integrity and for iden-tifying thresholds at which simplification remains effective before severe degradation. The empha-sis on executable structure rather than parameter count alone is also consistent with recent evi-dence that functional behaviour can be traced along constrained, approximately invariant paths in parameter space [35]. While this study demonstrates MixT’s poten-tial efficiency and effectiveness under a fixed post-compression recovery protocol, its full potential remains to be explored in more complex real-world settings. In particular, integrating reinforce-ment learning and high-quality long-reasoning-chain data remains an important direction for future work, especially for tasks requiring multi-step reasoning. The present results were obtained with an early MixT implementation and a com-paratively modest recovery protocol; substantial engineering work on tensor kernels, recovery data, alignment and hardware-aware execution may fur-ther improve the performance of MixT and related tensor-structured AI methods. MixT is not areplacement for existing LLM techniques such as quantization and training algorithms, but is complementary to them and may enable further resource reductions while preserving, or poten-tially enhancing, performance on complex tasks. This positions MixT as a promising framework for future model optimisation. 

# Methods 

## MixT architecture 

MixT (Mixture of Tensors) is a structured replace-ment for dense linear layers, including those in Transformer models. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the replacement is applied to all linear layers in each selected Transformer block, spanning both the self-attention and feed-forward submodules. In the back-to-front compression sweep, NB denotes the number of Transformer blocks in which all lin-ear layers are replaced under the block-level MixT configuration. For a linear layer f with input and output dimensions Din and Dout , we write Din = dn and 

Dout = dm, where d is the local bond dimen-sion used for tensorization. If an input or output dimension is not exactly expressible as a power of 

d, zero padding is applied before tensorization. The input is reshaped into n bonds of dimen-sion d and processed by NT parallel branches. Each branch applies one local tensor of input order 

n − NT + 1 and output order m − NT + 1 . After all branches are evaluated, their outputs are summed, 7multiplied by 1/N T , and reshaped back to the out-put dimension of the original linear layer. Taking

## Weight-matching initialization and post-compression recovery 

After structural replacement and weight-matching initialisation, each compressed checkpoint under-went a fixed-budget post-compression recovery stage. This was done to ensure that differences across compression depth reflected structural replacement rather than unequal recovery bud-gets. Unless otherwise stated, all checkpoints were aligned under the same training configuration and the same recovery budget of 3000 optimisa-tion steps, using a fixed general instruction-tuning mixture to restore broad language-model function-ality after replacing dense linear layers with MixT operators. General reasoning performance was evaluated primarily on MMLU [34] using the Language Model Evaluation Harness [36]. For each model family and local MixT setting, we progressively increased the number of back-to-front compressed blocks NB , constructed each checkpoint from the same dense base model, aligned it under the fixed protocol above, and measured the resulting post-alignment MMLU accuracy. We define the block transition threshold ˜NB

as the smallest compression depth at which the MMLU trajectory leaves the pre-transition plateau and enters a sustained sharp-degradation regime. Operationally, ˜NB was taken as the first onset of an abrupt drop after which performance did not recover to the pre-transition level at larger 

NB . Unless otherwise specified, compression pro-ceeded from the output side towards the input side. A front-to-back sweep was also evaluated for comparison.
