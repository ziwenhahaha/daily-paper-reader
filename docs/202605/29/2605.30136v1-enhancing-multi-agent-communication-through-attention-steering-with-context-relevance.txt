## Abstract 

LLM-based multi-agent systems have demonstrated remarkable performance on complex tasks through collaborative reasoning. However, these systems tend to rapidly accumulate extremely long conversation histories during interaction. As conversations lengthen, relevant information is increasingly diluted by irrelevant context, leading to degraded performance. In this work, we present A GENT -RADAR , a training-free context management method that dynamically steers each agent’s attention toward relevant context with a novel temporal and spatial decay mechanism. Our experiments demonstrate that A GENT -R ADAR outperforms state-of-the-art methods across five different benchmarks, yielding gains of up to 7.64 absolute points. Furthermore, our analysis shows that A GENT -R ADAR remains effective and robust as the number of agents and interaction rounds increases. Fi-nally, the ablation study shows that core components in A GENT -R ADAR are cru-cial to performance and generalizable in different settings. 

## 1 Introduction 

LLM-based multi-agent systems have demonstrated remarkable performance on complex tasks [Liang et al., 2024, Wang et al., 2024b, Du et al., 2024, Wu et al., 2024]. Despite these successes, agent communication often accumulates extremely long context windows rapidly [Zhang et al., 2025a] and suffers from communication redundancy [Wang et al., 2025b, Zhang et al., 2025a, Du et al., 2024, Liang et al., 2024]. As a result, the original task description, key constraints, and other critical evidence are often buried within the context, making it difficult for LLMs to reli-ably identify and attend to them. This phenomenon, also known as the “lost-in-the-middle” ef-fect, frequently induces hallucinations and logical errors during generation [Becker et al., 2026, Liu et al., 2024a, Cemri et al., 2025, Zhang et al., 2025b]. This issue is further exacerbated as systems scale. Increasing numbers of agents and interaction rounds cause communication histories to grow rapidly, amplifying context dilution and undermining the reliability of large-scale agent collabora-tions [Cemri et al., 2025, Becker et al., 2026, Zhang et al., 2025b]. Prior context management methods have sought to mitigate this challenge in two ways. One line of work applies periodic summarization to compress accumulated interaction history [Wang et al., 2025a, Wu et al., 2025]. Another adopts pruning strategies that limit context growth by removing redundant agents or communication edges [Zhang et al., 2025a, Wang et al., 2025b]. While effec-tive at improving efficiency, these methods introduce information loss. Specifically, compressing

or pruning messages can discard subtle but critical signals, potentially misleading downstream rea-soning and propagating errors across subsequent interactions [Shen et al., 2025b, Acharjee et al., 2025]. We propose a shift from context compression or pruning to selectively steering agents’ attention over relevant context. In the original Transformer architecture, the attention mechanism retrieves information by matching queries to keys, allowing the model to assign importance weights across preceding tokens in the context [Vaswani et al., 2017]. Accordingly, in multi-agent systems, an agent’s current generation step naturally acts as a query seeking to retrieve relevant information from the communication history. Unlike prior methods that act as hard, binary filters over context, we instead steer attention toward a subset of relevant content while preserving the full transcript, thereby avoiding information loss. To achieve this, we develop A GENT -R ADAR , a training-free method that dynamically guides agents’ attention toward relevant context without modifying the underlying communication history. To iden-tify which parts of the history should guide each generation step, A GENT -R ADAR selects context by jointly modeling semantic relevance, spatial distance, and temporal recency. First, inspired by organizational network theory [Borgatti et al., 2009, Allen, 1984], where influence decays with struc-tural distance, A GENT -R ADAR formulates the agent interaction topology as a graph and applies a spatial decay factor based on inter-agent distance, prioritizing messages from closely interacting agents. Second, A GENT -R ADAR incorporates temporal decay to down-weight stale information, re-flecting evidence from both long-context modeling and human memory that relevance diminishes over time [Finkenbinder, 1913, Sun et al., 2023, Ma et al., 2023]. Third, A GENT -R ADAR performs fine-grained semantic matching by segmenting messages into sentence-level units to extract relevant evidence at the current step. Finally, A GENT -R ADAR integrates these signals through a lightweight attention-steering mechanism, enabling agents to emphasize key instructions, critical constraints, and useful intermediate evidence, without compressing or pruning history tokens. We evaluate A GENT -R ADAR on five benchmarks using three base LLMs. Compared with SOTA con-text management methods, including compression-based approach [Wang et al., 2025a] and pruning methods [Zhang et al., 2025a, Wang et al., 2025b], A GENT -R ADAR achieves gains of up to 7.64 absolute points. As a plug-in method, A GENT -R ADAR can be seamlessly integrated into diverse multi-agent systems, including debate-based systems [Du et al., 2024], GPTSwarm [Zhuge et al., 2024], and AutoGen [Wu et al., 2024]. Across these frameworks, A GENT -R ADAR consistently im-proves their performance by up to 12.87 absolute points and generalizes across different interaction topologies. Furthermore, A GENT -R ADAR remains robust as systems scale to larger numbers of agents and longer interaction rounds. Finally, ablation studies confirm that each component con-tributes meaningfully to the overall performance gains.

## 3 Methodology 

We introduce A GENT -R ADAR (Figure 1), a training-free method that augments multi-agent systems (MAS) by steering an agent’s attention toward semantically relevant context that is both spatially proximate and temporally salient. Building on the formulation in Section 2, A GENT -R ADAR selects fine-grained context by jointly modeling semantic relevance, spatial structure, and temporal recency. Then, it steers the agent’s attention toward the selected context during generation.

Spatial Decay. The organizational network theory [Borgatti et al., 2009, Allen, 1984] finds that information relevance in collaborative networks typically decays with structural distance. Based on this insight, we use an exponential form because each additional relay can be viewed as retaining only a fixed fraction of the useful signal. ## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup Benchmarks. We evaluate A GENT -R ADAR on three categories of benchmarks. For open-ended question answering, we use HotpotQA [Yang et al., 2018], 2WikiMultihopQA [Ho et al., 2020], and MuSiQue [Trivedi et al., 2022] to evaluate the system’s ability to retrieve and synthesize infor-mation across multiple documents. We report F1 scores for these open-ended QA benchmarks. For mathematical reasoning, we use MATH-500 [Lightman et al., 2024], which represents competition-level math problems [Hendrycks et al., 2021]. We report accuracy for MATH-500. For general reasoning, we adopt MMLU-Pro [Wang et al., 2024a], an enhanced benchmark designed to assess robust reasoning capabilities across diverse domains. We report accuracy for MMLU-Pro. 

Base Models. We use Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 [Team, 2025] as the base model for all main experiments. To verify the generalizability across different LLMs, we experiment with Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct [Grattafiori et al., 2024] and Qwen3-32B [Team, 2025]. These results are reported in Appendix A.1. 

Baselines. We compare A GENT -R ADAR against single-agent, context management, and multi-agent system baselines. The single-agent baselines include Vanilla , which directly solves the task, and Self-Consistency (CoT-SC) [Wei et al., 2022, Kojima et al., 2022], which samples multiple reasoning paths and applies majority voting. The context management baselines include Com-paction method [Wu et al., 2025, Chen et al., 2025a], AgentPrune [Zhang et al., 2025a], and 

AgentDropout [Wang et al., 2025b], which are pruning-based methods that limit context growth by removing redundant agents or communication links. Finally, we evaluate plug-in compatibility with representative multi-agent systems, including GPTSwarm [Zhuge et al., 2024], AutoGen [Wu et al., 2024], and Multi-Agent Debate (MAD) [Du et al., 2024].

4.3 Ablation Study and Analysis 

In this section, we ablate the key design choices in A GENT -R ADAR on three representative bench-marks : MuSiQue, MATH-500, and MMLU-Pro. Specifically, we examine A GENT -R ADAR by replacing the semantic matching method and the attention steering method. Then, we isolate the contributions of spatial and temporal decay by removing them individually or jointly. We use the same experimental settings as the main evaluation. Table 4 reports the results. 

Different Semantic Matching Methods. To validate the dense semantic matcher in A GENT -RADAR , we compare it with a sparse lexical retrieval variant based on BM25. In this variant, we keep the A GENT -R ADAR framework unchanged, including spatial decay, temporal decay, and atten-tion steering, but replace the dense semantic matcher with BM25. As shown in Table 4, the BM25 variant improves over the AgentDropout baseline, increasing MATH-500 from 82.60 to 85.20, and MMLU-Pro from 67.40 to 68.40. However, it shows slightly lower performance on MuSiQue, de-creasing from 37.06 to 36.76. Furthermore, the default A GENT -R ADAR configuration consistently achieves performance gains of 2.96 points on MuSiQue, 3.60 points on MATH-500, and 1.00 points on MMLU-Pro over BM25. These results suggest that dense semantic matching is better suited for MAS communication, where agents may express related information through semantically related but lexically different descriptions. Different Attention Steering Methods. 

We then replace the default attention steer-ing method (SPA) with PASTA [Zhang et al., 2024b]. We follow the original PASTA setup, we conduct experiments with Llama [Touvron et al., 2023], since PASTA currently supports only limited backbones and uses Llama as its default evaluation model. These gains show that A GENT -R ADAR does not depend on a specific steering implemen-tation. 

Spatial Decay and Temporal Decay. Ablating spatial decay consistently degrades performance. The score drops from 39.72 to 28.41 on MuSiQue, from 88.80 to 84.40 on MATH-500, and from 69.40 to 66.20 on MMLU-Pro. Without spatial decay, messages from distant agents may receive similar weight as those from closely collaborating neighbors, potentially steering attention toward indirect or less relevant context. This suggests that modeling topology structure is important for maintaining effective context selection in MAS. Ablating the temporal decay shows a similar pat-tern. The score drops from 39.72 to 32.83 on MuSiQue, from 88.80 to 84.60 on MATH-500, and from 69.40 to 67.80 on MMLU-Pro. Without temporal decay, earlier messages may receive similar importance to more recent and relevant evidence, making the model more vulnerable to distraction from outdated context. This suggests that recency helps filter stale context in multi-round reasoning. We further ablate both decay terms and score context only by semantic similarity. This variant under-performs the full A GENT -R ADAR model. This shows that semantic similarity alone is not sufficient 8for robust context selection in MAS. By incorporating spatial and temporal signals, A GENT -R ADAR 

better distinguishes relevant evidence from merely similar but less useful historical messages. Interestingly, we observe that removing both decay variants can outperform the single-decay ablation variants on MuSiQue and MATH-500. This behavior is reasonable because pure semantic retrieval produces a monolithic score across all candidate messages, whereas removing one decay component may over-weight or under-weight certain messages. For example, spatial-only weighting may favor nearby but outdated messages, while temporal-only weighting may favor recent but structurally distant messages. This can make the selected context less reliable than using semantic similarity alone. Nevertheless, the full A GENT -R ADAR model consistently achieves the strongest performance, indicating that spatial and temporal signals are most effective when combined. 

Efficiency Analysis. We further evaluate whether the added retrieval and attention-steering steps introduce substantial runtime cost. The detailed efficiency comparison is provided in Appendix A.4. AGENT -R ADAR increases end-to-end latency from 968.8 to 1196.5 seconds per task over the orig-inal multi-agent system baseline. In contrast, Compaction and AgentDropout incur larger latency increases due to extra generation, pruning decisions, or topology updates. Overall, A GENT -R ADAR 

incurs a modest computational overhead from context retrieval and attention steering, while deliver-ing substantial improvements in reasoning performance. 

## 5 Related Work 

Context Management in Multi-agent Systems. LLM-based multi-agent systems have demon-strated significant progress in decomposing and resolving complex tasks through collaborative plan-ning, role specialization, and iterative discussion [Guo et al., 2024, Wu et al., 2024, Li et al., 2023, Tian and Zhang, 2026, Tran et al., 2025]. Despite their advantages, agent communication rapidly accumulates into long contexts, burying the original task instruction and other salient evidence in the middle of the interaction history, which can degrade reasoning quality and induce errors [Liu et al., 2024a, Cemri et al., 2025, Zhang et al., 2025b]. Recent work addresses this issue mainly from two directions. First, a line of research improves communication efficiency by sparsifying the communication topology [Li et al., 2024, Zhang et al., 2025a, Wang et al., 2025b, Shen et al., 2025a, Liu et al., 2024c, Zhang et al., 2024a]. Specifically, AgentPrune [Zhang et al., 2025a] and AgentDropout [Wang et al., 2025b] explicitly reduce context redundancy by pruning messages or eliminating redundant agents during multi-round collaboration, thereby lowering token cost while preserving or even improving task performance. Second, recent context learning approaches man-age context by dynamically updating each agent’s instruction during interaction. In particular, Wu et al. [2025] and Chen et al. [2025a] compress or filter the accumulated context history to reduce context length, while M2CL [Hua et al., 2026] learns a context generator to produce round-specific context prompts. In contrast, A GENT -R ADAR preserves the original multi-agent workflow and im-proves how each agent attends to salient information within the accumulated context, enabling more effective use of long interaction histories. 

Attention Steering. Compared to the aforementioned context management methods, attention steering methods directly guide LLMs during inference toward the user-specified part of the con-text, without shortening or rewriting the input. Selective Prompt Anchoring (SPA) [Tian and Zhang, 2025] adjusts the logit distribution to emphasize the specified context. SEKA [Li et al., 2026] learns a universal “relevance subspace" for a given task and constructs a projection matrix that amplifies the relevant features. PASTA [Zhang et al., 2024b] identifies and reweights a subset of attention heads to redirect the model’s attention to user-specified parts. Selective Self-Attention (SSA) [Zhang et al., 2024c] augments the softmax nonlinearity with a principled temperature scal-ing strategy. TOAST [Shi et al., 2023] learns feature selection modules that guide attention toward task-relevant information. However, these methods require manual specification of anchor content, limiting their adaptability to diverse reasoning contexts, especially in multi-agent systems where context evolves autonomously. A GENT -R ADAR addresses this limitation by automatically retriev-ing relevant context from the communication history and steering each agent’s attention toward that context during inference. 96 Limitation 

Despite the consistent improvements observed in our experiments, A GENT -R ADAR has some limi-tations. First, A GENT -R ADAR requires a selection of threshold and decay factors. Our parameter analysis shows that A GENT -R ADAR is relatively robust to these choices. Future work could explore adaptive thresholds and decay factors that are automatically adjusted according to the communica-tion topology, context length, or model uncertainty. Second, the quality of the retrieval signal can impact the performance of A GENT -R ADAR . Stronger retrieval models, uncertainty-aware scoring, or explicit contradiction detection may further improve robustness. Lastly, A GENT -R ADAR requires attention-steering backends such as SPA or PASTA. Our experiments show that the method is not tied to a single backend. Developing more efficient and broadly compatible steering backends would further improve the practicality of A GENT -R ADAR .

## 7 Conclusion 

We presented A GENT -R ADAR , a plug-and-play attention-based context management method for multi-agent systems. Across five benchmarks, A GENT -R ADAR significantly improves performance and outperforms existing context management methods. Our analysis further shows that A GENT -RADAR remains effective across various communication topologies and when the system scales to accommodate more agents and interaction rounds.

## C Experiment Details 

We set the decoding temperature to 1 for all experiments. For efficiency, all local models are run with quantized inference on NVIDIA A5500 GPUs. For SPA, we follow the original Selective Prompt Anchoring setup [Tian and Zhang, 2025] and use the same steering configuration across all experiments. Following Wang et al. [2025b], each multi-agent system consists of five agents and runs for two communication rounds. The final answer is produced by a FinalRefer decision node, which re-ceives the task together with the outputs from the connected agents and is instructed to return only one option letter. We evaluate several communication topologies. Fully connected graphs include all directed edges. Random graphs independently sample non-self spatial edges and temporal edges from Bernoulli masks. Layered graphs randomly assign agents to two layers and connect agents from the earlier layer to the later layer. For AgentPrune and AgentDropout, we follow their official implementations and default configurations. 

C.1 Different Communication Topologies 

In the main experiments, we initialize the communication graph as a random graph. In the anal-ysis experiments, we further evaluate two additional topologies, namely layered graphs and fully connected graphs. Figure 4 demonstrates all MAS communication topologies. 

Random Graph A random graph is constructed by sparsely sampling edges from a complete graph. We follow AgentPrune [Zhang et al., 2025a] for the random-topology setup to ensure a fair compar-ison with pruning-based MAS baselines. Prior work has shown that irregular sparse communication structures can outperform fully connected ones [Qian et al., 2025], partly because random shortcut connections reduce the average path length between agents and induce small-world properties. In-tuitively, such connections allow information to propagate more efficiently across otherwise distant agents. 

Complete Graph In the multi-agent setting, a complete graph is implemented as a directed graph that would become fully connected if converted into its undirected counterpart. After execution according to the topological order, the final agent aggregates the preceding discussion and produces the final answer or reflection. 

Layered Graph A layered graph organizes agents into multiple stages, analogous to the layered structure of a multilayer perceptron. Agents in the first layer first receive the query and generate their responses. These responses are then passed to agents in the next layer together with the original query, and the process continues sequentially across layers. Following Qian et al. [2025], the final layer contains a single agent that integrates the information from the previous layer and outputs the final solution. 

LLM-Debate LLM-Debate [Du et al., 2024] is designed as a multi-round debate process in which, at each round, every agent observes the outputs of all agents from the previous round before generating its own response. As a result, this framework can be viewed as forming a temporally fully connected communication graph. 17 C.2 Compression Implementation

A standard MAS treats all these sentences as ordinary context. Later agents may therefore focus on the caveats or lose track of the original question. A GENT -R ADAR keeps the full transcript available. At the same time, it amplifies the spans that are most relevant to the question. Spatial decay helps decide which earlier messages should matter more. It gives higher weight to messages from agents that are closer to the current receiver in the communication graph. This makes directly useful evidence from nearby collaborators easier to reuse. Temporal decay also helps filter the history. It gives higher weight to recent messages, which often contain refined or corrected reasoning. This prevents stale option discussions from dominating later agents.
