# Introduction 

Creativity—the capacity to generate ideas that are both novel and useful—has long been considered a defining feature of human intelligence [1–3]. For decades, creativity remained the domain where human superiority over machines seemed assured, often described as a hallmark of human intelligence and a “final frontier” for artificial intel-ligence (AI) [2, 4], even as AI systems surpassed structured, closed-ended tasks like image classification, medical diagnostics, and standardized academic exams [5–7]. Yet as large language models (LLMs) increasingly participate in knowledge work—from software engineering to scientific research [8–12], understanding whether and how artificial agents can be genuinely creative becomes a major scientific question. Multi-agent systems have already demonstrated transformative potential across AI domains. From AlphaGo’s self-play training [13] to multi-agent reinforcement learning in complex games [14], interactions among artificial agents have produced capabilities beyond those achievable by isolated agents. This pattern echoes findings from human studies: groups can exhibit measurable collective intelligence that is not reducible to the ability of any single member [15]. Multi-agent LLM systems, where multiple AI instances discuss, critique and refine ideas [16–19], extend this paradigm to artificial agents. Yet whether this approach can generate more creative outcomes than single agents and human teams remains unknown. Here we present a systematic comparison of creativity in human teams and multi-agent LLM systems, evaluating 4,541 ideas from LLMs and 341 ideas from human teams across 71 experimental conditions and six diverse creative problem-solving tasks. All ideas were blindly evaluated by human judges following the standard defini-tion of creativity [3] using the Consensual Assessment Technique [20]. To understand the generative processes underlying creative outcomes, we represent each conversa-tional turn as a vector using neural language model representations, then characterize the resulting trajectory—how broadly it explores semantic space, how coherently it progresses and how it converges toward solutions. 2We find that multi-agent LLM teams substantially outperform human teams in creativity (Cohen’s d = 1 .50 ), driven by novelty advantages while maintaining compa-rable usefulness. Trajectory analysis reveals different patterns: both LLM and human teams produce more creative ideas when conversations range widely, but LLM teams additionally benefit from efficient exploration, while human teams additionally benefit from maintaining smooth conversational flow. Critically, model choice and discus-sion structure function as complementary design levers, together explaining 26.8% of variance in LLM conversational dynamics. This demonstrates that the exploration strategies driving LLMs’ creative advantage can be optimized, providing actionable mechanisms for designing multi-agent ideation systems. 

# LLM Teams Substantially Outperform Humans in Creativity 

LLM Teams Generate More Creative Outputs than Single Agents and Human Teams 

We compared creative outputs from human teams ( N = 341 ideas from 114 teams of three or six members) and multi-agent LLM teams ( N = 4 ,541 ideas from teams of three or six agents) across six creative problem-solving tasks varying in domain and realism (see Methods). We also collected 179 single-agent LLM ideas as a reference baseline. All ideas were mixed and evaluated blind to source by five human raters on novelty and usefulness, with overall creativity operationalized as their product, consistent with standard definitions in creativity research [3]. LLM teams consistently outperformed human teams across all tasks (Table C2), demonstrating that this advantage generalizes across domains. A novelty-usefulness trade-off analysis (Figure 1b) revealed that LLM teams occupy a distinct region of the creativity space: they can generate ideas that are both highly novel and practically useful (upper right quadrant), whereas human ideas exhibit a salient trade-off pattern—ideas tend to be either novel or useful, but rarely both. Beyond mean differences, the LLM advantage extended to the upper tail of the creativity distribution, a critical consideration given that real-world applications often seek the single best solution rather than average performance. Figure 1c shows that the entire LLM distribution was shifted rightward relative to the human distribution, with a substantially higher 95th percentile threshold (LLM: 0.47; Human: 0.31, a 53% increase). The top-scoring LLM idea (creativity = 0.77) exceeded the top human idea (0.48) by 58%. Even among top-rated ideas, LLM teams maintained their advantage (Figure 1d): the top 5% of LLM ideas ( M = 0 .53 , SD = 0 .05 ) substantially exceeded the top 5% of human ideas ( M = 0 .37 , SD = 0 .06 ). This indicated that the LLM advantage extended beyond average performance to the upper tail of the evaluated idea distribution. 

Discussion Structure Shapes Model Creativity 

Discussion structure provided a way to test how interaction shapes creative output, beyond simply comparing single agents with multiple agents. Different protocols varied in how much they encourage idea pooling, critique, revision, and convergence, allowing us to ask which forms of interaction are most associated with higher-quality creative solutions. We tested five discussion structures: Open Discussion (unstructured brainstorm-ing with a summary at the end), Instructed Discussion (structured discussion cycles with options to agree, modify, or replace ideas), Iterative Refinement (proposal-and-selection cycles proposing a single new idea and comparing it to existing ideas), Progressive Improvement (explicit separation of divergent generation prioritizing novelty and convergent refinement enhancing usefulness), and None (no discussion following idea generation and rating). The resulting creative performance varied substantially by model type (Figure 2). For GPT-4.1, discussion structure significantly affected team creativity. Com-pared to no discussion ( M = 0 .212 ), iterative refinement produced the largest gain (M = 0 .308 , β = +0 .097 , p < 0.001 ), followed by progressive ( β = +0 .084 , p < 0.001 )and instructed discussions ( β = +0 .065 , p < 0.001 ). In contrast, o3-high showed min-imal sensitivity to discussion structure, with all protocols producing similar levels of creativity ( M = 0 .322 –0.345 , most p > 0.05 ). # Semantic Trajectory Analysis Reveals Distinctive Mechanisms of Human and LLM Team Creativity 

To investigate the generative processes underlying both human and LLM team cre-ativity, we developed a semantic trajectory analysis framework. Building on a line of work that analyzes semantic relationships through embedding geometry for cul-tural meaning [23], individual semantic production and semantic navigation [24, 25], semantic search and foraging [26, 27] and dialogue coherence [28], we represented multi-agent conversations as paths through neural embedding space [29, 30]. This methodology enabled quantitative characterization of exploration breadth, coherence, and convergence dynamics, which are key features underlying the mechanisms of team creativity. Because trajectory analysis requires sequential turn-by-turn dialogue, we analyzed LLM conversations from the open , iterative , and instructed discussion struc-tures (N=3,574) as well as human discussions (N=308). The Progressive structure, which uses independent generation followed by collaborative refinement rather than sequential turns, was excluded from trajectory analyses but included in all creativity outcome comparisons. We computed nine trajectory features capturing exploration breadth (path length, semantic spread, max distance), coherence (local and global), and dynamics (convergence ratio, curvature, topic switching rate, revisit score) for each conversation (Table C16; see Methods).

However, the additional trajectory features that predict creativity differ between human and LLM teams. # Discussion 

LLMs as Creative Agents: A Large-Scale Validation 

Our study provides the first large-scale, systematically controlled comparison of LLM and human team creativity across model type, team size, persona assignment, discussion structure, and other conversation features. Across six applied creative problem-solving tasks, LLM teams substantially outperformed human teams ( M =0.297 vs. M = 0 .151 , d = 1 .50 ), suggesting that the observed advantage is not con-fined to a single task or domain. This advantage also extended to the upper tail of the distribution: the top LLM idea exceeded the top human idea by 58%, and the top 5% of LLM ideas ( M = 0 .53 ) substantially exceeded the top 5% of human ideas (M = 0 .37 ). This upper-tail result is notable because prior work on individual LLM creativity has found a more mixed pattern: LLMs often match or exceed average human performance on divergent-thinking tasks, but the most creative humans can still outperform LLMs [31–34]. Our findings therefore extend the literature beyond constrained divergent-thinking tasks to applied creative problem-solving, where nov-elty must be integrated with practical usefulness and where the models evaluated here substantially exceeded human team performance. Several factors likely contribute to this divergence from prior work. First, the tasks differ fundamentally. The Divergent Association Task (DAT) and Alternative Uses Task (AUT) are constrained divergent-thinking exercises: generating distant words or unusual object uses. DAT is explicitly scored by semantic distance, while AUT is traditionally evaluated using human or expert ratings of originality, fluency, flex-ibility, and related dimensions, with semantic-distance methods increasingly used as 10 automated proxies [35, 36]. Our tasks instead require integrated creative problem-solving: generating ideas that are simultaneously novel and useful across complex real-world domains, evaluated holistically by blinded human judges using a Consen-sual Assessment Technique-style procedure [20]. LLMs’ extensive training on diverse knowledge domains may confer a particular advantage in associating concepts across fields to produce applied solutions, a strength less relevant when the task is simply to name unrelated words. Second, automated semantic-distance scoring captures only one dimension of creativity, whereas human judges can assess both novelty and useful-ness, dimensions where LLMs’ capacity for cross-domain synthesis may be especially apparent. Third, we tested more recent models, including GPT-4.1 and o3, that may reflect capability advances over those in many prior studies. 

Distinct Conversational Patterns Underlie LLM and Human Team Creativity 

Trajectory analysis reveals distinct conversational predictors of creativity. Both LLM and human teams benefit from thematic deviation (negative global coherence; LLM: 

β = −0.400 , p < 0.001 ; human: β = −0.463 , p < 0.001 ), a pattern consistent with what Nijstad et al. [37] term “cognitive flexibility.” However, the remaining predictive features diverge significantly between the two groups. For LLM teams, highly creative outcomes are strongly linked to efficient explo-ration, characterized by high semantic spread ( β = +0 .227 , p < 0.001 ) combined with shorter paths ( β = −0.122 , p < 0.001 ). This indicates that targeted divergence matters more than extensive wandering. Conversely, human creativity is most strongly asso-ciated with discourse coherence. Features such as trajectory curvature ( β = +0 .207 ,

p < 0.001 ) and local coherence ( β = +0 .233 , p < 0.001 ) suggest a pattern of smooth transitions and frequent directional pivots that maintain conversational flow. Ultimately, LLM creative outcomes appear more strongly associated with observ-able semantic geometry, including exploration and convergence patterns measurable directly from text. In contrast, human creativity likely depends on unobserved psychosocial factors beyond semantic trajectories, such as tacit knowledge, social dynamics, embodied intuitions, and serendipitous associations [1, 38, 39]. As trajec-tory features possess substantial predictive power for LLMs, these metrics can inform the optimization of conversational design. This establishes a foundation for actively engineering, rather than merely evaluating, multi-agent creativity. 

Design Levers Enable Systematic Control of Conversational Patterns 

Our results identify two experimentally manipulable levers that together shape con-versational patterns: model choice and discussion structure. These operate through distinct mechanisms, creating a two-dimensional design space. 

Model choice determines the baseline level of internal reasoning. Trajectory features explained 46.4% of creativity variance for reasoning models (e.g., o3-high)—substantially more than those of standard models (GPT-4.1: 27.9%). This reflects o3’s capacity for extended internal deliberation: reasoning models perform 11 structured exploration without external prompting. The practical implication is that reasoning models require less external scaffolding but show ceiling effects where additional structure provides diminishing returns. 

Discussion structure modulates the exploration-exploitation balance. Relative to the no-discussion multi-agent baseline, iterative refinement increased GPT-4.1 cre-ativity by 45.3% ( M = 0 .308 vs. M = 0 .212 ), but produced smaller gains for o3-high (+6.5%; M = 0 .343 vs. M = 0 .322 ), suggesting that internal and external reasoning capabilities may substitute for each other. This aligns with recent evidence that rea-soning models perform internal multi-agent-like deliberation [40], reducing the benefit of external scaffolding. Discussion structure primarily controlled exploration breadth (max distance: 40.6% variance explained by discussion vs. 2.7% by model), while model type primarily controlled coherence patterns (local coherence: 31.4% by model vs. 11.4% by discussion). Together, these levers explained 26.8% of trajectory variance, demonstrating that conversation dynamics are experimentally controllable design parameters rather than fixed model properties. Several highly controllable features were also linked to cre-ative outcomes: global coherence and max distance were associated with LLM team creativity, while local coherence was more strongly associated with human team cre-ativity. This suggests that design choices can target conversational patterns relevant to creativity. The core implication is that maximizing team creativity requires pairing the model with a complementary discussion structure: standard models appear to benefit most from sequential, iterative scaffolding to push them into broad semantic exploration, while reasoning models maintain high creativity across both structured and unstruc-tured protocols (like the open condition), consistent with the possibility that their internal reasoning processes reduce the need for external conversational scaffolds. 

Trajectory Analysis as a Generalizable Framework 

The semantic trajectory framework provides a quantitative lens for understanding how creative outcomes emerge from conversation dynamics. By representing discussions as paths through embedding space, we operationalized abstract concepts like explo-ration, coherence, and convergence as measurable geometric features drawing on prior work in semantic memory, discourse coherence, and creative search (see Table C16 for definitions and theoretical foundations). Our findings establish the practical utility of this framework. First, trajectory features predict 32.6% of creativity variance in LLMs, confirming that discussion struc-ture carries meaningful information about creative outcomes. Second, design choices explain 26.8% of trajectory variance, confirming that these predictive features are sensitive to experimental intervention. The key insight is not that any individual pre-dictor is controllable in isolation, but that the features most strongly associated with creativity, e.g., global coherence and max distance, are also among the most exper-imentally responsive, creating a tractable pathway from design decisions to creative outcomes. Beyond validating existing methods, our framework offers a hypothesis-generating approach for designing new interaction mechanisms. While state-of-the-art LLMs 12 remain black boxes that prohibit direct manipulation of model weights or training pro-cedures, our results demonstrate that multi-agent creativity can be shaped through accessible structural levers. When deploying new foundation models, it is challenging to predict a priori which discussion structure will maximize creativity. However, the trajectory framework provides a generalizable diagnostic heuristic: practitioners can compute trajectory features from a small set of pilot conversations to diagnose a new model’s default semantic behavior, and then select the discussion structure that corrects its geomet-ric deficits. If a baseline model produces narrow, highly coherent paths (low max distance, high global coherence), developers should impose forcing protocols (like iter-ative refinement) to artificially broaden exploration. Conversely, if a model naturally generates highly divergent leaps, designers should implement structures that help agents synthesize those disparate concepts cohesively. The complementary findings established here support the practical utility of this approach: trajectory features predict 32.6% of creativity variance in LLMs, and exper-imental design choices explain 26.8% of trajectory variance. By understanding a model’s default semantic trajectory, practitioners can rely on quantifiable geometric targets rather than trial-and-error to optimize the exploration-exploitation balance. More broadly, this work offers a form of system-level interpretability into multi-agent AI systems, shedding light on how agent interactions produce creative outputs even as individual models remain inscrutable. As multi-agent systems are increasingly applied to scientific discovery [10, 12], the same trajectory diagnostics could also help assess whether AI research agents explore solution spaces effectively or become trapped in narrow search patterns. 

Implications for Creativity Theory 

Our findings contribute to creativity theory and practice in multiple ways. First, we demonstrate that the divergent-convergent cycle [41] can be computationally implemented through multi-agent discussions, with measurable geometric signatures distinguishing exploration from exploitation phases. Second, we provide the first quan-titative comparison of human and LLM team-level creative processes using a common metric (semantic trajectories), revealing that trajectory features capture a substantial proportion of the creative process for both LLM and human teams. Third, we extend team creativity research [42, 43] to artificial agent collectives, showing that coor-dination mechanisms and structural interventions transfer from human to machine teams. 

Limitations 

Several considerations contextualize the scope of our findings. First, creativity is conceptually broad, encompassing everyday problem-solving, artistic expression, and scientific discovery. We operationalize creativity as the product of novelty and useful-ness [3], assessed through the Consensual Assessment Technique [20]—the definition that has enabled cumulative progress in creativity research and captures the dimen-sion most relevant to practical applications. This operationalization is well-suited to 13 the problem-solving domain we examine where both originality and feasibility mat-ter. Domains with different evaluative criteria—such as pure aesthetic judgment or paradigm-shifting theoretical insight—may require adapted metrics, though the fun-damental relationship between conversation structure and creative outcomes should generalize. Second, our trajectory framework analyzes text-based conversations through semantic embeddings. This focus enables rigorous quantification and experimental control while addressing problems of immediate practical importance. The underlying principle of representing creative exploration as movement through representa-tional space is domain-general, and the geometric patterns we identify (exploration breadth, coherence, convergence) reflect fundamental cognitive processes. Exten-sion to multimodal creativity (e.g., visual design, musical composition) will require domain-appropriate representational spaces but can build on the mechanistic insights established here. Finally, our study compares LLM teams to human teams but does not examine human-AI collaboration. Our contribution is foundational: we establish that multi-agent LLM systems achieve high creativity and identify the mechanisms (trajectory patterns) and design levers (model choice, discussion structure) associated with cre-ative outcomes. Human-AI collaboration introduces distinct design considerations including real-time coordination protocols, integration of complementary knowledge, and dynamic task allocation between human and AI team members. Our mechanistic findings that specific trajectory features predict creativity and respond to experimen-tal manipulation provide the empirical foundation for addressing these questions in human-AI hybrid systems. 

Conclusion 

In creativity, a domain often considered distinctively human, we observe that multi-agent LLM teams substantially outperform human teams ( d = 1.50 ), primarily driven by novelty advantages while maintaining comparable usefulness. Based on a novel semantic trajectory analysis framework, we reveal distinct exploration patterns between human and LLM teams: both groups produce more creative ideas when conversations range widely, but LLM teams additionally benefit from efficient explo-ration (high semantic spread, shorter paths), while human teams additionally benefit from maintaining smooth conversational flow (high local coherence, high curvature). These exploration strategies are shaped by design choices: model selection and discus-sion structure function as complementary levers that together explain a substantial proportion of trajectory variance. This framework offers a systematic approach for understanding how design decisions influence conversational dynamics and creative outcomes. Rather than relying on trial-and-error experimentation, researchers can use these insights to target specific trajectory features, such as exploration breadth or coherence, to increase creativity. By identifying quantifiable targets and manip-ulable parameters, this work provides a foundation for more principled design and optimization of multi-agent creative systems. 14 Methods 

Human Data Collection 

Participants. We recruited 462 participants (40 teams of six and 74 teams of three) through university and college mailing lists and the SONA Research Management system maintained by Cambridge Judge Business School. Eligibility criteria required participants to be at least 18 years old and fluent in English. Participants received £15 for approximately one hour of participation. Of the 462 participants who took part, 460 provided demographic information. 270 (58.7%) were female, and 188 (40.9%) male, and two (0.4%) identified as other. In terms of ethnicity, 96 (20.9%) were White (British, Irish, other), 72 (15.7%) Black/Black British (Caribbean, African, other), 114 (24.8%) Asian/Asian British (Indian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi, other), 124 (27.0%) Chinese/Chinese British, 22 (4.8%) of mixed race, 24 (5.2%) of other ethnic group, and eight (1.7%) chose not to reveal ethnicity. Mean age was 27.8 (SD = 6.0, range 18–60). 168 (36.5%) had or were studying for an undergraduate degree, 255 (55.4%) a graduate degree, and 28 (6.1%) a doctorate degree. Eight (1.7%) had a high school diploma or A-levels and one (0.2%) chose not to reveal their highest educational level. 249 (54.1%) were currently students, 82 (17.8%) unemployed and job-seeking, 52 (11.3%) full-time employed, 34 (7.4%) part-time employed, 16 (3.5%) self-employed, 14 (3.0%) due to start a new job, one (0.2%) not in paid work (looking after family or home), one (0.2%) not in paid work (sick or disabled), five (1.1%) in other employment status, and six (1.3%) chose not to reveal employment status. There are a total of 342 human ideas, among which 341 are valid (one was removed during rating). 308 (from 103 unique teams) of them have complete recordings of team discussions. 

Procedure. When signing up for the experiment, participants formed teams of three or six members. Each team completed one of two task sets comprising three problems each in an random order. Each team was allowed up to 12 minutes per task and required to submit a single solution. Following each session, participants indi-vidually completed surveys capturing demographic variables (age, gender, ethnicity, education, employment, work experience, professional area, discipline, etc.) and Big Five personality profiles. 

Transcription. Discussions were transcribed using Microsoft Azure’s speech-to-text service with speaker diarization. Validation on 12 discussions (1,501 utterances) showed 93.3% semantic accuracy and 89.7% speaker diarization accuracy. 

LLM Data Collection 

Multi-Agent System. We implemented a multi-agent system using four types of LLM settings: all agents based on GPT-4.1 [44], o3-high, or o3-low (o3 with reasoning effort set to high/low) [45], and mixed model ensembles combining DeepSeek-R1 [46], Gemini-2.5-Pro [47], and o3-default. Models were accessed via Azure OpenAI API (GPT-4.1, o3, DeepSeek-R1) and Google AI Studio (Gemini-2.5-Pro). For GPT-4.1 and mixed teams, temperature was set to 1.0; o3 models do not support tempera-ture parameters. Teams comprised three or six agents. GPT-4.1 was tested across all 15 experimental conditions, while o3-high, o3-low, and mixed models were tested with three-agent teams using “different” personas. This design choice allowed us to compre-hensively map GPT-4.1’s behavior while efficiently testing whether reasoning models exhibit qualitatively different patterns. 

Persona Manipulation. Agents were assigned one of three persona conditions according to prior work in persona prompting [48, 49]: (1) none : generic agent identity (“You are Agent 1”), (2) same : all agents received identical creative persona descrip-tions drawn from real human participants, (3) different : each agent received distinct persona descriptions from different human participants, emphasizing varied creative thinking styles. 

Experimental Design. We systematically varied design features including model type, team size (three or six agents), persona assignment (none/same/different), discussion structure (open/instructed/iterative/progressive), and conversation struc-ture (with/without initial generation phase), yielding 71 experimental conditions. The study was pre-registered prior to data collection (registered 2 May 2025; https: //aspredicted.org/4tht-m898.pdf). The pre-registration specified the dependent vari-able (creativity ratings via Consensual Assessment Technique), independent variables (conversation mechanism, number of agents, persona configuration, discussion length and order, model choice), and planned analyses (t-tests, ANOVA, linear mixed mod-els, and exploratory linguistic feature analysis). The trajectory analysis framework extends the pre-registered exploratory text-analysis plan. See Section A.1 for complete implementation details. 

Discussion Structures. We tested five protocols operationalizing distinct col-laborative mechanisms [42, 43, 50]: (1) Open Discussion : unstructured brainstorming with a summarization step at the end, (2) Instructed Discussion : structured discus-sion cycles with options to agree/modify/replace ideas from a shared pool generated by the model in the initial idea generation stage, (3) Iterative Refinement : proposal-and-selection cycles proposing a single new idea and comparing it to existing ones, (4) Progressive Improvement : explicit separation of divergent generation (prioritizing novelty) and convergent refinement (enhancing usefulness), and (5) None : no collabo-rative discussion following initial idea generation and rating. Some conditions included initial independent idea generation and rating before discussion; others proceeded directly to collaborative discussion. See Section A.1 for detailed implementation of each protocol. 

Data Collection. A total of 4,740 ideas were generated. Excluding ideas identified as either identical to other ideas or non-relevant to the problem-solving tasks (receiving at least three zero ratings among the five judges) yielded a total of 4,541 multi-agent ideas and 179 single-agent ideas across six creative problem-solving tasks. 

Creative Problem-solving Tasks 

Six creative problem-solving tasks were designed to span diverse domains and sys-tematically address potential memorization concerns (Table 3). Tasks comprised two societal challenges (plastic waste, educational inequality), two business problems (sup-ply chain, employee attrition), and two hypothetical scenarios deliberately unlikely to appear in training data (sorry pandemic, singing in shower). This balanced design 16 tests whether LLM performance stems from generalization and novel combinatorial generation rather than the retrieval of memorized solutions. Empirically, LLM teams exhibit higher creativity across all six tasks (see Results), providing evidence against memorization as the primary mechanism.

Creativity Evaluation 

To ensure that creativity ratings reflected idea content rather than stylistic differ-ences between human and LLM outputs, all ideas underwent a standardization process before evaluation. Using GPT-4.1 (temperature=0), each idea was paraphrased into a consistent format: third-person perspective, active voice, accessible language, and approximately 100 words. The paraphrasing prompt instructed the model to pre-serve core meaning while removing stylistic markers such as branded concept names, excessive technical jargon, and transformation language (e.g., “evolve X into Y”). We developed the harmonization prompt through iterative pilot testing to reduce stylistic cues while preserving each idea’s substantive mechanism, and then applied the final-ized prompt uniformly to all human and LLM-generated ideas. This harmonization minimized surface-level differences, encouraging judges to evaluate the substance of ideas rather than stylistic cues that might signal their origin. See Section A.2 for the complete harmonization prompt. To mitigate presentation bias and maintain evaluator blinding, ideas were pre-sented to judges in a single randomized order that intermixed human and LLM-generated ideas within each task. All five judges evaluated ideas in the same 17 randomized sequence, controlling for presentation order while preserving rating independence across judges. All solutions (human and LLM-generated) were independently evaluated by five trained human judges on novelty (originality relative to other solutions) and use-fulness (practicality and feasibility) using a 10-point scale, following the Consensual Assessment Technique [20]. If an idea was considered irrelevant to the task, it would receive a zero rating. Before full annotation, judges rated pilot sets (80 ideas for each task) and participated in calibration sessions to establish shared anchors. Inter-rater reliability was assessed using intraclass correlation coefficient (two-way mixed-effects for consistency average measure ICC 3,5): Novelty (Mean ICC = 0.83, minimum 0.73), Usefulness (Mean ICC = 0.75, minimum 0.70). Trajectory Analysis 

Turn Embeddings. Each conversational turn was embedded using Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B [51], chosen for its balance of semantic fidelity and computational efficiency. 

Trajectory Analysis Scope. Semantic trajectory analysis requires sequential conversational turns to compute features like local coherence, path length, and curva-ture. The progressive discussion structure—where agents independently generate ideas followed by a single collaborative refinement phase—does not produce the multi-turn sequential structure necessary for trajectory computation. Consequently, trajectory analyses were conducted on conversations from the open , iterative , and instructed 

structures, which all feature turn-by-turn dialogue. The progressive conversations were included in overall creativity comparisons but excluded from trajectory feature analyses. 

Trajectory Features. Motivated by prior work on semantic memory, discourse coherence, semantic foraging, and divergent-thinking assessment, we computed nine trajectory features for each conversation [26–28, 52]. These features are not intended as previously validated one-to-one measures of creativity mechanisms; rather, they are study-specific operationalizations of three theoretically motivated dimensions of semantic exploration: coherence (local and global coherence), exploration breadth (path length, semantic spread, max distance), and convergence dynamics (convergence ratio, curvature, topic switching rate, revisit score). Formal definitions are provided in Appendix Table C16. Trajectory features were standardized before analysis. We assessed multicollinear-ity by computing variance inflation factors (VIF) for all features. All VIF values were 18 below 10, indicating that multicollinearity does not pose a concern for regression coefficient estimation. We retained all nine features in subsequent analyses. 

LLM Usage 

Large language models were used in two distinct ways in this study. First, LLMs were the experimental systems under investigation: GPT-4.1, o3-high, o3-low, DeepSeek-R1, Gemini-2.5-Pro, and mixed-model teams were used to generate the LLM-team conversations and final ideas analyzed in the paper, as described in the Methods. Second, GPT-4.1 was used as a preprocessing tool to harmonize human and LLM-generated ideas before blinded human evaluation; the finalized harmonization prompt is reported in Section A.2. The authors also used LLM-based tools to assist with code drafting, debugging, data-analysis workflow organization, and language editing. A.1 Experimental Design and Implementation Details 

A.1.1 Complete Experimental Design 

This appendix provides comprehensive implementation details for all experimental manipulations described in the Methods section. Table C3 presents the complete factorial experimental design spanning 71 conditions. 

Conversation Structure. A variety of conditions were designed following Osborn’s original principles [50]: defer judgment, encourage wild ideas, build on others’ contributions, and aim for quantity. Some conditions included an initial generation-and-selection phase where agents generated ideas before discussion. In conditions without initial generation, agents proceeded directly to the collaborative discussion phase, developing ideas from scratch during discussion. 

A.1.2 Discussion Structure Implementation Details 

Each experimental condition comprised up to two components: an idea-generation-and-selection phase and a discussion phase . The idea-generation-and-selection phase, when present, produced an initial candidate idea and, in some conditions, a replace-ment pool. The discussion phase specified how agents interacted with the current idea: freely brainstorming, modifying or replacing the idea under explicit action rules, iteratively proposing and rating alternatives, or separating divergent generation from convergent refinement. Thus, “discussion structure” refers to the protocol governing agent interaction after, or in the absence of, the initial generation-and-selection phase. 

No Discussion/None. This condition served as a multi-agent baseline without subsequent collaborative discussion. Agents first generated candidate ideas in response to the task prompt. In the interactive generation mode, agents could see previously generated ideas from other agents and were instructed to produce different ideas. In the nominal generation mode, agents generated ideas independently without seeing others’ contributions, mirroring nominal group technique [21]. Agents then rated all generated ideas for creativity on a 1–10 scale, explicitly considering both novelty and usefulness. The highest- rated idea, based on the mean agent rating, was selected as the final team output. No further discussion or refinement occurred. 

Open Discussion. This method implemented unstructured brainstorming, where agents were not provided with explicit turn-taking rules or discussion structure beyond the task description. After reaching the maximum turn limit, one randomly selected agent was prompted to synthesize the entire discussion history into a single coherent final idea (80-100 words), mimicking the consensus-building phase typical of human brainstorming sessions. 

Instructed Discussion. This condition imposed an explicit revise-or-accept structure on the conversation. The discussion began either from the top-ranked idea selected during the initial generation-and-selection phase or, in conditions without 29 that phase, from an idea generated at the start of discussion. At each turn, the active agent evaluated the current idea and selected one of three actions: 

• Agree : Accept the current idea without changes (response: “Agree: No changes needed”). In some conditions, agreement was disabled until a minimum number of discussion rounds (30) had elapsed, forcing extended exploration. 

• Modify : Propose a substantially revised version of the current idea, with an explicit requirement for major improvements rather than minor edits (response format: “Modify: [full revised idea] - Reason: [justification]”). 

• Replace : Substitute the current idea with an alternative either from a replacement pool or newly generated by the agent (response format: “Replace: [full replacement idea] - Reason: [justification]”). The replacement pool varied by condition. In no-pool conditions, agents gener-ated a new replacement idea when choosing to replace. In pool conditions, the five next-highest-ranked ideas from the initial generation-and-selection phase formed a shared replacement pool: the top-ranked idea became the initial current idea, while ideas ranked 2–6 were available as replacements. Once a pooled idea was used, it was removed to prevent reuse. If the pool was exhausted, agents generated new replace-ment ideas. The instructed discussion ended when all agents agreed on the current idea or when the turn limit was reached. 

• No pool (size = 0): If replacement was attempted, agents were instructed to generate an entirely new idea rather than selecting one from the pool. 

• Replacement pool (size = 5): In some conditions, the top-rated five ideas (ranked 2-6, excluding the top-ranked idea which became the first current idea) formed a shared replacement pool accessible to all agents. When an agent replaced the current idea with one from this pool, the used idea was removed from the pool, preventing reuse. If all ideas from the replacement pool were used, the agent would be asked to generate a new idea The discussion concluded when all agents agreed on the current idea or the turn limit was reached. 

Hand-Raising Mechanism. A subset of GPT-4.1 instructed-discussion condi-tions tested an alternative turn-taking rule inspired by human meeting dynamics. Instead of using a fixed or random speaking order, agents self-selected based on their perceived ability to contribute. At each turn, agents who had not yet agreed rated their desire to speak on a 1–7 scale according to how much value their potential contribution would add. The agent with the highest self-rated desire spoke next. 

Iterative Refinement. This method implemented iterative refinement with back-tracking capability [53]. Unlike instructed discussion, which only allowed replacement from a pool of alternatives, iterative refinement maintained a constrained memory of recently explored ideas. The process began with the initial idea, which could be newly generated by the starting agent or the top-ranked idea from the initial generation-and-selection phase. At each turn: 30 1. One agent generated a new candidate idea intended to be more creative than existing ideas. 2. All agents rated the new candidate, the current idea, and recently explored past ideas on creativity (1-10 scale). 3. The highest-rated idea became the new current idea for the next iteration. This allowed the system to either adopt the new modification, retain the current idea, or backtrack to a recently considered alternative. This proposal-and-evaluation cycle continued until one of two stopping criteria was met: 

• Convergence : The same idea was selected (rated highest) for three or six consecutive rounds depending on the team size, indicating the team had reached a consensus. 

• Turn limit : No convergence condition was reached despite reaching the turn limit. 

Progressive improvement. This method explicitly separated divergent and convergent thinking phases [41], operationalizing the theoretical distinction between exploration (generating novel possibilities) and exploitation (refining promising can-didates). After the initial generation-and-selection phase, two further phases were implemented: 

Phase 1 - Divergent Generation : Each agent generated 5 radically novel ideas inspired by, but distinct from, the top-ranked ideas from the initial generation-and-selection round. Agents were explicitly instructed to prioritize novelty over usefulness, to think transformatively about fundamental shifts in approach, and to avoid simply combining existing ideas. 

Phase 2 - Convergent Refinement : Agents rated all ideas on novelty (not overall creativity), and the top-ranked novel ideas advanced to a refinement phase. Each agent then refined these ideas to enhance usefulness while carefully preserving the core novelty. This two-phase structure tests whether explicitly separating exploration and exploitation yields higher creativity than methods that interleave these processes. 

A.1.3 Conversation Length and Termination 

Conversation length varied systematically across conditions: 

• 30 turns : Standard length for most conditions, balancing exploration time with computational cost. 

• 60 turns : Extended length for open discussion conditions, testing whether longer conversations enable more complex semantic trajectories. 

• Minimum thresholds : Some instructed discussion conditions enforced a minimum of 30 discussion rounds before agreement was permitted, preventing premature convergence.

Within 3-agent teams with “different” personas and discussion phases (N=2,983), the hand-raising mechanism reduced creativity relative to random ordering (β = −0.015 , p = 0 .003 ), while fixed ordering showed no significant effect ( β = −0.003 ,

p = 0 .40 ). Within instructed discussions specifically (N=1,785), where all models had all three order conditions, the hand-raising effect remained ( β = −0.014 , p = 0 .009 ). This suggests that the hand-raising mechanism may reduce exploration by favoring confident agents, hence undermining team-level creativity. 

Idea generation mode. Among no-discussion conditions (N=240 with three-agent “different” persona teams), nominal brainstorming (independent generation) reduced creativity compared to interactive generation ( β = −0.033 , p = 0.008 ;36 Table C8), driven primarily by reduced novelty. This indicates that exposure to oth-ers’ ideas during generation, rather than sheer quantity of independent ideas, drives creative outcomes. 

Discussion length. We tested whether discussion length (30 vs. 60 for open discussion and 30 vs. 60 with minimum 30 rounds for instructed discussion) affected creativity, controlling for discussion structure, model, persona, team size, and task (N=3,702; Table C7). Results showed no significant effect of discussion length (LR test p = 0 .72 , ∆R2 < 0.001 ), indicating that discussion protocol quality matters more than duration. 

A.4 Trajectory Analysis 

We analyzed semantic trajectories by computing turn-level embeddings for all con-versations using Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B and extracting nine trajectory features capturing exploration dynamics, coherence, and refinement patterns. This appendix provides complete technical details, regression results, and illustrative examples. 

A.4.1 Trajectory Features 

Table C16 defines all trajectory features used in our analysis. These features capture three key aspects of semantic exploration: coherence (how connected ideas are), explo-ration (how broadly the conversation searches semantic space), and dynamics (how the trajectory evolves over time). All features are grounded in prior work on semantic memory [54], discourse coherence [55], and creative search [27]. 

A.4.2 Regression Results 

Tables C17 and C18 present full regression results for LLM and human team creativity, respectively. We fit hierarchical models to isolate the contribution of trajectory fea-tures while controlling for task, model type, and discussion structure. For LLM teams, trajectory features explain 32.6% of creativity variance (conditional R ²), with global coherence and path length as the strongest predictors. For human teams, trajectory features explain 17.0% of variance, with trajectory curvature and local coherence as key predictors. Table C20 directly compares coefficients, revealing both shared and distinct mechanisms: Both groups benefit from broad semantic exploration (negative global coherence), but differ in their additional strategies—LLMs benefit from efficient explo-ration (high semantic spread, low path length), while humans benefit from smooth iterative refinement (high curvature, high local coherence). 

A.4.3 Model-Specific Variance Decomposition 

Trajectory features explained different amounts of variance across LLM model types (Table C19). Substantially more variance was explained in reasoning models (o3-high: 46.4%, o3-low: 36.1%) than in standard models (GPT-4.1: 27.9%), suggesting that reasoning models produce more structured and predictable conversation dynamics. The mixed model condition showed the lowest explained variance (19.1%), consistent with less structured generation patterns. 37 A.4.4 Experimental Manipulability of Trajectory Features 

To assess whether trajectory features can be experimentally controlled, we regressed each trajectory feature on discussion structure and model type (controlling for task). Hierarchical regression models revealed that discussion structure and model type together explained 26.8% of trajectory variance on average across all nine features (Table C21). The additive model (discussion + model, no interaction) explained 22.8%, with interaction effects contributing an additional 4.0%, indicating that opti-mal discussion structures vary by model type. Highly manipulable features included max distance, global coherence, and local coherence, for which the full design model explained 51.7%, 52.6%, and 43.4% of feature variance, respectively. Discus-sion structure primarily controlled exploration breadth (e.g., max distance: 40.6% from discussion vs. 2.7% from model), while model type primarily controlled coher-ence patterns (e.g., local coherence: 31.4% from model vs. 11.4% from discussion). The interaction model outperforms the additive model, with features like seman-tic spread showing substantial interaction effects (13.6% additional variance, 55% of total; Table C22). This supports an effective pathway: design choices explain 26.8% of trajectory variance, and trajectory patterns predict 32.6% of creativity variance, demonstrating that conversation structure is both experimentally manipulable and associated with creative outcomes. 

A.4.5 Illustrative Examples 

Figure B1 and Table C24 illustrate these mechanistic differences through two con-versations from the same condition (instructed discussion, GPT-4.1, plastic waste task). The high-creativity conversation (rating = 0.551) exhibits dramatic conceptual leaps—from economic incentives to blockchain to biotechnology to living organ-isms—resulting in broad semantic exploration (global coherence = -0.91). In contrast, the low-creativity conversation (rating = 0.090) iteratively refines a single core con-cept (competitive plastic reduction events), staying tightly clustered in semantic space (global coherence = +2.20). These examples demonstrate how trajectory features capture the process by which creative ideas emerge through multi-turn multi-agent conversations. 

A.5 Robustness Checks Robustness to nested idea structure 

The primary LLM-human comparison is reported at the idea level because creativity ratings were assigned to individual ideas. However, ideas are not fully independent: they are nested within tasks and within generating teams or system conditions. We therefore estimated robustness models that account for this structure. First, we fit an ordinary least squares model predicting normalized product-based creativity from source condition (LLM versus human), including task fixed effects and heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors. The LLM advantage remained large, 

β = 0 .146 , 95% CI [0.135, 0.156], p < 0.001 .38 Second, we repeated the task-adjusted model with standard errors clustered by generating cluster, defined as the LLM condition-by-task cluster for LLM ideas and the human team/session identifier for human ideas. This produced a similar estimate, 

β = 0 .146 , 95% CI [0.134, 0.157], p < 0.001 .Third, we aggregated to task-level LLM-human differences as a conservative check. The LLM advantage was positive for all six tasks, with a mean task-level difference of 0.148 , t(5) = 8 .68 , p < 0.001 .Finally, a mixed-effects model with random effects for task and generating cluster also supported the same conclusion, β = 0 .132 , 95% CI [0.086, 0.179], p < 0.001 . Thus, the main LLM-human difference was robust to analyses that relax the assumption of independent idea-level observations. 

A.5.1 Embedding Model Robustness 

To ensure our trajectory metrics were not artifacts of the specific embedding model, we re-computed all semantic trajectory features using a substantially larger model (Qwen3-Embedding-4B, 4 billion parameters) compared to the Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B model used in the main analysis. Table C23 shows that while explained variance decreased modestly (LLM: 17.6% → 14.7%; Human: 15.3% → 13.7%), key trajectory features retained their sign and statistical significance. Specifically, global coherence remained negatively associated with creativity (LLM: β = −0.400 vs. −0.211 , both 

p < 0.001 ), while semantic spread remained positively associated (LLM: β = 0 .227 

vs. 0.131 , both p < 0.001 ). This consistency across embedding models with differ-ent capacities and training procedures supports the validity of our trajectory-based approach. 

A.5.2 Creativity Operationalization Robustness 

Our main analysis defined creativity as the product of novelty and usefulness (Novelty 

× Usefulness), following established practice in creativity research. To test sensitiv-ity to this choice, we re-ran all analyses using an additive operationalization (Novelty + Usefulness). Table C25 demonstrates highly consistent results: coefficient patterns were nearly identical, and the core LLM creativity advantage remained robust (mul-tiplicative: Cohen’s d = 1 .50 ; additive: d = 1 .37 , both p < 0.001 ). The slight decrease in effect size for the additive measure is expected, as the multiplicative definition bet-ter captures the synergistic relationship between novelty and usefulness that defines genuinely creative ideas. 

A.5.3 Inference-Time Scaling Control 

To rule out the possibility that discussion structure effects reflect inference-time scaling (i.e., more tokens leading to higher creativity), we regressed creativity on dis-cussion structure while controlling for total token count. As shown in Table C26, token count contributed negligible additional variance ( ∆R2 = 0 .06% ) and had a non-significant coefficient near zero. Discussion structure effects (iterative vs. progressive: 39 β = +0 .016 , p < 0.01 ; open vs. progressive: β = −0.030 , p < 0.001 ) remained sig-nificant with this control, indicating that creativity gains reflect discussion structure rather than computational scaling.
