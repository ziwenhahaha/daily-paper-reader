# Micro-Swarm Locomotion Optimization in Dynamic Flow using Multi-Objective Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning 

Josef Berman and Oren Gal 

Abstract вҖ”Coordinating micro-robotic swarms in physiologi-cally realistic, time-dependent fluid environments remains an unsolved challenge for biomedical and environmental applica-tions. We present a hybrid Computational Fluid DynamicsвҖ“Multi-Objective Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (CFD-MO-MARL) framework that directly couples a high-fidelity in-compressible Navier-Stokes solver with decentralized proximal policy optimization to learn physically consistent swarm control strategies in oscillatory flow. Sixteen magnetically actuated micro-robots navigate a pulsatile arterial waveform (peak systolic velocity 400 mm/s) within a 2 mm channel, simultaneously op-timizing upstream progression, energy conservation, and motion smoothness. Competing policy gradients across these objectives are reconciled using Projected Conflicting Gradient (PCGrad) surgery, which projects conflicting gradient components into orthogonal subspaces to prevent destructive inter-objective in-terference. Without PCGrad, energy efficiency and smoothness rewards collapse to near zero within 10,000 training steps while progress exhibits persistent large-amplitude oscillations, confirming that gradient conflict resolution is a structural re-quirement rather than an optional refinement in this domain. The converged policy achieves a progress reward of 6.5-7.0, a sustained energy efficiency of 0.63вҖ“0.65, and near-maximum smoothness (0.97вҖ“0.99), representing improvements exceeding 8 reward units over brute-force baselines on the primary objective while both baselines yield negative energy efficiency throughout. Training reveals three emergent behavioral phases not encoded in the reward function: a collective two-layer hydrodynamic throttling formation that suppresses peak channel velocities from 700 mm/s to approximately 400 mm/s during forward flow, a cycle-synchronized ratchet mechanism that exploits flow reversals for upstream repositioning, and an individualized final approach as agents near the success boundary. These results establish that time-dependent fluid-agent interactions can be captured directly within multi-objective reinforcement learning loops, offering a scalable and physically grounded paradigm for micro-swarm control in biomedical navigation, environmental monitoring, and industrial microfluidics. 

Index Terms вҖ”Multi-Robot Systems, Model-Learning for Con-trol, Learning and Adaptive Systems, Swarms. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# COORDINATING the locomotion of micro-robotic swarms in time-dependent fluid environments remains a pivotal challenge for both biomedical and environmental applications. At microscopic scales, viscous forces dominate inertial effects, such that reciprocal actuation produces no net displacement вҖ“ a phenomenon known as PurcellвҖҷs вҖңScallop TheoremвҖқ [1]. To achieve propulsion, micro-swimmers must therefore employ non-reciprocal strategies that break time-reversal symmetry. When the ambient flow oscillates, as in

Moreover, hydrodynamic coupling among micro-swimmers produces time-varying wakes and vortices, which may either stabilize or destabilize swarm formations depend-ing on phase relationships and inter-swimmer spacing [4]. The core problem therefore lies in devising control paradigms that enable a micro-swarm to maintain smoothness of action, conserve energy, and sustain directed progression against or across oscillatory flows. 

A. Micro-swimmer Propulsion Mechanisms 

At the microscale, where viscous forces dominate inertial effects, propulsion must circumvent the constraints of the вҖңscallop theoremвҖқ, which prohibits net motion via reciprocal strokes in a Newtonian fluid. Seminal theoretical investigations by Hancock and Purcell demonstrated that non-reciprocal, traveling-wave undulations of slender filaments generate a net thrust by continuously breaking time-reversal symmetry [1], [5]. These insights established the fundamental role of helical and flexural deformation modes in producing locomotion at vanishing Reynolds numbers and underscored the necessity of cyclic shape changes or external field interactions to achieve propulsion. Based on these principles, a variety of bio-mimetic micro-swimmers have been engineered to emulate the solutions of nature. Magnetic helical swimmers employ rigid ferro-magnetic helices actuated by rotating magnetic fields; by tuning field frequency and amplitude, they achieve precise three-dimensional steering, though deployment often requires bulky coils or permanent magnets proximate to the swimmer [6], [7]. In contrast, acoustic streaming motors exploit ultra-sonic transducers to establish steady streaming flows around asymmetric micro-structures. These contactless actuators offer high actuation rates and localized control but can generate undesirable heating and cavitation effects in surrounding fluids [8]. A second class of autonomous swimmers leverages chem-ical energy: Janus particles with asymmetric surface coatings catalyze reactions (e.g., peroxide decomposition) to produce self-generated concentration gradients, driving phoretic motion without external fields. While inherently autonomous, these swimmers contend with limited directional persistence and fuel depletion over time [9]. Similarly, photophoretic micro-swimmers absorb focused light to create localized thermal gra-dients, which induce thermophoretic forces; these approaches

Despite the rich diversity of propulsion strategies, the vast majority of studies have characterized swimmer perfor-mance in quiescent or steadily driven flows. The behavior of these microscale propellers under oscillatory or pulsatile forcings, ubiquitous in biological environments such as blood vessels and respiratory airways, remains largely unexplored. Understanding how unsteady background flows interact with swimmer kinematics is critical for translating micro-swimmer technology into practical biomedical and environmental appli-cations. 

B. Swarm Coordination in Steady Flows 

Swarm robotic systems draw inspiration from collective behav-iors in biological populations, such as fish schooling and bac-terial clustering, where simple, local interaction rules give rise to resilient, emergent patterns [11]. In microfluidic Poiseuille flows, magnetic micro-robotic swarms exploit rotating or oscillating external fields to trigger self-assembly into one-dimensional chains or compact clusters. By synchronizing field frequency and orientation with the background flow speed, these swarms can navigate upstream against the pressure-driven profile or drift downstream in a controlled manner [12], [13]. At the heart of these maneuvers lies the delicate balance between magnetic dipole interactions, which draw neighboring particles into aligned formations, and viscous drag forces that tend to disperse them and oppose motion. Researchers have demonstrated that by tuning the field strength and rotation rate, one can modulate the dipole-dipole attraction to overcome Brownian dispersion, preserving coherent formations over distances comparable to channel lengths. Such field-modulated navigation strategies have been validated in straight micro-channels and simple bifurcating networks, enabling swarms to reverse direction or traverse junctions without distributed sensing or inter-agent communication. Despite these successes, existing coordination methods rest on the assumption of a time-invariant flow field. In many real-world settings, blood vessels, respiratory pathways, and environmental conduits, pressure and velocity profiles oscillate or pulsate. The influence of such unsteady flow conditions on swarm coherence, steering accuracy, and collective stability remains largely uncharacterized, presenting a critical gap in the translation of micro-robotic swarms to dynamic, physiological, and industrial environments. 

C. Challenges in Oscillatory Fluid Environments 

Pulsatile flow in blood vessels features phase-lagged velocity profiles and evolving boundary layers that fluctuate throughout the cardiac cycle, creating time-varying shear stresses on suspended particles [3], [14]. Similarly, engineered pipelines subject to cyclic pumping generate oscillatory currents that impose rapidly changing pressure gradients and recirculation zones [2], [15]. Under these conditions, micro-swarms may experience trapping in transient vortices during deceleration phases or be driven into vessel walls when flow direction reverses. The unsteady shear environment also modulates lift forces on individual agents, potentially disrupting previously stable formations. Crucially, the spatiotemporal interplay of wakes shed by neighboring swimmers can either reinforce col-lective motion or lead to intermittent clustering and dispersion, depending on the synchronization of their actuation cycles [4]. 

D. Conventional Control Approaches 

Historically, micro-swimmer control has relied on global actuation schemesвҖ”magnetic, acoustic, or optical fieldsвҖ”augmented by model-predictive control (MPC) for trajectory planning in steady flows [16]вҖ“[18]. However, these approaches require continuous high-fidelity state feedback and assume invariant flow fields, assumptions that fail in opaque or deep-tissue environments [19]. Computational costs scale poorly with swarm size, and model uncertainties from unmodeled hydrodynamics cause rapid performance degradation [20]. Decentralized bio-inspired heuristics like chemotaxis and fluxotaxis enable local-only sensing but can trap agents in oscillatory loops under periodic flow reversals [21], [22]. These controllers lack temporal integration to anticipate flow changes and explicit inter-agent communication for large-scale coordination in unsteady environments [23]. 

E. Reinforcement Learning in Fluid Locomotion 

Reinforcement learning (RL) frames control as a Markov decision process where agents learn policies by maximizing cumulative rewards through interaction [24]. Model-based RL improves sample efficiency using learned dynamics surrogates [25], [26], but constructing accurate models for oscillatory fluid environments is challenging. High-fidelity CFD exhibits strongly nonlinear, high-dimensional dynamics where small approximation errors accumulate, producing physically in-consistent predictions [27]. For N-agent swarms, modeling pairwise hydrodynamic coupling requires O(N 2)-dimensional state spaces, making surrogate learning intractable [28]. Model-free algorithms like PPO, SAC, and TRPO by-pass explicit dynamics modeling, directly optimizing policies through sampled interactions [29], [30]. Despite higher per-sample costs from expensive fluid simulations, they eliminate surrogate-modeling bottlenecks and learn from physically con-sistent simulations, yielding adaptive, decentralized controllers [31]. Recent work has demonstrated model-free RL for single-agent swimming in steady flows, achieving efficient gaits [9], [32]. 

F. Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning and Multi-Objective Optimization 1) Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL): Micro-swarm coordination requires multi-agent reinforcement learn-ing (MARL), which extends RL to multiple interacting decision-makers [33], [34]. MARL faces challenges beyond single-agent settings: partial observability, non-stationary envi-ronments as policies evolve, and ambiguous credit assignment 3

for joint actions [35], [36]. Fully decentralized approaches suffer from instabilities, while centralized methods are im-practical for micro-swarms with limited communication [37]. Centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE) has emerged as the dominant paradigm, leveraging global information during training while executing local policies [38]. Key algorithms include MADDPG with centralized critics [35], QMIX using value factorization [39], COMA with counterfactual advantages [36], and MAPPO extending PPO to multi-agent settings [40]. Communication mechanisms like CommNet and graph neural networks further enhance coordination [41], [42]. 

2) Multi-Objective Reinforcement Learning (MORL): 

Multi-objective reinforcement learning (MORL) balances competing goals by treating rewards as vectors rather than scalars, seeking Pareto-optimal policies [43], [44]. Simple scalarization via weighted sums requires a priori preferences and may miss non-convex Pareto regions [45]. Alternative methods like Pareto Q-learning maintain non-dominated Q-vector sets [46], while deep MORL uses multi-head architec-tures with gradient conflict resolution [47]. When objectives conflict, naively summing gradients under-mines individual progress [48]. Projected Conflicting Gradient (PCGrad) resolves this by projected conflicting gradients onto each otherвҖҷs normal planes, removing destructive interference while preserving complementary components [49]. This en-sures updates for one objective donвҖҷt degrade others, acceler-ating convergence. PCGrad has been successfully applied to multi-task learning and recently extended to RL domains [50]. 

3) Multi-Objective Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MO-MARL): MO-MARL combines multi-agent coordination with multi-objective optimization, where agents must balance competing goals while cooperating [51], [52]. This introduces compounded complexity: non-stationary environments, gradi-ent conflicts, and coordinated trade-offs across objectives. Ap-plications include robotics (speed, energy, safety), autonomous driving (efficiency, comfort, safety), and resource management (fairness, efficiency) [44]. Algorithmic frameworks include MOMAPPO with multi-objective critics [53], PAC-MARL maintaining per-agent Pareto sets [54], and MO-QMIX extending value factoriza-tion to vector returns [55]. Recent integration of PCGrad into MARL shows improved stability in cooperative multi-objective tasks [50]. Despite advances, MO-MARL remains unexplored in physically grounded domains like fluid-structure interaction where objective coupling depends on continuous-time PDE dynamics. 

G. Reinforcement Learning in Computational Fluid Dynamics 

Integrating RL with CFD enables learning control strategies that respect physical constraints (incompressibility, momentum conservation) while discovering non-intuitive solutions [56], [57]. However, high-fidelity simulations are computationally expensive, and RL training requires millions of steps [58]. Recent advances in differentiable physics engines and GPU-accelerated CFD enable gradient backpropagation through solvers and massively parallel simulation [59], [60]. Early RL-CFD applications focused on active flow control for drag reduction and mixing enhancement. [61] pioneered deep RL for cylinder flow control using rotating cylinders to suppress vortex shedding, achieving drag reductions exceeding 30% [62]. Subsequent work applied PPO to airfoil separa-tion control [63], multi-agent RL for wake stabilization [64], and LES-based turbulent channel control [65]. These studies established that RL can discover non-trivial periodic control strategies exploiting flow physics [66]. [32] pioneered RL for optimizing undulatory locomotion using immersed boundary methods. [31] introduced вҖқSmar-ties,вҖқ applying PPO with DNS to learn fish-like swimming with realistic C-start maneuvers and rheotactic behaviors. [67] demonstrated navigation in turbulent flows, while [68] showed schools could exploit wake interactions to reduce collective drag. [9] discovered non-reciprocal strokes satisfying PurcellвҖҷs scallop theorem at low Reynolds numbers. 

1) CFD-MARL for Swarm Systems: Extending RL-CFD coupling to multi-agent swarms remains nascent due to com-putational costs: N agents require O(N 2) interaction evalu-ations, and training demands millions of steps [58]. Early work used simplified potential flow or Stokesian dynamics, sacrificing accuracy for speed [69]. These models fail to capture vortex shedding, wake interactions, and transient flow reversals critical for realistic swarm behavior. Recent advances in differentiable physics engines enable gradient backpropagation through Navier-Stokes solvers with full hydrodynamic fidelity [59], [60]. Initial studies explored coordinated particle manipulation in microfluidic flows [70] and multi-robot coordination in viscous fluids, but remain constrained to quasi-steady or low-Reynolds regimes with sim-plified dynamics. None have addressed multi-objective swarm control balancing speed, energy conservation, and smoothness simultaneously. 

2) Gaps in CFD-MO-MARL: No prior work has inte-grated multi-objective multi-agent RL with high-fidelity, time-dependent CFD for swarm locomotion in oscillatory flows. Existing CFD-RL studies address single-objective tasks with single agents or homogeneous swarms sharing scalar rewards [58]. Multi-objective formulations in CFD use offline op-timization (evolutionary algorithms, Bayesian optimization) rather than online RL [71]. Pulsatile flows in physiological sys-tems and industrial applications remain unexplored for micro-robotic swarms, despite being ubiquitous [72]. The challenge involves multi-scale temporal dynamics where agents must exploit flow phases, synchronize with neighbors, and maintain formation over millisecond-to-second timescales while balanc-ing competing objectives decentrally. This work addresses this gap by coupling a Navier-Stokes solver with decentralized multi-objective policies learned via PPO and reconciled through PCGrad. We demonstrate that time-dependent fluid-agent interactions can be captured di-rectly within RL loops, enabling physically grounded swarm control balancing upstream progression, energy conservation, and motion smoothness. Each micro-robot optimizes three concurrent objectives whose competing gradients are recon-ciled using PCGrad to prevent destructive interference and accelerate convergence toward Pareto-optimal policies. E. Control Problem and Optimization Framework 

To optimize micro-robotic locomotion in time-dependent flow, we cast the task as a multi-agent multi-objective Markov Decision Process (MA-MOMDP) and employ model-free deep reinforcement learning (PPO) with a Centralized Training, De-centralized Execution (CTDE) paradigm. The following sum-marizes the scientific conceptualization and technical specifi-cations of this framework. 

1) MDP Specification and Transition Dynamics: The state space S is defined at each discrete timestep by the concate-nation of decentralized, per-agent observation vectors. Each individual agentвҖҷs local feature space consists of 8 dimensions: its Cartesian coordinates (x, y ), current velocity components 

(vx, v y ), and four pressure samples sampled at equispaced points around its circumference to capture local hydrodynamic gradients. While execution is decentralized, training is central-ized; the joint state s вҲҲ S is provided to the critic to alleviate the non-stationarity inherent in multi-agent environments. The action space A allows each agent i to exert a continuous 2D propulsive force Fi,internal = ( ai,x , a i,y ), which is normalized within [вҲ’Fmax , F max ] based on physical magnetic actuation limits. Transition dynamics P are governed by the coupled fluid-structure solver, where the Navier-Stokes propagator updates flow fields and agent trajectories are determined by linear accelerations integrating hydrodynamic, drag, internal, and contact forces.

F. Reproducibility and Data Management 

All physical parameters (geometry, fluid properties, inflow waveform), swarm configurations (agent spacing, initial lat-tice), and RL hyperparameters are specified in version-controlled configuration files [77]. During each run, pressure and velocity fields, agent trajectories, and reward histories are logged at every time step. Post-processing generates quantita-tive metrics (e.g., mean upstream velocity, success rate) and visualizations (scalar field maps, trajectory overlays, reward curves) to support systematic comparison across parameter studies. III. R ESULTS 

A. Multi-Objective Reinforcement Learning Convergence and Policy Stabilization 

Fig. 6 presents the per-episode learning curves for the three reward objectives вҖ“ progress, energy efficiency, and smooth-ness вҖ“ normalized by episode length, across approximately 110,000 global environment steps. All three objectives are evaluated against two fixed brute-force baselines: a strategy that applies maximal thrust directly upstream, and one that drives the swarm members toward the channel walls while moving upstream. The progress reward, measuring the swarmвҖҷs ability to navigate against the fluid flow, exhibits the clearest learning dynamic. From an initial value near zero вҖ“ comparable to the brute-force baselines, which remain stagnant at approx-imately -2.0 and -2.5 throughout training вҖ“ the learned policy improves rapidly between approximately 5,000 and 25,000 global steps, converging to a sustained mean of roughly 6.5-7.0. This represents an improvement of more than 8 reward units over either baseline, demonstrating that the learned pol-icy acquires non-trivial flow exploitation strategies that fixed control schemes cannot achieve. A notable period of elevated inter-environment variance is observed between approximately 30,000 and 70,000 steps, reflected in the widened confidence band, suggesting that the policy undergoes iterative refinement and temporary destabilization as it explores higher-reward be-havioral modes before restabilizing. By approximately 75,000 steps, the mean progress reward recovers and the variance narrows, indicating policy consolidation. The energy efficiency reward exhibits a smoother conver-gence profile. Starting at approximately 0.30 in early training, it rises monotonically to a plateau near 0.63-0.65 by 20,000 steps and remains broadly stable for the remainder of training, with only moderate variance. Critically, both brute-force base-lines yield negative energy efficiency (approximately -0.15 and -0.27, respectively), indicating that fixed-force strategies ex-pend thrust in directions misaligned with actual displacement вҖ“ a consequence of fighting the flow rather than exploiting it. 8

The learned policy, by contrast, consistently achieves positive energy efficiency, confirming that it develops force application patterns that are directionally coherent with member motion. The smoothness reward displays the fastest convergence among all three objectives. After an initial transient near 0.87 in the first few thousand steps, the learned policy rapidly stabilizes in the range of 0.97-0.99 by approximately 5,000 global steps and sustains this level for the remainder of training with very low variance. Notably, both brute-force baselines achieve a smoothness of 1.00, the theoretical maximum, as their fixed action directions produce perfectly consistent, zero-variance action sequences by construction. The learned policy falls marginally short of this ceiling, reflecting the inherent cost of adaptive, state-dependent control: a policy that mod-ulates its actions in response to local flow conditions will necessarily incur some degree of temporal variation that a static rule does not. The gap between the learned policy and the brute-force baselines on this objective is nonetheless small in absolute terms (below 0.03 across the entire stable training regime), and must be interpreted in the context of the far larger improvements the learned policy achieves on the progress and energy efficiency objectives, where both baselines fail severely. This trade-off confirms that the MOMAPPO agent learns to balance the competing demands of navigational effectiveness and action consistency, accepting a modest smoothness cost in exchange for the substantially superior upstream progress and energy performance that define the primary task. Taken together, the convergence profiles across all three objectives demonstrate that the MOMAPPO framework suc-cessfully learns a Pareto-improving policy relative to both brute-force baselines simultaneously вҖ“ without sacrificing any individual objective вҖ“ and that the learned policy generalizes stably across parallel environment instances, as evidenced by the narrow confidence bands in the steady-state regime. 

B. Emergent Behavior 

The trained policy gives rise to a repertoire of collective behaviors that were not explicitly encoded in the reward function, but emerged organically from the interplay between the multi-objective signal, the triphasic fluid dynamics, and the decentralized control architecture. Comparing the untrained baseline (Fig. 7) with the trained agent (Fig. 8) reveals a qualitative shift in swarm strategy that can be decomposed into three temporally distinct phases, each exploiting a different feature of the pulsatile flow cycle. 

1) Phase 1: Collective Flow Throttling During Peak For-ward Flow: The most striking and counterintuitive emergent behavior is the formation of a deliberate two-layer hydro-dynamic obstruction during the peak forward-flow phase of the triphasic waveform. Under the untrained policy, all swarm members cluster against the upper wall ( y вүҲ 2 mm ). Rather than sheltering the members from the bulk flow, this config-uration creates a severe constriction between the swarm and the opposite wall that locally accelerates the fluid to velocities exceeding 700 mm/s . The resulting drag force is sufficient to drive the entire swarm past the episode-failure boundary at 

x = 80 mm within a few time steps (Fig. 7, panels 1вҖ“4).

This work demonstrates that coupling a high-fidelity Navier-Stokes solver with a multi-objective multi-agent reinforce-ment learning framework is both computationally tractable and scientifically productive for micro-swarm locomotion in physiologically realistic flow conditions. The results estab-lish three principal contributions: a training paradigm that achieves stable Pareto-improving convergence across compet-ing objectives, a repertoire of non-obvious emergent collective behaviors arising from the interplay of fluid physics and decentralized reward signals, and a quantitative demonstration that gradient conflict resolution is functionally necessary in this domain. The most consequential finding is not the convergence of the reward curves per se, but what the converged policy actually does. Rather than applying maximum thrust upstream вҖ“ the strategy both brute-force baselines approximate, and which fails catastrophically вҖ“ the trained swarm discovers a multi-phase ratchet mechanism in which the forward-flow phase is

Limitations and future directions: The two-dimensional setup precludes out-of-plane dynamics and full three-dimensional wake structure; extension to 3D simulation with GPU-accelerated solvers [60] is a natural next step. The uniform magnetic gradient assumption will require spatial resolution for translational relevance, and generalization to variable swarm sizes would benefit from graph neural network policy parameterizations [42]. Validation under pathological flow profiles вҖ“ stenotic jets, irregular pulsatility, or elevated viscosity remains necessary before clinical application. More broadly, the demonstration that time-dependent fluid-agent interactions can be embedded directly within multi-objective RL loops, without surrogate modeling, establishes a method-ological template for any control domain governed by PDE dy-namics, with direct applicability to environmental monitoring and industrial microfluidics as differentiable physics solvers continue to mature [59], [60].
