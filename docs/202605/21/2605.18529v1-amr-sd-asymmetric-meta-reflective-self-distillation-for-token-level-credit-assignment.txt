Abstract 

The alignment of Large Language Models (LLMs) for complex reasoning heavily relies on Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Re-wards (RLVR). However, standard algorithms like GRPO apply sequence-level rewards uni-formly to all tokens, creating a severe credit-assignment bottleneck. While on-policy self-distillation attempts to resolve this by condition-ing a self-teacher on privileged contexts, direct exposure to raw oracle solutions often induces over-conditioned teacher distributions, implicit answer leakage, and late-stage training collapse. To overcome these limitations, we propose Asymmetric Meta-Reflective Self-Distillation (AMR-SD). Instead of conditioning directly on raw reference traces, AMR-SD inserts a reflection bottleneck: it compresses diagnos-tic signals—from verifier outcomes, peer roll-outs, or reference feedback—into concise, self-generated Socratic hints and critiques. Further-more, we introduce Causal Information Gain (CIG) with an asymmetric, ReLU-gated thresh-old to translate these reflections into sparse, highly precise token-level advantage modu-lations. Combined with temporal annealing, this mechanism preserves the base environ-mental reward while filtering out distributional noise. Experiments across scientific, mathe-matical, and tool-use benchmarks demonstrate that AMR-SD significantly outperforms exist-ing baselines, achieving robust long-horizon stability and successfully preventing late-stage collapse.

Standard policy gradient algorithms, such as Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Guo et al., 2025; Schulman et al., 2017), efficiently estimate advan-tages from sparse sequence-level outcomes. How-ever, GRPO applies a uniform advantage scalar to all tokens within a rollout. on-policy exploration and SFT’s dense supervi-sion, the community turned to On-Policy Distil-lation (OPD) (Agarwal et al., 2024). While highly effective (Xiao et al., 2026; Team et al., 2026), maintaining an external teacher incurs prohibitive computational overhead. Consequently, On-Policy Self-Distillation emerged, utilizing the model itself as the teacher conditioned on privileged informa-tion (e.g., ground-truth solutions) (Figure 1) (Zhao et al., 2026; Hübotter et al., 2026; Shenfeld et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2026). However, directly forc-ing a conditionally independent student to match a conditionally dependent teacher introduces infor-mation asymmetry. The student blindly imitates confident reasoning without accessing the under-lying context, leading to privileged information leakage, degraded intrinsic reflective capabilities, and late-stage training collapse (Kim et al., 2026; Li et al., 2026c). Recent advancements attempt to mitigate this by anchoring optimization to environment rewards for continuous magnitude adjustment (Yang et al., 2026a; Li et al., 2026a). Unfortunately, a subtle lim-itation remains: because the privileged context nar-rows the teacher’s output distribution onto specific oracle tokens, the teacher’s probabilities for most student-generated tokens are systematically lower than the student’s own. This causes the continuous multipliers to be predominantly less than 1, damp-ening the primary reward signal. Furthermore, the privileged information here is often simply the di-rectly provided ground truth; directly relying on it during training is clearly inefficient. Consequently, such global distribution alignment inevitably al-lows privileged information to implicitly leak into the parameters, inducing late-stage training col-lapse. To fully unlock self-distillation without these drawbacks, we propose the Asymmetric Meta-Reflective Self-Distillation (AMR-SD) framework, which retains the reward-anchoring principle of (Yang et al., 2026a) while replacing continuous scaling with an asymmetric, ReLU-gated threshold (τ ), powered by a novel metric: Causal Information Gain (CIG). To mitigate direct oracle-solution leak-age, AMR-SD operates as a reflection-mediated self-training loop. Instead of exposing the teacher to raw ground-truth solutions during token rescor-ing, we enforce a Meta-Reflection phase: the model generates an encouraging <hint> for successful tra-jectories or a targeted <critique> for failed trajec-tories, using verifier-approved peer rollouts when available, which then serve as the conditioning con-text for the teacher. Crucially, to overcome the signal dampening in-herent in continuous scaling, CIG introduces an asymmetric, threshold-gated filtering mechanism. Rather than diluting the reward by modulating ev-ery token, this mechanism acts as a strict gate-keeper: it preserves the baseline environmental reward intact by default, triggering sparse, targeted adjustments only when the Socratic teacher iden-tifies a highly confident divergence from the stu-dent. This approach effectively filters out trivial distributional noise, delivering precise token-level credit assignment without compromising the over-all strength of the base reinforcement signal. We emphasize that AMR-SD does not entirely discard privileged information (e.g., verifier out-comes, peer rollouts, or reference answers). In-stead, it prevents the token-rescoring teacher from directly seeing raw oracle traces. Diagnostic sources are first compressed into concise natural-language reflections, acting as a low-bandwidth bottleneck. This mitigates over-conditioned teacher distributions and answer leakage while preserving essential token-level guidance for credit assign-ment. Our main contributions are threefold: • We introduce Meta-Reflection, prompting the model to generate Socratic self-teaching targets (hint / critique ) from verifier outcomes, on-policy peer rollouts, or reference-assisted diag-nostic signals when available. This reflection-mediated paradigm does not remove privileged information, but introduces a low-bandwidth bottleneck between diagnostic sources and token-level rescoring, thereby reducing direct oracle-trace conditioning and improving cali-bration of token-level credit signals. • We design CIG as a threshold-gated (ReLU) ad-vantage adjustment mechanism. Integrated with temporal annealing, it concentrates dense super-vision during early learning and smoothly shifts to unadulterated environmental rewards, ensur-ing rapid early alignment and long-horizon sta-bility. • Building upon these mechanisms, we propose the AMR-SD framework. Extensive experi-ments across scientific, mathematical, and tool-use benchmarks demonstrate that AMR-SD out-performs existing baselines, achieving robust 2long-horizon stability and successfully prevent-ing the late-stage training collapse. 

2 Related Work and Background 

RL with Verifiable Rewards and GRPO. Prox-imal Policy Optimization (Schulman et al., 2017) established the foundation for RLVR-based LLM alignment (Yu et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025). Re-cent works have further analyzed the properties of verifiable reward signals (Wen et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2026; Lin et al., 2026). To optimize the pol-icy under sparse rewards without a separate value network, GRPO (Guo et al., 2025) estimates ad-vantages via group baseline normalization. For a given prompt x, the policy samples G independent trajectories. The advantage for the i-th trajectory is calculated as Ai = ( ri − μgroup )/(σgroup + ε),where μgroup and σgroup are the mean and standard deviation of the G rewards. Although subsequent systems such as DAPO (Yu et al., 2026) improve training stability, GRPO’s ad-vantage estimates are known to be biased (Yang et al., 2026b). More critically, applying a uni-form scalar advantage Ai to every token creates a severe credit-assignment bottleneck (Yang et al., 2026b; Li et al., 2026d; Xie et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026b). Some works attempt to address this through uncertainty-aware advantage shaping (Xie et al., 2025), outcome-grounded advantage reshap-ing (Li et al., 2026d), and token-level policy gradi-ent redesign for tool use (Lin et al., 2025a,b, 2026), yet fine-grained credit assignment remains chal-lenging. 

On-Policy (Self-)Distillation. To provide denser, token-level supervision, On-Policy Distillation (OPD) (Agarwal et al., 2024) trains a student on its own online rollouts evaluated by a stronger external teacher (e.g., MiMo (Xiao et al., 2026), Kimi (Team et al., 2026)). However, maintaining an external teacher imposes substantial computa-tional overhead. To eliminate this dependency, On-Policy Self-Distillation (Zhao et al., 2026; Hübot-ter et al., 2026; Shenfeld et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2026) uses the model itself as the teacher. In this paradigm, the active student policy πθ generates tra-jectories, and the exact same model, detached via a stop-gradient operator ( πθsg ), acts as the teacher conditioned on privileged information c.Despite its efficiency, prior work (Kim et al., 2026; Li et al., 2026c) identifies that directly align-ing the student to these conditional log-likelihoods introduces privileged information leakage and late-stage collapse. Recent methods (Yang et al., 2026a; Li et al., 2026a) attempt to stabilize train-ing by anchoring to environment rewards and us-ing the teacher only for continuous magnitude adjustment. Yet, their continuous scaling pre-dominantly dampens the overall learning signal. Our AMR-SD framework resolves these issues via autonomous Meta-Reflection and a ReLU-gated threshold, achieving sparse, unbiased token-level credit assignment without external dependencies. 

3 Method 

We introduce the AMR-SD framework. Unlike standard sequence-level RL which broadcasts a uniform advantage to all tokens, AMR-SD resolves the spatial credit-assignment bottleneck by dynami-cally mapping the sequence-level GRPO advantage into a dense, state-action specific tensor. Formally, we model the language generation pro-cess as a MDP defined by the tuple (S, A, P, R).Let the state st = (x, y <t ) ∈ S denote the prompt and the generated prefix, and the action 

at = yt ∈ A represent the current token. 4.2 Main Result Scientific Reasoning and Tool Use. We evaluate AMR-SD on the SciKnowEval benchmark across four scientific disciplines (Chemistry, Physics, Bi-ology, and Materials Science) as well as a Tool Use task, using two backbone models: Qwen3-8B and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Table 1). Across both backbones, all training methods substantially improve over the untrained baselines, confirming that reinforcement learning provides a consistent benefit for scientific reasoning. At final conver-gence (step 75), AMR-SD achieves the strongest or near-strongest performance in the majority of categories. Most significantly, AMR-SD attains the highest Biology scores on both backbones by a substantial margin: 63.7 on Qwen3-8B and 66.4 on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, surpassing GRPO by ap-proximately 3.2 and 18.2 percentage points respec-tively, and exceeding RLSD by roughly 7.2 and 11.8 percentage points. On Physics and Materials Science, AMR-SD similarly reaches competitive or best-in-class scores at step 75—79.7 on Physics and 80.3 on Materials for Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, the highest figures among all methods in both cat-egories. These results suggest that the token-level credit assignment provided by the CIG mechanism enables more reliable generalisation across diverse scientific domains compared to trajectory-level or coarser reward signals.

Mathematical Reasoning. To assess general-isation beyond scientific knowledge tasks, we further evaluate AMR-SD on four mathemati-cal competition benchmarks—AIME24, AIME25, AMC23, and HMMT—using Qwen3-8B in think-ing mode (Table 2). AMR-SD achieves the best performance on every individual benchmark and the highest average score of 62.7, compared to 60.3 for GRPO (+2.4 points) and 57.2 for RLSD (+5.5 points). The gains are particularly pronounced on the most challenging benchmarks: 69.6 on AIME24 (vs. 66.7 for GRPO), 55.2 on AIME25 (vs. 53.1), and 34.6 on HMMT (vs. 31.7). Notably, RLSD shows constrained optimization efficiency on harder problems, scoring 27.6 on HMMT and 47.3 on AIME25. This indicates that directly using raw ground-truth solutions as privileged informa-tion introduces a distributional shift that makes the resulting reward signal insufficiently discriminative for complex multi-step reasoning. Furthermore, we provide deeper analyses of op-timization stability and generation dynamics in Appendices C and F. As shown, while standard continuous self-distillation methods like RLSD ex-hibit late-stage performance fluctuations and com-pressed reflection behavior due to direct answer exposure, AMR-SD maintains a stable, monoton-ically improving reward signal. Crucially, AMR-SD improves reasoning efficiency by reducing un-necessary verbosity while successfully preserving critical cognitive explorations. This confirms that introducing a reflection bottleneck successfully mit-igates the vulnerabilities inherent in direct answer conditioning, effectively translating training stabil-ity to superior out-of-domain accuracy. 

4.3 Analysis: Quality of Meta-Reflections 

The efficacy of AMR-SD hinges on the quality of self-generated reflections, as they directly calibrate the CIG-based advantage modulation. A precise reflection concentrates the learning signal on se-mantically informative tokens, whereas a vague or factually incorrect one risks propagating miscali-brated supervision. Qualitative analysis of sampled reflections from SciKnowEval and DAPO-Math-17k rollouts re-veals two consistent patterns. First, hints for suc-cessful trajectories effectively distill latent core principles rather than merely repeating the answer path. Second, critiques for failed trajectories 

demonstrate precise fault localization, diagnos-ing root causes and reframing them in a positive, prospective manner. This "positive-framing" is cru-cial; as the critique is prepended to the teacher’s forward pass, it provides constructive guidance that enables the model to resolve the underlying reasoning divergence. Detailed representative ex-amples of these Socratic targets are provided in Appendix B.

Effect of Meta-Reflection. In this ablation vari-ant, we replace the model’s self-generated meta-reflection with the ground-truth solution directly as the teacher’s conditioning context. Performance drops from 74.3 to 70.5, a reduction of 3.8 points. This result reveals a counterintuitive finding: in-jecting privileged ground-truth information as the conditioning signal is strictly inferior to the model’s own Socratic self-reflection. The underlying cause lies in the information asymmetry problem: when the teacher is conditioned on the ground-truth an-swer, its token probability distribution presupposes information unavailable to the student, creating a fundamental distributional gap that inflates CIG es-timates and induces miscalibrated advantage mod-ulation. By contrast, self-generated hints and cri-tiques share the same information boundary as the student policy, yielding better-calibrated CIG sig-nals and more precise gradient interventions. 

Effect of the ReLU Threshold τ . Removing the margin τ while retaining the ReLU gate yields 72.0, surpassing the no-reflection baseline but falling 2.3 points below the full model. The threshold enforces sparsity: without it, low-magnitude CIG signals that correspond to distributional noise rather than genuine knowledge deficits are allowed to modu-late the advantage, diluting gradient quality. Fur-ther ablating the ReLU gate itself reduces accuracy to 71.3. In this setting, the mechanism loses its asymmetric directional filtering. Without the gate, “counter-directional” signals—such as positive CIG in a failed rollout or negative CIG in a successful one—can freely offset the intended advantage mod-ulation, partially undermining the primary reward signal. These two ablations validate that gate and margin are necessary for robust, noise-resistant, and directionally-aware credit assignment. 

Effect of Temporal Annealing. Disabling the decay schedule reduces performance to 70.6. In late training stages, as the student policy becomes highly optimized, its residual errors become in-creasingly complex. This not only degrades self-reflection quality but also exhausts the teacher model’s inherent capacity to provide reliable token-level guidance. Without annealing, persistent dis-tillation forces the policy to learn from these noisy, depleted signals. Temporal annealing mitigates this by smoothly shifting the optimization focus entirely to the pure environmental reward. 

Full Model. The complete AMR-SD configura-tion achieves 74.3, demonstrating that all four com-ponents are mutually reinforcing rather than indi-vidually redundant. 

4.5 Analysis: CIG Token Value Distribution 

As detailed in Appendix E, the empirical distribu-tion of non-zero CIG values across n = 50 ,000 

sampled tokens exhibits a pronounced left skew, with the bulk of mass concentrated in the interval 

[−1, 0] and a peak just below zero. This shape is a direct consequence of the teacher’s privileged information. When the teacher model conditions on the meta-reflection, its output distribution is re-calibrated, systematically driving the bulk of CIG values below zero. The asymmetry has a direct consequence for hy-perparameter selection. Because tokens satisfying the critique condition vastly outnumber those satis-fying the hint condition (as shown in the appendix), setting λ > γ compensates for this imbalance by up-weighting the sparser positive-CIG tokens. The result is a more balanced effective gradient that leverages both signals without allowing either to overwhelm the other. 

5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduced AMR-SD to resolve the token-level credit-assignment bottleneck in RLVR. To overcome the privileged information leakage and signal dampening inherent in standard self-distillation, AMR-SD employs an autonomous Meta-Reflection phase to generate Socratic hints and critiques. We formalized CIG with an asym-metric, ReLU-gated threshold to translate these reflections into sparse, precise token-level inter-8ventions, strictly preserving the base environmen-tal reward. Extensive experiments across scien-tific (SciKnowEval), mathematical (AIME, AMC, HMMT), and tool-use (ToolAlpaca) benchmarks demonstrate that AMR-SD significantly outper-forms state-of-the-art baselines. By effectively fil-tering distributional noise and utilizing temporal annealing, our framework guarantees long-horizon stability and avoids late-stage training collapse, pro-viding a highly robust, scalable solution for align-ing large language models on complex reasoning and agentic tasks. 

Limitations 

Despite the strong empirical results, AMR-SD has two notable limitations. 

Dependence on Reflection Quality. The effec-tiveness of the CIG mechanism is fundamentally contingent on the quality of the self-generated meta-reflections. When the base model lacks sufficient introspective capability, the produced hints and cri-tiques tend to be vague or factually miscalibrated, propagating corrupted supervision signals through the CIG modulation and ultimately degrading per-formance. This manifests most clearly in mathe-matical reasoning tasks: on weaker or non-thinking-mode models such as Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen3-8B with enable_thinking=False , the re-flection quality is insufficient to yield reliable CIG signals, and the method fails to outperform the GRPO baseline. AMR-SD is therefore most effec-tive when deployed on models with strong chain-of-thought or self-evaluation capabilities. 

Inference Overhead from Meta-Reflection Gen-eration. The meta-reflection phase introduces an additional autoregressive generation step per trajec-tory during training rollouts. Although this over-head is bounded by the reflection budget (capped at 4096 tokens for SciKnowEval and 16384 tokens for math), it nonetheless increases the wall-clock time per training iteration relative to standard GRPO or RLSD. However, this computational overhead is highly manageable in practice. Empirically, our method generally maintains a training speed of at least 80% (e.g., in the mathematical reasoning task, during the first 50 steps before method degener-ation, GRPO and RLSD took 18 hours, whereas AMR-SD took 22 hours) compared to standard GRPO. For severely resource-constrained settings, this latency may still be a practical concern, and future work could explore lightweight reflection distillation or early-exit strategies to further reduce this cost.

D Hyperparameters and Implementation Details 

Datasets and Tasks. We evaluate AMR-SD on two complementary tracks. For scientific knowl-edge reasoning and agentic capabilities, we adopt the SciKnowEval benchmark (Feng et al., 2024), spanning four domains (Chemistry, Physics, Biol-ogy, and Materials Science), alongside a dedicated Tool Use benchmark (Tang et al., 2023). We follow exactly the training and test splits used in SDPO, enabling a direct and fair comparison. For math-ematical reasoning, we train on the preprocessed DAPO-Math-17k dataset (Yu et al., 2026) and eval-uate on four standard benchmarks: AIME 2024, AIME 2025, AMC 2023, and HMMT. 

Base Models and Baselines. We conduct ex-periments on two instruction-tuned base mod-els: Qwen3-8B and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Yang et al., 2025; Bai et al., 2023). For all SciKnow-Eval and Tool Use experiments, we evaluate on both models with the thinking mode disabled (enable_thinking=False ) to ensure a controlled comparison. We compare AMR-SD against the following base-lines: (1) the pretrained base model without any RL fine-tuning; (2) GRPO , the standard sequence-level policy gradient baseline; (3) SDPO ,a representative on-policy self-distillation method; and (4) RLSD , a state-of-the-art reward-anchored magnitude-adjustment approach. Hyperparameter Configurations. To account for the highly skewed empirical distribution of CIG values (as detailed in Appendix E), we employ asymmetric modulation coefficients. Specifically, the reward amplification coefficient is set to λ =0.2, while the penalty amplification coefficient is set to γ = 0 .1. This configuration effectively up-weights the sparser positive-CIG tokens, ensuring a balanced gradient signal that leverages both hint bonuses and critique penalties. 

Evaluation Protocol. During evaluation, we strictly control the generation boundaries to match the distinct requirements of each track. For Sci-KnowEval and the Tool Use benchmark, we set the maximum generation length to 2048 tokens. We report per-domain accuracy at two training checkpoints—step 15 and step 75—to assess both early convergence and final performance, with vali-dation performed every 5 training steps. For math-ematical reasoning, we expand the maximum gen-eration length to 16384 tokens to accommodate extended and complex reasoning chains. Perfor-mance is evaluated using acc@16, computed as the average accuracy over 16 independently sam-pled rollouts per problem at temperature 1.0, across AIME 2024, AIME 2025, AMC 2023, and HMMT. 

E Detailed Analysis: CIG Token Value Distribution 

This section provides the visual empirical distri-bution and a detailed theoretical analysis of the Causal Information Gain (CIG) values discussed in Section 4.5 of the main text. 

Figure 4: Empirical distribution of non-zero CIG values. The prominent left skew (peaking just below zero) is caused by the teacher’s conditioning on privileged in-formation. This intrinsic asymmetry necessitates differ-ential scaling coefficients ( λ > γ ) to balance penalties and bonuses. 

As presented in Figure 4, the empirical distri-bution of non-zero CIG values across n = 50 ,000 

sampled tokens exhibits a pronounced left skew, with the bulk of mass concentrated in the interval 

[−1, 0] and a peak just below zero. This shape is 16 a direct consequence of the teacher’s privileged information. When the teacher model conditions on the meta-reflection—a hint or critique unavailable to the student—its output distribution is recalibrated: probability mass shifts toward alternative continua-tions, and by the softmax normalization constraint, the probability assigned to the token actually pro-duced by the student decreases. This effect is sys-tematic and occurs across both correct and incorrect trajectories, driving the bulk of CIG values below zero. The asymmetry has a direct consequence for hy-perparameter selection. Recall that the hint bonus activates on correct trajectories when CIG t > τ ,while the critique penalty activates on wrong tra-jectories when −CIG t > τ . As Figure 4 shows, the negative tail is substantially heavier: tokens satisfying the critique condition vastly outnumber those satisfying the hint condition. If λ = γ, the aggregate gradient signal would be dominated by critique penalties, suppressing the learning contri-bution from hint bonuses. Setting λ > γ compen-sates for this imbalance by up-weighting the sparser positive-CIG tokens. The result is a more balanced effective gradient that leverages both the reinforce-ment of teacher-preferred tokens on correct trajecto-ries and the attenuation of teacher-opposed tokens on wrong ones, without allowing either signal to overwhelm the other. 

F Analysis of Reflection Capabilities and Reasoning Efficiency 

Motivation. While our Asymmetric Meta-Reflective Self-Distillation (AMR-SD) framework significantly improves accuracy and reasoning effi-ciency, a critical concern in On-Policy Distillation is whether the dense supervision alters the model’s intrinsic ability to explore and reflect. Standard self-distillation often forces the student to mimic an oracle-conditioned teacher. When this teacher is directly exposed to ground-truth answers, it can induce over-alignment and restrict natural trial-and-error, resulting in more short-sighted generation. To investigate this, we analyze the gen-eration dynamics of different alignment strategies by quantifying their reflection capabilities. 

Methodology. We sample n = 480 evaluation traces from the AIME 24 benchmark. To measure the degree of internal deliberation, we track the fre-quency of ten explicit reflection and self-correction markers (e.g., Wait, Maybe, Check, Alternatively, Seems, Might, Hmm, Perhaps, Actually, Likely , fol-lowing Kim et al., 2026) across four distinct mod-els: the Base model (Qwen3-8B), standard GRPO, RLSD, and our proposed AMR-SD.

Results and Discussion. As shown in Table 4, the Base model exhibits extreme verbosity (140.90 markers per trace), often over-thinking without con-verging to a solution. While GRPO reduces some redundancy (117.57), it still relies heavily on pro-longed generation to maximize rewards. Crucially, standard RLSD experiences a shift in reflection markers, dropping to an average of 73.59 per trace. This strongly confirms that previ-ous self-distillation methods—which directly ex-pose the ground-truth answer as privileged infor-mation—tend to over-condition the teacher’s dis-tribution. Because the teacher already contains the terminal answer explicitly, its rescored proba-bility landscape implicitly disincentivizes the stu-dent’s natural logical branching or explicit trial-and-error attempts. Consequently, the model shifts towards more accelerated, direct paths, compress-ing its long-horizon exploration. In contrast, our AMR-SD framework strikes an optimal balance, maintaining a healthy reflec-tion frequency of 105.32 markers per trace. This "Goldilocks effect" is directly attributable to re-placing direct answer exposure with an informa-tion bottleneck (Meta-Reflection) combined with the Asymmetric Causal Information Gain (CIG) threshold. Rather than penalizing cognitive explo-rations, the asymmetric threshold acts as a pre-cise filter: it heavily penalizes blindly confident deviations (baseless waffle) while tolerating and protecting valid analytical trajectories (e.g., “Wait, alternatively...” ). This demonstrates that AMR-SD effectively enhances reasoning efficiency without stripping the model of the crucial critical thinking 17 capabilities required for solving complex mathe-matical problems. 18
