# Resource-Element Energy Difference for Noncoherent Over-the-Air Federated Learning 

Hao Chen, Member, IEEE, and Zavareh Bozorgasl, Member, IEEE 

Abstract 

Over-the-air federated learning (OTA-FL) reduces uplink latency by exploiting waveform superposition, but conventional analog aggregation schemes typically require instantaneous channel state information (CSI), channel inversion, and coherent phase alignment, which can be difficult to maintain in practical wireless systems. This paper proposes resource-element energy difference (REED), a noncoherent aggregation primitive for continuous signed updates that avoids instantaneous CSI. REED maps the positive and negative parts of each real-valued update to transmit energies on two orthogonal resource elements with independent phase dithers, and the server estimates the signed aggregate from their energy difference. With only slow-timescale calibration of average channel powers, REED is unbiased for the desired signed sum and admits an exact closed-form variance under Rayleigh fading. We incorporate REED into full-participation FedAvg and prove a smooth nonconvex stationarity bound. Under an average per-client energy budget, the aggregation gain can be scheduled so that the REED-induced perturbation scales quadratically with the local stepsize, yielding the canonical O(1 /√T ) stationarity rate. Experiments on MNIST and Fashion-MNIST demonstrate that REED closely matches clean FedAvg and coherent CSIT aggregation in IID settings, while maintaining stable convergence with a moderate performance degradation under strong data heterogeneity.

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Federated learning (FL) enables a set of distributed clients to train a shared model without sending their raw data to a central server [1], [2]. In each communication round, the server broadcasts a common global model to all clients, whereas the clients return distinct high-dimensional local updates. The uplink aggregation phase is therefore often the dominant communication bottleneck, especially as the number of clients and model dimension grow. Importantly, the server typically does not need to decode every client update individually; it only needs an aggregate, such as a weighted sum or average of the local updates. This observation turns wireless FL into a functional communication problem over a multiple-access channel. Over-the-air computation (AirComp) exploits this structure by using waveform superposition to compute functions directly in the air, reducing latency and bandwidth consumption relative to orthogonal digital uploading [3]–[5]. Building on this principle, over-the-air federated learning (OTA-FL) has emerged as a communication-efficient framework for wireless model aggregation [6]–[12]. Most existing OTA-FL schemes implement this idea through coherent analog aggregation, where clients pre-equalize or scale their transmitted updates so that the desired sum is formed at the receiver. Such coherent designs are powerful, but they typically require instantaneous channel state information, channel inversion or truncation, and sufficiently accurate phase alignment across devices. These requirements can be difficult to maintain in practical wireless FL systems with many clients, fading channels, oscillator offsets, and repeated high-dimensional transmissions. A direct move to noncoherent reception is not straightforward. If the receiver observes only received energies, the channel naturally supports nonnegative energy accumulation rather than signed linear summation. In particular, an energy measurement is invariant to a sign flip of the transmitted scalar when the encoding depends only on magnitude. This sign-loss obstacle is fundamental for FL, since model updates and stochastic gradients are real-valued signed quantities. Therefore, a noncoherent OTA-FL primitive that avoids instantaneous CSI must solve a signed function-computation problem: it must recover signed linear sums from energy observations. 

A. Related work 

Coherent analog OTA aggregation. The dominant OTA-FL line treats the wireless multiple-access channel as an analog summation device for model updates or gradients. Representative works include AirComp-based FL and distributed SGD [7], [8], broadband analog aggregation for federated edge learning [9], and power-control or transceiver designs for AirFedAvg-type systems [13], [14]. Convergence-aware variants further show that channel noise, power control, and data heterogeneity must be analyzed jointly [10]. These works are the most direct continuous-aggregation baselines for REED, but their coherent operation typically relies on instantaneous CSI, phase compensation, channel inversion or truncation, device selection, or receiver-side scaling/beamforming. Low-CSI, blind, and weighted alternatives. Several recent works reduce or remove transmitter-side CSI requirements without using the same primitive as REED. Weighted OTA-FL adapts aggregation weights to mitigate channel effects without CSIT [16]. Random orthogonalization exploits massive-MIMO channel hardening and favorable propagation to enable natural over-the-air model aggregation without transmitter-side CSI [17]. Blind digital OTA-FL via q-QAM uses digital modulation and multiple receive antennas to combat fading without channel knowledge at the devices [18]. Blind AirComp/data-fusion formulations also recover functions without explicit CSI, but generally solve a different inverse problem rather than providing a simple per-coordinate signed-sum estimator [19]. These methods are important adjacent designs, but their operating assumptions differ from REED’s single-stream, memoryless energy-difference estimator. 

Quantized and majority-vote aggregation. Another line avoids continuous analog aggregation by quantizing the learning signal, most commonly through signs. SignSGD and majority-vote learning motivate one-bit OTA aggregation [20], [21]. Noncoherent majority-vote methods use energy detection over orthogonal resources to recover sign majorities without CSI [22], and one-bit OTA aggregation has also been studied under Byzantine attacks [23]. These schemes are robust and bandwidth efficient for sign-based learning, but the computed object is a sign majority rather than a continuous signed sum. 

Noncoherent energy-based learning and consensus. The closest conceptual relatives to REED are noncoherent energy-based methods. NCOTA-DGD uses energy superposition for decentralized consensus/optimization without scheduling, topology design, or CSI [24]; related noncoherent consensus designs study noisy average consensus over wireless networks [25]. NCAirFL is a centralized CSI-free AirFL design based on unbiased noncoherent detection, binary dithering, and long-term error compensation [26]. These works establish the feasibility of noncoherent learning, but they do not isolate the same minimal, memoryless continuous signed-sum primitive with an exact scalar distortion law. 

Position of REED. REED targets the missing middle ground between coherent continuous AirComp and noncoherent sign/majority aggregation. It estimates a continuous signed sum in expectation using only paired energy measurements and slow-timescale average-power calibration. It does not require instantaneous CSIT, channel inversion, phase alignment, massive-MIMO asymptotics, or long-term error-feedback memory. The price is explicit: each scalar uses two orthogonal resource elements, and the resulting distortion depends on the positive and negative update masses, the receiver noise, and the aggregation gain. This paper proposes resource-element energy difference (REED), a noncoherent over-the-air estimator for continuous signed real-valued sums. REED splits each scalar into positive and negative parts, maps them to transmit energies on a matched pair of orthogonal resource elements, and estimates the signed sum by subtracting the two received energies. The construction requires no instantaneous CSI or phase-coherent combining; it uses only slow-timescale average channel-power calibration. Under Rayleigh fading, REED is unbiased for the desired signed sum and admits an exact variance expression that separates noncoherent self-noise from receiver-noise terms. This estimator characterization makes the learning analysis transparent: when applied coordinate-wise to FedAvg updates, REED appears as an additive zero-mean aggregation perturbation with a controllable second moment.

V. E XPERIMENTAL RESULTS 

The experiments quantify the cost of replacing instantaneous CSIT and coherent phase alignment with REED’s CSI-free noncoherent energy aggregation. In particular, we examine three questions: whether this cost is negligible in benign IID regimes, how it changes under heterogeneous data, and whether the observed degradation is consistent with the REED-induced aggregation-noise term in the convergence analysis. 8 

> Fig. 2. The CNN architecture used in the experiments for both MNIST and Fashion-MNIST. The model consists of two Conv–ReLU–MaxPool blocks followed by two fully connected layers. The final layer outputs 10 logits, which can be converted to soft labels for the 10-class classification task.

A. Setup 

We evaluate REED as an end-to-end uplink aggregation primitive for FedAvg and compare it with two references: clean FedAvg, which uses ideal noiseless aggregation, and an oracle coherent CSIT baseline, which assumes instantaneous channel knowledge and perfect coherent over-the-air alignment. Clean FedAvg represents the ideal communication-free aggregation limit. The oracle coherent CSIT baseline represents an optimistic coherent AirComp reference with instantaneous channel knowledge and perfect phase compensation. REED uses only long-term average channel-power calibration and therefore removes the strongest synchronization and CSIT assumptions of the coherent reference. This comparison quantifies the performance cost of replacing instantaneous CSIT and coherent phase alignment with REED’s long-term power calibration and noncoherent energy detection. Experiments are conducted on MNIST and Fashion-MNIST using their standard 60,000/10,000 train/test splits. MNIST is used as a standard handwritten digit classification benchmark [28] and Fashion-MNIST is used as an image classification benchmark with 10 fashion-product classes [29]. The federated system has K = 10 clients, and all clients participate in each communication round. To numerically evaluate the proposed OTA-FL framework, we use the simple convolutional neural network shown in Fig. 2. The network is used for both MNIST and Fashion-MNIST, since both datasets contain 28 × 28 

grayscale images with 10 classes. In the figure, “Conv cin → cout , 5 × 5” denotes a convolutional layer with cin input channels, 

cout output channels, and a 5 × 5 kernel; “MaxPool 2 × 2” denotes 2 × 2 max pooling; “FC m → n” denotes a fully connected layer with input size m and output size n; and ReLU denotes the rectified linear unit activation. The final fully connected layer produces 10 logits, which are converted to soft-label outputs for the 10-class classification task when needed. We test two data-partition regimes. In the IID regime, the training data are split uniformly across clients. In the heterogeneous regime, labels are partitioned using a Dirichlet split with concentration parameter α = 0 .3. The local minibatch size is 64. Each communication round consists of Q = 10 local minibatch SGD updates per participating client, i.e., clients communicate once after every 10 local minibatches rather than after a full local epoch. The learning rate is initialized at 0.05 and decayed by communication round as

The effective average receive SNR is fixed at −10 dB for the results reported here. B. Convergence across datasets and partitions 

Figure 3 shows the test-accuracy trajectories for the four dataset/partition settings. The main qualitative trend is consistent across both datasets: Under IID splitting, the REED curve closely overlaps the clean FedAvg and coherent CSIT references. This suggests that, in benign statistical regimes, the variance introduced by REED’s energy-difference estimator is small relative to the stochasticity already present in FedAvg training. The heterogeneous Dirichlet split is more revealing. On MNIST, REED remains close to the two references. On Fashion-MNIST, however, the gap becomes clearly visible. A plausible explanation is that the non-IID split increases client drift, which makes the additional REED aggregation noise more pronounced in the final model. This produces both lower mean accuracy and larger run-to-run variation. D. Summary of observations 

The experiments support three conclusions. First, REED can support end-to-end FedAvg training without instantaneous CSIT or phase-coherent alignment. Second, for IID data, its empirical cost is small relative to clean FedAvg and coherent CSIT aggregation. Third, under strong statistical heterogeneity, especially on Fashion-MNIST, REED remains stable but incurs a visible accuracy and variance penalty. This last point is important for positioning the contribution: REED is a CSI-free aggregation primitive with controlled distortion, not a universal drop-in replacement for coherent AirComp in all heterogeneous regimes. VI. C ONCLUSION 

We developed REED, a CSI-free resource-element energy-difference primitive for continuous signed aggregation in OTA-FL. REED uses two matched resource elements to encode the positive and negative parts of an update and recovers the signed sum from their energy difference. Under slow-timescale channel-power calibration, the estimator is unbiased over fading, dithers, and receiver noise. For Rayleigh fading, its variance has a closed form that separates noncoherent self-noise from receiver-noise terms. Embedding this primitive in full-participation FedAvg yields a standard smooth nonconvex convergence bound with an explicit REED perturbation term.
