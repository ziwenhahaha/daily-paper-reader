## Abstract 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards ( RLVR ) has emerged as a promising paradigm for training agentic retrieval-augmented generation (RAG ) systems from outcome-only supervision. Most existing methods optimize policies from uniformly sampled rollouts, implicitly treating all trajectories as equally informative. However, trajectories differ substantially in search depth and are therefore not equally informative: deeper-search trajectories contain more retrieval decision points and provide denser direct supervision for the retrieval sub-policy. Moreover, this heterogeneity grows over training as the within-batch depth distribution shifts toward higher values, yet uniform rollout sampling remains blind to this shift. To ad-dress this, we propose CuSearch , a curriculum rollout sampling framework built on Search-Depth Greedy Allocation (SDGA) , a batch-level operator that reallocates a fixed update budget toward deeper-search trajectories. 

SDGA-Auto always targets the deepest available trajectories in the current batch, yielding an implicit training-aligned curriculum as the depth dis-tribution shifts upward. SDGA-Phase explicitly advances the curriculum threshold as deeper trajectories become sufficiently abundant. Experiments across model types and retrieval frameworks show that CuSearch con-sistently improves performance, achieving up to 11.8 exact-match points over standard GRPO on ZeroSearch . These results establish per-trajectory search depth as a reliable, annotation-free proxy for retrieval supervision density in RLVR-based agentic RAG training. The code is available at 

https://github.com/MrToser/CuSearch .

## 1 Introduction 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has become an effective approach for training large language models (LLMs) from outcome-only supervision (Lee et al., 2009; Shao et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025). This paradigm has been extended to agentic retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020), where models learn to decide when to retrieve and what to query during reasoning. For knowledge-intensive tasks, model param-eters alone cannot cover all relevant facts, making the ability to actively retrieve external information at inference time essential. Earlier systems relied on heuristic triggers (Jiang et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2023) or supervised fine-tuning on expert trajectories (Asai et al., 2023; Nakano et al., 2021). Contemporary RLVR-based methods (Jin et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025a; Sun et al., 2025; Team et al., 2025) now optimize retrieval policies solely through final answer rewards, bypassing manual design or expert traces. Despite this potential, existing methods optimize policies from uniformly sampled roll-outs (Shao et al., 2024; Schulman et al., 2017), implicitly treating all trajectories as equally informative. For the same prompt, trajectories can vary substantially in search depth, defined as the number of valid search steps they issue. Search depth provides a simple

Treating these unequally informative trajectories as equivalent allocates the update budget indiscriminately. This dilutes the influence of deeper-search trajectories and assigns optimization budget to rollouts with minimal retrieval learning value. Existing work on non-uniform sample usage has largely operated at the prompt level, filtering or reallocating the training budget based on prompt-level signals such as reward variance, estimated difficulty, or expected policy improvement (Yu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025b; Wang et al., 2026; Gu et al., 2026; Chen et al., 2025b). By aggregating statistics across rollouts for the same prompt, these approaches discard within-batch variation in individual trajectory search behavior. Process-based supervision (Lightman et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2025) provides denser intermediate feedback but requires additional annotation or auxiliary models. None of these approaches exploit search depth, an annotation-free and trajectory-intrinsic structural signal for retrieval supervision density. Over the course of training, answer-correctness reward reinforces retrieval behavior, and per-trajectory search depth increases monotonically. As shown in Figure 1, the within-batch depth distribution shifts toward larger values as RLVR progresses, deepening the informativeness gap across trajectories and making the misallocation of the update budget increasingly consequential. This temporal shift motivates a trajectory-level curriculum that concentrates the update budget on the deepest available trajectories within each batch and naturally advances as the depth distribution shifts upward. Motivated by these observations, we propose CuSearch , a curriculum rollout sampling framework for agentic RAG. At the core of CuSearch is Search-Depth Greedy Allocation (SDGA) , a batch-level operator that selects K trajectories from a larger pool of N · G rollouts, restricting reward normalization, advantage estimation, and gradient updates to this subset. Crucially, SDGA selects by search depth rather than by reward. It prefers trajectories that expose more retrieval decision points, even when final rewards are similar. We instantiate SDGA in two variants. SDGA-Auto always targets the deepest available trajectories in the current batch, yielding an implicit training-aligned curriculum as the depth distribution shifts upward. SDGA-Phase makes this curriculum explicit, advancing a phase threshold irreversibly toward deeper trajectories as they become sufficiently abundant in the rollout pool. Experiments across model types and retrieval frameworks show that CuSearch consistently improves final performance under comparable rollout budgets. Gains reach up to 11.8 

exact-match points over standard GRPO on ZeroSearch, with substantial margins over prompt-level adaptive methods such as DAPO and AR3PO.

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Agentic RAG Training with Reinforcement Learning 

Early RAG methods retrieve a fixed set of documents and condition generation on them statically (Lewis et al., 2020). Subsequent agentic retrieval methods interleave reasoning and retrieval over multiple steps, enabling models to gather evidence adaptively during inference (Singh et al., 2025; Wei et al., 2022). Representative examples include ReAct (Yao et al., 2022), FLARE (Jiang et al., 2023), and IRCoT (Trivedi et al., 2023). While these ap-proaches improve retrieval–reasoning coordination, their interaction protocols are typically predefined or rely on heuristic retrieval control, rather than being optimized end-to-end from answer rewards. Recent work extends RLVR to agentic RAG and directly optimizes retrieval behavior from outcome-only supervision. Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025), R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025), Ze-roSearch (Sun et al., 2025), ReZero (Dao & Le, 2025), RAG-Gym (Xiong et al., 2025), and ReasonRAG (Zhang et al., 2025a) study RL-based retrieval optimization through different training recipes, retrieval environments, and reward formulations. Our work is comple-mentary to this line of research. Rather than introducing a new retrieval environment or auxiliary reward signal, we study how a fixed update budget should be allocated across trajectories during training. Specifically, we use per-trajectory search depth as an annotation-free structural proxy for trajectory informativeness, and prioritize trajectories that expose denser supervision signal for the retrieval sub-policy. 

2.2 Curriculum and Adaptive Sampling in RL 

Curriculum learning improves optimization by prioritizing training samples with greater learning value (Bengio et al., 2009). Prioritized experience replay (Schaul et al., 2015) selects transitions by temporal-difference error, and DART-Math (Tong et al., 2024) applies difficulty-aware rejection sampling in supervised fine-tuning. Recent RLVR methods extend this principle to prompt-level allocation (Yu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025b; Wang et al., 2026; Gu et al., 2026; Chen et al., 2025b), filtering or reallocating gradient budget based on prompt-level signals such as reward variance, estimated difficulty, or expected policy improvement (Liu et al., 2025; Le et al., 2025; Bae et al., 2026). However, these approaches primarily focus on prompt-level allocation, loss-based prioritization, or offline data construction. None of them exploit the per-trajectory structural signal that governs retrieval informativeness within a training batch. Complementary work studies rollout structure: Depth-Breadth Synergy (Yang et al., 2025) balances reasoning depth and diversity, InfoFlow (Obregon et al., 2019) optimizes reward density, and process supervision (Lightman et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2025) provides step-level annotations. CuSearch operates at the trajectory level rather than the prompt level, and uses search depth as a structural signal rather than a reward-derived one. SDGA-Auto. SDGA-Auto is the stateless instantiation of SDGA. It sets ps = Smax − s + 1 to give higher priority to deeper search buckets and sets tSmax = K with ts = 0 for s < Smax to ideally allocate all budget to the deepest bucket. At every step, it greedily concentrates the full update budget on the deepest-search trajectories available in the current batch. When the deepest bucket is under-capacity, the shortfall is redistributed to shallower buckets in descending order of search depth. We deliberately decouple the selection criterion from reward to isolate the retrieval supervision signal. Reward information is incorporated downstream through within-group advantage normalization. Although stateless, SDGA-Auto implicitly follows a curriculum aligned with the model’s training dynamics: because the within-batch depth distribution shifts monotonically toward higher search counts during training, the depth level that SDGA-Auto actually concentrates on rises progressively, advancing from moderately deep trajectories early in training to the genuinely deepest trajectories as they become prevalent. This implicit curriculum emerges naturally from the interaction between the greedy selection rule and the evolving rollout distribution, without any explicit phase management. See Appendix C.2 for details. 

SDGA-Phase. SDGA-Phase is the phase-adaptive instantiation of SDGA, motivated by curriculum learning (Bengio et al., 2009). It maintains a monotonically advancing phase threshold k ∈ { 0, . . . , Smax − 1} and sets tk+1 = K and ts = 0 for s̸ = k + 1, targeting bucket 

k + 1 at phase k. The priority ordering favors bucket k + 1 first, then deeper buckets, then shallower ones. The phase advances from k to k + 1 when ∑s>k+1 cs ≥ K, ensuring the target bucket is deep enough to provide informative retrieval supervision yet abundant enough to avoid falling back to shallow trajectories. This design prevents the fallback to shallow-bucket selection that SDGA-Auto can suffer early in training, when the deepest bucket is sparsely populated. See Appendix C.3 for details. 

3.3 Implementation and Training Setup 

CuSearch is inserted as a lightweight trajectory selection layer between rollout generation and policy optimization, requiring no changes to the reward function, model architecture, or agent environment. The complete training loop is in Appendix B. We follow the standard setup from prior RLVR-based agentic RAG work, keeping the prompt template, output format, and reward design fixed so that all experiments vary only the rollout allocation strategy. Complete training details are in Appendix A. 

## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup Models and training. We use Qwen2.5-3B (Qwen Team, 2024) as the primary backbone for ablation and analysis, LLaMA-3.2-3B-Base (Llama Team, 2024) to test cross-model generalization, and Qwen2.5-7B (Qwen Team, 2024) to examine scalability across model sizes. All models are trained with VERL and GRPO. Unless otherwise specified, we set 

N = 64, G = 3, K = 96, M = 32, Smax = 5, learning rate = 1 × 10 −6, and β = 0.001. All experiments are conducted on 8 × A100 80G GPUs. Unless otherwise noted, all figures in this paper report results on Qwen2.5-3B with ZeroSearch. 

Retrieval frameworks. We evaluate CuSearch under two representative agentic retrieval settings. ZeroSearch (Sun et al., 2025) uses LLM-simulated retrieval, and we adopt Alibaba-NLP/Simulation-LLM-google-7B-V2 as the retriever simulator in the main experiments. To test whether the learned policy transfers beyond the simulator, we further evaluate it with a real search API, as detailed in Appendix D.4. Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) uses a chunk-based offline retrieval corpus for both training and evaluation. Together, these two settings allow us to study CuSearch under retrieval environments with different levels of stochasticity. 6Datasets and metric. We evaluate on seven open-domain QA benchmarks. The single-hop datasets are NQ (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), and PopQA (Mallen et al., 2023). The multi-hop datasets are HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), 2WikiMulti-HopQA (Ho et al., 2020), MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022), and Bamboogle (Press et al., 2023). We use Exact Match (EM) as the evaluation metric. 

Baselines. We compare CuSearch against both rollout-selection baselines and representative prompt-level adaptive methods. GRPO-Full uses all N · G rollouts without trajectory selection, corresponding to the standard training protocol of ZeroSearch or Search-R1. 

GRPO-Half uses K/G prompts with G rollouts each, yielding the same K trajectory updates as CuSearch but without depth-aware selection. Unlike CuSearch, it generates only K

rollouts directly rather than selecting from a larger pool. SDGA-Auto and SDGA-Phase 

are our two search-depth-based allocation strategies, both using Smax = 5 in the main experiments. SDGA-Anti reverses the search-depth priority order to deliberately select shallow-search trajectories first, serving as an anti-curriculum directional ablation. Random 

draws K trajectories uniformly at random from T , matching CuSearch in both rollout generation and update cost, so any performance gap isolates the contribution of the depth-aware selection signal. Top-K Reward selects the K highest-reward trajectories from T ,matching CuSearch in compute, and tests whether reward magnitude alone provides an effective selection criterion. To position CuSearch relative to prior adaptive RL methods, we additionally compare with DAPO (Yu et al., 2025), which removes prompts whose rollouts all receive the same reward, and AR3PO (Zhang et al., 2025b), which adaptively reallocates rollout budget across prompts based on estimated difficulty. Both are implemented within the same VERL/GRPO pipeline under matched total compute.

Similar gains are observed for Qwen2.5-7B, with improvements of 11.4 EM under ZeroSearch and 8.1 EM under Search-R1, and for LLaMA-3.2-3B-Base, confirming generalization across model families and scales. While the largest gains are obtained under the ZeroSearch stochastic simulator, SDGA-Phase also yields consistent improvements under a real search API and chunk-based retrieval, as analyzed in Section 4.4 and Appendix E. 

(2) Explicit phase-adaptive curriculum outperforms implicit curriculum early in training. 

Both SDGA-Auto and SDGA-Phase constitute forms of curriculum learning: SDGA-Auto follows an implicit curriculum aligned with the model’s training dynamics, as the greedy selection naturally concentrates on progressively deeper trajectories as the depth distribution shifts upward. SDGA-Phase makes this curriculum explicit through a managed phase threshold. Across settings, SDGA-Phase outperforms SDGA-Auto in nearly all cases, with the sole exception of LLaMA-3.2-3B-Base under Search-R1 where SDGA-Auto leads by 0.2 average EM. The advantage of SDGA-Phase is most pronounced early in training: when deep-search buckets are sparsely populated, SDGA-Auto falls back to shallow buckets and its selection becomes indistinguishable from random, whereas SDGA-Phase targets the depth level the batch can reliably supply and advances only when deeper trajectories become sufficiently abundant. 

(3) The benefit depends on the retrieval environment. The gain of SDGA-Phase is larger on ZeroSearch than on Search-R1. For Qwen2.5-3B, SDGA-Phase improves GRPO-Full by 

11.8 EM on ZeroSearch and by 5.0 EM on Search-R1, raising the average from 35.9 to 40.9. We analyze the contributions of search depth growth and retrieval stochasticity to this gap in Section 4.4 and Appendix E. Figure 3(c) additionally shows that SDGA-Phase accelerates the growth of average search depth during training, indicating that the proposed allocation changes not only final task accuracy but also the model’s retrieval behavior. 

4.3 Ablation Studies 

We conduct six ablation studies, with full results and tables in Appendix D. (1) Sampling signal and compute fairness. Random trajectory sampling, which shares the identical rollout generation and update cost as CuSearch, performs close to GRPO-Half but far below SDGA-Auto and SDGA-Phase. This directly rules out a compute-based explanation: the gains of CuSearch stem from the depth-aware selection signal, not from training on fewer trajectories per step. (2) Robustness to Smax . Both SDGA variants consistently outper-form GRPO-Full across Smax ∈ { 3, 4, 5 }, confirming robustness to the depth discretization granularity. (3) Direction of the signal. SDGA-Anti, which reverses the priority order, yields a drastic performance drop to 20.4 average EM compared to 52.9 for SDGA-Auto, confirming that the signal is directionally meaningful and that shallow-trajectory preference is actively harmful. (4) Transfer to a real search API. A policy trained with SDGA-Phase 8under ZeroSearch retains a 5.1 EM advantage over GRPO-Full when evaluated with a real SERPER API, confirming that learned retrieval behaviors transfer beyond the simu-lator. (5) Reward-based trajectory selection. Top-K Reward achieves 41.8 average EM, close to Random (41.9) and 14.5 points below SDGA-Phase. Notably, this score also falls below GRPO-Full, which achieves 44.5 EM. This suggests that concentrating updates on the highest-reward trajectories can hurt training by introducing selection bias, confirming that reward magnitude alone does not provide an effective selection signal and that search depth captures a structurally distinct and more informative criterion. (6) Prompt-level adaptive baselines. SDGA-Phase outperforms DAPO by 5.9 EM and AR3PO by 6.7 EM under matched compute, indicating that rollout-level structural selection is both distinct from and more effective than prompt-level filtering for agentic RAG. 

4.4 In-Depth Analysis (C1) Search depth grows during training. Under both ZeroSearch and Search-R1, answer-correctness reward alone drives a monotonic increase in per-trajectory search counts, provid-ing a progressively larger supply of deep-search trajectories. The growth is more pronounced under ZeroSearch, partially explaining the larger gains in that setting. 

(C2) Retrieval-environment stochasticity amplifies the gain. In more stochastic environ-ments, diverse search decisions produce varied evidence, making each additional search step more informative. In deterministic environments, repeated queries return overlapping re-sults, reducing the marginal value of deeper trajectories. ZeroSearch uses an LLM-simulated retriever that produces varied results across queries. Search-R1 uses a fixed offline cor-pus where repeated or similar queries tend to return overlapping documents, reducing the marginal benefit of additional search steps. Table 8 shows gains of 11.8 EM under ZeroSearch, 5.1 EM with a real search API, and 5.0 EM under Search-R1, consistent with C1 as the primary driver and C2 as a secondary amplifier in high-stochasticity settings. A detailed analysis is provided in Appendix E. Taken together, these results confirm that search depth is a reliable and annotation-free signal for selecting informative trajectories. Allocating the update budget toward deeper-search rollouts yields consistent improvements over both uniform sampling and prompt-level adaptive methods across diverse training conditions, providing direct evidence for the central claim of this work. 

## 5 Conclusion 

Per-trajectory search depth is a reliable, annotation-free proxy for retrieval supervision density in RLVR-based agentic RAG training. Search counts increase monotonically over training, and the within-batch depth distribution shifts toward higher values, deepening the informativeness gap that uniform rollout usage fails to exploit. To address this, we propose CuSearch , built on the SDGA operator. SDGA-Auto always targets the deepest available trajectories in the current batch, yielding an implicit training-aligned curriculum as the depth distribution shifts upward. SDGA-Phase explicitly advances the curriculum threshold as deeper trajectories become sufficiently abundant. Consistent gains across ZeroSearch and Search-R1 confirm that depth-aware rollout allocation improves retrieval sub-policy learning across model families and retrieval frameworks. Gain magnitude is governed primarily by search depth growth during training and amplified by retrieval-environment stochasticity. 

Limitations. The method yields smaller gains when search depth growth is limited or when the retrieval environment has low stochasticity. It uses search depth as a single scalar feature and does not condition on reward quality, though within-group advantage normal-ization mitigates this by suppressing the gradient contribution of low-reward trajectories even when they are selected. Combining search depth with reward-derived signals and extending CuSearch to other agentic behaviors such as tool use and code execution are natural directions for future work. 9References

A.2 Reward Design 

We follow the reward design used in Search-R1 and adopt a rule-based reward that combines answer quality with format compliance (Jin et al., 2025). Following ZeroSearch (Sun et al., 2025), we use the F1 score rather than exact-match accuracy as the answer-quality signal. Specifically, the reward for a trajectory τ is defined as 

r(τ) = F1 (τ) ( 1 − λ f ) + λ f rfmt (τ), (3) where F1 (τ) ∈ [0, 1 ] denotes the F1 score between the extracted final answer and the ground-truth answer, and rfmt (τ) ∈ { 0, 1 } is a binary indicator of format validity. A trajectory is considered format-valid if it follows the prescribed agent format, with rea-soning enclosed by <think>...</think> , search queries enclosed by <search>...</search> ,and the final answer enclosed by <answer>...</answer> . This design encourages the model not only to produce high-quality answers but also to adhere to the interaction protocol required for search-engine invocation. Following the empirical findings of Search-R1, we do not use an additional intermediate retrieval reward. Instead, we use the answer-quality reward together with the format-compliance signal throughout all experiments. Table 2: Main training hyperparameters used in our experiments. D.5 Ablation 5: Reward-Based Trajectory Selection 

To test whether search depth provides a selection signal beyond reward magnitude, we compare against Top-K Reward , which selects the K trajectories with the highest individual rewards from the rollout pool T . Results are included in Table 3 alongside the other selection-signal baselines. Top-K Reward achieves 41.8 average EM, close to Random (41.9) and substantially below SDGA-Auto (52.9) and SDGA-Phase (56.3). This result is consistent with a structural obser-vation: high-reward trajectories are not necessarily deep-search trajectories. A model that answers correctly without issuing any retrieval calls receives a high reward but contributes zero retrieval gradient coverage. Preferring such trajectories concentrates the update budget on answer-generation behavior while depriving the retrieval sub-policy of direct supervi-sion. In contrast, search-depth-based selection explicitly targets trajectories that provide the most retrieval decision points per update, regardless of whether those trajectories receive high or low rewards. Advantage normalization within GRPO then handles the reward signal: low-reward deep-search trajectories receive suppressed gradient weights, while high-reward deep-search trajectories receive amplified weights. D.6 Ablation 6: Comparison with Prompt-Level Adaptive Methods Implementation. For DAPO, we implement only the dynamic sampling component: at each training step, prompt groups in which all G rollouts receive the same reward are removed before the policy update, as these groups produce zero group-normalized advantage and contribute no learning signal. All other hyperparameters, including the loss formulation, clipping coefficient ε, and KL penalty weight β, are kept identical to the GRPO baseline. For AR3PO, we implement the adaptive rollout allocation: we track the per-prompt pass rate (fraction of G rollouts receiving a positive reward) over a sliding window, and reallocate the rollout budget to favor prompts with intermediate pass rates. Prompts whose recent pass rate falls near 0 or 1 receive fewer rollouts, while prompts with pass rates in the intermediate range receive more. The total rollout budget per step is held fixed at N · G = 192 across all prompt-level baselines and CuSearch to ensure a fair comparison. As shown in Table 7, DAPO and AR3PO improve average EM over GRPO-Full by 5.9 and 5.1 points respectively, whereas SDGA-Phase achieves an 11.8-point gain under the same rollout budget. This comparison highlights the difference between the two levels of adaptation. Prompt-level methods decide which prompts deserve more training signal, while our approach decides which trajectories within a batch carry more informative retrieval supervision. Our results suggest that, for agentic RAG, exploiting rollout-level structural heterogeneity yields substantially larger gains than prompt-level filtering alone. Because the two operate at different levels, they are potentially complementary.

To better understand when CuSearch is effective, we analyze the method through the lens of two conditions. 

Condition C1: Search depth grows during training. CuSearch reallocates the within-batch update budget toward deeper-search trajectories. This mechanism is only meaningful if deeper-search trajectories emerge with non-trivial frequency. Figure 1(a) shows that under ZeroSearch, answer-correctness reward alone drives a monotonic increase in average search count throughout training. The same trend holds under Search-R1, where average valid searches per rollout rise from approximately 1.0 to 1.3 and stabilize around 1.2. This confirms that C1 is a general property of RLVR-based agentic RAG training, not specific to a particular retrieval framework. However, the magnitude of growth differs: ZeroSearch exhibits a steeper and more sustained increase, while Search-R1 reaches a lower plateau. This difference in growth rate partially explains why CuSearch yields larger gains under ZeroSearch. When search depth growth is absent, the within-batch depth distribution 20 remains concentrated at low counts, and search-depth-based allocation collapses toward random selection. 

Condition C2: Retrieval-environment stochasticity amplifies the gain. Beyond the supply of deep-search trajectories, the value of each additional search step depends on how much new evidence it contributes. In a more stochastic retrieval environment, different search decisions are more likely to produce diverse observations, so deeper trajectories carry more unique supervision signal per step. In a more deterministic environment, repeated or similar queries often return overlapping evidence, reducing the marginal utility of additional search steps. Table 8 is consistent with this account: the gain of SDGA-Phase over GRPO-Full is 11.8 EM in the stochastic ZeroSearch simulator, 5.1 EM with a real search API, and 

5.0 EM in chunk-based Search-R1. The simulator yields the largest gain, while the two lower-stochasticity settings yield smaller and comparable improvements. Importantly, the near-identical gains under the real API and Search-R1 suggest that C1 is the primary driver of the large ZeroSearch advantage: ZeroSearch exhibits substantially stronger search depth growth, which provides a larger selectable budget of deep trajectories at each training step. C2 acts as a secondary amplifier when retrieval diversity is high, but the available three-condition comparison does not allow a clean separation of the two effects. In summary, C1 governs the supply of informative trajectories and is the primary determi-nant of gain magnitude, while C2 governs per-step information density and provides an additional boost in high-stochasticity environments. 

E.1 CuSearch Changes Retrieval Behavior, Not Only Final Answers 

Beyond final EM, Figure 3(c) shows that SDGA-Phase consistently produces rollouts with higher average search count than GRPO-Full throughout training. Moreover, the gap widens as the training distribution shifts toward larger search counts, as shown in Figure 1. This reflects a reinforcing selection dynamic: as training progresses and the model naturally produces more deep-search trajectories, CuSearch continues to concentrate gradient updates on those trajectories, progressively strengthening the policy’s retrieval tendency. In contrast, GRPO-Full averages informative and weakly informative trajectories uniformly at every step, diluting the retrieval-specific supervision signal. Figure 3(b) provides a complementary view from the reward side: SDGA-Phase maintains higher mean reward throughout training, indicating that this difference in retrieval behavior is accompanied by stronger overall policy improvement.

## G Why Search Depth Maximizes Retrieval Gradient Coverage 

From a gradient optimization perspective, the central question is: given a fixed update budget K, how should we select T ⊆ T to maximize the amount of direct gradient infor-mation devoted to retrieval behavior in each policy update? We formalize this question by decomposing the policy gradient and defining a retrieval gradient coverage metric. The analy-sis shows that CuSearch, by selecting trajectories with the highest search counts, provably maximizes this metric under any fixed budget. 

G.1 Policy Gradient Decomposition

Remark 1. For any fixed budget |T| = K , retrieval gradient coverage Cr (T) is maximized by selecting the K trajectories with the highest search counts. This follows directly from the monotonicity of |R (τ)| in cnt (τ): since Cr (T) = ∑τ∈T |R (τ)| is a sum of non-decreasing terms, replacing any included trajectory with an excluded one that has a strictly higher search count increases the total coverage. The unique maximizer (up to ties) is therefore the top-K selection by search count. 

This establishes the theoretical basis of CuSearch: selecting trajectories by descending search depth is the optimal strategy for maximizing retrieval gradient coverage under a fixed update budget .

Why advantages are treated as a stochastic factor. In practice, advantages A(τ) also modulate the gradient magnitude. However, advantages are computed from within-group reward normalization and are therefore only available after rollout generation completes. At selection time, the advantage of a candidate trajectory is unknown. Moreover, within a prompt group, advantages are zero-mean by construction, so their expected contribution to the gradient is zero in expectation. This means that, in expectation over the rollout distribution, the structural component Cr (T) is the dominant deterministic factor governing how much retrieval-specific gradient signal is accumulated per update. Advantage values introduce variance around this expectation but do not systematically favor shallow-search trajectories over deep-search ones. We note that across prompt groups, search count and advantage could in principle be correlated: for instance, prompts that consistently elicit many search steps may exhibit different within-group reward profiles than prompts that rarely trigger retrieval. However, within each group, advantages remain zero-mean by construction, so this cross-group correlation does not break the within-step structural argument. The ablation in Appendix D.1 confirms this empirically: random trajectory selection, which preserves the original within-batch depth distribution and ignores the structural informativeness signal, performs substantially worse than search-depth-based selection (41.9 vs. 52.9 average EM), consistent with the theoretical prediction that structural coverage is the primary driver of retrieval learning efficiency. 

G.3 Connection to the Two Effectiveness Conditions 

The analysis above explains the two empirical conditions identified in Section 4.4. 

Condition C1 (Search depth increases during training). If the policy rarely produces trajectories with cnt (τ) > 0, few retrieval decision points exist across all rollouts, and Cr (T)

remains small regardless of the selection strategy. The monotonic increase in average search count shown in Figure 1 ensures a growing supply of informative trajectories, making the coverage gap between search-depth-aware and uniform selection increasingly large. 

Condition C2 (Retrieval environment is stochastic). Retrieval gradient terms are most informative when each search step yields genuinely new evidence, producing diverse gradient contributions at retrieval positions. In a deterministic environment where repeated queries return identical results, successive search steps yield near-redundant gradient terms, reducing the marginal coverage value of each additional retrieval step. In a stochastic environment such as a web search simulator, different queries return diverse evidence, preserving the informational value of each additional step and amplifying the benefit of higher Cr (T). This explains why the gain of CuSearch is larger under ZeroSearch than under Search-R1, as reported in Table 8. 24
