## Abstract 

Large Language Model (LLM)-based multi-agent systems rely on optimized col-laboration topologies to balance performance and communication costs. However, current methods struggle with the inherent stability-extensibility trade-off and often misalign computational budgets with query difficulty. We propose ATOM, an adap-tive framework that generates budget-controllable collaboration graphs via a novel task-driven reinforcement learning paradigm. Inspired by atomic structures, ATOM employs a nucleus-electron hierarchy: it maintains a stable, offline-learned collab-oration backbone (the nucleus) while dynamically activating query-conditioned agents (electrons) during inference. Crucially, a complexity-aware budgeting strat-egy aligns resource consumption with task demands by estimating query difficulty to strictly regulate electron instantiation. Extensive experiments across six diverse benchmarks demonstrate that ATOM achieves state-of-the-art performance while improving token efficiency by up to 30% compared to strong baselines.

To overcome the limitations of fixed architectures, researchers have recently explored dynamic topol-ogy generation methods [ 45 , 26 ]. Without explicit difficulty estimation, modification methods over-spend tokens on trivial queries before simplification occurs. Similarly, generative methods fall into an вҖңaverage-complexityвҖқ trapвҖ”over-provisioning resources for simple tasks while under-provisioning for complex ones. To address these gaps, we propose ATOM, an Adaptive Topology Optimization Mechanism using task-driven reinforcement learning. electrons to expand communication on demand, via two key features: 

(1) NucleusвҖ“Electron Two-Stage Topology Learning. To prevent structural volatility and mitigate the topology search overhead of from-scratch generation, we decouple topology learning into an offline and online phase. We first condense a stable nucleus (core backbone) offline to serve as a resilient reasoning conduit. During inference, the online generator only predicts dual-channel edge probabilities (spatial and temporal) for query-conditioned electrons , drastically reducing the search space while ensuring structural control. 

(2) Complexity-Aware Budgeted Instantiation. To break the вҖңaverage-complexityвҖқ trap inherent in static or generative baselines, we explicitly align computational resources with task demands. We introduce a predictor to estimate query difficulty, which dynamically allocates a adaptive collaboration budget. This ensures true token efficiency by instantiating a sparse electron subgraph strictly when the task complexity warrants it, preventing resource waste on trivial queries. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: вҖў Hierarchical Nucleus-Electron Architecture. We propose a novel structural paradigm that decouples offline stable backbone condensation (nucleus) from online task-driven expansion (elec-trons). This inherently resolves the stability-extensibility trade-off while significantly mitigating the topology search overhead of from-scratch generation. вҖў Complexity-Aware Budgeted Instantiation. We introduce a difficulty estimator that dynami-cally allocates multi-agent computation and regulates electron subgraph instantiation based on query complexity. To optimize this budget-constrained routing policy, we design a task-driven reinforcement learning objective with structural regularization. вҖў State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance. Extensive experiments across six diverse benchmarks demonstrate the superiority of ATOM. It consistently achieves state-of-the-art accuracy while improving token efficiency by up to 30% compared to strong baselines, establishing a new Pareto frontier for MAS collaboration.

## 3 ATOM for MAS Topology Design 

To overcome the inherent stability-extensibility trade-off in existing architectures, ATOM employs a two-tier nucleusвҖ“electron hierarchy. Specifically, we partition the global agent pool into a persistent, offline-learned nucleus backbone ( Vnuc ) and a dynamic electron reservoir ( Velec ). Crucially, this backbone refers strictly to a fixed partition of agent nodes ; all inter-agent communication edgesвҖ” including those within the nucleus itselfвҖ”remain fully dynamic and query-conditioned during the online phase to maintain maximum reasoning flexibility.

4.1 Experimental Settings Benchmarks: We evaluate ATOM on six benchmarks spanning three domains: General Reasoning 

(MMLU [ 10 ]), Mathematical Reasoning (GSM8K [ 6], MultiArith [ 25 ], SVAMP [ 22 ], AQuA [ 18 ]), and Code Generation (HumanEval [4]). 

Baselines: We benchmark against a comprehensive suite of baselines across four paradigms: (1) 

Single-agent methods , including standard prompting and CoT [ 32 ]; (2) Static MAS topologies , such as Chain, Star, Random, and Complete graphs [ 24 ]; (3) Debate frameworks , exemplified by LLM-Debate [ 8]; and (4) Learnable MAS topologies , including AgentPrune [ 37 ], AgentDropout [ 31 ], G-Designer [38], and ARG-Designer [17]. 

Implementation Details. Our backbone LLMs are Meta-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (served locally via vLLM) and DeepSeek-V3.2. We primarily conduct evaluations on the constrained 8B model to rigor-ously isolate our architectural gains from raw model capabilities. We use all-MiniLM-L6-v2 [ 30 ]as encoder. Following standard MAS practice [45], we default to 5 agents with explicit profiles.

4.2.2 Ablation Study 

To validate our core design choices, we conduct an ablation study on the complexity-aware budgeting strategy and architectural RL objectives (Table 2). 

Impact of Adaptive Budgeting. We compare our adaptive strategy against Zero (nucleus only), Fixed, and Random budgets. Ignoring query difficulty via Fixed or Random budgets inflates token consumption and degrades performance (e.g., Fixed Budget accuracy drops to 63.64% on HumanEval while nearly doubling MMLU prompt tokens from 0.29 to 0.51). Conversely, the highly token-efficient Zero Budget lacks topological capacity for complex tasks, causing a sharp 6.61% accuracy drop on HumanEval. By explicitly modulating electron excitation based on estimated complexity, ATOM prevents resource waste on trivial tasks and under-provisioning on complex ones, achieving peak performance at near-minimal token cost.

4.2.3 Extended Analysis Scaling to Advanced Backbones. To test whether framework-level gains persist with stronger backbones, we evaluate ATOM on DeepSeek-V3.2. As shown in Fig. 5, ATOM maintains highest accuracy across all datasets while incurring computational overhead close to the Vanilla single-agent baseline. By contrast, alternative MAS frameworks such as AgentPrune and G-Designer require up to 4Г— more tokens. These results demonstrate that ATOM remains a cost-effective and scalable topology generation framework even for frontier large language models. 

Efficacy of Adaptive Budgeting. Predicted task complexity and actual token consumption on MMLU show a strong positive correlation (Pearson r = 0 .737 , Fig. 6). This confirms ATOM dynamically aligns its topological scale with intrinsic difficulty. The minimal nucleus efficiently resolves simple queries, while constraint-heavy tasks unlock the peripheral electron reservoir, demonstrating that our theoretical budgeting mechanism translates directly into proportional computational scaling. 

Robustness Against Prompt Injection. Following [ 45 ], we evaluate resilience against prompt injection. While adversarial instructions catastrophically degrade fully dynamic MAS topologies, ATOM exhibits superior robustness, limiting the performance drop to 6.4% (Fig. 7). This stems from our two-tier hierarchy and structural regularization. By anchoring execution to the offline-verified nucleus, compromised electrons are structurally prevented from eclipsing the primary cognitive conduit, thereby mitigating cascading systemic failures. 

4.2.4 Case Study 

To illustrate ATOMвҖҷs structural adaptability, Fig. 8 compares its generated topologies against ARG-Designer [ 17 ] on GSM8K queries. Crucially, ATOM avoids the вҖңaverage-complexityвҖқ trap by explicitly aligning topological scale with task demands. For a simple arithmetic task (estimated complexity 0.331 ), it instantiates a minimal two-agent chain (Mathematical Analyst вҶ’ Math Solver) to prevent redundant overhead. Conversely, for a multi-step financial problem (complexity 0.493 ), it dynamically expands the reasoning network by recruiting a Programming Expert . In stark contrast, ARG-Designer exhibits structural rigidity, generating the exact same three-agent topology regardless of query difficulty, thereby validating ATOMвҖҷs dual advantage in efficiency and flexible scalability. 

## 5 Related Work 

LLM-based Multi-Agent System While LLM-based agents excel across diverse domains [ 19 , 42 ,7, 33 , 16 , 11 , 41 , 36 , 44 ], single agents inherently struggle with long-horizon tasks due to limited reasoning depth and expertise coverage [ 8, 28 , 15 ]. To address these limitations, Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) leverage collective intelligence [ 13 , 40 ] to achieve superior performance in complex, collaborative scenarios such as software development and world simulations [23, 12, 5, 21, 14]. 

MAS Topology Optimization Topology design has evolved from static structuresвҖ”like chains [ 32 ,12 ] or trees [ 35 ]вҖ”to adaptive frameworks [ 2]. Recent deployment optimization efforts span three categories: structural pruning and dropout (e.g., AgentPrune [ 37 ], AgentDropout [ 31 ]), budget-constrained initialization [ 1, 34 , 27 ], and from-scratch GNN-based generative design (e.g., G-Designer [ 38 ], ARG-DESIGNER [ 17 ]). Additionally, emerging works tackle communication costs by modeling bandwidth as a scarce resource via auction-based bidding to filter low-value messages [ 9]. Concurrently, GoAgent [ 3 ] reduces overhead by forming explicit task-specific groups rather than 9relying on dense node-centric connectivity. Our work departs fundamentally from both template modification and purely generative prediction. Inspired by flexible-firm architectures [ 29 ], ATOM introduces a hierarchical strategy, structurally decoupling a stable reasoning core from dynamic, budget-aware expansion. 

## 6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose ATOM, a reinforcement learning framework with a stable nucleus back-bone and dynamic electron extensions. By decoupling offline structure learning from online query-conditioned expansion, ATOM adaptively allocates computation and instantiates task-specific topolo-gies. Experiments on six benchmarks show that ATOM achieves state-of-the-art performance with up to 30% higher token efficiency and strong scalability.
