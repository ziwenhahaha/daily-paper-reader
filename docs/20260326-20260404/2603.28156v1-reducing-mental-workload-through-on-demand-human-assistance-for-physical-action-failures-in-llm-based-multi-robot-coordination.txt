# Reducing Mental Workload through On-Demand Human Assistance for Physical Action Failures in LLM-based Multi-Robot Coordination 

Shoichi Hasegawa 1,∗, Akira Taniguchi 1, Lotfi El Hafi 1,Gustavo Alfonso Garcia Ricardez 1, Tadahiro Taniguchi 2,1

Abstract — Multi-robot coordination based on large language models (LLMs) has attracted growing attention, since LLMs enable the direct translation of natural language instructions into robot action plans by decomposing tasks and generating high-level plans. However, recovering from physical execu-tion failures remains difficult, and tasks often stagnate due to the repetition of the same unsuccessful actions. While frameworks for remote robot operation using Mixed Reality were proposed, there have been few attempts to implement remote error resolution specifically for physical failures in multi-robot environments. In this study, we propose REPAIR (Robot Execution with Planned And Interactive Recovery), a human-in-the-loop framework that integrates remote er-ror resolution into LLM-based multi-robot planning. In this method, robots execute tasks autonomously; however, when an irrecoverable failure occurs, the LLM requests assistance from an operator, enabling task continuity through remote intervention. Evaluations using a multi-robot trash collection task in a real-world environment confirmed that REPAIR significantly improves task progress (the number of items cleared within a time limit) compared to fully autonomous methods. Furthermore, for easily collectable items, it achieved task progress equivalent to full remote control. The results also suggested that the mental workload on the operator may differ in terms of physical demand and effort. The project website is https://emergentsystemlabstudent.github.io/REPAIR/. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Multi-robot systems are a promising approach for complet-ing complex tasks composed of numerous subtasks within a short time. Their applications are wide-ranging, and research has been conducted in diverse environments, including daily living settings [1], industrial applications [2], and disaster sites [3]. To address environmental diversity and task com-plexity, flexible task understanding and planning generation are required. In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have attracted attention as a method for controlling multiple robots [1], [4], [5], [6], [7], [8], [9]. LLMs function as high-level planners that can decompose and structure tasks from natural language instructions and generate action plans.

Moreover, their ability to flexibly handle new tasks in a few-shot prompting has driven the development of LLM-based multi-robot coordination techniques. For example, Kan-nan et al. proposed a framework called SMART-LLM [6]. This method consists of four steps: task decomposition, coalition formation, task allocation, and task execution. The LLM generates instructions for multiple agents while con-sidering each robot’s skills and allocation rules, thereby en-abling coordinated behavior. However, while these methods can assign suitable tasks to individual robots and execute actions efficiently, challenges remain in handling failures during physical actions. Although such failures can also occur in single-robot systems, their impact becomes more critical in multi-robot settings. In multi-robot coordination, tasks are often interdependent, and the failure of one robot can stall the progress of the entire system. For example, if a robot repeatedly fails to grasp an object, subsequent tasks assigned to other robots may also be delayed, resulting in reduced overall task efficiency. Furthermore, continuously relying on human teleoperation for multiple robots imposes a significant mental and physical burden on the operator, making full manual control impractical as the number of robots increases. To address these challenges, it is important to design a human-in-the-loop framework in which robots request assistance from an operator only when necessary, enabling both scalable operation and robust recovery from physical failures. Remote error resolution using Virtual Reality (VR) and Mixed Reality (MR) has been proposed as a framework in

For example, Kumar et al. applied remote error resolution to a warehouse pick-and-place task, in which a single robotic arm encounters perception or manipulation errors [10]. This study makes the following two contributions. 1) We demonstrated that introducing operator-assisted re-mote error resolution into an LLM-based multi-robot planning framework improves task progress compared to methods that do not incorporate it. 2) We suggested that differences may arise in the opera-tor’s mental workload —particularly in terms of physi-cal demand and effort —compared to teleoperation. A. LLM-Based Multi-Robot Coordination and Its Challenges 

In recent years, there has been growing interest in lever-aging an LLM to enable multiple agents to provide physical assistance to humans based on natural language instruc-tions in everyday environments [1], [4], [5], [6], [7], [8], [9]. Chen et al. classify LLM-based multi-robot planning frameworks into three categories: decentralized multi-agent systems (DMS), centralized multi-agent systems (CMS), and hybrid multi-agent systems (HMS) [13]. In DMS, each agent is equipped with an LLM, and agents communicate with one another in natural language to collaboratively execute tasks. In CMS, a single LLM receives an instruction, decomposes the task, converts it into executable commands for each robot, and assigns them accordingly to achieve coordinated behav-ior. HMS combines these two approaches. As a representative DMS approach, Zhao et al. demonstrated a tabletop task in which two robotic arms plan through natural language dia-logue and achieve coordinated behavior, including collision avoidance [8]. In addition, studies in home-like environments have shown that two agents can collaboratively perform tasks such as transporting objects to designated locations using containers and preparing meals through dialogue [9]. On the other hand, as a representative CMS approach, Kannan et al. 

proposed SMART-LLM, which consists of four stages: task decomposition, coalition formation, task allocation, and task execution [6]. In this method, the LLM generates instructions while considering each robot’s skills and allocation rules, thereby enabling coordinated behavior. Furthermore, other approaches have been proposed in which multiple robots learn object placement on each floor using probabilistic models based on Bayesian theory, and the LLM assigns tasks based on the inferred results [1]. Another line of work focuses on task priorities and dependencies, combining dependency graph generation by LLMs with task allocation using linear programming [5]. Thus, LLM-based multi-robot planning has developed due to its ability to readily translate natural language instructions into action plans through few-shot prompting. However, challenges remain in handling failures in physical robot actions. In particular, robots may fail to recover appropriately from errors and instead repeat the same actions. For example, when a robot fails to grasp an object and drops it, it may not recognize the failure, repeatedly perform detection while having lost track of the object, and consequently fall into a loop of repeated actions. To address this issue, it is important not to rely solely on autonomous decision-making by LLMs, but to design a human-in-the-loop framework in which robots request assistance from an operator at an appropriate time. 

B. Remote Error Resolution and Its Challenges 

Remote error resolution using VR and MR has been proposed as a framework in which operators provide ap-propriate support in place of robots when they fail in task execution or perception [10], [11], [12]. Kumar et al. applied this approach to a warehouse pick-and-place task, where a single robotic arm encounters perception or manipula-tion errors [10]. As a result, compared to camera-based and VR-based methods, their approach achieved reduced task completion time, improved usability, and lower mental workload. Furthermore, Wozniak et al. and Kaipa et al. 

focused on perception errors in robot object detection and developed systems in which operators use VR to correct such errors [11], [12]. However, existing remote error resolution approaches us-ing MR and VR primarily focused on single-robot task exe-cution, and intermediate designs that allow human interven-tion when physical failures occur during multi-robot planning have not been sufficiently explored. Moreover, when remote error resolution is integrated into LLM-based multi-robot planning, its effects on task progress and the operator’s mental workload remain unclear. In this study, we propose REPAIR, a system that incorpo-rates a mechanism for remote error resolution —whereby a human can remotely intervene to resolve issues when a robot fails to perform a physical action —within an LLM-based multi-robot coordination system (Fig. 2). REPAIR consists of two components: (1) task decomposition, allocation, and action planning for multiple robots based on an LLM, and (2) remote error resolution, whereby an operator responds remotely when a robot fails to perform a physical action. 

A. LLM-Driven Multi-Robot Task Decomposition, Assign-ment, and Action Planning 

We consider a system consisting of two robots: a mobile robot and a mobile manipulator, and assume that task de-composition, allocation, and planning are performed. 

1) Task Decomposition and Allocation: First, the LLM receives an instruction from the user and decomposes it into a form executable by the robots based on a prompt designed for task decomposition and allocation. It then assigns appropriate tasks to each robot according to its respective capabilities. The prompt for task decomposition and allocation consists of the following elements: 1) Description of the task 2) Physical characteristics of each robot 3) Names of rooms in the environment 4) Locations of objects 5) Rules for task decomposition and allocation 6) Examples of task decomposition and allocation 7) User instruction As a result of task decomposition and allocation, tasks are assigned to each robot in the form of a Subtask Sequence, as shown in Fig. 2. The prompt used for task decomposition and allocation is constructed based on our previous work [1]. 

2) Action Planning: After task allocation to each robot, action planning is generated for each robot based on the LLM. The action planning is performed using robot-specific prompts prepared for each robot. These prompts include the names of rooms in the environment, object locations, executable action skills for each robot, planning rules, and examples of action plans. The mobile manipulator is equipped with the following five action skills: 1) navigation : specifies a destination by name and generates a path to the target location 2) object detection : detects a target object given its name as an argument 3) pick : grasps a target object specified by its name 4) place : places an object at a specified destination (in this study, the mobile robot) 5) help : requests assistance from the operator with a specified message The mobile robot is equipped only with the navigation .The conditions for issuing help are explicitly defined in the prompt. Specifically, when navigation , object detection , pick , or place fails, the mobile manipulator is required to request assistance from the operator using 

help . In particular, when object detection or pick 

fails, the operator is required to place the target object on the mobile robot. Therefore, in this setting, discretionary decisions by the robot —such as adjusting the timing of help 

based on the number of retries —are not assumed. On the other hand, the operator’s behavior may vary. Considering the possibility of the robot retrying, the operator may temporarily withhold intervention or return the task without performing teleoperation. However, these decision criteria and interven-tion strategies are not explicitly modeled in this study. In this paper, we assume that when the robot issues help , the operator provides the necessary assistance, after which the robot continues the task based on the response. Action plans are generated by the LLM, and the program corresponding to each output action is executed by the action engine. After executing each action, success or failure is returned, and the LLM determines the next action based on this result. The success or failure is determined according to the following criteria. For navigation , it is judged based on whether a valid path to the destination can be generated. For object detection , it is determined by whether a bounding box of the target object is detected in the image. For pick and place , success is evaluated based on whether inverse kinematics for the target position can be solved and whether the object is successfully grasped or placed, as determined through image recognition using a vision-language model. The prompt used for action planning is constructed based on our previous work [14]. 

B. Remote Error Resolution in Multi-Robot Planning 

In the proposed method, when a robot executes actions based on an LLM and encounters a failure, it can use help 

as one of its action skills. When help is invoked, the LLM generates a request message describing the current failure situation and the problem faced by the robot, and asks the operator for assistance. For example, if object detection fails, the system may generate a request such as: “The target object could not be detected; please grasp the object and place it into the clear box instead.” Once help is executed, the action planning by the LLM is temporarily suspended, and the system transitions to a state in which the operator can perform teleoperation. The operator then controls the robot using a remote interface and carries out the task based on the LLM’s request. After completing the task, the operator sends feedback to the LLM describing the actions performed. The LLM updates its understanding of the situation based on this information and regenerates the subsequent action plan. In this way, the proposed method incorporates a human-in-the-loop into LLM-based multi-robot planning, enabling flexible handling of physical failures that are difficult for robots to recover from autonomously. IV. E XPERIMENT 

The purpose of this study is to clarify the effects of introducing remote error resolution on task progress and mental workload in an LLM-based multi-robot collaboration system when robots fail to execute physical actions. In this experiment, two robots cooperate in an environment that simulates a household setting to perform a task of cleaning up trash and placing it in designated locations. Subjects operate three types of systems and evaluate their mental workload and task progress for each system. Mental work-load is measured using NASA-TLX, and task progress is evaluated based on the number of items of trash successfully cleaned up by the end of the task. 

A. Definition of Tasks 

In this experiment, a trash collection task is performed using two robots. Subjects operate the two robots using a system prepared by the developers and are required to gather trash to designated locations within a time limit. The task is terminated under the following three conditions: 1) All trash in the environment is collected and placed in the designated location within the time limit. 2) The subject gives up during task execution. 3) The time limit is exceeded. The time limit is set to 25 min. The trash collection location is the Trash area in the environment shown in Fig. 3. In this experiment, a mobile robot (Kachaka) and a mobile manipulator (Fetch) are used. Kachaka is equipped with a cart on which a clear box is placed. Subjects collect trash by placing it into this clear box. Trash that is transported by Kachaka to the trash area within the time limit is considered “collected.” Only Kachaka is allowed to move to the Trash area. Additional rules for the task are as follows. First, after the experiment begins, the developers do not provide subjects with any operational commands. Second, in cases such as robot collisions, as described below, the developers will restore the robots and return both robots to their initial positions (Fetch to the workspace and Kachaka to the trash area). However, the measurement of the time limit will not be paused. 1) When the robots collide and the cart attached to Kachaka becomes detached. 2) When Fetch’s arm or base collides with a wall, desk, or Kachaka and exhibits clearly dangerous behavior. When the robots are returned to their initial positions, any objects that were in Kachaka’s clear box are considered “collected” once Kachaka has returned to the Trash area. 

B. Experiment Environment 

The experimental environment used in this study is shown in Fig. 3. The environment consists of four room areas predefined by the developers: the trash area, dining room, living room, and workspace. A total of six pieces of trash, belonging to four categories, were placed in the environment. The categories include paper waste, a partially filled bottle, an empty snack cup container, and an empty bottle. The developers classified these items into two levels of collection difficulty. Level 1 consists of objects that are easy to collect, including the partially filled bottle and the empty snack cup container. In contrast, Level 2 consists of objects that are difficult to collect, including the empty bottle and paper waste. For the empty bottle, pick-and-place operations may fail due to its tendency to tip over, while for paper waste, object detection may fail due to its irregular shape. By defining two levels of difficulty, the experiment assumes a scenario in which tasks that robots can handle autonomously and tasks that require human teleoperation are mixed. The placement of the trash is shown in Fig. 3; under the condition that one Level 1 and one Level 2 object are placed at each location, the specific positions are determined randomly. C. Experiment Preparation 

First, Fetch and Kachaka generated an occupancy grid map of the environment in advance using SLAM. In addition, both robots were assumed to have prior knowledge of the room region information on the occupancy grid map, and the target navigation poses corresponding to each room name were predefined. Furthermore, both robots were assumed to have prior knowledge of the class names of the trash present in the environment and their locations. For object detection on Fetch, Detic [16] was adopted. In Detic, the detection targets were limited to the predefined trash classes, and a pretrained model was used. For task decomposition and allocation, action planning, and state recognition, OpenAI’s o3-2025-0416 was used. All other parameters were set to their default values. In this experiment, task decomposition and allocation were performed in advance before the start of the task. As the task instruction for the trash collection task, the sentence “Collect all the trash in the rooms and transport it to the trash area.” was used. This instruction was used as the prompt for task decomposition by the LLM. The experiment was conducted with a total of 12 subjects (8 males and 4 females) 1. The subjects consisted of 11 undergraduate and graduate students and 1 researcher, with ages ranging from their 20s to 30s. Additionally, six subjects had prior experience using a head-mounted display for MR.

D. Comparison Methods 

Three methods were prepared. First, Teleop is a system in which subjects operate two robots using teleoperation devices. Next, REPAIR (ours) allows subjects to provide instructions to the two robots, after which the LLM performs task decomposition, allocation, and planning in an integrated manner based on the given instruction. Furthermore, when problems occur with the robots, the LLM requests assistance from the subject depending on the situation. Finally, Auto is a system in which the LLM performs task decomposition, allocation, and planning similarly to REPAIR; however, when problems occur, it does not request human intervention, and instead, the LLM automatically replans actions based on the success or failure of previous actions. Next, we describe the reasons for adopting Teleop and Auto as comparison methods. Teleop was included to demon-strate that directly operating two robots via teleoperation devices imposes a greater mental and physical workload on subjects compared to REPAIR. Auto was included to demon-strate that, when physical failures such as trash detection or object grasping occur, the robots alone cannot sufficiently re-cover, resulting in lower task progress compared to REPAIR. 

E. Evaluation Metrics 

The following two metrics were used for evaluation. The first is NASA-TLX [17]. NASA-TLX is a subjective measure of mental workload proposed by Hart et al. Subjects evaluate six subscales —mental demand, physical demand, temporal demand, performance, effort, and frustration —using ratings ranging from 0 to 100 after operating the system. In this experiment, NASA-TLX was administered after each trial for each system. The overall NASA-TLX score was treated as the primary outcome, while the scores of each subscale were treated as secondary outcomes. Another metric is the number of pieces of trash collected by the two robots by the end of the task (task progress). 

F. Experiment Flow 

The experiment was conducted in person according to the following procedure: 1) Subjects were asked to read the informed consent form, and their consent was confirmed. Subsequently, the developers explained the purpose and overview of the experiment, as well as the three robot operation systems (Teleop, REPAIR, and Auto) (10 min). 2) For Teleop, subjects practiced the operation methods (including the use of Quest 3 and the Kachaka appli-cation, 30 min), followed by the experiment (25 min). Afterward, a questionnaire survey was conducted (5 min). 3) For REPAIR, subjects practiced the operation methods (10 min), followed by the experiment (25 min). After-ward, a questionnaire survey was conducted (5 min). 4) For Auto, subjects practiced the operation methods (10 min), followed by the experiment (25 min). A comparison of the mean values further shows that REPAIR improved performance compared to Auto, and that in Level 1 Object, REPAIR achieved performance comparable to Teleop. One possible explanation for these results is that, in the Auto condition, the system was unable to recover from physical action failures and tended to repeat the same behavior. Specifically, behaviors such as repeatedly attempting object detection without successfully locating the target, or failing to notice when a grasped object was dropped and repeatedly performing object detection again, were observed. In contrast, in the REPAIR condition, the robot was able to continue the task by requesting assistance from the participant upon recognizing a failure, which likely contributed to the improvement in task progress. This differ-ence is considered to have led to the observed gaps between REPAIR and Auto, as well as between Teleop and Auto. Furthermore, the reason why the Level 1 results for REPAIR were close to those of Teleop is likely because object grasp-ing could be successfully performed in autonomous mode.

Second, although teleoperation (Teleop) is evaluated in this study under the assumption of an MR environment, the specific impact of MR itself on workload has not been clearly isolated. VI. C ONCLUSION 

This study enhances the robustness of an LLM-based multi-robot coordination system by introducing REPAIR, a method for remote error resolution. When robots encounter physical failures and fall into repetitive recovery loops, the LLM requests assistance from an operator, who resolves the issue remotely, allowing task execution to continue. In the experiments, we evaluated a cleanup task with two robots to examine how different human-robot collaboration strategies affect workload and task performance. Robots collected trash from multiple rooms in a real-world environ-ment. Results showed that REPAIR significantly improved task progress over Auto and achieved performance compa-rable to Teleop for easily collectible Level 1 objects. While overall workload differences were not significant, variations in physical demand and effort suggest distinctions between REPAIR and Teleop. For future work, we will investigate how the timing of human intervention affects system performance. While this study focused on human intervention only during physical action failures, intervention at earlier stages —such as task decomposition, allocation, or action planning — may also be beneficial.
