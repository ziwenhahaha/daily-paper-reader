# Agentic Neurosymbolic Collaboration for Mathematical Discovery: A Case Study in Combinatorial Design 

## Hai Xia ∗ Carla P. Gomes † Bart Selman † Stefan Szeider ∗

Abstract 

We study mathematical discovery through the lens of neurosymbolic reasoning, where an AI agent powered by a large language model (LLM), coupled with symbolic computation tools, and human strategic direction, jointly produced a new result in combinatorial design theory. The main result of this human-AI collaboration is a tight lower bound on the imbalance of Latin squares for the notoriously difficult case n ≡ 1(mod 3). We reconstruct the discovery process from detailed interaction logs spanning multiple sessions over several days and identify the distinct cognitive contributions of each component. The AI agent proved effective at uncovering hidden structure and gen-erating hypotheses. The symbolic component consists of computer algebra, constraint solvers, and simulated annealing, which provides rigorous verification and exhaustive enumeration. Human steering supplied the critical research pivot that transformed a dead end into a productive inquiry. Our analysis reveals that multi-model deliberation among frontier LLMs proved reliable for criticism and error detection but unreliable for constructive claims. The resulting human-AI mathematical contribution, a tight lower bound of 4 n(n−1) /9, is achieved via a novel class of near-perfect permutations .The bound was formally verified in Lean 4. Our experiments show that neurosymbolic systems can indeed produce genuine discoveries in pure mathematics. 

# 1 Introduction 

The question of whether artificial intelligence can contribute to genuine mathematical discov-ery has attracted a lot of attention in recent years. Prominent examples include the discov-ery of new extremal constructions via program search with large language models [Romera-Paredes et al., 2024], AI-guided conjectures in knot theory and representation theory [Davies et al., 2021], and automated geometry theorem proving [Trinh et al., 2024]. All these efforts have in common that they tightly integrate a neural component that generates candidates or conjectures with a symbolic component that verifies or refines them. This interplay between neural and symbolic reasoning, neurosymbolic AI [d’Avila Garcez and Lamb, 2023], is at the heart of many such advances. The approach appears particularly relevant for mathematical discovery, where creative intuition and rigorous proof must work in tandem. However, in each of these cases the objective is fixed from the outset: prove a stated conjecture, optimize a known quantity, or search within a predefined space. In contrast, our case study involves open-ended exploration where the hypothesis itself was not given in advance but emerged from the interaction with the AI agent.

However, very little is known about how such discoveries actually unfold: the sequence of dead ends, pivots, and insights that lead from an open question to a new result. Post-hoc descriptions typically attribute success to “the AI system” in aggregate, obscuring the distinct contributions of neural reasoning, symbolic computation, and human judgment. Without process-level analysis, it is very difficult to replicate such discovery workflows, or to provide principled ways of improving them. In this paper, we present a detailed case study that addresses this gap. Drawing on interaction logs spanning several days and multiple sessions, we reconstruct the discovery of a tight lower bound on the imbalance of Latin squares, a fundamental problem in combi-natorial design theory with applications in experimental design [Jones et al., 2015, Van Es and Van Es, 1993]. Our result settles an open question for the case n ≡ 1 (mod 3). The discovery process involved an AI agent , an interactive LLM-based assistant with access to a terminal, file system, and external tools, together with computer algebra systems, constraint solvers, and human strategic direction. The key to our analysis is a fine-grained taxonomy of cognitive contributions. We find that the agent’s primary strength was uncovering hidden structure : noticing a parity con-straint in numerical data that had eluded symbolic analysis. The symbolic component provided rigorous verification and exhaustive search. Human steering contributed to the critical research pivot: changing the question from “find objects with zero imbalance” to “characterize the minimum positive imbalance.” We find that multi-model deliberation among frontier LLMs was reliable for criticism but unreliable for constructive claims. Our main contributions are as follows. 1. An agentic neurosymbolic collaboration framework. We propose a framework for math-ematical discovery in which an LLM-based agent orchestrates symbolic computation tools, parallel multi-model review, and a two-tier persistent memory system that en-ables multi-session research continuity without updating model weights, while a human researcher provides strategic direction. 2. Mathematical discovery in combinatorial design. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework on an open problem in combinatorial design theory. Our framework led to a new research direction and a concrete mathematical discovery comprising: (i) the introduction of the concept of near-perfect permutations for Latin squares; (ii) a theorem stating a tight lower bound of 4 n(n−1) /9 on the imbalance of Latin squares for n ≡ 1 (mod 3), with near-perfect permutations that achieve this bound; and (iii) empirical verification of their existence up to n = 52. 3. Asymmetry in multi-model deliberation. We show that multi-model deliberation among frontier LLMs is reliable for criticism and error detection but unreliable for construc-tive mathematical claims, an asymmetry with implications for neurosymbolic system design. 4. Process-level analysis of discovery. We provide a fine-grained process-level analysis of the discovery, reconstructed from interaction logs, that identifies the distinct cognitive contributions of neural pattern recognition, symbolic computation, and human strate-gic judgment across five phases. A counterfactual analysis shows that the discovery required all three components.

# 3 The Discovery Process 

We describe the discovery from interaction logs spanning five days and approximately fifteen sessions. The process consists of five phases, summarized below; throughout, the collabora-tion follows the architecture shown in Figure 1. 3The human researcher sets strategic goals and decides when to change direction; the AI agent translates these goals into executable code, runs the symbolic tools, and synthesizes results into conjectures and proof drafts. Data flows downward as generated code and parameters, and upward as results, counterexamples, and verification outcomes. A three-component persistent memory system (Section 3.6) carries project state, documented dead ends, and a searchable knowledge base across sessions, so that each new session begins where the previous one ended. The five phases illustrate different facets of this loop: Phase 1 (Section 3.1) explores an algebraic approach that ends in a documented dead end; Phase 2 (Section 3.2) is a human-initiated research pivot that reframes the problem; Phase 3 (Section 3.3) shows the agent discovering a parity constraint from numerical data and drafting an elementary proof; Phase 4 (Section 3.4) uses parallel consultation of frontier LLMs to catch errors in the proof; and Phase 5 (Section 3.5) extends the result computationally by combining simulated annealing with algebraic analysis. Section 3.6 then discusses how persistent memory enabled continuity across all phases.

## 3.2 The Research Pivot 

Two observations forced a change of direction. The first observation is that the exhaustive solver hit a combinatorial wall at n = 18: even with symmetry breaking and multi-core parallelism, no perfect permutation could be found within a one-hour timeout, and none can exist for n ≡ 1 (mod 3). Second, the algebraic analysis of Section 3.1 showed that even 

known perfect permutations resist closed-form description. The critical decision (made by the human researcher, not suggested by the agent) was to change the question entirely: instead of searching for objects with zero imbalance, we asked what is the minimum positive imbalance for n ≡ 1 (mod 3) ?

In retrospect, this pivot was the single most important step in the entire discovery process. This step reframed a search problem (find an SBLS) as an optimization problem (characterize the minimum), opening a different, new line of inquiry. 

## 3.3 Parity from Data Anomaly 

With the new question in hand, the agent computed a naive lower bound for n = 13. Since 

n(n+1) /3 = 60 .67 . . . is not an integer, each distance d(r1, r 2) must deviate from it by at 4least 1 /3 after the scaling in (1), giving a naive lower bound of I ≥ n(n−1) /6 = 26. However, when the agent ran a simulated annealing search, the best imbalance found was 69 .3, a factor of 2 .7 above the naive bound. This gap called for an explanation: why could the search not approach the theoretical minimum? The agent investigated by examining the shift correlations fσ (δ) for the best permutation found. The key observation: fσ (δ) was even for every shift δ. This was not specific to the particular permutation; a quick check confirmed that all permutations exhibit this parity constraint. The proof came together rapidly: 1. Since |a − b| ≡ a + b (mod 2) for all integers, the distance d(r1, r 2) ≡ P

> s

(pos( r1, s ) + pos( r2, s )) ≡ n(n−1) ≡ 0 (mod 2). 2. If every distance is even and the ideal value n(n+1) /3 is non-integer, then the minimum deviation per pair is not 1 but 2, doubling the lower bound. 3. Using a careful case analysis with the sum constraint P d = n2(n2−1) /6, this yields 

I ≥ 4n(n−1) /9. The agent verified this against exhaustive data: for n = 4, the minimum imbalance over all 576 Latin squares is exactly 16 /3 = 4 · 4 · 3/9, matching the new bound precisely. The total time from posing the question to a verified proof was less than one session, approximately one hour. This was the shortest and most consequential session in the entire process. The agent computed shift correlations for dozens of permutations and recognized the pattern ( all values even ) that a human mathematician would be unlikely to notice without systematic computation. The proof itself is not complex (four lines of modular arithmetic); however, finding it required the structure-discovery step that came from inspecting numerical data and preliminary lemmas (like Lemma 1). 

## 3.4 Formalization and Multi-Model Review 

Writing the formal proof exposed two errors that the initial discovery session had missed. 

The circulant trap. The first proof draft established the lower bound using properties specific to circulant Latin squares (the shift correlation function fσ ), but claimed the result for all Latin squares. This error was caught during multi-model review: four frontier LLMs were consulted in parallel, and one correctly identified that the proof’s generality did not match its machinery. The fix required stripping away all circulant structure and reproving the bound purely from the Parity Lemma and the sum constraint. 

The σ/σ−1 mismatch. The circulant construction L[i][ j] = ( i + σ(j)) mod n has row distances determined by σ−1, not by σ. This subtle error was caught during formalization and resolved by redefining the construction with τ = σ−1.

Multi-model reliability. The same multi-model deliberation that caught the circulant trap also confidently claimed that the modular inversion map σ(j) = j−1 mod p would achieve O(n5/2) imbalance. Our experiments showed this claim was wrong: inversion scales as Θ( n3.6). This pattern recurred throughout the project: multi-model deliberation proved reliable for criticism but unreliable for constructive claims .

## 3.5 Computational Extension 

To match the lower bound, the agent introduced near-perfect permutations : permutations σ

with fσ (δ) ∈ { a, a +2 } for all δ, where a = ( n(n+1) −2) /3. These are the minimal relaxation 5of perfect permutations compatible with parity and divisibility constraints. A simulated annealing search with O(n) incremental updates per move found near-perfect permutations for all n ≡ 1 (mod 3) up to n = 52, each achieving exactly I =4n(n−1) /9. In contrast, the prior computational work did not reach the optimum even at 

n = 10 as the results show in the recent paper [Ramaswamy and Szeider, 2023]. The agent also investigated whether algebraic constructions could produce near-perfect permutations for large n. An analysis of character sums over finite fields, informed by multi-model consultation, identified the Weil bound as a fundamental barrier: any polynomial or rational map over GF( p) has character sum deviations bounded by C√p, forcing total imbalance to scale as Θ( n3.5) rather than the Θ( n2) achieved by near-perfect permutations. The gap between Ω( n2) and Θ( n3.5) cannot be closed by algebraic methods. 

## 3.6 Persistent Learning Across Sessions 

We equipped the agent with a structured persistent memory organized in two tiers following a progressive disclosure design. The first tier is a project instruction file that the agent reads at the start of every session. This file records accomplished goals, open questions, key formulas, tool usage patterns, and, critically, documented dead ends. At the end of each session, a handover protocol updates this file with new results, failed approaches, and next steps. The second tier consists of a collection of topic files, each tagged with keywords and a short context description. At session start, the agent receives only a compact index of these files; the full content of any topic file is retrieved on demand through keyword search. This two-tier design keeps the agent’s context window small at startup while providing access to the entire accumulated knowledge base when a specific topic becomes relevant. This memory mechanism played a concrete role in the discovery. When the agent began the pivotal session (Section 3.3), it had access to a detailed record of the algebraic dead end: which constructions had been tried, why they failed, and the explicit instruction “do not retry.” This prevented the agent from revisiting unproductive directions and focused its effort on the new research question. Similarly, the memory of all 672 perfect permutations for n = 12, the Rust solver’s performance characteristics, and the exact formulas used in prior sessions provided the agent with accumulated context that no single session could have built from scratch. From a neurosymbolic perspective, this persistent memory serves as a form of external symbolic state that compensates for the agent’s lack of long-term memory. Each session, the LLM begins with a fresh context window; the memory system augments sessions by externalizing knowledge into structured, human-readable files that both the agent and the researcher can inspect and modify. This design gives rise to a form of incremental learning that is purely symbolic (no model weights are updated) yet provides the continuity essential for multi-session research projects.

# 5 Analysis of the Discovery Process 

## 5.1 Role Taxonomy 

Table 2 maps each phase of the discovery to its primary cognitive function and the compo-nent that provided it. We distinguish three roles. 

The agent contributed primarily by uncovering hidden structure and hypothesis gen-eration . The critical insight, that all shift correlations are even, came from the AI agent inspecting numerical data for dozens of permutations. Once the pattern was noticed, the agent formulated the parity hypothesis, drafted a proof, and extended it to the full lower bound argument. The agent also served as a critic during multi-model review, successfully identifying the circulant generality error. 

The symbolic component (Rust solver, SageMath, SA search) provided capabilities that the agent lacks on its own: exhaustive enumeration (all 672 PPs for n = 12), exact arithmetic (polynomial interpolation over finite fields), and high-performance stochastic search (near-PP existence up to n = 52). Crucially, symbolic tools also confirmed negative results , the absence of algebraic structure, which required checking that no pattern exists among hundreds of objects. The agent orchestrated these tools by writing and executing code in its terminal environment. 

The human component made the most consequential single decision: changing the research question. The agent did not propose this pivot; it continued to work productively within whatever frame was provided. The human also served as a quality controller, deciding which outputs to pursue and which to discard.

## 5.2 Failure Modes 

Each component presents distinct failure modes. 

Agent failures. We now consider two particular types of agent errors. Confident wrong claims : during multi-model deliberation, one model asserted that the modular inversion map achieves O(n5/2) imbalance; empirical testing showed the actual scaling is Θ( n3.6). 

Overgeneralization : the initial proof used circulant-specific machinery but claimed a result for all Latin squares. In both cases, the agent stated conclusions with high confidence that did not withstand verification. 

Symbolic failures. The exhaustive solver hit a combinatorial wall at n = 18: the search space grows super-exponentially, and no amount of engineering (symmetry breaking, paral-lelism) could extend the feasibility horizon. It should be noted that this is a fundamental limitation, not a failure of implementation. 

Human failures. The initial three-day investment in algebraic reverse-engineering, while producing useful negative results, could have been shortened. In fact, the absence of patterns for n = 6 can, in retrospect, be seen as evidence that larger cases would be equally opaque. 

## 5.3 Counterfactual Analysis 

Without the agent, the parity observation would have been unlikely: the pattern emerges only from systematic numerical exploration across many dimensions. Without symbolic tools, the observation could not have been verified; the agent’s proofs contained errors that only formal checking exposed. Without human steering, the research would have remained in the algebraic dead end; the agent did not propose the pivot. This suggests that current AI agents do not yet possess the meta-cognitive ability to recognize when a research direction should be abandoned. 96 Related Work 

Recent work on AI for mathematical discovery includes AI-guided conjectures [Davies et al., 2021], LLM-based program search [Romera-Paredes et al., 2024], automated geometry prov-ing [Trinh et al., 2024], conjecture generation on fundamental constants [Raayoni et al., 2021], and LLM-assisted formal proving [Yang et al., 2023]. We focus on the process rather than on the end result; the central role of human steering sets our work apart. d’Avila Garcez and Lamb [2023] articulate the neurosymbolic vision of integrating neural and sym-bolic reasoning. Evaluations of LLM mathematical capabilities [Frieder et al., 2023] are consistent with our findings. LLMs excel at pattern recognition but struggle with rigor and strategic planning. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We have presented an agentic neurosymbolic collaboration framework for a genuine math-ematical discovery, a tight lower bound on Latin square imbalance together with a novel class of near-perfect permutations. Our analysis reveals that the discovery required all three components: neural pattern recognition to notice the parity constraint, symbolic computa-tion to verify and extend the result, and human judgment to redirect the research when the initial approach stalled. The failure modes are equally instructive. Multi-model deliberation among frontier LLMs proved reliable for criticism (catching errors in proofs) but unreliable for constructive claims .The human’s most important contribution was not any specific computation but a meta-cognitive act: recognizing that the research question itself needed to change. We see several possibilities for improving neurosymbolic discovery systems. First, in-tegrating systematic verification into the agent’s reasoning loop could catch errors like the circulant trap earlier. Second, developing meta-cognitive capabilities, the ability to recog-nize unproductive research directions, could reduce dependence on human steering. Third, there are close ties between criticism and construction in multi-model deliberation and this asymmetry deserves further study. Understanding why LLM-based agents are better critics than constructors has implications for both system design and training objectives.
