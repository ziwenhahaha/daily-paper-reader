# Abstract 

Echo State Networks (ESNs) are a reservoir com-puting framework widely used for nonlinear time-series prediction. However, despite their effec-tiveness, the randomly initialized reservoir often contains redundant nodes, leading to unnecessary computational overhead and reduced efficiency. In this work, we propose a graph centrality-based pruning approach that interprets the reservoir as a weighted directed graph and removes structurally less important nodes using centrality measures. Experiments on Mackey-Glass time-series pre-diction and electric load forecasting demonstrate that the proposed method can significantly reduce reservoir size while maintaining, and in some cases improving, prediction accuracy, while pre-serving the essential reservoir dynamics. 

# 1. Introduction 

Echo State Networks (ESNs), introduced by Jaeger (Jaeger, 2001; 2007), are a reservoir computing framework designed for modeling nonlinear temporal dynamics. More broadly, reservoir computing provides an efficient alternative to train-ing recurrent neural networks, as it avoids costly optimiza-tion of recurrent connections while still achieving strong performance in time-series forecasting and chaotic systems (Lukoˇ seviˇ cius et al., 2012). A key idea behind ESNs is to use a fixed, randomly initial-ized recurrent network, known as the reservoir (Maass et al., 2002), to transform input signals into high-dimensional dy-namic representations. In this setting, only the output layer is trained, which simplifies the learning process and reduces computational cost, while still capturing complex temporal dependencies (Jaeger, 2001). Due to these advantages, ESNs have been widely applied to tasks such as chaotic time-series prediction, energy load forecasting, signal processing, and control systems (Jaeger & Haas, 2004; Bianchi et al., 2016; Pathak et al., 2018). They are particularly effective for modeling systems with

nonlinear and long-term temporal behavior. However, despite their efficiency, ESNs rely on randomly constructed reservoirs, which often contain redundant or weakly contributing nodes (Rodan & Tino, 2010). This can lead to unnecessary complexity and increased com-putational cost without a corresponding improvement in predictive performance (Gallicchio et al., 2018). As a result, several studies have explored reservoir optimization through sparsification, structural adaptation, and topology design (Bala et al., 2018; Scardapane et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2022). At the same time, neural networks can be viewed as graph-structured systems, where neurons correspond to nodes and connections correspond to edges (Battaglia et al., 2018). This perspective allows the use of tools from graph theory to better understand network structure and information flow (Estrada, 2012). In particular, centrality measures provide a principled way to quantify the relative importance of nodes in such graph-structured representations, and have been explored in the context of pruning and influence estimation in neural networks (Li et al., 2019; Ayoub et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2019; Freeman, 1978; Valente et al., 2008). Motivated by these insights, we explore a centrality-based approach for pruning ESN reservoirs. Specifically, we model the reservoir as a weighted directed graph and use centrality measures to identify structurally less important nodes. These nodes are then removed to obtain a more compact reservoir while aiming to preserve the essential dynamics of the system. We investigate this approach on both synthetic and real-world time-series tasks, including Mackey-Glass prediction and electric load forecasting. 

# 2. Related Work 

Echo State Networks (ESNs) and reservoir computing have been extensively studied for nonlinear time-series modeling, with applications in chaotic systems, energy forecasting, and signal processing (Jaeger & Haas, 2004; Luko ˇsevi ˇcius et al., 2012; Bianchi et al., 2016; Pathak et al., 2018). Recent work has highlighted that reservoir structure, including connec-tivity patterns and weight distributions, plays a crucial role in determining prediction accuracy and stability, motivating research on systematic reservoir design and optimization (Liu et al., 2022; Gonbadi et al., 2025; Sima et al., 2025). Early work proposed minimal and deterministic reservoirs to reduce redundancy while preserving dynamical richness (Rodan & Tino, 2010; Ozturk et al., 2007; Chen et al., 2016). More recent ap-proaches introduce pruning and adaptive selection mech-anisms to identify and remove less informative reservoir nodes. For example, Broad ESNs with pruning remove re-dundant nodes while maintaining or improving performance on air quality and power load datasets (Liu et al., 2022), while multi-reservoir ESNs with top-down pruning strate-gies design sparse and structured reservoirs to improve both accuracy and training efficiency (Wang et al., 2024). Other approaches employ correlation analysis, partial-correlation-based pruning, and adaptive selection strategies to retain only the most informative reservoir units (Sima et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022). In parallel, input-driven and data-dependent reservoir de-sign has gained attention. Input-aware optimization methods suggest that reservoir topology and weight scaling should be adapted to the statistical properties of the input rather than chosen randomly (Gonbadi et al., 2025). Similarly, reservoir state selection methods based on particle swarm optimization and Bayesian optimization demonstrate that selecting salient states can significantly improve forecasting performance (Sima et al., 2025). These works complement earlier studies on deep, modular, and physically inspired reservoirs, which emphasize the importance of structured architectures for capturing multiscale temporal dynamics (Gallicchio et al., 2017; Sun et al., 2022; Nakajima & Fis-cher, 2021; Tanaka et al., 2019). Neural networks can also be analyzed as graph-structured systems, where neurons correspond to nodes and connec-tions correspond to edges (Battaglia et al., 2018; Estrada, 2012). This perspective enables the use of graph theory and centrality measures to analyze node importance and informa-tion flow (Freeman, 1978; Estrada & Rodriguez-Velazquez, 2005; Newman, 2018). In deep learning, centrality-based pruning methods model neural networks as graphs and use measures such as eigenvector centrality to rank and remove less important neurons, resulting in more compact models with competitive performance (Li et al., 2019; Fan et al., 2019; Chowdhury et al., 2025). However, most existing centrality-based pruning approaches focus on feedforward or convolutional architectures. The application of graph centrality to ESN reservoirs, which are governed by recurrent dynamics, remains limited. Build-ing on prior work in ESN pruning and reservoir optimiza-tion (Rodan & Tino, 2010; Scardapane et al., 2014; Bala et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024), we propose a centrality-based pruning approach that models the ESN reservoir as a weighted directed graph and removes struc-turally less important nodes while preserving its essential temporal dynamics. 

# 3. ESN Architecture 

An Echo State Network consists of three main components: an input layer, a recurrent reservoir, and an output layer. The input layer maps external signals into the reservoir through fixed input weights, while the reservoir is a ran-domly connected recurrent network that transforms inputs into high-dimensional dynamic representations. The output layer then produces predictions based on these reservoir states using trainable readout weights.

# 4. Centrality Models 

To address the redundancy in ESN reservoirs, we model the reservoir as a directed weighted graph, where each neu-ron corresponds to a node and each connection corresponds to a weighted edge. From this perspective, the reservoir can be analyzed using tools from graph theory, where cen-trality measures provide a principled way to quantify the importance of individual nodes (Freeman, 1978; Estrada & Rodriguez-Velazquez, 2005; Bloch et al., 2023; Newman, 2018). Intuitively, centrality measures capture how influen-tial a node is within the network structure. Nodes that play a more significant role in information flow or connectivity are assigned higher centrality values, while less important nodes receive lower scores; previous work has shown that such measures correlate with influence, diffusion, and con-trol in complex networks (Kitsak et al., 2010; Valente et al., 2008). In ESNs, reservoir states evolve through recurrent interac-tions, and nodes with stronger or more connected positions are expected to have a greater influence on the system dy-namics. These centrality measures are used to rank reservoir nodes according to their structural importance. Nodes with low centrality values are considered less influential and are pro-gressively removed during the pruning process, after which the output weights are retrained using the reduced reservoir. As a result, the reservoir becomes more compact while pre-serving the essential dynamics required for prediction, in line with centrality-based pruning strategies that aim to re-move low-importance units while maintaining performance in neural networks (Li et al., 2019; Chowdhury et al., 2025). Removing nodes with low centrality is therefore expected to reduce redundancy while maintaining the dominant path-ways of information flow in the reservoir. Moreover, prior work has shown that centrality-based mea-sures, including spectral and eigenvector variants, can ef-fectively capture influence propagation and important path-ways in complex networks (Estrada & Rodriguez-Velazquez, 2005; Perra & Fortunato, 2008; Kitsak et al., 2010). In deep learning, centrality has been successfully used to guide prun-ing and fine-tuning of large models by modeling neurons as a graph and ranking them with eigenvector centrality before pruning (Chowdhury et al., 2025), which further supports the use of centrality as a meaningful criterion for pruning ESN reservoirs.

5.1. Mackey-Glass Time Series 

The Mackey-Glass chaotic time-series is a standard bench-mark for evaluating nonlinear prediction models (Mackey & Glass, 1977; Jaeger & Haas, 2004). It is defined by the delay differential equation 

∂o (t)

The dataset consists of 10,000 generated time-series sam-ples. The first 10% of the data is used for initialization, 80% for training, and the remaining 20% is equally split into validation and testing sets. For each configuration, 84-step ahead predictions are performed, and the experiment is repeated 50 times to obtain stable RMSE estimates. 

5.2. Electric Load Data 

To evaluate the proposed method in a real-world setting, we use electricity consumption data obtained from AI Hub (https://www.aihub.or.kr/ ). These datasets ex-hibit complex temporal patterns and are widely used for benchmarking forecasting models. We follow the same data splitting strategy as in the Mackey-Glass experiments, using 10% of the data for initialization, 80% for training, and the remaining 20% equally divided into validation and testing sets. We consider reservoir sizes of N = 200 , 300 , 500 , and 

700 to analyze the effect of centrality-based pruning. The proposed method progressively removes structurally less important nodes while preserving the dynamics required for accurate prediction. 

5.3. Evaluation Protocol 

We evaluate the proposed method relative to the standard unpruned ESN, which serves as the primary baseline. This controlled comparison allows us to isolate the effect of centrality-based pruning on reservoir structure and predic-tion performance. By comparing against the original ESN under identical settings, we assess whether pruning im-proves efficiency without degrading accuracy. Across different reservoir sizes, the pruning process removes approximately 25% to 40% of the nodes, depending on the configuration. This enables us to analyze the trade-off between model complexity and predictive performance under varying pruning levels. All experiments were conducted on a CPU-based environ-ment without GPU acceleration. Since ESN training pri-marily involves solving a linear regression problem for the readout layer, the computational requirements remain rela-tively low. While comparisons with other forecasting models are im-portant, the focus of this work is on structural optimization within the ESN framework. Therefore, we first validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in improving the baseline ESN. Extensions to comparisons with other state-of-the-art models are left for future work. 

# 6. Results 

The experimental results demonstrate that centrality-based pruning can effectively reduce reservoir size while maintain-ing or improving prediction accuracy across different tasks and reservoir sizes (see Tables 1 and 2). For the Mackey-Glass task, pruning consistently reduces RMSE as the number of reservoir nodes decreases (see Fig. 2). In particular, the C2 measure achieves the most consistent improvements across different reservoir sizes, as also reflected in Table 1. This suggests that considering both incoming and outgoing connections provides a bet-ter representation of node influence in chaotic dynamics. Since Mackey-Glass involves complex temporal dependen-cies, nodes that contribute to both information reception and propagation appear to be more important. As a result, removing nodes with low C2 values helps eliminate redun-dant structure while preserving the essential dynamics of the system. For electric load forecasting, a different behavior is observed (see Fig. 3 and Table 2). In this case, Cout often achieves the largest improvements, especially for larger reservoirs. This indicates that outgoing connections play a more impor-tant role in this task, where the propagation of information through the reservoir has a stronger impact on prediction per-formance. Removing nodes with weak outgoing influence helps reduce noise and improves generalization. Across both tasks, moderate pruning generally leads to im-proved performance, while excessive pruning eventually degrades accuracy. This trend is clearly visible in the per-formance curves in Fig. 2 and Fig. 3, where RMSE initially decreases and then increases as more nodes are removed. This behavior suggests that some nodes are redundant, but removing too many nodes may disrupt the flow of informa-tion within the reservoir. Additionally, separating positive and negative weights pro-vides a more accurate characterization of node influence. Measures such as C1 and C2, which explicitly account for this separation, consistently outperform simple degree-based metrics in several settings (see Tables 1 and 2). In contrast, the C3 measure, which only considers the total magnitude of connections, does not consistently improve performance. This suggests that overall connectivity alone is not sufficient to capture the functional importance of nodes. Since C3 ignores the balance between positive and negative weights, it may retain nodes that are strongly connected but do not contribute meaningfully to the reservoir dynamics. Overall, these results show that graph-based structural anal-ysis provides a simple and effective approach for optimizing ESN reservoirs. The results also highlight that the effective-ness of different centrality measures depends on the task and the underlying dynamics of the system. 

# 7. Conclusion 

In this work, we proposed a graph centrality-based pruning approach for improving the efficiency of Echo State Net-works. By modeling the reservoir as a weighted directed graph, centrality measures were used to evaluate the struc-tural importance of nodes and guide the pruning process. Experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets show that the proposed method can reduce reser-voir size while maintaining, and in some cases improving, prediction accuracy. These results highlight the potential of combining reservoir computing with graph-based analysis for improving both efficiency and performance. In future work, we plan to explore additional graph-based and spectral centrality measures, as well as adaptive pruning strategies. We also aim to extend this approach to other reservoir computing models and recurrent neural networks.
