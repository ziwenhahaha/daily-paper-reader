Abstract 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have been widely deployed, especially through free Web-based applications that expose them to diverse user-generated inputs, including those from long-tail distributions such as low-resource languages and encrypted private data. This open-ended exposure increases the risk of jailbreak attacks that undermine model safety alignment. While recent studies have shown that leveraging long-tail distributions can facilitate such jailbreaks, existing ap-proaches largely rely on handcrafted rules, limiting the systematic evaluation of these security and privacy vulnerabilities. In this work, we present EvoJail, an auto-mated framework for discovering long-tail distribution attacks via multi-objective evolutionary search. EvoJail formulates long-tail attack prompt generation as a multi-objective optimization problem that jointly maximizes attack effectiveness and minimizes output perplexity, and introduces a semantic-algorithmic solution representation to capture both high-level semantic intent and low-level structural transformations of encryption-decryption logic. Building upon this representa-tion, EvoJail integrates LLM-assisted operators into a multi-objective evolutionary framework, enabling adaptive and semantically informed mutation and crossover for efficiently exploring a highly structured and open-ended search space. Ex-tensive experiments demonstrate that EvoJail consistently discovers diverse and effective long-tail jailbreak strategies, achieving competitive performance with existing methods in both individual and ensemble level.

1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success in natural language processing and are increasingly deployed through Web platforms, powering search engines [ 43 ], chat inter-faces [ 28 ], social media [ 31 ], and other online applications. These Web-mediated deployments enable unprecedented research opportunities, such as large-scale behavioral studies and real-time data collec-tion [ 4]. At the same time, they expose critical security and privacy risks, as the generation of harmful or unsafe content can compromise user safety and erode the integrity of the Web ecosystem [ 39 ]. Existing safeguards mitigate some risks but remain far from foolproof, making systematic evaluation of LLM vulnerabilities a pressing need for model developers, Web platform operators, and the broader AI safety community [24, 33, 42]. Jailbreak attacks have emerged as a key methodology for probing LLM safety in Web-facing ap-plications, by inducing models to bypass alignment mechanisms and produce otherwise restricted outputs [ 47 ]. Such attacks reveal vulnerabilities that could be exploited in online environments and prior research has explored various strategies such as rule-based strategies [ 1], greedy coordinate gradient [ 16 , 53 ], optimization [ 3, 20 ], psychological weaknesses exploitation [ 44 ]. However, most existing approaches rely on conventional natural language patterns, whereas real-world Web environ-ments expose LLMs to diverse, user-generated inputs that often deviate from such distributions. Recently, a growing line of research has focused on exploiting long-tail distributions to bypass safety alignment [ 35 ]. This is achieved by crafting inputs that deviate substantially from the training distribution and are often unintelligible to humans, yet retain latent semantic representations that cause models to produce harmful or policy-violating outputs. A number of studies has been proposed to explore such inputs from long-tail distributions, including low-resource or multilingual jailbreaks [ 6], cryptographically encoded or code-style prompts [ 15 , 23 , 48 ], noise-induced mode flipping [ 21 ], symbol-level perturbations [14], and tokenization vulnerabilities [9]. Despite these advances, most long-tail distribution attacks remain depend on expert-crafted rules or templates, requiring extensive manual effort. In contrast, the observation of the above diverse existing strategies highlights the vast and highly non-uniform nature of the adversarial prompt space and underscores the need for systematic exploration. These limitations hinder the development of automated, generalizable jailbreak frameworks, which are crucial for ensuring the security of LLMs in practical Web contexts. To address these challenges, this paper presents EvoJail, an automated framework for discovering long-tail distribution attacks via multi-objective evolutionary search that jointly optimizes attack effectiveness and output quality. At its core, EvoJail introduces a semantic-algorithmic representation in which candidate solutions are explicitly modeled as reversible encryption-decryption algorithm pairs, enabling the simultaneous manipulation of high-level semantics and low-level structural transformations. To efficiently explore the resulting highly discrete and structured search space, EvoJail integrates LLM-assisted initialization, mutation, crossover and repair operators into a unified multi-objective evolutionary loop, where evolutionary feedback is explicitly incorporated to guide adaptive and semantically informed variation. The main contribution of this paper is summarized as follows. 1) The EvoJail method is introduced as the first automated framework that leverages inputs from long-tail distributions to attack LLMs via multi-objective evolutionary search. It eliminates the reliance on handcrafted rules and enables systematic evaluation of security and privacy vulnerabilities across diverse user-generated inputs exposed by real-world Web environment. 2) The framework constructs the search space by combining semantic information and algorith-mic descriptions, and incorporates LLM-assisted operators to enhance the search process. This design allows the algorithm to efficiently explore the encryption-decryption prompt space while providing adaptive and interpretable attack strategies. 3) Extensive experiments demonstrate that EvoJail achieves competitive performance in both attack effectiveness and output quality while producing a diverse Pareto front of attack strategies. This diversity is essential for supporting flexible and adaptive attack strategies in different evaluation scenarios. 2The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews related work on evolutionary optimization for jailbreak attacks and long-tail distribution attacks. Section III presents the proposed EvoJail framework in detail, including the problem formulation, evolutionary operators, and overall optimization procedure. Section IV reports and analyzes the experimental results. Finally, Section V concludes the paper and discusses potential directions for future research. 

2 Related Works 

2.1 Evolutionary Jailbreak Attacks 

Evolutionary algorithms have recently been explored as powerful black-box strategies for generating adversarial prompts against LLMs. AutoDAN [ 19 ] employs a hierarchical genetic algorithm, while AutoDAN-Turbo [ 20 ] extends this approach with a lifelong self-bootstrapping mechanism. GPT-Fuzzer [ 45 , 46 ] combines mutation and prompt classification to generate and evaluate large-scale adversarial variants. TAP [ 27 ] adopts an iterative refinement framework and ReNeLLM [ 7 ] focuses on compositional and nested prompting schemes. These approaches share a common principle of iterative generation and selection to efficiently explore the vast adversarial prompt space, showcasing the effectiveness of evolutionary paradigms for jailbreak attacks. However, most of these methods operate in natural computational logics, where fluency and semantic coherence impose strong structural constraints. As a result, the search space is substantially restricted, and the generated prompts tend to be sensitive to the choice of gradient-based surrogate models. Moreover, these approaches primarily circumvent high-level semantic safety filters but do not sufficiently probe the model vulnerability at the token or representation level. Consequently, they offer only a partial view of the true safety boundary and often fail to expose its worst-case behavior under extreme distribution shifts. 

2.2 Long-Tail Distribution Attacks 

Long-tail distribution attacks have recently emerged as a promising class of jailbreak techniques targeting LLMs and recent research has explored several approaches within this paradigm. One prominent strategy is cryptographic obfuscation [ 40 , 48 ], where malicious queries are transformed using classical encryption schemes such as Caesar, Base64, or Rot13, accompanied by decryption logic reconstructing the underlying harmful intent. Another approach leverages flipping-based noise injection, exemplified by FlipAttack [ 21 ], which injects syntactic perturbations, particularly at the left side of prompts where autoregressive comprehension is more fragile, to obscure malicious intent while preserving semantic recoverability. A recent development is coding style transformation, which embeds adversarial prompts within programming-like constructs to exploit the strong code-completion priors of LLMs. Frameworks such as CodeChameleon [ 23 ] integrate both encryption and decryption logic into a code-completion format, whereas CodeAttack [ 35 ] encodes harmful prompts as data structures (e.g., lists, strings, stacks) to conceal malicious intent. Collectively, these studies highlight the diversity of techniques within the long-tail distribution attack paradigm and underscore its significance as an emerging adversarial framework for evaluating LLM safety. Despite their potential, these methods mainly rely on manually crafted rules, predefined encryption and decryption functions, and fixed code structures, lacking the capability for automatic attack design.

3.1 Formulation of Long-Tail Distribution Attacks 

From an optimization perspective, long-tail distribution attacks exhibit an inherent conflict between attack effectiveness and output quality, making them naturally amenable to a multi-objective formula-tion. However, unlike conventional multi-objective optimization problems studied in evolutionary computation, the decision variables in this setting are neither low-dimensional nor numerically param-eterized, but instead consist of intertwined natural-language semantics and algorithmic transformation logic. In particular, the core of an encoded jailbreak attack lies in constructing a pair of encryption and decryption functions (E, D ) that jointly satisfy three properties: 1) semantic obfuscation, where the encrypted query appears benign or nonsensical at the surface level; 2) algorithmic consistency, requiring exact recoverability of the original malicious intent; and 3) distributional deviation, where the resulting prompt lies in the long-tail region of the model’s training distribution. These requirements give rise to highly discrete, non-linear, and non-differentiable transformation spaces. For example, practical encryption-decryption schemes often involve conditional branching, sequence re-grouping, index-dependent operations, and rule-based reversals, as illustrated in Fig. 2. Such structures cannot be faithfully captured by traditional vector-based encodings or parametric representations commonly assumed in classical multi-objective optimization. As a consequence, directly applying standard mathematical formulations with explicitly defined decision variables severely restricts the expressive power of the search space, making it difficult to uncover structurally novel and semantically coherent jailbreak strategies. 3.1.1 End-to-End Attack Pipeline 

The long-tail distribution attacks is modeled as an end-to-end transformation pipeline, in which malicious intent is concealed through algorithmic obfuscation and later reconstructed within the model’s internal reasoning process. Let q denote the original malicious query. An encryption function E and a corresponding decryption function D constitute the core components of the attack. For the approach to be valid, the decoding process is required faithfully recover the original query, i.e., 

D(E(q)) = q. 

The encrypted query q′ = E(q) and the decryption logic D are embedded into a predefined prompt template T , yielding the adversarial prompt T [q′, D ]. This construction aligns with prior observations that template-based contextual embedding provides a more reliable interface for guiding model behavior [15, 23, 35, 48]. Formally, the long-tail distribution attack pipeline consists of four sequential stages: • Encryption: transform q into an obfuscated query q′ = E(q) to conceal malicious semantics; • Decryption specification: provide explicit or implicit instructions D to recover the original semantics; • Prompt construction: embed (q′, D ) into a template T to establish contextual coherence; • Model invocation: submit T [q′, D ] to the target LLM to elicit responses that reveal the original intent without triggering safety mechanisms. This pipeline highlights that attack success emerges from the joint interaction of semantic context and algorithmic structure, rather than from isolated perturbations. 53.1.2 Semantic-Algorithmic Solution Representation 

To overcome the representational limitations of classical optimization formulations, EvoJail adopts a semantic-algorithmic solution representation, which unifies high-level semantic intent with executable algorithmic logic. Each attack instance is represented as a structured tuple 

x = ( h, e, d, t ),

where • h is a natural-language heuristic describing the design principle of the encryption-decryption scheme, • e and d denote the concrete implementations of the encryption and decryption functions, respectively, • t is a prompt template sampled from a predefined template pool Tprompts .The natural-language heuristic h captures abstract strategies such as reordering, symbolic substitution, or conditional decoding, which are difficult to enumerate explicitly yet critical for exploring diverse long-tail attack patterns. The algorithmic components (e, d ) ensure functional correctness and reproducibility, while the template component t is decoupled from optimization to constrain search complexity. Under this representation, the attack instances constitute a hybrid semantic-algorithmic search space that cannot be expressed as a conventional Euclidean decision space.

Secondly, the code-level constraints reduce the prevalence of ill-formed individuals in early generations. Thirdly, throughout the search process, character-level encodings (e.g., Base64 or Rot13) are explicitly excluded, as such transformations are known to degrade attack effectiveness on smaller models due to their limited capability to interpret low-level symbol manipulations [51]. Mutation and crossover, pmutation and pcrossover , are performed using a shared genetic prompt, as shown in Fig. 4, distinguished by their operational context that the mutation operates on a single parent, while crossover combines two. The evolutionary feedback is explicitly injected into the generation process. Each parent individual is annotated with a parent improvement status (Better, Equal, Worse, Mixed, or Unknown) based on changes in its expected performance relative to its predecessors. This information enables the design model to reason about which structural components are beneficial and which are potentially harmful. The prompt further provides refined and advanced structural guidance, encouraging the model to retain effective components while increasing token distribution unpredictability, introduce structure-aware reordering, grouping, or indexing strategies, embed lightweight, reversible metadata, and exploit data structures such as stacks, trees, or probabilistic groupings. As a result, mutation and crossover become adaptive, semantics-aware transformations rather than random perturbations, substantially improving search efficiency in the highly structured solution space. Besides, since strict reversibility is difficult to guarantee in natural language-driven generation, EvoJail incorporates a verification-repair loop prepair , as shown in Fig. 5. For each candidate, the encryption-decryption pair is tested against a reference input. If reversibility fails, the design model is allowed up to R targeted repair attempts using a dedicated repair context. Importantly, individuals that remain irreversible after repair may still be retained if the decoded output is executable, preventing excessive contraction of the search space. This mechanism balances structural rigor with exploratory flexibility and is critical for discovering long-tail strategies. Finally, a template t is sampled at random from the predefined template pool Tprompts , extended from [ 23 , 35 ], and combined with the encryption-decryption logic to form a final candidate attack. 

3.2.2 Details of the Evolutionary Search

The algorithm starts from an ancestor set A, which contains representative long-tail distribution strategies [ 23 , 51 ], including the Queue, OddEven, Length, Stack, and Reverse. 4 Experimental Studies 

In this section, comprehensive experiments are conducted to evaluate the proposed algorithm against six existing methods. We first examine the performance of these algorithms in terms of attack effectiveness and output quality, analyzing results at both the individual level (single attack strategy) and the ensemble level (integrated multiple attack strategies). Next, the adaptability to unseen instances of these algorithm is examined to assess their generalization ability. Finally, the evolutionary dynamics of the proposed algorithm are analyzed to provide deeper insight into its search behavior. 

4.1 Experimental Setup 4.1.1 Problem Instances. 

A total of 18 instances are examined in the experiments. Three target victim models are considered: Llama-2-7b-chat-hf [ 37 ], Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct [ 10 ], and the closed-source gpt-4.1-Nano [ 29 ]. The instances are derived from two widely used benchmark question sets. GPTFuzzer [ 45 , 46 ], provides harmful queries spanning diverse violation categories such as hate speech, self-harm, and malware generation. JBB-Behaviors [ 2], contains representative misuse behaviors collected from established datasets including the Trojan Detection Challenge [ 25 ], HarmBench [ 26 ], and AdvBench [ 53 ]. For each target model, six instances are drawn from the two benchmarks to ensure reproducibility and balanced category coverage. The details of all evaluated instances are summarized in Appendix. 

4.1.2 Baselines and Their Configurations. 

The proposed EvoJail is compared with six state-of-the-art algorithms, including ChiperChat [ 48 ], Jailbroken [ 40 ], FlipAttack [ 21 ], CodeAttack [ 35 ], CodeChameleon [ 23 ], and ReNeLLM [ 7 ]. The baseline methods are implemented based on the EasyJailbreak framework [ 51 ], and the default 10 parameter configurations reported in their original papers are used. The configuration of the proposed EvoJail is as follows. The total number of iterations is set to 20, N is set to 10 and Rmax is set to 10. 

4.1.3 Evaluation Metrics. 

The DeepSeek-V3 model [ 18 ] is employed as the evaluator for computing ASR, while GPT2-XL [ 34 ]is used to calculate PPL. Furthermore, the widely-used HyperVolume metric (HV) that has been proven to provide a comprehensive and quantitative evaluation of the overall algorithm performance in balancing multiple conflicting objectives [ 11 , 52 ] is employed. Specifically, ASR and PPL are first normalized according to their empirical value ranges in the experiments, and the hypervolume metric is then computed using [0 , 1] as the reference point.

The framework combines semantic and algorithmic representations with LLM-assisted operators to efficiently explore the encryption-decryption prompt space, producing adaptive, interpretable, and diverse attack strate-gies.
