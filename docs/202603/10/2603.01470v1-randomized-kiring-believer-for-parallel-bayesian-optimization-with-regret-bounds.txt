Abstract 

We consider an optimization problem of an expensive-to-evaluate black-box function, in which we can obtain noisy function values in parallel. For this problem, parallel Bayesian optimization (PBO) is a promising approach, which aims to optimize with fewer function evaluations by selecting a diverse input set for parallel evaluation. However, existing PBO methods suffer from poor practical performance or lack theoretical guarantees. In this study, we propose a PBO method, called randomized kriging believer (KB), based on a well-known KB heuristic and inheriting the advantages of the original KB: low computational complexity, a simple implementation, versatility across various BO methods, and applicability to asynchronous parallelization. Furthermore, we show that our randomized KB achieves Bayesian expected regret guarantees. We demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method through experiments on synthetic and benchmark functions and emulators of real-world data. 

# 1 Introduction 

Bayesian optimization (BO) [Kushner, 1964, Mockus et al., 1978] is a promising approach for optimization of expensive-to-evaluate black-box functions with a smaller number of function evaluations. For this purpose, BO sequentially queries the input that maximizes the acquisition function (AF) based on a Bayesian model. BO has been applied to a wide range of applications, such as AutoML [Snoek et al., 2012], robotics [Berkenkamp et al., 2023], and materials informatics [Ueno et al., 2016]. However, in many real-world applications, observations can be obtained in parallel. For example, if the objective function involves computer simulation and multiple computational resources are available, parallel execution of simulations is important to minimize wall-clock time for optimization. Na¨ ıvely selecting parallel evaluation points using vanilla BO methods, which are originally designed for sequential evaluation, can result in wasting the querying cost since the input points often concentrate and provide redundant information. Parallel BO (PBO) [Shahriari et al., 2016] aims to improve optimization efficiency by selecting diverse input points that effectively leverage parallel evaluations. AF-independent approaches that extend vanilla BO methods to PBO by promoting diversity [Ginsbourger et al., 2010, Azimi et al., 2010, Gonzalez et al., 2016] are widely used due to their advantages, such as simple implementation, low computational complexity, and applicability to asynchronous parallelization. A representative heuristic is kriging believer (KB) [Ginsbourger et al., 2010], which promotes diversity by sequentially imputing predictive values as fictitious observations at input points currently under evaluation. While these heuristics have been shown to perform well empirically, they generally lack theoretical guarantees. In contrast to heuristic approaches, several PBO methods with theoretical guarantees have been proposed, in-cluding parallel Thompson sampling (PTS) [Kandasamy et al., 2018, Nava et al., 2022] and batched upper confidence bound (BUCB) [Desautels et al., 2014]. Under standard regularity assumptions, these methods have guarantees regarding regret [Srinivas et al., 2010]. However, such methods typically suffer from poor practical performance, lack theoretical guarantees for practical tuning of the confidence width parameter, or involve complex implementa-tion. This gap between strong theoretical guarantees and practical effectiveness motivates the development of PBO methods that are both theoretically principled and empirically competitive. This paper proposes a randomized variant of the KB heuristic, referred to as randomized KB (RKB), shown in Fig. 1. Then, the next input is selected by maximizing an AF based on the model trained on both the observed (real) data and the fantasized data. As a result, BO avoids redundant evaluation and ensures diversity among the evaluated inputs. the RKB instead uses one posterior sample for the ongoing evaluation points. By construction, RKB retains the main practical advantages of KB, including ease of implementation, low computational complexity, applicability to asynchronous parallelization, and versatility to a wide range of BO methods. Moreover, we establish upper bounds on both Bayesian cumulative regret (BCR) and Bayesian simple regret (BSR) for RKB when combined with several BO methods that admit regret guarantees [Srinivas et al., 2010, Takeno et al., 2023a, 2024, 2025b,a]. Furthermore, we show the effectiveness of RKB through experiments on synthetic and benchmark functions and emulators of real-world data. Our contributions are summarized as follows: 1. We propose a PBO method, RKB, that selects a diverse input set by conditioning on one random posterior realization for the ongoing evaluation points, inheriting the practical advantages of the original KB. 2. We show Bayesian expected regret upper bounds for the proposed method in Theorems 4.1, 4.2, and 4.3, which especially provide the BSR upper bound without dependence on the number of parallel workers. 3. We demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method via a wide range of experiments, including emulators of real-world data. 

## 1.1 Related Work 

BO has been extensively studied [Shahriari et al., 2016, Frazier, 2018]. Accordingly, a wide variety of AFs have been proposed, including probability of improvement (PI) [Kushner, 1964], expected improvement (EI) [Kushner, 1964, Mockus et al., 1978], knowledge gradient (KG) [Frazier et al., 2009], UCB [Kushner, 1964, Srinivas et al., 2010], TS [Russo and Van Roy, 2014, Takeno et al., 2024], predictive entropy search (PES) [Hern´ andez-Lobato et al., 2014], and max-value entropy search (MES) [Wang and Jegelka, 2017]. Building on these developments, various extensions of AFs have been proposed to address PBO. Hereafter, we briefly review prior work on PBO, particularly focusing on an algorithmic difference. We denote the number of parallel workers as Q + 1 ∈ N. We employ this definition to set the number of ongoing evaluations to be at most Q.

Joint Selection Methods. Several studies have proposed PBO methods that select a set of inputs either by jointly optimizing a utility-based AF that explicitly quantifies a batch’s utility, or by sampling from a joint distri-bution designed to encourage diversity. Representative examples include PBO methods based on EI [Ginsbourger et al., 2010, Chevalier and Ginsbourger, 2013, Marmin et al., 2015, Wang et al., 2020], PES [Shah and Ghahramani, 22015], and KG [Wu and Frazier, 2016], all of which jointly optimize AFs over a batch of O(Q) inputs. These methods are, in principle, optimal with respect to the utility defined by the AF. Alternatively, determinantal point process (DPP)–based approaches, such as DPP sampling [Kathuria et al., 2016] and DPP-TS [Nava et al., 2022], gener-ate input sets by sampling from a DPP distribution that explicitly incorporates diversity. These methods admit regret guarantees [Kathuria et al., 2016, Nava et al., 2022]. However, both approaches incur substantial computa-tional costs that grow rapidly with Q, due to high-dimensional AF optimization or sampling from high-dimensional distributions using Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method. 

Greedy Selection Methods. Motivated by these computational challenges, greedy PBO methods, including our RKB, have also been actively studied. Importantly, these greedy approaches have low computational complexity in 

Q, comparable to sequential BO, and naturally support asynchronous parallelization. BUCB [Desautels et al., 2014], UCB with pure exploration (UCB-PE) [Contal et al., 2013], and DPP-based greedy approximations [Kathuria et al., 2016] have regret guarantees but often suffer from over-exploration, similarly to phased elimination [Li and Scarlett, 2022]. In contrast, KB [Ginsbourger et al., 2010], constant liar [Ginsbourger et al., 2010], simulation matching [Azimi et al., 2010], and local penalization (LP) [Gonzalez et al., 2016] often show superior performance. Furthermore, Monte Carlo (MC)–estimation-based PBO for utility-based AFs, such as EI [Snoek et al., 2012, Wilson et al., 2018, Balandat et al., 2020, Wang et al., 2020] and MES [Takeno et al., 2020, 2022b,a] has also been widely used. The MC estimation-based methods greedily select inputs by maximizing the AF conditioned on ongoing evaluations and averaging over multiple posterior samples. Thus, our RKB can be interpreted as a special case that uses a single MC sample, although our regret analysis applies only to this single-sample setting. The empirical effectiveness of these heuristics has been demonstrated in multiple studies shown above, but lacks theoretical guarantees. 

Distributed Selection Methods. A third line of work is fully distributed PBO, especially for the case that even linear computational dependence on Q is prohibitive. Distributed PBO methods, including PTS [Kandasamy et al., 2018, Hern´ andez-Lobato et al., 2017, Vakili et al., 2021b, Nava et al., 2022] and Boltzmann policies derived from vanilla BOs [Garcia-Barcos and Martinez-Cantin, 2019], select input sets in a fully distributed manner, such as independent posterior sampling, by which they avoid computational dependence on Q in terms of wall-clock time. However, by design, these methods do not incorporate other selected inputs, which can result in batches with insufficient diversity. In addition, PTS can result in the over-exploration of the nature of TS [Shahriari et al., 2016, Takeno et al., 2024]. 

Regret Analysis for PBO. Regarding PBO, several works have established regret guarantees. High-probability regret bounds have been derived for parallel UCB variants [Desautels et al., 2014, Contal et al., 2013, Kathuria et al., 2016]. Desautels et al. [2014] showed that the deterioration of regret with respect to the batch size Q can be avoided by performing uncertainty sampling O(Qc) time at an initial phase, where c ≥ 1 is a constant. Contal et al. [2013] showed that UCB-PE achieves a cumulative regret upper bound without deterioration as Q increases. Bayesian expected regret bounds have been established for PTS and DPP-TS [Kandasamy et al., 2018, Nava et al., 2022]. Notably, Nava et al. [2022] derived a BSR bound that is independent of Q, for which we obtain the comparable upper bound for RKB. However, the above algorithms with regret guarantees suffer from practical disadvantages discussed already. Finally, the analysis of regret lower bounds in our problem setup is limited to the vanilla BO for one-dimensional objectives [Scarlett, 2018].

# 5 Experiments 

We conducted experiments to demonstrate the efficiency of our RKB. As objective functions, we used synthetic functions, benchmark functions, and emulators trained on real-world data from [H¨ ase et al., 2021] in Sections 5.1, 5.2, and 5.3, respectively. As a performance measure, we report the simple regret f (x∗)−max i∈[t] f (xi) for synthetic and benchmark functions and the best objective value max i∈[t] f (xi) for the emulators of real-world data since the optimum f (x∗) is not known. 

Compared Methods. We compared RKB, KB, and LP in combination with UCB, EI, and PIMS. These combi-nations are denoted by connecting the names of the parallelization method and the AF with a hyphen, for example, KB-EI. Note that LP-UCB is not included because LP can only be applied to positive-valued AFs. Other methods included for comparison are BUCB, PTS, uncertainty sampling (US), and random search (RS). For the methods involving the posterior sampling, including our RKB, we employ random Fourier features [Rahimi and Recht, 2008] based approximation [Wilson et al., 2020, 2021] consistently. 

Common Setup. We consider simple synchronous parallelization with 8 workers to facilitate the interpretation of the methods’ effectiveness. In this setting, all ongoing evaluations are completed at the end of each iteration whose index is a multiple of 8. Therefore, when selecting an input to evaluate, there may already be up to Q = 7 evaluations in progress. We report the mean and standard error of the performance measure across 100 random trials for initial data generation, PBO algorithm randomness, and synthetic objective function generation (in synthetic experiments only).

## 5.3 Experiment on Emulator 

As the objective function, we used emulators provided by Olympus H¨ ase et al. [2021], a benchmarking framework for optimization. The experiments were conducted on all emulators, except the alkox emulator, which returns only 0 and seems to have bugs. The experiment conditions other than the objective function are the same as those in the benchmark function experiments. Figure 4 shows the results on the 9 emulators. Consistent with the earlier experiments, RS and US exhibit inferior performance. Moreover, the RKB and KB families, as well as PTS, are among the top-performing approaches across the experiments, whereas the LP family and BUCB perform less favorably, particularly on the fullerenes and hplc emulators. These results indicate that RKB also demonstrates stable performance on real-world datasets. 

# 6 Conclusion 

We proposed the general-purpose PBO method, called randomized kriging believer (RKB), based on the widely used KB heuristic, inheriting the practical advantages of KB. Furthermore, we showed the BCR and BSR upper bounds for RKB comparable to TS-based PBO methods [Nava et al., 2022]. Finally, we demonstrated the effectiveness of the proposed method via extensive experiments. There are several directions for future work. First, since our RKB is a general-purpose algorithm for paral-lelization, extensions to various problem settings, such as multi-fidelity [Takeno et al., 2020, 2022a], multi-objective [Paria et al., 2020, Inatsu et al., 2024], and constrained BO [Takeno et al., 2022b], are promising. Second, extending our regret analysis is of interest; for example, this includes analysis in the frequentist setting and its application to the recent tighter regret bound in [Iwazaki, 2025b]. Finally, as in prior work, our BCR analysis requires uncertainty sampling in the initial phase to avoid an additional O(√Q) term. Addressing this common limitation in PBO analysis remains an important open problem.
