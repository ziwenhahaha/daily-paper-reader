Abstract 18 19 Biological agents flexibly adapt their behavior to novel goals and environmental demands 20 without additional training, yet the computational principles enabling such control remain 21 unclear. Here, we propose that three cognitive constructs constitute minimal computational 22 motifs for flexible control: relational structure, spotlight attention, and affordance computation. 23 We examine whether these constructs underpin flexible control in an embodied dynamic pursuit 24 task requiring continuous integration of inter-entity relations, reward, and action feasibility, 25 making it a suitable testbed for real-time control. By implementing these constructs within a 26 multi-module graph convolutional network, we show that the model achieves zero-shot transfer 27 across novel pursuit scenarios without additional training. Although not explicitly trained to do 28 so, the model also exhibits change-of-mind behavior, a hallmark of flexible control exhibited by 29 biological agents. Neural recordings from the primate dorsal anterior cingulate cortex revealed 30 population-level signatures linking these constructs to neural dynamics, providing biological 31 support for the proposed computational architecture. 32

Introduction 33 34 The capacity for flexible control under novel environmental demands is a hallmark of 35 intelligence 1,2 . Consider wolves hunting elk across seasons: in deep snow or open grassland, 36 they single out a target from the herd, sustain pursuit as terrain and group structure shift from 37 moment to moment, and disengage when conditions render capture infeasible. Such behavior 38 requires adapting to a changing environmental structure without additional training on novel 39 configurations. What cognitive constructs enable this form of real-time, zero-shot adaptation, and 40 how are they implemented in biological circuits? 41 Dynamic pursuit is a prime example of flexible control, posing foundational 42 computational challenges for zero-shot transfer. First, an agent must adapt when new entities 43 appear, because introducing agents with new behavioral policies can alter pursuit dynamics 3.44 Second, an agent must selectively commit to one entity among many, as encoding all entities 45 simultaneously risks combinatorial explosion 4–6 . Third, target selection must be grounded in 46 physical feasibility: persisting toward a high-reward target may be suboptimal if capture is 47 unattainable 7,8 . Dynamic pursuit requires all three continuously and in real time, making it a 48 stringent testbed for naturalistic zero-shot generalization. 49 Our central hypothesis posits that three cognitive constructs – relational structure, 50 spotlight attention, and affordance computation – constitute the minimal set of computational 51 motifs for flexible control of dynamic pursuit in novel environments. The explicit encoding of 52 relational structure enables abstraction across entities with distinct goals and behavioral policies 53 — such as prey versus predator — thereby allowing rapid behavioral adaptation 9–11 . Spotlight 54 attention prevents a combinatorial explosion as the number of entities grows, allowing stable 55 commitment 12–15 . Affordance computation grounds target selection based on physical feasibility, 56 enabling the agent to disengage regardless of reward magnitude 16–18 . We test this tripartite 57 hypothesis by instantiating each construct as a separable architectural module and evaluating 58 generalization upon its selective, systemic removal. 59 Despite advances in computational models capable of zero-shot transfer in domains such 60 as physical and conceptual navigation 10,19,20 and categorization 21,22 , zero-shot generalization in 61 dynamic, embodied pursuit remains largely unexplored. In these prior domains, generalization 62 has been linked to hippocampal relational abstraction 23,24 or structured priors in parietal cortex 63 25 . However, embodied and interactive pursuit imposes a qualitatively different demand: 64 generalization must unfold through continuous monitoring and real-time control of action under 65 evolving physical feasibility constraints. This requirement implicates neural mechanisms that not 66 only represent static information from the current configuration but also dynamically arbitrate 67 commitment, monitor conflict, and select actions as the configuration evolves. Given these 68 additional demands, the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC) is a candidate region to support 69 this form of control. dACC has been implicated in conflict monitoring 26,27 , evidence 70 accumulation 28 , and contextual adaptation during decision-making 29–31 , functions that are well 71

Behavioral Similarity Between Computational Model and Subjects 76 To test whether three cognitive constructs constitute a minimal subset for flexible control, 77 we used a dynamic prey-pursuit task 32,33 , in which a subject navigates a circular avatar using a 78 joystick to pursue a square-shaped prey item with varying speeds and reward values. In the 79 biological experiments, the task consisted of two conditions: in task 1, the subject pursued a 80 single prey (Figure 1a, top), whereas in task 2, it pursued two prey (Figure 1a, bottom). The trial 81 goal and termination condition (capture of one prey within 20 seconds) were identical across 82 both conditions (task 1: n = 5,847; task 2: n = 6,022). We translated this task into a training 83 environment for artificial agents. The model was trained only under the task 1 condition, with 84 slower prey speeds and a smaller arena (see Model Training in Methods), to learn the task 85 structure. As a result, all subsequent conditions, including multi-prey scenarios and altered 86 environmental dynamics (see Generalization Protocols and Evaluation Metrics in Methods), 87 constituted genuine zero-shot tests that required the model to generalize without any additional 88 parameter updates. 89 The model consists of four modularized components, each reflecting a distinct cognitive 90 construct of a biological agent. First, inspired by embodied accounts of action selection, the 91 network computes an affordance signal from the state representation of the avatar and prey, 92 including their positions, velocities, and reward values 34 . Second, drawing on relational accounts 93 of the cognitive map, a graph convolutional network (GCN) encodes entity representations 94 through affordance-weighted edges, enabling selective information exchange across objects in 95 the environment 35,36 . Third, a recurrent neural network (RNN) captures the temporal dynamics 96 of the state representations transmitted by the GCN 37 . Finally, a proximal policy optimization 97 (PPO)-based actor-critic module generates an action based on the temporally integrated graph 98 representation, while an edge-entropy term in the loss function controls the spread of edge 99 weights (Figure 1b) 38 .100 To assess whether the model can reproduce pursuit behaviors of biological agents, we 101 compared the rollout trajectories from matched initial states between the model and subjects in 102 both 1-prey (Task 1, 1,496 trials) and 2-prey (Task 2, 1,485 trials) conditions under matched 103 environmental settings (Figure 1c). Spotlight attention resolves the combinatorial explosion problem in flexible control 162 In a multi-entity pursuit scenario, agents encounter entities with distinct behavioral 163 policies and action possibilities. As the number of entities increases, encoding all entities 164 simultaneously leads to a combinatorial explosion of information. We therefore hypothesized 165 that selective attention (i.e., spotlight attention) mitigates this problem and constrains effective 166 dimensionality. To test this, we introduced an edge-entropy regularization term in the loss 167 function and varied its coefficient (Figure 3a). Reducing this regularization term forces the 168 model to distribute attention more uniformly across entities, effectively removing the spotlight 169 property while keeping all other architectural components constant. 170 As expected, the model with spotlight attention mimicked subjects’ behavior better than 171 models with distributed attention (Figure 3b; Figure S2; Medium - Task 1:   = 1.25; p = 9.247 x 172 10 -13 ; task2:   = 0.70; p = 2.575 x 10 -5 ; High - Task 1:   = 1.47; p = 5.947 x 10 -17 ; task2:   =173 0.90; p = 5.211 x 10 -8 ; linear mixed-effects model). To further investigate the necessity of 174 spotlight attention in flexible control, we increased the number of prey the agent encountered in 175 each trial. Increasing the number of options also raises the demand for encoding multiple entities 176 with distinct actions and behavioral policies. The model with spotlight attention (i.e., low 177 regularization) consistently outperformed the other models as the number of prey increased 178 (Figure 3c; vs control model (high regularization; uniform attention), 2: t(55.16) = 17.51; p = 179 3.440 x 10 -24 ; 3: t(56.79) = 16.34; p = 3.964 x 10 -23 ; 4: t(54.59) = 19.38; p = 3.837 x 10 -26 ; 5 180 prey: t(57.65) = 17.09; p = 3.105 x 10 -24 ; Welch’s t-test). Only through selective attention can we 181 observe that the agent maintains a stable commitment to pursuing higher-reward prey across all 182 other generalization conditions (Figure S2). These results demonstrate that spotlight attention 183 effectively addresses the combinatorial explosion by constraining the encoding space, thereby 184 enabling stable commitment as environmental complexity increases. 185 If similar computational principles are implemented in biological circuits, neural 186 population dimensionality should remain stable despite increases in the number of competing 187 entities. Indeed, we found that population dimensionality was comparable across the two tasks 188 (Figure 3d), with similar participation ratios despite the increase in available options (Figure 3d 189 inset; task 1 = 5.15; task 2 = 6.60). Computing affordance resolves encountering a prey that exceeds the agent’s own 201 

physicality 202 Pursuit is an embodied behavior in which success depends on the relative physical 203 relationships between the agent and its targets, including distance, speed, and heading direction 204 39 . Consequently, targets with identical rewards can differ in their actionability depending on 205 their physical configuration. Hence, models that fail to represent these relationships are expected 206 to generalize poorly under changing conditions. We hypothesized that flexible control depends 207 on encoding such relative structure, enabling the agent to evaluate feasibility and redirect pursuit 208 when necessary. 209 To test this, we compared a model that computes relative physical information with one 210 that relies only on reward-based features (Figure 4a). As expected, the affordance-based model 211 more closely matched subjects’ behavior than the reward-only model (Figure 4b), despite the 212 latter achieving comparable or higher reward rates under standard conditions (Figure S3, Figure 213 S4; Task 1: β = 0.39, p = 1.87 × 10 ²; Task 2: β = 0.51, p = 2.30 × 10 ³; linear mixed-effects 214 model). A potential concern was that similar behavior could arise from different underlying 215 representations; however, affordance representations were closely aligned between the model 216 and subjects (Figure 4c; mean cosine similarity = 0.64; 95% CI = [0.63, 0.65]; p = 5.00 × 10  ;217 sign-flip permutation test). Notably, under standard conditions, both models could successfully 218 capture prey, suggesting that reward-based strategies may suffice when physical feasibility is not 219 limiting. To directly test the necessity of affordance computation, we introduced a valuable but 220 uncatchable prey whose speed exceeded that of the agent. Under this condition, the reward-based 221 model exhibited near-zero success compared to the affordance-based model (Figure 4d; t(33.76) 222 = 34.01; p = 1.043 × 10 ²; Welch’s t-test), indicating a failure to account for physical 223 constraints. Together, these results demonstrate that affordance-based commitment is more 224 robust than reward-based selection when physical feasibility is critical, enabling flexible 225 disengagement and redirection of pursuit. 226 To test whether dACC encodes affordance, we examined neuronal tuning to both its 227 constituent variables (e.g., distance and angle to prey) and their composite. Change of Mind as Emergent Testbed and Neural Substrates in Biological Agents 251 As an emergent behavior in biological agents, change-of-mind (CoM) reflects the 252 dynamic re-evaluation of competing options and provides a natural test of real-time adaptive 253 control 40 . Because CoM arises across a wide range of relational and affordance configurations, it 254 provides a mechanistic probe of flexible generalization beyond specific task settings. Compared 255 to the preceding analyses, CoM constitutes a more stringent test, as it unfolds in real time and is 256 not directly optimized during training. We therefore hypothesized that agents equipped with all 257 three cognitive constructs would reproduce CoM behavior comparable to that of biological 258 agents. In our paradigm, we defined CoM trials as instances in which the agent redirected pursuit 259 from one prey to another as action feasibility evolved (Figure 5a). 260 Consistent with this prediction, the model exhibited CoM behavior despite not being 261 explicitly trained to do so. During CoM trials, the artificial agent was able to reallocate attention 262 from the initially selected prey to an alternative target and subsequently commit to the new 263 pursuit (Figure 5b). Therefore, to examine the conditions under which CoM behavior emerges, 264 we manipulated factors expected to increase fluctuations. Increasing the number of prey in the 265 scene introduces greater competition and more frequent shifts in the relative feasibility of 266 actions. Consistent with this prediction, the model exhibited a higher rate of mid-course reversals 267 as prey number increased (Figure 5c; β = 2.68 ± 0.12; p = 1.783 × 10 -48 ; linear regression). In 268 contrast, the reward-only model, which does not compute affordance, rarely reversed its 269 decisions. Similarly, when the reward gap between prey was small, resulting in more frequent 270 shifts in relative affordance, the model exhibited increased reversal frequency (Figure 5d; β = -271 3.83 ± 0.68; p = 8.957 × 10 -8 ; linear regression). Together, these results indicate that CoM 272 behavior scales with affordance instability rather than reward magnitude alone. 273

Discussion 301 302 Previous studies on zero-shot generalization have often emphasized either the training 303 curriculum 41 , representational constraints 42 , or structural priors that constrain the hypothesis 304 space 21 . We extend this perspective by showing that several classic cognitive constructs— 305 relational structure, spotlight attention, and affordance computation—jointly enable 306 generalization in embodied, closed-loop control, where feasibility evolves moment-to-moment 307 rather than remaining fixed, unlike traditional cognitive neuroscience tasks. Notably, no single 308 construct was sufficient: robust transfer and biologically characteristic decision dynamics 309 emerged only when these mechanisms operated in concert. This suggests that flexible behavior 310 may not reduce to a single computational motif, but instead arises from the coordinated 311 interaction of multiple cognitive constructs. More broadly, this work illustrates how integrating 312 multiple cognitive constructs within a single architecture enables the study of complex behavior, 313 moving beyond the common practice of modeling isolated functions with neural networks. 314 Several limitations still constrain our conclusions. First, our model assumes full access to 315 the environment state, whereas real-world pursuit is typically partially observable and better 316 described as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP), requiring belief 317 maintenance and active information sampling rather than direct access to the state 43,44 .318 Biological agents reduce uncertainty through selective sensing (e.g., gaze shifts and foveated 319 vision), processes that our current framework does not implement 45 . Extending the model to 320 include egocentric, limited observations with explicit belief-state inference and active sensing 321 would provide a stronger test of whether the same components remain sufficient under partial 322 observability. Second, our neural analysis focuses exclusively on the dorsal anterior cingulate 323 cortex (dACC), offering only a partial view of the circuitry underlying attention, relational 324 structure, and affordance-like prioritization. These computations are likely distributed across 325 sensory, attentional, sensorimotor, valuation, and action-selection networks. Future work with 326 multi-region recordings will clarify how these representations interact and whether dACC acts as 327 an integration hub or reflects computations formed elsewhere. 328 Traditional reverse engineering has followed two main paths: fitting behavioral data with 329 latent process models and searching for neural correlates of inferred parameters 46, 47 , or 330 reproducing observed neural dynamics by imposing architectural constraints or modifying 331 learning rules in network models to match them 48, 49 . Both strategies assume that the relevant 332 computational motifs can be parameterized a priori and that each motif independently shapes 333 behavior. However, in complex, closed-loop tasks such as dynamic pursuit, it is difficult to 334 derive a simplified latent process or isolate a single defining computational motif that fully 335 captures the underlying neural dynamics. In this regard, our use of network models suggests a 336 complementary approach to reverse-engineering such complex behavior. We use artificial agents 337 as computational testbeds to evaluate multiple candidate architectural constraints and 338 representations when the underlying decomposition is uncertain. More recently, related 339 approaches have embedded modular learning structures to adjudicate among competing memory 340

Animal Procedure We utilized a previously published behavioral dataset collected from two 430 male rhesus macaques to benchmark the agent’s performance 33 . The subjects were aged 9 years 431 (subject K) and 10 years (subject H). All procedures adhered to the guidelines of the Public 432 Health Service’s Guide for the Care and Use of Animals and were approved by the University 433 Committee on Animal Resources at the University of Rochester and/or the University of 434 Minnesota. 435 

Behavioral Task We adopted a primate-inspired pursuit task to evaluate decision-making in 436 dynamic environments 33 . In each trial, an agent controlled an avatar that pursued a moving prey 437 within a bounded two-dimensional arena (training: 1280×720; testing: 1920×1080 pixels). The 438 prey followed an adaptive avoidance policy that balanced keeping distance from the agent with 439 staying within the arena. At each time step, it evaluated 15 candidate future positions and chose 440 the one minimizing a cost function combining an environment-dependent term (including a bias 441 toward the arena center) and an agent-dependent term. The agent term was a sigmoidal function 442 of agent–prey distance, rising nonlinearly as the agent approached. If the best position violated 443 boundaries, the prey selected the next-best feasible option. 444 

Experimental Apparatus and Controls Subjects controlled the avatar using a modified joystick 445 (Logitech Extreme Pro 3D) capped with a 3-cm sphere. Analog input was sampled and mapped 446 to avatar motion using custom MATLAB code. Movement obeyed a kinematic constraint: 447 maximum displacement was capped at 23 pixels per frame (16.67 ms). We used the same 448 constraint to define the artificial agent’s maximum velocity, ensuring kinematic equivalence 449 between biological and computational control. 450 

Training Environment for Agent. The RL objective used a composite reward balancing 451 efficiency and value. On capture, the agent received a scalar reward (12–108) proportional to the 452 prey’s maximum speed, incentivizing pursuit of faster, higher-value prey. Each time step 453 incurred a small time penalty (-0.0001) to encourage rapid interception, and failure to capture 454 within 500 timesteps triggered a terminal penalty. 455 

Artificial Networks : We developed a modular deep RL architecture comprising four sequential 456 components: affordance computation, attention-weighted relational structure (graph 457 convolutional network; GCN), temporal integration (RNN), and decision-making (PPO actor– 458 critic). At each time step t, the agent observed an object-centric state matrix X tR 6×5 , where the 459 first row represented the agent, and the remaining five rows represented potential prey. To 460 prevent position-dependent heuristics tied to input ordering, prey rows were randomly permuted 461 at every step. Each row contained five normalized features (scaled to [0,1][0,1][0,1]): 2D 462 position, 2D velocity, and an intrinsic reward value determined by the prey’s maximum speed. 463 During 1-prey trials, unused positions were zero-padded. The affordance module first produced 464 per-target scores, which were then used as attention weights in the GCN to construct a weighted 465 relational graph that emphasizes task-relevant objects. Graph embeddings were aggregated and 466 passed to an RNN to capture the temporal dynamics of motion and interaction. The RNN output 467

Ablation of the Spotlight Attention Mechanism. To examine the contribution of the spotlight 503 attention mechanism, we implemented ablation models in which the selective capacity was 504 systematically attenuated. We modified the distribution of affordance-derived attention weights 505 to approximate a divided-attention regime. This was implemented by adding an edge-entropy 506 regularization term to the PPO objective function. This term penalizes highly peaked attention 507 weights, encouraging the model to distribute its focus more evenly. Specifically, the modified 508 total loss function is defined as follows: 509 510 where Hedge represents the edge entropy, calculated over the attention weights i as: 511 512 where is a small constant(10 -8 ) for numerical stability. By scaling the edge entropy coefficient, 513 we control the degree of focus during target selection. Under a high edge-coefficient condition, 514 this formulation produces a network that learns a uniform attention distribution across entities, 515 reducing sensitivity to differences in affordance scores. Under a medium edge-coefficient 516 condition, the distribution became less sharp, leading to partial dispersion of attention across 517 entities. Unlike the base model, which was trained in a 1-prey environment, this ablation model 518 was trained in a 2-prey environment. This design choice was motivated by a preliminary 519 observation that when trained on a 1-prey, the model converged on trivial selective behavior 520 regardless of entropy strength, because the absence of competing targets eliminated the need for 521 attentional allocation. Training with 2-prey introduced a minimal competitive context, in which 522 the high edge coefficient setting led to equally distributed attention rather than a focus on a 523 single target. 524 

Ablation of Relational Structure. To assess the role of explicit relational structure in 525 representing interactions among multiple entities, we implemented an ablation model that 526 removed graph-based relational modeling. In this variant, the GNN module was replaced with a 527 standard MLP, eliminating inductive biases that explicitly encode relationships between the 528 agent and surrounding entities. To ensure that any observed performance differences were 529 attributable to the absence of relational structure rather than to differences in representational 530 capacity, the substituted MLP was designed to match the original GCN in total learnable 531 parameters. The MLP consisted of a fully connected layer with 64 hidden units, followed by a 532 ReLU activation. To prevent the loss of affordance information in this ablation, the affordance 533 scores computed for each entity were concatenated to the MLP output before being passed to the 534 subsequent temporal processing module. This design ensured that affordance signals remained 535 available to the policy, isolating the effect of removing relational structure while preserving 536 information about target relevance. The model was trained in an environment containing exactly 537 two targets. Preliminary experiments showed that non-relational models trained with a single 538 target failed catastrophically when evaluated in multi-target settings, precluding meaningful 539 assessment of reward-based decision-making, such as selecting higher-value targets in 540

Ablation of Affordance Computation. To assess the role of the state-dependent affordance 543 computation module, we implemented an ablation model that removed the learned affordance 544 computation. In the base model, affordance scores reflect not only the intrinsic value of a target 545 but also its feasibility relative to the agent’s current state. This ablation was designed to examine 546 the behavioral consequences of eliminating this adaptive evaluation process. In this without-547 affordance model, the learnable multi-layer perceptron that computes affordance scores was 548 removed and replaced with a deterministic heuristic. Instead of deriving attention weights from 549 learned affordance values, the model selected targets solely based on their predefined intrinsic 550 reward magnitudes. Consequently, attention was assigned using an argmax operation over the 551 reward values of all visible entities: 552 553 This substitution forced the agent to adopt a reward-based strategy, in which it always pursued 554 the target with the highest reward. As a result, the agent disregarded contextual factors such as 555 spatial distance, relative velocity, and time constraints that influence whether a target is 556 practically attainable. By contrasting this heuristic policy with the base model, this ablation 557 isolated the contribution of learned affordance computation, quantifying the difference between 558 blind pursuit of high value and adaptive pursuit of achievable value. 559 

Generalization Protocols and Evaluation Metrics To evaluate the robustness and adaptability 560 of the learned policies, we subjected all agents to a series of zero-shot generalization tests. These 561 evaluations were conducted in environments that deviated substantially from the training 562 distribution, probing the agents’ ability to cope with unseen physical dynamics, altered kinematic 563 demands, and increased task complexity without additional learning. During training, all models 564 were exposed to a fixed environmental configuration with a resolution of 1280 x 720, prey speed 565 sampled uniformly from [4, 8] pixels per frame, and a nominal surface-friction parameter (agent 566 = 0.1, prey = 0.15). Generalization performance was assessed across four categories of 567 environmental perturbations. 568 First, to test spatial generalization, agents were evaluated in expanded arenas with 569 resolutions of 1920 x 1080 (corresponding to the primate experimental setup) and 2560 x 1440. 570 Second, to assess robustness to kinematic shifts, prey speed ranges were increased to [7, 11] and 571 [10, 14] pixels per timestep, exceeding the range encountered during training and requiring more 572 precise predictive control. Third, to examine sensitivity to changes in control dynamics, surface 573 friction coefficients were systematically altered. Relative to the nominal condition, we 574 introduced reduced-friction environments ( -0.1) and increased-friction environments ( +0.1), 575 perturbing the mapping between control actions and resulting motion. Finally, to evaluate 576 performance under increased task complexity and attentional demands, agents were tested in 577 multi-prey environments with three to five targets. Behavioral Affordance We developed a method to quantify the moment-to-moment action 622 relevance of each prey. This temporal discount affordance model assigns an affordance value to 623 each prey at each frame to explain the prey's immediate pursuability. First, we computed the 624 time-to-contact (TTC) function between the self and each prey as a function of time, where D is 625 denoted as the Euclidean distance between self and prey, and V denotes the respective speeds at 626 a specific time (t). TTC can only be defined when v self > v prey, reflecting that capture is not 627 kinematically feasible under the condition where v self < v prey 628 629 The TTC value is then transformed into an affordance value via an exponential temporal 630 discounting function: 631 632 R represents the theoretical reward associated with the specific prey, and (   = 0.4) is a temporal 633 discounting parameter controlling the sensitivity to delay. Theoretically, this equation assigns 634 higher affordance to prey that are closer, relatively slower, and higher-reward, consistent with 635 the normative principles for chasing. Notably, the affordance measure is computed continuously 636 over time and does not assume explicit planning or value learning. Instead, it captures the 637 instantaneous action potential of each prey given the current kinematic state, providing a 638 behaviorally grounded signal suitable for comparison with both choice behavior and neural 639 activity. Unlike conventional value presentation, this affordance signal is inherently state-640 dependent and dynamically evolves with the kinematic feasibility of capture, rather than 641 reflecting a stationary expected reward. 642
