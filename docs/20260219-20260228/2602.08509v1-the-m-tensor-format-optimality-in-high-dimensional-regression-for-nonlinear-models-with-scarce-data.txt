Abstract 

We present a nonlinear regression framework based on tensor algebra tailored to high dimensional contexts where data is scarce. We exploit algebraic properties of a partial ten-sor product, namely the m-tensor product, to leverage structured equations with separated variables. The proposed method combines kernel properties along with tensor algebra to prevent the curse of dimensionality and tackle approximations up to hundreds of parame-ters while avoiding the fixed point strategy. This formalism allows us to provide different regularization techniques fit for low amount of data with a high number of parameters while preserving well-known matrix-based properties. We demonstrate complexity scal-ing on a general benchmark and dynamical systems to show robustness for engineering problems and ease of implementation. 

Keywords Multilinear algebra, Tensor format, High-dimensional regression, Kernel-based regression

# 1 Introduction 

Engineering problems are often made dependent on parameters. In certain cases, the number of those parameters might be reduced by identifying fewer latent variables as is done by model order reduction techniques. In certain scenarii the dependence has to be explicit. In that case even for problems with low latent dimensionality, making explicit use of those parameters becomes intractable. This issue is often found in design optimization problems for instance. Manipulating a large number of parameters often becomes numerically intractable because of a wall of complexity coined curse of dimensionality [4]. This curse translates the exponential growth of models ( i.e. the number of coefficients) and that of the required amount of data with respect to the number of parameters. In contexts where data is scarce and costful, that curse makes models simply inaccessible. Solutions proposed to circumvent this curse can rely on a modification of the grid required by the model, e.g. with a sparse grid [15], or with an adaptive selection of the pertinent points of that grid to retain [6]. A reduction of complexity can also be found by leveraging the structure of the equations. A common approach is to formulate the problem with separated variables. A prime example of this approach can be found in the works of Beylkin and Mohlenkamp [5]. The separated variables representation inherently relies on a tensorial factorization of the operators, 2The M-Tensor Format often based on the use of Kronecker product-based linear systems [2, 19]. This reliance on tensor representation can explain the recent development of plenty of methods based on tensor network formalisms, more specifically the Tensor-Train (TT-) format [23] and the overall gain of interest toward tensor methods [17, 12, 13]. A popular variable separation technique stemming from computational mechanics is the Proper Generalized Decomposition (PGD) [1] and its sparse data-based version, the s-PGD [14]. Those approaches rely on a Canonical Polyadic Decomposition (CPD) of the field that is learnt from one-dimensional modes, i.e. a rank-1 tensor is added at each loop on all axes. It resembles the CP-ALS method [17] and solves for each mode employing a fixed-point strategy. This fixed-point approach implies a sequential construction and may append modes late in the algorithm that serve only as correctives for early ones that were not optimal. Many recent approaches are based on the TT-format and its good scaling with high-dimensional problems. A framework that inspired this paper is that of MANDy [10]. In this work, Gelß et al. adapt the popular SINDy framework [7] to high-dimensional dynamical sys-tem identification, which remains a limitation for SINDy. To achieve good scaling, the authors built sparse TT cores from a choice of one-dimensional basis functions. The same authors later expanded the same methodology to image classification [16] where they started mixing their tensorial framework with kernel interpretations to tackle efficiently higher dimensional prob-lems without relying on the TT-SVD sequential algorithm which they demonstrated became a computational bottleneck. At the same time as Baddoo et al. with their fully kernel-based approach to dynamic model learning [3], Gelß et al. published the kernel Feature Space Approx-imation (kFSA) method that once again mixes TT-formulated feature maps to kernel methods to provide a high-dimensional regularization (and model reduction) technique. We further in-vestigated the work of Engel et al. in signal processing [8] that develop a kernel recursive least squares algorithm for online learning of signals. In their work, they provide nice proofs, noticeably on the limitation of the number of retained signals (finite dictionary) and a bound with respect to ℓ2 optimality provided by the kernel Principal Component Analysis (kPCA). The evaluation of distance in the feature space that defines their Almost Linearly Dependent (ALD) criterion is the same later used by Gelß and Baddoo in their respective works. We use the same approach in our framework. In this work, we keep the philosophy of structuring the equations that build the regression operator from a separated variables perspective. Considering the lack of data as a structural constraint, we isolate it in the structure of the proposed operator and define a partial tensor product. We consider it an algebraically minimalistic approach to generalize matrix-based re-gression. It allows us to preserve properties of usual matrix-based linear systems while avoiding the curse of dimensionality. By circumventing the fixed point strategy usually encountered in high dimensional contexts, we can produce an optimal result in the ℓ2 sense from available data at a very low cost. We present the approach as algebraically minimalistic for its conceptual simplicity compared to tensor networks or abstract kernel feature spaces for instance. Working with tensor algebra and then identifying the kernel from the dot products also allows us to avoid the usual limitation of kernel methods to a few well-known kernels while leveraging their properties. For instance we are able to use the properties of kPCA. Furthermore, the sought minimalism allows us to avoid the necessity for sparse operators in practice for computational efficiency in the case of TT-formulation. The structure of the solution we build relates to the state of the art as a rank-wise unconstrained version of the s-PGD [14]. The proposed approach is structured around a tensor formalism from which we derive nice algebraic properties and computational tricks. We call it the m-tensor format as it directly relates to the unmanageable matrix that would be built naively to solve the problem. This is how we retain many of the desirable properties of the matrix-based approach in a fully (tensor-)factorized manner. By preserving a structure of higher order, we enjoy the great efficiency 3The M-Tensor Format of tensor-based approaches. Those properties would otherwise be lost by the matrix-based structure. As it is tailored for regression operators, this formalism is not investigated as a potential data structure for high order problems. The tools used in this paper are available on GitHub 1 along with numerical examples. The paper is organized as follows. In section 2, we set the problem of the least squares approximation of a scalar function in a general form. Section 3 builds the link between separable variables and tensor structure, this leads us to present the tensor operations we will use within our framework. The m-tensor format is presented in the case of least squares regression in section 4. Section 5 provides different regularization techniques for m-tensor regression that we relate to kernel regression in section 6. We apply our method to demonstrate scalability and robustness in section 7 on a polynomial benchmark with growing dimension and then on two dynamical systems: a Lorenz attractor and Kuramoto oscillators. We propose a short discussion comparing the m-tensor formalism to other techniques in section 8. Section 9 draws conclusions and presents perspectives for future works. 

Notations 

We present the notations adopted in the remainder of the paper. Operation symbols are given with their definition. Working with high dimensional objects relies on burdensome notations in order to disambiguate definitions, therefore we try to strictly stick to conventions given here. Uppercase bold symbols ( e.g. T) are used for tensors. Uppercase symbols ( e.g. M ) are used for matrices. Lowercase bold symbols ( e.g. v) are used for vectors. The notation [ · ]ijk... denotes the ijk . . . -th element of the tensor in brackets, each index evolving along an axis of the tensor. Due to the high order nature of the object of the paper, indices are more frequently subscripted with axis number, i.e. elements of a tensor T of order n will be denoted as [T]i1i2...i n .

# 6 Kernel regression interpretation 

In this section we make a few remarks on the parallel between our approach and the kernel regression framework. In practice, we manage to always take advantage of the high efficiency of the dot product of rank-1 tensors. We do not need to select a type of kernel or combine from a limited set of kernels, instead we directly work on the mapping with a tensor product structure so as to build implicitely all combinations. We leverage the tensor product’s ability to augment dramatically the row dimensions. Therefore we can always consider the linearly independent rows pseudoinverse of the m-tensor. In that case, we make P = Φ ⋉ Φ⊺ appear in the pseudoinverse formulation so that only dot products are needed. That way, an element of P is given by the inner product ⟨φ(xi), φ (xj )⟩,considering the mapping

From those observations we managed to derive powerfull methods, making the use of m-tensors a salient tool to solve high dimensional regression prob-lems.

7.3 A practical note 

A detail left until now in the implementation is that of numerical overflow. If we use the kernel trick to avoid computing all elements of the tensors at hand, it is strictly equivalent in practice. This means that working with a problem involving a dot product between two rank-1 tensors of order n and dimension (p, p, . . . , p ) will effectively lead to a result that multiplies and sums 

pn elements together. This leads to results that are unmanageable numerically. Therefore the one-dimensional mappings ψψψ are built with a hyperparameter that scales the input down in high-dimensional contexts. 19 The M-Tensor Format This hyperparameter is inherent with the method and a right tuning provides massive gains in term of precision. As it results purely from numerical issues, i.e. the mathematical background is sound otherwise, there must exist a normalization dependent on the input to be systematically applied, however this normalization has not yet been determined. In our examples, bases in low dimension (<10) are not scaled down, but higher dimensions are scaled with a factor 10 −7.The real significant choice that remains is that of one-dimensional bases ψψψ. This choice of basis function can be made different for each axis depending on the problem at hand but the first choice we have systematically made was to start with 

ψψψ(x) = 1 x .

This choice, although simple builds all combinations of parameters at the lowest computational cost. If more precision is sought, the degree can be adapted incrementally, seing how low the cost is to compute a solution. Cyclic behaviour can be represented using a sinusoïdal polynomial basis or Fourier basis. A solution left unexplored in this paper is the choice of local bases along each axis. One may discretize each axis with local bases, even with compact support. The resulting tensor regression operator will capture localized effects in the n-dimensional mesh that is implicitely built. Moreover cores would be sparse, providing a potential further speedup to the method. All examples presented here are publicly available from data generation to result plotting. The implementation uses a toolbox leveraging the previously presented operations based on m-tensors. Curious readers can access this code at: https://github.com/RemiC-OV/MTensor. The examples and tools presented were developped fully in a NumPy implementation, it is therefore simplistic and can be optimized. We leveraged algebraic structures to gain in memory use and computational efficiency but we did not implement any form of parallelism. It is one of the main development perspectives to demonstrate the gains possible. Most operations presented in this paper are parallelizable throughout dimensions and/or samples. 

# 8 Discussion 

We briefly discuss the advantages and drawbacks of the proposed approach. We chose to compare the performances of our formalism to that of kernel regression, TT-based approaches and s-PGD as we consider those to readily compare. We consider our framework to fall into the category of kernel regression methods as we have seen in section 6. The way we enrich the area of kernel regression is by efficiently reaching all combinations of a choice of one-dimensional functions. We suffer the same limitations as kernel methods i.e. the necessity to select the bases to work with and poor conditioning, to which we answer by several regularization approaches. Our formalism differs from that of tensor trains by specialising our tools to regression. Seeing the works of Gelß et al. with MANDy, one can see the direct equivalence of m-tensors to the diagonal slice structured cores. They gained efficiency in high dimensions by switching to kFSA which resorts to the kernel trick through inner products of tensor trains instead of the sequential computation of the TT-SVD and pseudoinverse. By scrapping the need for generalized algebra offered by tensor trains, we avoid storing and operating on a massive amount of zeros as is done in the TT equivalent to our method. It follows that the m-tensor formalism is not fit for as much generalization as the tensor trains. This might prove limitting in more complex applications of the m-tensor formalism. We view our approach as a generalization of the PGD framework and the sparse-PGD for the case of scarce data. We avoid seeking greedily the best rank-1 tensor to add to the sought 20 The M-Tensor Format coefficients and instead find without iterative scheme the least squares optimal coefficients. We avoid the ALS solution and gain greatly in efficiency at the solving stage. However the m-tensor regression results in coefficients that are not readily manageable as would be in the case of the PGD that may store them as a sum of few rank-one tensors. A key advantage for future works that is inherited from the structure of s-PGD is the ability to compute inverse problems [25]. The separated variables structure allows to evaluate derivatives with respect to each parameter by only computing only the derivatives of one-dimensional bases. We did not explore comparisons with any Tucker-based formalism. Even though the Tucker format is a natural choice, it still inherently suffers the curse of dimensionality through the storage of its core. In fact few approaches are found that use the Tucker format above dimen-sion 3. This is a huge limitation that requires tricks that make working with them in higher dimensions more conceptually complex. For instance one may decompose the Tucker core as a tensor train but we argue that it does not provide a straightforward understanding of the relationship to the choice of bases. 

# 9 Conclusion and perspectives 

In this paper we presented a tensor formalism for high dimensional regression of nonlinear models. The introduced m-tensor formalism is tailored for high dimensional regression in scarce data contexts. By leveraging tensor algebra, we provided properties from kernel methods with good scalability. The formalism smoothly generalizes well-known properties of matrix-based approaches to provide optimality to tensor structured models. We generalized different regularization techniques that seek to make models robuts and generalizable from the low amount of data available. Those regularization techniques prove robust and yield low errors while limitting the scaling when faced with a high number of parameters. The proposed ALI regularization technique allows for further gains in computational and memory costs, achieving linear scaling at online stage on the provided examples. The m-tensor formalism was shown to break the curse of dimensionality with, in the worst case, cubic scaling on the number of parameters. Linear scaling of memory use with the dimension of the problem and parallel capability are inherited from the separated variables approach. The proposed approach was applied on a general benchmark to demonstrate scaling and low relative errors. Robustness was demonstrated by applying the different regularization techniques to identify the nonlinear dynamical behaviour of two systems. In the first case, we show good generalizability for a Lorenz chaotic system. In the second one we show again both generalizability and scalability up to 100 parameters with limitted data on Kuramoto oscillators. In the future, we will seek different developments, most probably stemming from randomized numerical linear algebra to further accelerate our tools and enhance their scaling. The same approach might lead us to ℓ0 norm minimization. We will seek to expand the validity domain by exploiting rational forms of approximation. On the application side, the tool seems most adapted to model order reduction, data-driven operator inference, large-scale optimization or classification. Generalization of the m-tensor formalism might prove useful also for solving parametric PDEs, at this stage most likely through a collocation method.
