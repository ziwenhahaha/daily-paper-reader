Abstract 

Vision-language Models (VLMs), despite achieving strong performance on multimodal bench-marks, often misinterpret straightforward visual concepts that humans identify effortlessly, such as counting, spatial reasoning, and viewpoint understanding. Previous studies [ 29 , 9] manually identified these weaknesses and found that they often stem from deficits in specific skills. However, such manual efforts are costly, unscalable, and subject to human bias, which often overlooks subtle details in favour of salient objects, resulting in an incomplete understanding of a model’s vulnerabilities. To address these limitations, we propose a Reinforcement Learning (RL)-based framework to automatically discover the failure modes or blind spots of any “candidate VLM” on a given data distribution without human intervention. Our framework trains a questioner agent that adaptively generates queries based on the candidate VLM’s responses to elicit incorrect answers. Our approach increases question complexity by focusing on fine-grained visual details and distinct skill compositions as training progresses, consequently identifying 36 novel failure modes in which VLMs struggle. We demonstrate the broad applicability of our framework by showcasing its generalizability across various model combinations. 

## 1 Introduction 

Vision Language Models (VLMs) have demonstrated remarkable ability to reason about the visual world by answering human queries [ 23 , 1 ]. These capabilities are attributed to the effective integration of strong visual encoders [ 25 , 35 ] with Large Language Models (LLMs) [ 20 , 4 ], enabling them to solve complex tasks. Despite their impressive performance on multimodal benchmarks, VLMs are prone to errors that humans rarely make. For example, they fail to understand viewpoints and perspectives [ 29 ], hallucinate object existence [ 17 ], and struggle with low-level visual concepts [ 26 ]. Identifying failure modes of VLMs is an active area of research, where past research has successfully identified a subset of failure modes. For instance, one study found that VLMs struggle with tasks involving orientation, viewpoint, and perspective [ 29 ], and another identified challenges in depth perception, reflectance, and multi-view reasoning [ 9]. Despite their value, these studies are limited for two reasons. First, they manually design benchmarks for failure mode identification, which is time-consuming and labour-intensive. Second, humans play a major role in discovering such failure modes, which limits the coverage of discovered failure modes due to their cognitive biases [ 3]. To overcome these challenges, recent work leverages LLMs as questioners to automatically generate questions and datasets for probing VLMs [ 24 ]. These methods offer a scalable solution, as LLMs can generate diverse, high-quality queries that are not limited by fatigue or cognitive biases,

VLM Benchmarking and Failure Probing: Vision models were traditionally evaluated using aggregate benchmarks [ 2], but more recently, task-specific benchmarks are used to get a fine-grained performance portfolio. For instance, benchmarks such as MME [ 7] and SEED-Bench [ 16 ] explicitly separate perception (e.g., OCR, colour, object existence) from cognition (e.g., commonsense reasoning, visual reasoning). Likewise, MMBench [ 21 ] and MM-Vet [ 33 ] further refine these benchmarks to provide comprehensive performance reports in hierarchical and compositional structures, respectively. Similarly, in the failure-mode discovery literature, POPE [ 32 ] evaluates object hallucination via adversarial negative sampling, and HallusionBench [ 12 ] tests for inconsistencies in visual reasoning. BLINK [ 9] and MMVP [ 29 ] further isolate fine-grained perceptual gaps, including depth estimation, orientation, and viewpoint dependence in their benchmarks. However, constructing these benchmarks requires substantial human effort for data curation and annotation, thereby limiting the scalability of their approach. Moreover, these benchmarks do not tailor failure modes to a given VLM. In contrast, our framework automatically generates the dataset, eliminating the need for manual intervention. 

Automated Probing and Adversarial Policies : To overcome the costs associated with manual curation, recent works have explored automated probing strategies. In the adversarial robustness literature, such as AttackVLM [ 36 ], pixel-level perturbations are used to break the vision model. However, these methods use noise to expose a model rather than systematically addressing its semantic understanding deficits. More recent works use prompt-based techniques to identify semantic weaknesses [ 15 ], but these approaches are “open-loop”, as the questioner’s parameters are not updated based on the feedback. In contrast, in our approach, the questioner is trained to adapt its strategy to the target model’s specific failure patterns using RL. Our work parallels adversarial policy research that leverages RL [ 10 , 14 ], which exploits fixed opponents or red-team LLMs, and EvoVLM [ 13 ], which uses RL to automatically improve VLM’s performance. However, our framework focuses on discovering failure modes. In our approach, we leverage a non-stationary reward signal to train the questioner to generate diverse, fine-grained questions that eventually uncover novel failure modes in the target VLMs. 

## 3 Approach 

3.1 Problem Formulation 

We formulate automated failure-mode discovery as a RL problem, given its broad success on adjacent problems [ 28 , 5 ] and its ability to discover novel concepts outside the training data. In our framework, the questioner is modelled as an RL agent that interacts sequentially with its environment, which is a candidate VLM. The questioner is tasked with posing diverse questions to the candidate VLM that are answered incorrectly. To achieve this within the RL formulation, we use a reward function comprising several components, as discussed in Section 3.5. Our overall approach is illustrated in Figure 2 and comprises three components: Questioner , Answerer , and Verifier .

3.3 Answerer 

The answerer is the target VLM whose failure modes are being discovered. At each step, it generates responses to the questioner’s queries based on the given image, I. The parameters of the answerer are frozen throughout the training. 

3.4 Verifier 

The verifier is a reasoning VLM that computes the reward described in Equation 2 given the image 

I, the questions generated by the questioner Q, and their corresponding answers produced by the answerer. In particular, the verifier evaluates the questions with respect to the validity component and the answers with respect to the correctness component to determine the reward, described below. To evaluate the question validity , the verifier assesses the given generated question ( Q), condi-tioned on the given image ( I), across four criteria: (1) grammatical correctness; (2) atomicity (the question is standalone and non-compositional); (3) visual grounding (answering the question requires information present in the image); and (4) objectivity (the question requires an unambiguous answer). 4The question validity component, VQ ∈ { 0, 1}, is assigned a value of 1 only if the question satisfies all aforementioned criteria; otherwise, VQ = 0. The verifier computes the correctness component of the reward, CQ, by analyzing the target VLM’s response against the given generated question and the image. For each reward component, the verifier initially generates thinking tokens to internalize the image’s context, then produces a final response and a reason. 

3.5 Reward 

We use a reward signal that consists of various sub-components to train the questioner agent: 

RQ = ∆ Q · VQ · (1 − CQ) − PQ, (2) where RQ is the overall reward given to the questioner as a result of generating the question Q. It consists of various components: 

• CQ ∈ { 0, 1}, the correctness component, to ensure that the questioner is rewarded only when the generated question is incorrectly answered by the candidate VLM; 

• VQ ∈ { 0, 1}, the validity component, to ensure that the generated question is semantically and grammatically meaningful; 

• ∆Q ∈ [0 , 1], the diversity component, that scales the positive reinforcement to encourage the generation of diverse question types; 

• And, PQ ∈ [0 , 1], the penalty component, to provide negative reinforcement to the agent to accelerate the training. We set this to (1 − VQ) to accelerate the generation of valid questions by penalizing invalid questions. Intuitively, our reward function provides a positive reinforcement to the agent if the generated question is valid, meets the diversity requirement, and is incorrectly answered by the candidate VLM ( CQ = 0, VQ = 1, and ∆ Q = 1). Otherwise, the agent doesn’t receive any reward (if CQ = 1 or VQ = 0 or ∆ Q = 0). Further, we decompose the diversity component, ∆ Q, into semantic and lexical sub-components. 

Semantic Diversity: This term penalizes the generation of questions that are semantically similar to those previously answered incorrectly by the target VLM. For each image I, we maintain a memory bank HI of incorrectly answered questions. We compute the semantic diversity score using the cosine similarity between the embedding of the current question e(qi) (extracted via a sentence embedding model all-MiniLM-L6-v2 [30]) and its nearest neighbour in HI :

δemb = 1 − max q′∈H I cos (e(qi), e(q′)) , (3) where HI is image-level memory bank to encourage localized semantic variety. 

Lexical Diversity: To encourage varied question types (e.g., “How many...” vs. “What is...”), we compute the inverse frequency of a question’s prefix (the first L tokens) within the global dataset-level memory bank H:

δifreq = 11 + Count (prefix (qi), H) , (4) where prefix (qi) returns the first L tokens in the given question qi, and Count (prefix (qi), H)returns the counts of the prefix in the memory bank H.51

## 4 Categorizing Failure Modes 

To understand the failure modes of the candidate VLM, we map the question pool from the memory bank to the skills and meta-skills required to answer them using a novel clustering pipeline. Our approach leverages LLMs at various stages, and we provide all the details, including the prompts used, in the Appendix. As illustrated in Figure 3, our approach consists of four stages: identification of primitives, topic modelling, extraction of skills, and identification of meta-skills. We briefly describe each step below. 

Identification of primitives: In this step, we identify the primitive skills required to solve a particular question for each method separately. To obtain this, we first select valid questions among all the generated questions. Then, we pass these questions to a VLM along with the corresponding image, prompting it to identify the set of primitive skills required to answer them. 

Topic modelling: Here, we use topic modelling to map thousands of primitive skills identified across all the questions into a handful of meaningful categories. For each method, we remove duplicates among the primitive skills identified in the previous step, then map semantically similar primitive skills into clusters using a standard topic modelling pipeline (BERTopic [ 11 ]). For each cluster, we use an LLM to generate a descriptive label for all primitive skills within it, yielding the topic name. 

Skill extraction: In this step, we standardize the topic names across all methods. Note that the first two steps are carried out separately for each approach to capture nuances within them. To accomplish this, we sample a batch of questions from every topic for each method. Then, we pass these questions, along with their corresponding topics, to an LLM and prompt it to perform one of the four operations: keep, rename, merge, or split. The keep operation retains the topic name if the LLM deems it appropriate; the LLM produces a new name for the topic if it decides to perform the 6rename operation; in the merge operation, the LLM combines topics if they are similar; and the LLM separates a particular topic into different topics in the split operation. These operations also ensure that topics mistakenly identified in the previous step are corrected, since they have access to more nuanced information in the form of questions. 

Meta-skill identification: In this final step, we leverage an LLM to group skills into meta-skills in order for us to have two hierarchical levels of taxonomy for studying failure modes. 

## 5 Experiments 

Dataset and Models: Our framework is agnostic to the target VLM and dataset, and the failure modes discovered are tailored to the target VLM-dataset combination. For the experiments, we randomly sample 1000 images from the training set of the COCO [19 ] dataset to probe the target VLM’s failure modes. We use a variety of target VLMs (answerer) that span scale (3B-8B parameters) and architecture families ( Qwen2.5-VL and Qwen3-VL series) in experiments. For the questioner ,we experiment with Qwen2.5-VL-3B and Qwen2.5-VL-7B . And, use Qwen3-VL-30B-Thinking as a 

verifier for all the experiments, as it provides a robust reward signal due to its strong reasoning capabilities. 

Baselines: We compare our approach against competitive baselines that cover a broad range of techniques used in the failure mode discovery problem. Note that we implement all these baselines from scratch (except for ConMe [ 15 ]), as there is no prior work that tailors the failure-discovery process to the target VLM-dataset combination. Our baselines include: 1. Zero Shot where we use a directly prompt the static, untrained questioner. 2. ConMe where we implement this baseline following the original in-context learning and prompting strategy from [ 15 ]. This approach employs a two-turn process where second-turn queries are conditioned on the VLM’s initial responses to expose reasoning failures. To isolate the specific benefits conditioning on the VLM’s responses, we provide an additional comparison in the Appendix using the same prompt within our RL framework. 3. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) where we fine-tune the questioner using questions from the existing failure mode discovery benchmarks [ 9 , 31 , 18 , 29 , 34 , 8 , 22 ] before prompting them to generate questions. 4. Expert Iteration where we iteratively fine-tune the questioner for 5 rounds. In each round, the questioner generates questions that are filtered via rejection sampling using the same verifier as our RL method. These failure cases are then used to fine-tune the questioner for the next round, ensuring the generation is progressively tailored to the vulnerabilities of the target VLM. 5. RL+SFT where we first fine-tune the questioner using the existing failure mode datasets, then we train it using our RL framework.

5.1 Does RL Help In Failure Discovery In VLMs? 

Table 1 compares our RL framework against the baselines across the different evaluation axes. Regarding the Question Validity Rate (QVR), all methods perform comparably. While the base RL model shows a marginal decrease in QVR due to the inherent trade-off between failure elicitation, diversity, and validity (see Sec.5.4), this is mitigated by applying RL to a post-SFT model (RL+SFT), which achieves a QVR of 86.70%. 

Potency of Elicited Failures: Our RL-based approaches achieve significantly higher Failure Discovery Rates (FDR) (47 .58% and 50 .73%) compared to all the non-RL baselines. This ranking is supported by our human study (see the Appendix), which confirms that these FDR gains represent

To verify that these results do not arise from over-exploiting narrow failure modes, performance was analyzed across the discovered skills. Our RL method maintains the highest average per-skill failure rate (47 .58%) across 131 unique skills (see the Appendix). Moreover, RL-generated questions exhibit higher Skill Coverage , requiring 3.30 simultaneous skills per query compared to the baseline’s 3 .10. As shown in Table 1, our RL-based approaches produce more semantically diverse questions, RL at 64 .96 and RL+SFT at 85 .41, compared to ConMe’s 61 .78, the next best baseline. The biggest difference is in the range of question types; RL methods far exceed ConMe in lexical diversity. This means the RL agent does not rely on a few question types but rather generates a variety of different question types ( Is , Does , How many , What , Where etc). In summary, RL-based methods demonstrate superior performance over all baselines by jointly optimizing for failure discovery and diversity metrics. These results confirm that our multi-objective reward signal is vital for uncovering a wide array of genuine VLM vulnerabilities hidden from other methods. 

5.2 Are the Discovered Failures Novel? 

To evaluate how novel the failures identified by our framework are, we look at two factors: the variety of skills involved and the uniqueness of the questions within those skills. Figure 4 shows how shared and unique skills are spread across different methods. It reveals that RL-based methods cover a wider range of skills (131 for RL) than the baselines (89 for SFT), and they also find more exclusive skills (36 compared to 17). These exclusive skills include Prominence Detection, Threat Assessment, Path Tracking, and others (see the full list in the Appendix). We further compare the complexity of generated questions across different methods in Figure 5a. Here, complexity is measured as the total number of unique skill combinations across all questions generated by a method. We observe that RL methods outperform other baselines in terms of the number of unique skill combinations probed. This suggests that RL discovers 3,595 more novel combinations than the next-best-performing baseline, SFT. Further looking at the percent of generated questions with more than 1 skill in Figure 5b, we again see that RL has a higher percentage of questions with 4 or more skills per question. This suggests that RL-generated questions often require proficiency in multiple skills to answer correctly. For instance, rather than simple identification, the RL questioner probes the model’s “spatial reasoning” with complex queries such as: “What traffic light is located to the immediate left of the train and south-west of the stop sign above it?” . Such questions essentially require multi-hop reasoning: first locating all the “traffic lights” to the immediate left of the train, then localizing the “stop sign”, and finally locating the traffic light “southwest” of it. Overall, the emergence of these exclusive skills and multi-reasoning queries suggests that our framework can identify a more nuanced class of novel failures that test a fine-grained understanding of the target VLM.

5.5 Are Discovered Failures Model-Specific or Generalizable? 

To determine whether the discovered failures are specific to particular architectures or universal weaknesses, we evaluate our framework across various Questioner-Answerer combinations, all trained using identical hyperparameters (see the Appendix for Implementation Details). Table 5 presents a cross-model evaluation, pairing Qwen-2.5-VL (3B and 7B) questioners against target models of increasing capacity for both RL and Expert Iteration. As expected, the FDR decreases as the target VLM gets larger. For example, the 3B questioner achieves a 47 .58% FDR against a 3B answerer, but this falls to 29 .37% against a stronger 8B answerer. Using the stronger 7B questioner raises the FDR to 38 .97% with the 8B answerer. Compared to Expert Iteration, RL consistently achieves higher FDR across all combinations, with the gap becoming more pronounced for larger answerer models. Notably, diversity metrics and skill cluster counts remain high across all pairings, suggesting that the framework continues to explore a broad failure space regardless of the specific model combination or target difficulty. 

Universal vs Model Specific Failures: By analyzing the skills discovered across different answerer scales, we find that certain categories remain universally challenging regardless of model capacity. For example, all three answerers struggle with Exclusion Reasoning and Anatomical Recognition . When probed by the 7B questioner, the larger 8B model occasionally underperforms compared to its 7B counterpart in specific areas. Interestingly , the 8B answerer shows higher failure rates in Domain Knowledge Application (50.0% vs. 40.9%) and Terrain Analysis (20.9% vs. 12.1%). 13 Conversely, some vulnerabilities are model-specific; for instance, Negation Processing is a significant blind spot for the 3B model (FDR ¿ 55%), whereas this issue is largely mitigated in the 8B model (FDR < 8%). More details are presented in the Appendix. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We introduced an RL-based framework for automatically discovering failure modes in VLMs. Unlike previous approaches that require significant manual intervention, ours trains a questioner agent fully automatically using a VLM-based verifier. Our experiments demonstrate that the RL-trained questioner substantially outperforms competitive baselines across various metrics. In particular, the SFT fine-tuned RL variant generated questions that elicited approx. 13% more failures from the target VLM than the next-best baseline. It also identified 36 new skills that previous work and other approaches failed to identify, while maintaining significant lexical and semantic diversity in the generated questions. Our analysis further revealed that the RL-based approaches generate increasingly complex questions over time, starting with simpler ones. We also observed that some of the newer skills are discovered later in the training, after exploiting the model’s basic vulnerabilities. These findings generalize across model scales and architectures. Unlike static benchmarks, our framework dynamically targets the specific weaknesses of a given VLM, offering a scalable alternative for discovering model-specific failure modes. 

Limitations and Future Work. While our framework effectively discovers failure modes, several directions remain open. First, the current approach focuses on single-image, single-turn interactions; extending to multi-turn dialogues or video understanding could reveal additional failure patterns. Second, integrating the discovered failure modes into fine-tuning pipelines could close the loop from diagnosis to remedy. Finally, scaling the framework to probe larger proprietary models remains an important challenge for comprehensive VLM evaluation. In summary, our work represents a paradigm shift from static, manually curated benchmarks toward adaptive, RL-driven failure discovery. By transforming VLM evaluation into a continual learning problem, we enable systematic identification of cognitive blind spots that would otherwise remain hidden, ultimately contributing to the development of more robust and reliable vision-language systems.
