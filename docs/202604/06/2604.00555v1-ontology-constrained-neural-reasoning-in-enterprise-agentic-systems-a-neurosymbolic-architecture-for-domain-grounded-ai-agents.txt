Abstract 

Enterprise adoption of Large Language Models (LLMs) is constrained by hallucination, domain drift, and the inability to enforce regulatory compliance at the reasoning level. We present a neurosymbolic architecture implemented within the Foundation Agenti-cOS ( FAOS ) platform that addresses these limitations through ontology-constrained neural reasoning . Our approach introduces a three-layer ontological framework—Role, Domain, and Interaction ontologies—that provides formal semantic grounding for LLM-based en-terprise agents. We formalize the concept of asymmetric neurosymbolic coupling , wherein symbolic ontological knowledge constrains agent inputs (context assembly, tool discovery, governance thresholds) while proposing mechanisms for extending this coupling to constrain agent outputs (response validation, reasoning verification, compliance checking). We evalu-ate the architecture through a controlled experiment (600 runs across five industries: Fin-Tech, Insurance, Healthcare, Vietnamese Banking, and Vietnamese Insurance), finding that ontology-coupled agents significantly outperform ungrounded agents on Metric Accuracy (p < . 001 , W = .460 ), Regulatory Compliance ( p = .003 , W = .318 ), and Role Consis-tency ( p < . 001 , W = .614 ), with improvements greatest where LLM parametric knowledge is weakest—particularly in Vietnam-localized domains. Our contributions include: (1) a formal three-layer enterprise ontology model, (2) a taxonomy of neurosymbolic coupling patterns, (3) ontology-constrained tool discovery via SQL-pushdown scoring, (4) a proposed framework for output-side ontological validation, (5) empirical evidence for the inverse para-metric knowledge effect—that ontological grounding value is inversely proportional to LLM training data coverage of the domain, and (6) a production system serving 21 industry verticals with 650+ agents. 

Keywords: Neurosymbolic AI, Enterprise Ontology, Large Language Models, Agentic AI, Domain-Driven Design, Knowledge-Grounded Reasoning, Multi-Agent Systems 

# 1 Introduction 

The rapid proliferation of Large Language Models (LLMs) in enterprise settings has exposed a fundamental tension: LLMs excel at natural language understanding and generation but lack the formal semantic grounding necessary for reliable operation in regulated industries [Ji et al., 2023, Huang et al., 2023]. An insurance underwriting agent that hallucinates policy terms, a financial advisor that confuses Basel III capital ratios, or a healthcare agent that misapplies HIPAA guidelines poses not merely a quality problem but a compliance and liability risk.

This tension is not new in artificial intelligence. The “neuro-symbolic debate” has persisted for decades, with neural approaches offering flexibility and generalization while symbolic ap-proaches provide verifiability and formal guarantees [Garcez and Lamb, 2023, Hitzler and Sarker, 2022]. What is new is the enterprise context : modern AI agents must operate within complex organizational structures, adhere to industry-specific regulations, and produce outputs that can withstand audit scrutiny. We contend that the resolution lies not in choosing between neural and symbolic paradigms but in their principled integration —a neurosymbolic architecture where formal ontologies con-strain, guide, and verify neural reasoning. This paper presents such an architecture, implemented within the Foundation AgenticOS ( FAOS ) platform, and proposes extensions that would ad-vance the state of neurosymbolic enterprise AI. 

1.1 The Enterprise Grounding Problem 

Enterprise AI agents face a unique variant of the grounding problem [Harnad, 1990]. Unlike general-purpose assistants, enterprise agents must: (i) Speak the domain language : Use industry-specific terminology correctly (e.g., “com-bined ratio” in insurance, “net revenue retention” in SaaS, “average length of stay” in healthcare). (ii) Reason within regulatory bounds : Ensure outputs comply with applicable regulations (SOX, Basel III/IV, HIPAA, GDPR, EU AI Act [European Parliament and Council, 2024]). (iii) Follow organizational workflows : Respect handoff protocols, approval chains, and escalation paths defined by the organization. (iv) Adapt to role-specific perspectives : A CFO and a product manager examining the same data should receive analyses framed through their respective decision-making lenses. Current approaches address these requirements through prompt engineering [Wei et al., 2022, Wang et al., 2023], retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) [Lewis et al., 2020, Gao et al., 2024], and fine-tuning [Hu et al., 2022]. While effective for individual dimensions, none provides a uni-fied formal framework for enterprise grounding. Prompt engineering is brittle and unverifiable; RAG retrieves but does not reason; fine-tuning is expensive and domain-locked.

# 2 Background and Related Work 

2.1 Neurosymbolic AI 

The integration of neural and symbolic AI has been a persistent research theme since the connectionist-symbolic debates of the 1980s [Fodor and Pylyshyn, 1988]. Modern neurosym-bolic AI has been catalyzed by three developments: the success of deep learning, the maturation of knowledge representation standards (OWL, RDF, SKOS), and the emergence of LLMs as general-purpose reasoning engines. Garcez and Lamb [2023] propose a taxonomy of neurosymbolic integration patterns, dis-tinguishing between symbolic[neural] (symbolic systems enhanced by neural components), neu-ral[symbolic] (neural systems enhanced by symbolic knowledge), and neural:symbolic (tight bidi-rectional integration). Our work falls primarily in the neural[symbolic] category, with proposals toward neural:symbolic integration. Hitzler and Sarker [2022] argue that ontologies provide a natural formalism for neurosymbolic integration, offering type hierarchies, property constraints, and inference rules that can bound neural computation. We extend this argument to the enterprise domain, where ontologies must capture not only domain concepts but organizational roles and interaction protocols. 

2.2 Knowledge-Grounded Language Models 

Knowledge grounding for LLMs has been pursued through several mechanisms: 

• Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) : Augmenting LLM context with retrieved documents [Lewis et al., 2020, Gao et al., 2024]. RAG addresses factual grounding but does not enforce structural or regulatory constraints. 

• Knowledge Graph Integration : Injecting structured knowledge from knowledge graphs into LLM reasoning [Pan et al., 2024, Zhang et al., 2022]. This approach provides relational grounding but typically lacks the formal semantics of ontological reasoning. 

• Ontology-Enhanced Prompting : Using ontological definitions to structure LLM prompts [Babaei Giglou et al., 2023]. This is closest to our current implementation but has not been formalized for multi-layer enterprise ontologies. 

• Constrained Decoding : Restricting LLM output tokens to conform to grammatical or structural constraints [Willard and Louf, 2023]. This provides output-side control but at the syntactic rather than semantic level. Recent work on augmented language models [Mialon et al., 2023] surveys the broader land-scape of tool-augmented LLMs, while Kambhampati et al. [2024] demonstrate that LLMs strug-gle with planning tasks without external symbolic scaffolding—a finding that supports our argu-ment for ontological grounding. Marcus [2020] argues broadly for hybrid architectures combining neural and symbolic components. 3Recent empirical work has begun to validate these approaches quantitatively. Agrawal et al. [2024] survey knowledge graph augmentation techniques for hallucination reduction, finding promising but inconsistent results across domains. Giordano et al. [2025] demonstrate that ontology-grounded LLMs exhibit reduced digression in industrial conversational settings. As-prino and Presutti [2025] compare ontology-guided knowledge graph construction against vector retrieval baselines for RAG, finding that structured approaches outperform flat retrieval when domain knowledge is specialized. Sansone et al. [2024] propose a KG-based evaluation framework for measuring hallucination at the triple level, complementing our metric-based approach. Our work synthesizes these approaches within a unified ontological framework, extending beyond document retrieval to encompass role-aware context injection, domain-hierarchical tool discovery, and interaction-protocol enforcement. Unlike prior empirical work that evaluates general-purpose QA, our evaluation targets enterprise-specific tasks requiring domain metrics, regulatory compliance, and role-aware reasoning—capabilities that test the boundaries of LLM parametric knowledge. 

2.3 Enterprise Ontologies 

Enterprise ontology engineering has a rich history, from the Toronto Virtual Enterprise (TOVE) [Fox et al., 1996] to the Financial Industry Business Ontology (FIBO) maintained by the EDM Council [Bennett, 2013]. Upper ontologies such as the Basic Formal Ontology (BFO) [Arp et al., 2015] and DOLCE [Borgo et al., 2022] provide foundational categories for domain ontology alignment. However, existing enterprise ontologies were designed for data integration and semantic inter-operability, not for constraining AI agent behavior. We propose that enterprise ontologies must be extended with three additional layers: role ontologies (how domain actors reason), interaction ontologies (how actors coordinate), and governance constraints (what regulatory bounds apply). 

2.4 Multi-Agent Systems and Coordination 

The multi-agent systems (MAS) literature provides formal frameworks for agent coordination [Wooldridge, 2009, Shoham and Leyton-Brown, 2008]. However, classical MAS assumes agents with well-defined utility functions and communication protocols. LLM-based agents introduce stochastic behavior that challenges these assumptions. Our work addresses this gap by using ontological constraints to bound the stochastic behavior of LLM agents within formally defined operational envelopes. 

# 3 Three-Layer Enterprise Ontology Framework 

We define an enterprise ontology O as a triple: 

O = ⟨R , D, I⟩ (1) where R is the Role Ontology , D is the Domain Ontology , and I is the Interaction Ontology .Each layer serves a distinct function in grounding agent behavior. 

3.1 Layer 1: Role Ontology ( R)

The Role Ontology encodes how specific organizational roles think, decide, and communicate. Formally: 

R = {r1, r 2, . . . , r n} (2) where each role ri is defined as a tuple: 4ri = ⟨id , δ i, μ i, σ i, ϵ i, α i⟩ (3) 

• δi: Decision patterns — a set of reasoning strategies (e.g., {strategic , data-driven , risk-averse })

• μi: Metrics focus — domain-specific KPIs the role prioritizes (e.g., {ARR , NPS , churn_rate })

• σi: Communication style — output framing (e.g., executive, technical, compliance-oriented) 

• ϵi: Expertise domains — knowledge areas the role is authoritative in 

• αi: Approval authority — decisions the role can authorize Example: Role Definition (Product Manager in SaaS) 

> product_manager: decision_patterns: [strategic, user-centric, data-driven] metrics_focus: [ARR, NPS, feature_adoption, churn_rate] communication_style: executive expertise_domains: [product_strategy, user_research] approval_authority: [feature_releases, roadmap_changes]

The Role Ontology enables what we term perspective-aware reasoning : the same business question, posed to agents grounded in different roles, produces analyses framed through the role’s decision patterns and metric priorities. This is not prompt engineering—it is ontologically grounded persona specification with formal properties: 

Property 1 (Role Consistency) . For a given query q and role ri, the agent’s response a must reference metrics m ∈ μi and apply decision patterns d ∈ δi.

3.2 Layer 2: Domain Ontology ( D)

The Domain Ontology captures industry-specific concepts, their relationships, and associated metrics. Formally: 

D = ⟨V , E, M, G ⟩ (4) 

• V: Verticals — industry segments organized hierarchically (e.g., fintech.payments. card_networks )

• E: Entities — domain concepts with formal definitions and relationships (e.g., “Annual Recurring Revenue: annualized value of recurring revenue, MRR × 12”) 

• M : Metrics — quantitative measures with healthy ranges and world-class benchmarks 

• G: Governance constraints — regulatory frameworks applicable to each vertical The hierarchical vertical structure enables domain-scoped reasoning : an agent operating in 

fintech.payments inherits concepts from the parent fintech domain while accessing payment-specific terminology. This hierarchy is exploited in tool discovery (Section 6). ancestors (v) = {v′ | v′ is a prefix of v in the hierarchy } (5) 53.3 Layer 3: Interaction Ontology ( I)

The Interaction Ontology formalizes organizational workflows as typed handoff patterns between roles. Formally: 

I = ⟨H, A, P ⟩ (6) 

• H: Handoff patterns — directed edges between roles with trigger conditions, required artifacts, and approval flags 

• A: Approval chains — ordered sequences of roles that must authorize decisions, with timeout constraints 

• P : Escalation paths — fallback routing when conditions exceed an agent’s authority A handoff pattern h ∈ H is defined as: 

h = ⟨rfrom , r to , τ, ϕ, β ⟩ (7) where τ is the trigger condition, ϕ is the set of required artifacts, and β is the approval require-ment. Example: Interaction Pattern (Design to Development) 

design_to_development_handoff: from_role: ux_designer to_role: senior_developer trigger: design_complete required_artifacts: [UI Designs, Design Specs, Flows] approval_required: true escalation_path: [product_manager] 

3.4 Ontology Composition Across Industries 

A key property of the three-layer framework is its composability : the same structural schema supports radically different industries by varying the content of each layer. 

Ofintech = ⟨R fintech , Dfintech , Ifintech ⟩ (8) 

Ohealthcare = ⟨R healthcare , Dhealthcare , Ihealthcare ⟩ (9) The FAOS platform currently instantiates this framework across 21 industry verticals (bank-ing, healthcare, fintech, manufacturing, insurance, software, retail, and 14 others), demonstrat-ing that the three-layer schema is industry-invariant while the content is industry-specific .

# 4 Taxonomy of Neurosymbolic Coupling Patterns 

We propose a taxonomy of how symbolic ontological knowledge can be coupled with neural LLM reasoning in enterprise systems. This taxonomy distinguishes three coupling points in the agent execution pipeline. 

4.1 Input-Side Coupling (Currently Implemented) 

Input-side coupling constrains what information the LLM receives before reasoning begins. In 

FAOS , three mechanisms implement input-side coupling: 64.1.1 Context Injection 

The ContextResolver loads the appropriate industry ontology at runtime and the PromptInjector 

converts symbolic ontological definitions into natural language context sections: 

Cinjected = finject (Rri , Dv, Ih) subject to |C injected | ≤ Tmax (10) where Tmax is the token budget (default: 2000 tokens) and finject is a priority-ordered serialization function (Role > Domain > Interaction). 

4.1.2 Tool Discovery Filtering 

Ontological domain hierarchies filter which tools are visible to an agent (Section 6), ensuring agents can only invoke capabilities relevant to their operational domain. 

4.1.3 Governance Thresholds 

Domain-specific quality gates prevent low-quality skills from being available in regulated indus-tries. For a skill s in domain d:available (s, d ) ⇐⇒ quality (s) ≥ θgov (d) (11) where θgov (d) is the governance threshold for domain d, with regulated domains (fintech, health-care) having strictly higher thresholds. 

4.2 Process-Side Coupling (Partially Implemented) 

Process-side coupling constrains the reasoning process itself. In FAOS , this is partially imple-mented through: 

• Autonomy gates : A block-first approval model where sensitive operations require human authorization before execution, based on a risk classification matrix. 

• Quality judge verification : A post-reasoning verification node in the agent execution graph that assesses output quality (though not ontological compliance). 

• Escalation mechanisms : When agent confidence falls below thresholds, the system escalates to a supervisory agent or human operator. 

4.3 Output-Side Coupling (Proposed) 

Output-side coupling would constrain LLM outputs against ontological definitions after gener-ation. This is the primary research frontier and is not yet implemented in FAOS . We formalize the requirements in Section 7. 

4.4 Coupling Maturity Model 

We propose a six-level maturity model for neurosymbolic coupling in enterprise AI systems: The FAOS platform currently operates at L2–L3 , with mechanisms for L1 (context injec-tion), L2 (tool discovery filtering), and partial L3 (autonomy gates, quality judge). # 5 System Architecture 

5.1 Platform Overview 

The Foundation AgenticOS ( FAOS ) is a multi-tenant enterprise platform built on Python/-FastAPI with LangGraph-based agent orchestration, PostgreSQL for persistence, Redis for caching and event streaming, and Qdrant for vector search. The backend comprises 300+ modules organized into 7 bounded contexts following Domain-Driven Design (DDD) principles [Evans, 2004].

# 7 Toward Closed-Loop Neurosymbolic Reasoning 

The current FAOS architecture implements input-side neurosymbolic coupling (L2–L3 in our maturity model). We now propose extensions toward output-side validation (L4) and closed-loop reasoning (L5). 

7.1 Output-Side Ontological Validation 

We propose an OntologyValidator component that checks LLM outputs against Domain On-tology constraints: 

Definition 1 (Ontological Compliance) . An agent response a is ontologically compliant with domain d ∈ D if: (a) All domain terms in a are defined in d.E (terminological consistency) (b) All metric references in a cite values within defined ranges in d.M (metric validity) (c) All workflow references in a follow handoff patterns in I.H (interaction compliance) (d) All regulatory claims in a reference frameworks in d.G (governance alignment) 

7.2 Constraint Checking via Ontological Inference 

For industries with formal regulatory requirements, we propose integrating lightweight OWL reasoning to verify output compliance: valid (a, O) ⇐⇒ O̸ | = ¬a (15) That is, an output a is valid if and only if the ontology does not entail its negation. This requires translating relevant portions of the Domain Ontology into OWL axioms and running a description logic reasoner (e.g., HermiT, ELK) on the output. 10 7.3 Feedback Loop: Ontology Evolution from Agent Experience 

The most ambitious extension is ontology evolution : using agent experience to refine the ontology itself. We propose a supervised evolution mechanism: 1. Discovery : Agents encounter domain concepts not in the current ontology 2. Proposal : The system proposes ontology extensions based on recurring unrecognized concepts 3. Validation : Domain experts review and approve proposed extensions 4. Integration : Approved extensions are merged into the ontology with provenance tracking This creates a learning ontology that improves with use, closing the loop between neural experience and symbolic knowledge.

8.2 Experimental Design 

Conditions. We compare four grounding conditions corresponding to maturity levels in our coupling taxonomy (Section 4): 1. C1 – Ungrounded (L0): System prompt only; no domain context. 2. C2 – RAG-Only: Unstructured text chunks extracted from ontology blueprint docu-ments (8 curated paragraphs per industry, ∼2,000 tokens total, injected as flat reference text). 3. C3 – Ontology-Coupled (L2): Structured three-layer injection (Role + Domain + Interaction) via the FAOS PromptInjector format. 4. C4 – Ontology+Process (L3): C3 plus a post-generation quality judge that scores output quality and flags sub-threshold responses for escalation. 

Task Set. 50 tasks across five regulated industries—FinTech, Insurance, Healthcare, Viet-namese Banking (banking_vn), and Vietnamese Insurance (insurance_vn)—with 10 tasks per industry spanning four categories: terminology (3), metric interpretation (2), regulatory com-pliance (2), role-based analysis (2), and cross-cutting scenarios (1). Each task has ground truth derived from FAOS ontology blueprints. The two Vietnamese industries are particularly inter-esting because they test ontological grounding in a domain where LLM training data is sparse: bilingual Vietnamese/English regulatory frameworks, SBV circulars, MoF insurance decrees, and market-specific terminology not well-represented in English-centric pretraining corpora. 

Execution. Each task was presented under all four conditions with three repetitions ( 50 ×

4 × 3 = 600 runs). The agent LLM was Claude Sonnet 4 [Anthropic, 2025] (temperature 0.3). An independent judge LLM (Claude Sonnet 4, temperature 0.0) scored each response against ground truth using metric-specific rubrics, following the LLM-as-judge paradigm [Zheng et al., 2024]. Cross-cutting tasks were scored on all four metrics simultaneously.

8.3.4 RAG vs. Ontology 

A notable finding is that C2 (RAG) is competitive with C3 (Ontology) on several tasks. This is partially an artifact of our experimental design: the RAG chunks were curated from the same ontology blueprints that define the structured injection, making them unusually well-organized compared to real-world document retrieval. In production deployments where RAG chunks are drawn from heterogeneous, noisy document collections, we expect a larger ontology advantage. The C2–C3 post-hoc comparison was not significant for any metric ( pcorr > 0.20 ), supporting this interpretation. 15 9 Discussion 

9.1 Why Ontologies, Not Just RAG? 

A natural question is whether formal ontologies add value beyond well-organized RAG. Our empirical results provide a nuanced answer. On the significant metrics (MA, RC, RS), structured ontology injection (C3) and RAG (C2) achieved comparable scores. However, we argue ontologies remain essential for three reasons beyond what our pilot study measured: 1. Structural constraints : Ontologies define relationships (hierarchies, handoff patterns, approval chains) that flat documents cannot express. The RS metric captures only a subset of this structural value; full governance enforcement requires machine-readable ontological definitions. 2. Composability : The three-layer framework allows independent evolution of roles, do-mains, and interactions. Adding a new role does not require rewriting domain documen-tation. 3. Verifiability : Ontological definitions are machine- readable and can be checked program-matically. RAG documents are opaque to formal verification. This distinction becomes critical at coupling levels L4–L5, where output-side validation requires structured defini-tions. Our pilot study also revealed that the RAG advantage diminishes when chunks are drawn from curated sources. In production settings with noisy, heterogeneous document corpora, on-tological structure is expected to provide clearer differentiation. 9.2 The Asymmetric Coupling Gap 

The most significant finding from our architectural analysis is the asymmetric coupling gap :the current system rigorously constrains LLM inputs through ontological context but does not validate LLM outputs against the same ontological definitions. This asymmetry means that an agent can receive perfect domain context and still generate outputs that violate domain constraints—the ontology guides but does not enforce .Closing this gap (moving from L2–L3 to L4–L5) is the primary research and engineering challenge. The difficulty is computational: running OWL reasoners on every agent output introduces latency that may be unacceptable in interactive settings. We believe lightweight constraint checkers (domain-term lookup, metric range validation, handoff pattern matching) can capture the most critical validation requirements without full ontological reasoning. 16 9.3 The Inverse Parametric Knowledge Effect 

Our pilot study reveals a phenomenon we term the inverse parametric knowledge effect : the value of ontological grounding is inversely proportional to the LLM’s pre-existing parametric knowledge of the domain concept. Three observations support this: 1. Metric Accuracy benefits most (p < . 001 , W = .460 ). KPI benchmarks (healthy ranges, world-class targets, SLA thresholds) are enterprise-specific and rarely appear in public training corpora. Ontological injection directly supplies this missing knowledge. 2. TF regression on well-known concepts. For insurance terms like “combined ratio” and “persistency rate”—concepts extensively covered in textbooks and public documents— ontology injection reduced scores (IN-T1: .81 → .50 ; IN-T2: .87 → .57 ). We hypothesize a context displacement mechanism: injected ontological context occupies system prompt capacity that would otherwise be available for the LLM’s natural parametric recall. 3. Vietnamese industries show the largest ∆. The 5-industry expansion provides the strongest evidence yet: banking_vn ( ∆ = + .29 ) and insurance_vn ( ∆ = + .28 ) show 2 ×

the improvement of English-language industries (avg ∆ = + .12 ). Vietnamese regulatory concepts—SBV Circular 11/2021 NPL thresholds, MoF solvency margin requirements per TT 132/2023, bancassurance cooling-off periods—are severely underrepresented in English-centric pretraining corpora, making ontological grounding essential rather than merely beneficial. 4. RS improvements are language-invariant. Role Consistency improved by +.27 (C1 →C3) across all five industries regardless of language, confirming that role-specific decision pat-terns are uniformly underrepresented in parametric knowledge. This effect has practical implications for system design: a production ontology injection pipeline should estimate the LLM’s parametric confidence per concept and suppress injection for well-known domains, reserving context budget for enterprise-specific knowledge where the marginal value is highest. The Vietnamese results suggest that ontological grounding becomes 

mandatory —not optional—for non-English enterprise domains where LLM parametric coverage is structurally insufficient. 

9.4 Multi-Tenancy as Neurosymbolic Challenge 

The multi-tenant nature of FAOS introduces a unique neurosymbolic challenge: the same onto-logical framework must support different domain instantiations per tenant. Tenant A (a bank) and Tenant B (a hospital) share the three-layer schema but populate it with entirely different domain concepts. This requires ontological polymorphism —the ability to reason generically over the ontology structure while respecting domain-specific content. 

9.5 Implemented vs. Proposed Contributions 

We emphasize the distinction between contributions grounded in a production system and those that remain proposals: 

• Implemented (L1–L3) : Three-layer ontology framework, context injection pipeline, ontology-constrained tool discovery with SQL-pushdown scoring, governance-aware filter-ing, autonomy gates, quality judge verification. These are deployed in production serving 21 industry verticals. 

• Proposed (L4–L5) : Output-side ontological validation, OWL-based constraint checking, supervised ontology evolution. These are formalized architecturally but not yet imple-mented or empirically evaluated. 17 9.6 Threats to Validity 

1. Ontology completeness : If a domain concept or regulation is missing from the ontology, the grounding is incomplete. Ontology maintenance cost scales with regulatory change velocity. 2. OWL reasoning practicality : The proposed output-side validation via description logic reasoners (Section 7.2) requires translating free-text LLM outputs into OWL-compatible representations—itself an error-prone process that may introduce new failure modes. 3. Single-system analysis : Our findings are based on one platform ( FAOS ); generalization to other enterprise agentic systems (CrewAI, AutoGen, LangGraph standalone) requires further study. 4. LLM-as-judge reliability : Our evaluation relies on an LLM judge (Claude Sonnet 4) rather than human expert scoring. While LLM judges have shown strong correlation with human evaluators in recent benchmarks, our pilot study has not yet validated inter-rater reliability ( κ) against domain experts. The TF regression observed on well-known concepts (Section 8.3.2) may partially reflect judge sensitivity to context injection artifacts. 5. Curated RAG baseline : Our C2 (RAG) condition used chunks extracted from the same ontology blueprints that define C3, making C2 unusually well-structured. Production RAG deployments with heterogeneous source documents would likely show a larger ontology advantage. 6. Context interference : The TF regression on well-known concepts suggests that ontology injection can displace useful parametric knowledge. This interaction between injected context and model priors warrants further investigation with adaptive injection strategies. 

# 10 Conclusion and Future Work 

We have presented a neurosymbolic architecture for enterprise AI agents that uses a three-layer ontological framework (Role, Domain, Interaction) to ground LLM reasoning in formal domain knowledge. Our primary contribution is the formalization of asymmetric neurosym-bolic coupling —the observation that current enterprise AI systems constrain agent inputs but not outputs—and the proposal of a closed-loop neurosymbolic framework that would extend ontological constraints to output validation and ontology evolution. Our empirical evaluation across 600 experimental runs spanning five regulated industries— including two Vietnamese-language domains—provides strong evidence for the value of ontologi-cal grounding. Ontology-coupled agents significantly outperformed ungrounded agents on Metric Accuracy ( p < . 001 , W = .460 ), Regulatory Compliance ( p = .003 , W = .318 ), and Role Consis-tency ( p < . 001 , W = .614 ), with large effect sizes. Crucially, the improvement is greatest where LLM parametric knowledge is weakest: Vietnamese industries showed 2 × the improvement of English-language domains ( ∆ = + .29 vs. +.12 ), confirming that ontological grounding becomes mandatory—not merely beneficial—for non-English enterprise domains where LLM parametric coverage is structurally insufficient. The architecture is implemented in a production system serving 21 industry verticals with 650+ specialized agents, demonstrating that the three-layer ontological framework is both industry-invariant (in structure) and industry-specific (in content). Our proposed six-level maturity model provides a roadmap for the field, from ungrounded LLM deployment (L0) to full closed-loop neu-rosymbolic integration (L5). Future work will focus on three areas: 18 1. Implementing L4 validation : Building and evaluating the proposed OntologyValidator 

component, with particular focus on latency-quality trade-offs in interactive settings. 2. Adaptive context injection : Investigating strategies that selectively inject ontological context based on estimated LLM parametric knowledge, addressing the TF regression observed when injecting well-known domain concepts. 3. Full-scale evaluation : Expanding the pilot study with human expert validation (inter-rater reliability against LLM judge), noisy RAG baselines, and additional industry verticals to strengthen external validity. We believe neurosymbolic architectures are not merely beneficial but necessary for enterprise AI agents operating in regulated industries. The question is not whether to integrate symbolic and neural reasoning, but how to do so with minimal latency, maximal coverage, and provable guarantees.
