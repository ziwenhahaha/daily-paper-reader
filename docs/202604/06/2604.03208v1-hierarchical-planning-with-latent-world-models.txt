Model predictive control (MPC) with learned world models has emerged as a promising paradigm for embodied control, particularly for its ability to generalize zero-shot when deployed in new environments. However, learned world models often struggle with long-horizon control due to the accumulation of prediction errors and the exponentially growing search space. In this work, we address these challenges by learning latent world models at multiple temporal scales and performing hierarchical planning across these scales, enabling long-horizon reasoning while substantially reducing inference-time planning complexity. Our approach serves as a plug-in planning abstraction that applies across diverse latent world-model architectures and domains. We demonstrate that this hierarchical approach enables zero-shot control on real-world non-greedy robotic tasks, achieving a 70% success rate on pick-&-place using only a final goal specification, compared to 0% for a single-level world model. In addition, across physics-based simulated environments including push manipulation and maze navigation, hierarchical planning achieves higher success while requiring up to 3× less planning-time compute.

# 1 Introduction 

Planning with learned world models has emerged as a powerful paradigm for embodied decision making. By learning predictive models of environment dynamics from experience and using them to simulate future outcomes, world models enable agents to reason about the long-term consequences of actions (Sutton, 1981; Deisenroth and Rasmussen, 2011). Recent advances in representation learning and latent-space modeling have improved the scalability of this approach, making it possible to train world models directly from high-dimensional observations such as images and videos (Ha and Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2023; Hansen et al., 2023). More recently, self-supervised learning has enabled training world models from large collections of unlabeled, task-agnostic offline trajectories, allowing zero-shot deployment in downstream robotic control and navigation (Assran et al., 2025; Sobal et al., 2025; Bar et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2024; Terver et al., 2026b). Despite this progress, learned world models continue to struggle with long-horizon control due to two fundamental challenges: (i) prediction errors compound over time, leading to increasingly inaccurate rollouts (Talvitie, 2014; Janner et al., 2019), and (ii) even under a perfect model, planning becomes computationally expensive as the search space grows exponentially with horizon (Ichter et al., 2020). A classic approach to long-horizon control is to introduce hierarchy into the decision-making structure. By reasoning at multiple temporal scales, agents can plan coarsely over long horizons while retaining fine-grained control at short horizons. This intuition underlies a large body of work on temporal abstraction in reinforcement learning, including options, skills, and hierarchical policies (Sutton et al., 1999; Bacon et al., 2017; Hafner et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023), as well as hierarchical world models that capture dynamics at multiple timescales (Gumbsch et al., 2023; Chitnis et al., 2024; Schiewer et al., 2024; Hansen et al., 2024). In parallel, the optimal control literature has explored hierarchical model predictive control (MPC) for multi-task settings (Fang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021; Kögel et al., 2025). However, these approaches remain limited in complementary ways. Reinforcement learning methods typically rely on task-specific rewards, which restrict their applicability in zero-shot settings; even goal-conditioned and zero-shot RL methods (Park et al., 2023, 2024b) still struggle 1

Conversely, existing hierarchical MPC methods are not task-specific but are largely restricted to low-dimensional state spaces, hand-engineered representations, or known dynamics, limiting their applicability to high-dimensional observation spaces such as raw pixels. As a result, it remains unclear how to realize hierarchical planning for zero-shot control directly on learned world models from high-dimensional observations. In this work, we propose Hierarchical Planning with Latent World Models (HWM), a framework for zero-shot hierarchical model predictive control using learned latent world models. Rather than learning hierarchical policies, HWM introduces temporal hierarchy directly at inference time by planning over multiple timescales on task-agnostic world models trained via next-latent prediction. Crucially, our approach is defined by how the hierarchical levels interact. HWM learns world models operating at different temporal resolutions within a shared latent space, enabling a direct coupling between levels: high-level predictions act as subgoals for low-level MPC via latent-state matching. This removes the need for inverse models, skill learning, or goal-conditioned policies, and allows hierarchical planning to operate entirely on pretrained world models. To support efficient long-horizon planning, we further introduce a learned action encoder that compresses sequences of primitive actions between waypoint states into latent macro-actions. This reduces the dimensionality of the high-level search space and makes planning more tractable. We summarize our contributions as follows: 

• We introduce HWM, a hierarchical MPC framework that couples multiple learned world models via a shared latent space, enabling direct subgoal transfer across levels without hierarchical policies, skill learning, or task-specific rewards. 

• We show that HWM unlocks a new capability for zero-shot world-model planning: solving non-greedy real-robot tasks from visual inputs, where success requires temporarily moving away from the goal. HWM achieves 70% success on Franka pick-&-place from a single goal image, compared to 0% for VJEPA2-AC, and also outperforms strong vision-language-action models trained on ∼ 77 × more robotic interaction data. 

• We demonstrate that HWM is a modular, model-agnostic planning abstraction, consistently improving zero-shot performance across diverse latent world models, including VJEPA2-AC (robot manipulation), DINO-WM (push manipulation), and PLDM (maze navigation). 

• We show that hierarchical planning improves both performance and efficiency on long-horizon tasks, achieving substantial success-rate gains (up to +44% and +39% ) while reducing inference-time planning cost by up to 4× compared to single-level planners. 

# 2 Hierarchical Planning with Latent World Models 

2.1 Problem Setting 

We consider a Markov decision process (MDP) M = ( S, A, μ, p ), where S is the state space, A is the action space, μ ∈ P (S) is the initial state distribution, and p : S × A → S denotes the transition dynamics. We work in the offline setting with access to a dataset D of state–action trajectories τ = ( s1, a 1, s 2, . . . , a T −1, s T ).Our objective is to use D to enable zero-shot control across a variety of goal-conditioned tasks. Rather than learning an explicit parametric policy, we learn world models to approximate the true transition dynamics p,and select actions at inference time via model predictive control (MPC), which induces a goal-conditioned policy. We consider goal-reaching tasks at evaluation time, where the agent is provided with a goal state sg

and succeeds by reaching it. In this setting, the task space Z coincides with the state space S.

2.2 Latent World Models 

A world model is a learned dynamical model that approximates how an environment evolves in response to an agent’s actions, estimating transition dynamics of the form p(st+1 | st, a t). In high-dimensional settings, world models are typically learned in a latent space to improve efficiency and robustness. An encoder maps 3observations st to latent states zt, and a latent world model predicts future latents conditioned on actions, 

p(zt+1 | zt, a t). Recent work has shown that latent world models learned from reward-free, task-agnostic offline data can support zero-shot, goal-conditioned control in downstream planning tasks (Assran et al., 2025; Sobal et al., 2025; Bar et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2024; Terver et al., 2026b). However, planning directly with such models using flat MPC becomes increasingly brittle as task horizons grow, due to compounding prediction errors and the expanding search space. 

2.3 Top-Down Hierarchical Planning 

Latent world models naturally support hierarchical planning, as different levels of abstraction can reason over different temporal horizons within a shared predictive framework. By planning at an appropriate temporal resolution, hierarchical methods avoid direct optimization over long sequences of primitive actions while retaining fine-grained control. In this work, we adopt a top-down hierarchical planning strategy that operates entirely in a learned latent space (Fig. 2). At a high level, the agent plans abstract latent actions toward a final goal using a long-horizon world model. The resulting predicted latent states serve as intermediate subgoals. A low-level planner then optimizes short-horizon sequences of primitive actions to reach the next subgoal, repeating this process in a receding-horizon MPC manner. We now describe the latent representations and world models used by the hierarchical planner. Let E denote an encoder that maps observations st to latent states zt = E(st). We assume access to two learned latent world models operating at different temporal resolutions: (i) a low-level model P (1) (zt+1 | zt, a t) that predicts short-horizon transitions conditioned on primitive actions, and (ii) a high-level model P (2) (zt+h | zt, l t) that predicts long-horizon transitions conditioned on latent macro-actions lt.The macro-actions lt are produced by a learned action encoder that summarizes temporally extended sequences of low-level actions. Unlike fixed-stride temporal abstraction methods, we do not assume a fixed high-level horizon h, allowing each high-level transition to correspond to a variable-length segment of low-level execution.

# 3 Empirical Evaluation of Hierarchical Planning Across World Models and Tasks 

We evaluate whether hierarchical planning (HWM) enables zero-shot long-horizon control in regimes where planning with a single-level learned world model fails. We apply HWM to three representative latent world-model architectures—VJEPA2-AC (Assran et al., 2025), PLDM (Sobal et al., 2025), and DINO-WM (Zhou et al., 2024)—and, for each, consider a benchmark domain from the original work while substantially increasing task horizon and complexity. For VJEPA2-AC, we study real-world pick-&-place and drawer manipulation on a Franka robot arm without explicit subgoals. For DINO-WM, we consider the Push-T task with task horizons extended from 25 to 75 timesteps. For PLDM, we evaluate point-maze navigation with train–test layout mismatch while quadrupling map size. We summarize our main findings as follows: 1. Zero-shot non-greedy control: Pick-&-place success improves from 0% to 70% on Franka with VJEPA2-AC. 2. Long-horizon reasoning: Push-T success increases from 17% to 61% with DINO-WM. 3. OOD generalization: HWM improves PLDM performance on navigation in larger, unseen mazes. 4. Compute efficiency: Comparable or better planning relative to single-level planners with 3× less compute.

> Data.

World models are trained on approximately 130 hours of unlabeled real-robot manipulation data from DROID (Khazatsky et al., 2024) and RoboSet (Kumar et al., 2023). Observations include RGB images and end-effector proprioception; actions correspond to end-effector delta poses (see appendix B.2 for details).  

> Hierarchical Planning Setup.

The agent is provided with the current and goal observations, each specified by RGB images and end-effector poses. Agent’s actions are end-effector delta poses that are mapped to joints delta poses via inverse kinematics. For each action, the corresponding joints delta is executed in a blocking fashion. The high-level planner optimizes over latent actions of dimension 4, using 3000 CEM samples with a prediction horizon of 2, while the low-level planner uses 800 CEM samples with a prediction horizon of 2. The agent replans every k = 1 step in standard MPC manner. Planning rollouts are executed in parallel across GPUs, making the approach computationally efficient even with large numbers of trajectory samples. Full CEM hyperparameters are provided in appendix C.  

> World Model Architectures.

We follow the VJEPA2-AC training recipe for the low-level world model (see appendix B.2). To train the high-level world model, We use N = 3 waypoint states sampled from trajectory segments spanning up to 4 seconds, with the middle waypoint chosen uniformly at random. Latent actions are encoded from the intervening low-level actions using a transformer-based action encoder, with the CLS token projected to the latent action space. For visualization, we decode latent representations into RGB images using a ViT decoder; these reconstructions are used solely for qualitative analysis and are not involved in planning or training (details in appendix E).

We conduct ablation experiments to analyze factors that influence hierarchical planning performance. All models in this section use a VJEPA2 backbone trained on real-world robotic datasets (training details in section 3.1). 

4.1 Do latent actions help with high level planning? 

We examine whether training the high-level world model benefits from encoding the full low-level action subsequence between waypoint states, as opposed to representing high-level actions solely as the net end-effector displacement between waypoints. We do not consider using raw action sequences directly, as this would resemble flat planning and would not reduce the action search space for planning.

As shown in table 4, compared to a high-level world model trained with delta-pose actions, a model trained with latent actions produces plans that more closely align with expert high-level behavior (see ap-pendix I for evaluation protocol). This improvement arises because waypoint states may be separated by long temporal windows, during which the underlying action sequence can be highly non-greedy (e.g., first moving upward, then downward). A delta-pose rep-resentation collapses such trajectories into a single displacement, discarding information critical for predicting feasible transitions, whereas a learned latent action can compactly encode the structure of the full action sequence.

# 5 Related Works  

> World Models and Planning.

Learning predictive models of environment dynamics is a core idea in model-based reinforcement learning (Sutton, 1981), with early work focusing on state-space dynamics for planning and policy optimization (Deisenroth and Rasmussen, 2011; Sutton, 1991). More recent approaches learn dynamics from high-dimensional observations, either directly in pixel space or in a learned latent space (Ebert et al., 2017; Ha and Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2019; Schwarzer et al., 2020; Hafner et al., 2023; Watter et al., 2015; Nair et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Bagatella et al., 2026), enabling efficient learning and planning from pixels in both simulation and real-world robotics. Recent work further demonstrates that task-agnostic generative world models trained on large-scale, real-world video can produce realistic and diverse simulations of physical environments (Hu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Bruce et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2025), highlighting the potential of scaling predictive modeling beyond task-specific datasets. Closely related to our setting, a line of work on latent world models shows that models trained on reward-free offline data via next-latent prediction can generalize zero-shot to downstream control tasks. These approaches leverage the learned dynamics to simulate action outcomes and select actions through cost-based optimization, without requiring task-specific policy training (Assran et al., 2025; Sobal et al., 2025; Terver et al., 2026b; Zhou et al., 2024).  

> Limitations of Long Horizon Planning.

Learned world models remain brittle when used for long-horizon planning due to compounding prediction errors (Talvitie, 2014; Janner et al., 2019). Moreover, long-horizon planning is challenging even under a perfect model because the search tree grows exponentially with the branching factor 10 and horizon (a manifestation of the curse of horizon) (Ichter et al., 2020).  

> Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning.

To address long-horizon control, prior work has explored hierarchical reinforcement learning through temporal abstraction via options and skills (Sutton et al., 1999; Bacon et al., 2017; Hafner et al., 2022). In parallel, many model-based RL approaches incorporate hierarchical world models to capture dynamics at multiple temporal resolutions (Gumbsch et al., 2023; Chitnis et al., 2024; Schiewer et al., 2024; Hansen et al., 2024). While these methods enable improved long-horizon reasoning, they typically rely on task-specific policy learning, which limits their applicability to zero-shot and general-purpose control.  

> Hierarchical Model Predictive Control.

In the optimal control literature, hierarchical model predictive control (MPC) has been explored for long-horizon, multi-task settings (Fang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021; Kögel et al., 2025). However, existing hierarchical MPC approaches are largely restricted to low-dimensional state spaces, hand-engineered representations, or known dynamics, limiting their applicability to high-dimensional observation spaces such as raw pixels. We provide a more detailed comparison of these approaches, including their relationship to our method and a summary across key design axes, in appendix A. 

# 6 Conclusion 

We presented HWM, a hierarchical planning framework that performs model predictive control over learned latent dynamics at multiple temporal scales. By decoupling long-horizon reasoning from fine-grained action execution, HWM mitigates compounding prediction errors and the exponentially growing search spaces that limit single-level world-model-based planners. Across diverse world-model architectures, hierarchical planning consistently outperforms flat planners on non-greedy, long-horizon tasks while substantially improving inference-time efficiency. Despite these gains, long-horizon manipulation remains challenging, and performance degrades for all methods as horizons increase. Addressing this limitation will likely require advances in representation learning, including more abstract representations for higher-level world models; uncertainty-aware planning to better handle the increased stochasticity and model uncertainty inherent to long-horizon real-world prediction; and improved hierarchical planning algorithms that allow feedback and interaction across levels of the hierarchy instead of strictly top-down decomposition. We view HWM as a step toward scalable, general-purpose planning with learned world models, and hope this work encourages future research on hierarchical abstractions for long-horizon decision making in complex environments. 11 References

> Hierarchical Model Predictive Control methods

B.2 Franka Arm with Robotiq Gripper  

> Data.

We use approximately 96 hours of unlabeled videos from the DROID dataset (Khazatsky et al., 2024) and 30 hours from RoboSet (Kumar et al., 2023). Both datasets consist of short video clips of manipulation trajectories collected from real robots. Observations include RGB images and end-effector proprioception; actions correspond to end-effector delta poses.  

> State, Actions, and Preprocessing.

Videos are sampled at a spatial resolution of 256 × 256 and with a frame rate uniformly sampled from (3 , 10) FPS for DROID and (1 , 5) FPS for RoboSet. At timestep k, the state is defined as sk = ( xk, p k), where xk is the raw RGB observation and pk ∈ R7 is the end-effector state expressed in the robot base frame. The first three dimensions of pk correspond to Cartesian position, the next three correspond to orientation represented as extrinsic Euler angles, and the final dimension represents the gripper state. Actions are defined as differences in end-effector state between consecutive frames: ak = pk+1 − pk. We apply random resize-and-crop augmentations to video clips, with the aspect ratio sampled uniformly from 

(0 .75 , 1.33) . 

> Low Level World Model.

Following the setup of VJEPA2-AC (Assran et al., 2025), we sample fixed-length trajectories of T = 15 timesteps, {(ak, p k, x k)}k∈15 . Each image xk is encoded using the pretrained, frozen ViT-g/16 backbone, producing latent feature maps (zk)k∈15 . The world model is instantiated as a ∼300M-parameter ViT. It takes temporally interleaved sequences of latent features, end-effector states, and actions 

{(zk, p k, a k)}k∈15 and predicts the next-step latent representations {ˆzk+1 }k∈15 . We refer to the VJEPA2 paper for full architectural details. 17 High-Level World Model. To train the high-level world model, we sample variable-length trajectory segments 

{(ak, p k, x k)}k∈T spanning (0 .33 , 4) seconds and select N = 3 waypoint indices per segment, with the middle waypoint sampled uniformly at random. The high-level world model shares the same ViT architecture as the low-level model, but is conditioned on latent macro-actions rather than primitive actions. Conditioned on the current and previous waypoints and latent actions, it is trained via teacher forcing to predict the next waypoint. Latent macro-actions are produced by a transformer-based action encoder Aψ , where the CLS token is passed through an MLP head to obtain the latent action.

B.3 Push-T  

> Data.

We use the offline dataset from DINO-WM, which consists of 18500 trajectories. Each trajectory consists of a sequence of (xt, a t, p t), where xt is an RGB observation, at is a 2D action vector, and pt is the proprioceptive state denoting current agent position and velocity.  

> Low Level World Model.

We use DINO-WM (Zhou et al., 2024) as our low-level world model. The encoder is a pretrained DINOv2 network (Oquab et al., 2023), which is kept frozen during training. The world model is a 25M params ViT with causal masking that attends to past actions and latent states (ak, z k)k∈T over a context window of T = 5 . Each transition k → k + 1 corresponds to a stride of 5 frames in the original dataset. As a result, DINO-WM models over an effective horizon of 5T = 25 timesteps in the original trajectory. The action ak is formed by concatenating the 5 primitive actions executed between k and k + 1 , yielding a 5 × 2 = 10 –dimensional action vector. Causal attention is implemented at the patch level: each latent patch vector zik attends only to its corresponding patches zik−T :k−1 from previous timesteps. Actions are first embedded using an MLP and then concatenated to each patch vector zik. Proprioceptive inputs are incorporated using the same embedding and concatenation scheme. We refer to the original DINO-WM paper for full architectural details.  

> High-Level World Model.

To construct training sequences, we subsample trajectory segments with lengths uniformly drawn between 25 and 70 timesteps. From each segment, we sample N = 5 waypoint states, which define the high-level transitions. The high-level world model is a scaled-up variant of the low-level world model (25M → 75M parameters), obtained by increasing the number of layers from 6 to 10, the embedding dimension from 384 to 768, and the MLP dimension from 2048 to 3072, while reducing the number of attention heads from 16 to 12. This increased capacity enables more effective modeling of long-horizon dynamics. Latent macro-actions are produced by a transformer-based action encoder Aψ , where the CLS token is passed through an MLP head to obtain the latent action representation. See table 7 for hyperparameters.

B.4 Diverse Maze  

> Environment.

The environment is a variant of Mujoco PointMaze (Todorov et al., 2012), which contains a point mass agent with a 4D state vector ( global x, global y, v x, v y ), where v is the agent velocity. The model receives a top down view of the maze rendered as (98 , 98 , 3) RGB image tensor as input, instead of relying on 18 (global x, global ) directly. Mujoco PointMaze allows for customization of the maze layout via a grid structure, where a grid cell can either be a wall or space. We opt for a 8 × 8 grid (excluding outer wall). Maze layouts are generated randomly. Only the following constraints are enforced: 1) all the space cells are interconnected, 2) percentage of space cells range from 50% to 80% . 

> Data.

Each episode is initialized by placing the agent at a random (x, y ) location in the maze and sampling an initial velocity (vx, v y ). A trajectory is then generated by randomly sampling and executing actions for 100 steps, using an action repeat of 4. We collect 2,000 episodes per map and use 25 maps for training; resulting in a total of 5M transitions.  

> Low Level World Model.

We train the low-level world model following the PLDM recipe (Sobal et al., 2025), jointly learning the encoder and world model from scratch using offline trajectories. Each training sample consists of a sequence of 15 timesteps of observations and actions subsampled from a trajectory. The observation sequence is encoded by a convolutional network into spatial latent features z1:15 . Conditioned on the initial latent z1 and the action sequence a1:15 , a convolutional world model autoregressively predicts the future latent sequence ˆz1:15 . To prevent representational collapse, we apply VICReg (Bardes et al., 2021) to the latent features z1:15 , following the original PLDM formulation. Refer to table 8 for hyperparameters. We use the same PLDM architecture for Diverse Maze, comprising a lightweight convolutional encoder (33k params) and predictor (20k params); we refer to the original paper for full details.  

> High-Level World Model.

To construct training sequences, we subsample 60 timesteps from each trajectory and extract 6 waypoint states using a fixed stride of 10 timesteps. Primitive action chunks are encoded into an 8-dimensional latent action via an MLP. We reuse the backbone of a pretrained low-level world model and keep it frozen, training only the high-level world model. The high-level model is a higher-capacity convolutional network, enabling long-horizon prediction. Conditioned on the initial waypoint latent z1 and the latent action sequence l1:6 , it autoregressively predicts subsequent waypoint latents. # C Planning Details 

For Franka and Push-T, we use the Cross-Entropy Method (CEM) (Rubinstein and Kroese, 2004) for trajectory optimization in continuous action spaces. At each planning step, CEM maintains a factorized Gaussian over action sequences, iteratively sampling trajectories, evaluating them with the world model, and refitting to elite samples by updating the mean and variance. To improve stability and avoid premature collapse, variance updates are smoothed with an exponential moving average. After a fixed number of iterations, the optimized mean action sequence is executed, and planning proceeds in a receding-horizon manner. See table 9 and table 10 for the single-level and hierarchical planner parameters, respectively. For Diverse Maze, we use Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) (Williams et al., 2017) for planning. MPPI updates a nominal action sequence by importance-weighting sampled trajectories using exponential costs; unlike CEM, it applies soft weights over all samples, yielding smoother updates without hard elite selection. We use the MPPI implementation from PLDM 1. See table 11 and table 12 for the single-level and hierarchical planner hyperparameters, respectively.

# E Visualizing World Model Predictions 

Franka Arm with Robotiq Gripper. To reconstruct RGB images from latent representations, we train on DROID a ViT decoder that maps the frozen V-JEPA 2 encoder features back to pixel space. The decoder takes as input the encoded features of dimension 1408 (matching the V-JEPA 2 ViT-Giant encoder output) and projects them 21 to an intermediate dimension of 1024 via a linear layer. The architecture consists of 12 transformer blocks, each with 32 attention heads and an MLP ratio of 4, using GELU activations, LayerNorm and sinusoidal 2D positional embeddings. The decoder operates on a 32 × 32 grid of spatial tokens (corresponding to patch size 8 for 256 × 256 images). The model is trained using an L1 reconstruction loss on normalized pixel values. We train the decoder for 100 epochs on the DROID dataset using the AdamW optimizer with a peak learning rate of 5 × 10 −4, linear warmup over 2 epochs, cosine decay to 10 −6, and weight decay of 0.1. Training is performed on 8 nodes with a total batch size of 512.  

> Push-T.

24 I Offline Planning Evaluation for Franka 

Since planning on a real robot is time-consuming and not parallelizable, we introduce a quantitative offline planning evaluation that enables faster iteration over model variants and planning hyperparameters. We evaluate on the same 10 pick-&-place tasks used in the interactive experiments, with manually defined subgoals corresponding to the grasp point. Expert actions are defined as the robot’s delta pose from the initial observation to the subgoal, ˜a1, and from the subgoal to the goal, ˜a2. Conditioned on the initial and goal observations, the hierarchical planner produces a sequence of subgoals along with a sequence of primitive actions (a∗

> 1: h

) toward the first subgoal. To assess high-level planning quality, we compare the aggregate inferred action Phi=1 a∗

with the expert action ˜a1 using cosine similarity and ℓ1 distance. High cosine similarity or low 

ℓ1 distance indicates that the planner has identified a plan that moves toward the non-greedy subgoal, which strongly correlates with real-world task success. 

# J Baselines 

J.1 Vision-Language-Action Models 

For our baselines, we reproduce the same setup as in the Droid platform. Specifically, we use a Franka Panda arm with a bi-finger Robotiq gripper, dual camera setup (one wrist camera plus one side camera looking at the robot from the left). Our wrist camera setup is identical to Droid (the same mount and model - ZED Mini), however, for the side camera, we use Intel RealSense. While RealSense has a different aspect ratio (4:3 instead of ZED Mini’s 16:9) we found that simply padding the side image to get 16:9 aspect ratio yields good success rate for VLAs trained on Droid with this aspect ratio. Note that we use the same RealSense side camera for all baselines and world model-based agents therefore, they all have access to the same amount of sensor signal from the side view. In fact, the π- family of models receive extra sensor information since they have access to wrist camera which is not used by Octo and world model-based agents. 

π0-FAST-DROID and π0.5-DROID (Pertsch et al., 2025; Intelligence et al., 2025). For a reliable control with the 

πx-DROID models family, we reproduce the same controller on our Franka as in the Droid benchmark. Both models use one side camera and one wrist camera and output chunks of actions being joint velocities and target gripper positions. For both models, we execute the entire chunk of outputted actions before re-querying the policy ( 15 actions for π0.5 and 8 actions for π0-FAST). We run both policies for 300 total actions as we found this to be enough for a successfull task attempt with a big space for suboptimality. We do not finetune these policies, therefore, the evaluation is zero-shot and out-of-distribution since they are only finetuned on Droid so our scene is new to both of them. Since π- models use text goal specification, we tuned text prompts to achieve the highest success rate. We arrived at the same conclusions as in the RoboArena (Atreya et al., 2025) benchmark, specifically, π- models do not understand colors and complex nouns for shapes (e.g. platform, shelf etc) and each prompt has to be written in a short and a simple way. Below are the prompts we tried (either “cup” or only “box” is present in the prompt): 1. “put the cup on the plane” 2. “put the cup (box) on the platform” 3. “put the cup (box) on the table” – the model was picking the object up and putting it on the the same position since the “ground level” surface is a table despite a small table was in front of it (See Figure 11). 4. “put the cup (box) on the yellow table” – same as 2. 5. “put the cup on the wooden table” – same as 2. 6. “put the cup on the shelf” – the policy tried to place the object on the shelf in the background. 7. “put the cup on the plate” – this prompt worked. Since it requires a plate, we started to use the one as the marker for the goal position for the object placement. We believe makes language goals a good 

25 substitution for image goals as the plate position unambiguously informs the model where the object should be placed and the plate is present in many training episodes. 

We run π0.5-DROID for 100 trials for the pick-&-place task with the cup and the pick-&-place task with the box. We use the same 10 task configurations as the ones present in Figure 11 to ensure fair comparison. This means we run 10 trials for each configuration which gives a low variance for the policy with highly stochastic outputs. Similarly, we run 100 trials for the pick-&-place task with the cup and 50 trials for the pick-&-place task with the box (i.e. 5 trials per configuration). Similarly to the evaluation of the world models and policies, we use binary success rate for evaluations. In particular, we simply give the policy a score of 1 if the object was placed in the target position and zero otherwise. Unfortunately, we were unable to run drawer tasks with π- models because during our evaluation of the drawer task with Octo, the drawer was physically destroyed by the robot into a non-operating state. Note that because of language goals (which can only be primitive), π- models cannot run the task with subgoals.

J.2 Goal-Conditioned & Zero-Shot RL Methods  

> GCIQL

(Park et al., 2023) – goal-conditioned version of Implicit Q-Learning (Kostrikov et al., 2021), a strong and widely-used method for offline RL.  

> HIQL

(Park et al., 2023) – a hierarchical GCRL method which trains two policies: one to generate subgoals, and another one to reach the subgoals. Notably, both policies use the same value function.  

> HILP

(Park et al., 2024b) – a method that learns state representations from the offline data such that the distance in the learned representation space is proportional to the number of steps between two states. A direction-conditioned policy is then learned to be able to move along any specified direction in the latent space. For GCIQL and HIQL, we use the implementations from the repository 2 of OGBench (Park et al., 2024a). Likewise, for HILP we use the official implementation 3 from its authors. For Push-T and Diverse Maze, we tune hyperparameters independently for each difficulty setting and report results using the optimal configurations.
