Abstract. While Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) demonstrate im-pressive visual perception, they remain epistemically constrained by their static parametric knowledge. To transcend these boundaries, multimodal search models have been adopted to actively interact with the external environment for evidence retrieval. Diverging from prevailing paradigms that merely retrofit general LMMs with search tools as modular exten-sions, we explore the potential of building a multimodal agentic search model from scratch . Specifically, we make the following contributions: (i) we introduce Agentic Seeding , a dedicated phase designed to weave the foundational precursors necessary for eliciting agentic behaviors; (ii) we uncover a performance bottleneck in long-horizon interactions, where the increasing volume of interaction history overwhelms the model’s abil-ity to locate ground-truth evidence. To mitigate this, we propose V-Fold , an adaptive history-aware compression scheme that preserves re-cent dialogue turns in high fidelity while folding historical context into the visual space via rendering; and (iii) we develop POINTS-Seeker-8B , a state-of-the-art multimodal agentic search model that consistently outperforms existing models across six diverse benchmarks, effectively resolving the challenges of long-horizon, knowledge-intensive visual rea-soning. ## 1 Introduction 

Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) [1, 14, 27, 28, 41] have fundamentally trans-formed the landscape of artificial intelligence, demonstrating impressive profi-ciency in static perception and reasoning. However, a critical epistemological gap remains: their internal knowledge is frozen upon training, rendering them prone to hallucinations when addressing real-time queries. To bridge this gap, the community is shifting towards agentic search, empowering LMMs to au-tonomously perceive, query, and synthesize information from the web environ-ment [29, 32, 49, 55] to solve complex visual question answering (VQA) tasks.

Currently, the prevailing paradigm [17, 32, 49, 61] for developing multimodal search agents predominantly relies on the post-training of general-purpose LMMs (e.g. , Qwen3-VL), typically through specialized supervised fine-tuning (SFT) or reinforcement learning (RL). While these methods effectively teach models to follow tool-calling protocols, they often treat agentic behavior as a superficial “task layer” rather than a fundamental cognitive substrate. In this paper, we break away from this post-hoc fine-tuning convention and explore the potential of building a multimodal search agent from scratch . We argue that embedding agentic behaviors as a core design principle, rather than adapting a generalist model, paves a more promising path toward robust autonomous inquiry. To realize this vision of a natively agentic model, we introduce Agentic Seeding , a critical phase that follows the pre-training stage and is designed to weave reasoning and planning capabilities directly into the model’s core repre-sentations. Unlike conventional pre-training that focuses on static knowledge, Agentic Seeding injects dense, long-horizon reasoning and ReAct-style [56] tra-jectories into the model’s learning curriculum. By exposing the model to these dynamic interaction patterns during its formative stages, we effectively “seed” the intrinsic capability for autonomous search. This allows our model to internalize the logic of active inquiry as a fundamental faculty rather than a superficial skill, ensuring that its search actions are driven by a deeply informed understanding. However, autonomous search creates an inherent tension between information accumulation and reasoning efficacy. Multi-turn investigations inevitably inflate the interaction history, leading to “attention dilution”, where the model fails to pinpoint ground-truth evidence despite its presence in the context (Figure 3b). This phenomenon, akin to “Lost-in-the-Middle” effect [25], renders complex rea-soning prone to failure as sequences expand. Unlike traditional strategies, such as naive truncation or summarization [23, 58], which risk discarding such vital cues, we propose V-Fold , an adaptive history-aware folding mechanism that Abbreviated paper title 3

shifts the information burden from the text processing to visual perception. Specifically, when the interaction history reaches a complexity threshold, V-Fold employs a sliding-window strategy that preserves recent interactions as high-precision text while “folding” stale history into the visual space via rendering. This dual-representation ensures the model can still “see” the raw historical evi-dence through its native visual-language alignment without being overwhelmed by token redundancy. Remarkably, V-Fold is highly training-efficient; by incor-porating a mere 5k rendered trajectories during SFT, the model natively inter-nalizes the folding capability, enabling robust, out-of-the-box deployment. Consequently, we present POINTS-Seeker-8B . Extensive experiments across six diverse and challenging benchmarks, including MMSearch, MMSearch-Plus, FVQA, SimpleVQA, LiveVQA, and BrowseComp-VL, demonstrate that our model consistently achieves state-of-the-art performance, significantly outperforming existing agents that rely on post-hoc fine-tuning. This success validates that agentic seeding effectively internalizes autonomous agentic behavior as a native faculty, which, combined with our V-Fold mechanism, effectively resolves the “Lost in the Middle” challenge in long-horizon interaction. To summarize, we make the following contributions in this paper: (i) we ex-plore the potential of building a multimodal agentic search model from scratch 

and introduce Agentic Seeding , a critical phase that internalizes autonomous inquiry during the model’s formative stages; (ii) we propose V-Fold , an adaptive history-aware compression mechanism designed to facilitate robust long-horizon interaction; it preserves recent dialogue turns in high fidelity while folding his-torical context into the visual space via rendering; (iii) we develop POINTS-Seeker-8B , a state-of-the-art multimodal agentic search model that outper-forms existing post-trained methods across six challenging benchmarks, demon-strating the superior efficacy of our proposed framework. 

## 2 Related Work 

Retrieval-augmented generation. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) addresses the static nature of LLM parameters by grounding responses in ex-ternal, retrieved information [20, 21]. This paradigm has effectively extended to multimodal domains, particularly for knowledge-intensive visual question an-swering (VQA) tasks. By retrieving external knowledge, which encompasses text, images, and multimodal structured data, these methods can leverage the evi-dence from such sources to better address complex VQA problems [2,3,16,26,30, 36,53,59]. Despite their empirical success, conventional RAG methods face criti-cal limitations: they predominantly rely on static knowledge bases and typically decouple retrieval from generation, preventing effective end-to-end optimization. 

Autonomous web-search agents. Recent advancements have pivoted from static corpora to real-time web interaction, empowering models to access dy-namic information sources [8, 12, 17, 64]. Leading organizations like OpenAI, Google, and Perplexity have introduced deep research agents to advance this paradigm. Inspired by this success, the multimodal community has also sought to 4 F. Author et al. 

incorporate real-time evidence. Notable works [32, 45, 49] such as MMSearch-R1 and DeepMMSearch-R1 enhance the capabilities of search agents by incorporat-ing real-time, question-relevant evidence via end-to-end reinforcement learning. However, these methods predominantly rely on post-training general-purpose LMMs. In contrast, we explore the potential of building a multimodal agentic search model from scratch, specifically investigating whether injecting agentic-related data during the model’s formative stages yields superior performance. 

Agent memory. Continuous environment interaction often leads to context explosion, which can easily exceed the context window limitations. To address this, various memory management strategies [6, 46, 52] have been proposed. For example, MemAgent [58] proposes maintaining a fixed-length memory that is proactively and selectively updated by the model. AgentFold [57] introduces a folding mechanism to intelligently fold segments of context during task execution. More recently, cross-modal compression [10,35] has emerged as a potent alterna-tive. DeepSeek-OCR [47] proposes that images can serve as an effective medium for compression. Similarly, Glyph [4] advances a related idea by transforming the long-text modeling problem into a multiple image–text modeling paradigm. Fur-thermore, AgentOCR [10] proposes a self-compression mechanism, whereby the model autonomously determines the compression ratio. However, in the domain of multimodal agentic search, limited attention has been devoted to memory management. In this paper, we extend the render-text-to-image paradigm to investigate the role of memory in multimodal agentic search.

3.1 Problem Formulation 

We consider the task of tackling complex visual question answering in a real-world web environment. Given a query ( q), which typically consists of a textual question and an image, the policy model ( Mθ ) determines the appropriate ac-tion at each interaction turn. Specifically, it may either invoke an external tool to acquire additional information or generate the final answer directly. The in-teraction process terminates once the final answer is produced. We define the core components as follows: 

• Action ( at). The atomic operation executed at step t. An action may either invoke an external tool (as listed in Section 4.1) or generate the final answer.

• Answer ( y). The final response is generated upon collecting sufficient evi-dence or reaching the maximum interaction limit.

3.2 Agentic Seeding 

Before the dedicated agentic training, the model undergoes a standard pre-training stage phase [22] to establish foundational visual-language understanding capabilities (see supplementary material for details). To internalize agentic ca-pabilities during the model’s formative learning stages, we introduce Agentic Seeding , an innate mid-training phase specifically designed to strengthen the model’s fundamental reasoning and planning abilities. The reasoning and planning capability of a model is crucial for addressing complex tasks, such as mathematics and coding, and becomes even more es-sential within the ReAct [56] framework. In the context of agentic search, a model’s ability to perform step-by-step reasoning and to extract critical infor-mation throughout long-horizon interactions is particularly important. For this purpose, we primarily employ three synergistic data categories: 

• Multi-modal reasoning data : High-quality VQA data featuring dense rea-soning traces to synchronize visual perception with complex logical deduction. 

• Text reasoning data : A diverse collection of logic-intensive datasets, primar-ily centering on mathematical and coding corpora, that instill system-2 thinking. This curriculum provides the cognitive foundation for the model’s internal world model, facilitating complex planning across modalities [42, 48]. 

• Text agentic data with ReAct style : ReAct-style textual agent data that foster fundamental tool-use logic and planning strategies. By exposing the model to these interaction patterns early on, we facilitate the seamless transfer of agen-tic priors from the textual to the multimodal domain [42, 48]. This strategic intervention ensures that reasoning and planning are internal-ized as intrinsic properties, thereby equipping the model with the logical consis-tency and contextual robustness necessary for complex, long-horizon search.

VQA data construction. We curate high-quality VQA data by leveraging search-oriented VQA datasets ( i.e. , LiveVQA [11] and FVQA [49]) or converting complex multi-hop QA datasets [50]. Our automated conversion pipeline involves the following three key steps: (i) Entity extraction & selection: We use Claude-4-5-Sonnet to identify visually representable entities from questions. (ii) 

Query de-biasing: To ensure retrieval accuracy, we generate context-aware search queries ( e.g. , appending “New South Wales politician” to “Mike Baird”) to resolve potential ambiguities. (iii) VQA reformulation: The original question is rewritten to implicitly reference the target entity as “the person/object in the image.” Finally, we pair the reformulated question with an image retrieved via a search engine to form a coherent, search-intensive VQA instance. 

Trajectory generation and filtering. After obtaining the ( image, question, answer ) triplets, we synthesize structured trajectory data consists of sequen-tial <think> , <tool_call> , <tool_response> , and ultimately <answer> seg-ments. Specifically, Claude-Sonnet-4-5 is employed to generate trajectories in the correct format with explicit tool-call annotations. When the model produces a <tool_call> , the corresponding tool is invoked to return the appropriate 

<tool_response> . This response is then appended to the context to form a new query, which is subsequently used to prompt Claude-Sonnet-4-5 again, iteratively constructing the complete trajectory. Finally, we employ Qwen3-235B-A22B as an LLM judge. Given the question, the predicted answer, and the ground-truth answer, it evaluates the correctness of the prediction, and only trajectories with correct answers are retained in the final dataset. Ultimately, this process yields 5,439 high-fidelity trajectories for SFT and 5,339 samples for RL training. Abbreviated paper title 7

3.5 Reinforcement Learning 

Following the SFT stage, the model acquires basic tool-use capabilities but still faces challenges such as redundant tool calls and inconsistent formatting. To address these, we employ reinforcement learning (RL) with outcome-level su-pervision, specifically adopting a tool-augmented variant of GRPO [13]. This approach bypasses the potential noise in trajectory-level annotations and fosters greater exploration, encouraging the model to solve complex VQA tasks through verified tool interactions while mitigating hallucinations. 

Rollouts. During this process, the policy model engages with image search, web search, and visit tools, utilizing pre-defined tool schemas. The responses from these tools are incorporated into subsequent interactions until either (i) a final response is produced or (ii) the maximum number of turns is reached ( e.g . 10 in this paper). For the RL loss, only the tokens belonging to the model’s response are retained, while user prompts and tool responses are masked out. 

Reward. The RL training employs a multi-component reward function to opti-mize both reasoning quality and interaction integrity: 

• Accuracy ( Sacc ): A correctness score {0, 1} assigned by Qwen3-235B-A22B 

by comparing the model’s output with the ground truth. 

• Format ( Sformat ): A binary reward ensuring reasoning and final answers are correctly enclosed within <think> and <answer> tags, respectively. 

• Tool-call ( Stool ): A binary score verifying if tool invocations adhere to the predefined schema and are properly wrapped in <tool_call> tags. 3.6 V-Fold: Adaptive History-aware Visual Compression The bottleneck of long sequences. After completing the aforementioned training stages, our model demonstrates strong agentic search capabilities. How-ever, during a more in-depth analysis, we observe that its performance declines significantly when the response length is relatively long, compared with cases where the response length is shorter. As illustrated in Figure 3a, we validate this phenomenon across multiple benchmarks. This performance degradation moti-vates us to introduce an adaptive compression mechanism to reduce the textual context length while maintaining the model’s overall reasoning efficacy. 

History-aware compression. Although reducing sequence length is desirable, naive truncation or aggressive summarization risks losing vital cues. As shown in Figure 3b, the ground-truth evidence is often already present in the interaction history of failure cases, indicating that the model suffers from attention dilution rather than retrieval failure. ## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup Training datasets. For the training dataset, the text reasoning data is sourced from AM-DeepSeek-R1-Distilled-1.4M [62], while the text agentic data is ob-tained from Toucan-1.5M [51]. The multi-modal reasoning datasets are derived from Reason-RFT [39] and VisualWebInstruct [18]. For multi-modal datasets, we utilize only the images and questions, and then distill the reasoning chain using Qwen3-VL-235B-A22B-Thinking , filtering based on the original answer. The dataset used for supervised fine-tuning (SFT) is drawn from LiveVQA and FVQA, supplemented with a portion of general-domain data from Honey-Data [60], whereas the reinforcement learning (RL) dataset consists of LiveVQA and our self-constructed VQA samples. Detailed information about these train-ing datasets is provided in the supplementary materials. 

Evaluation benchmarks. We evaluate our method on six challenging multi-modal benchmarks, including MMSearch [19], MMSearch-Plus [40], FVQA [49], SimpleVQA [5], LiveVQA [11], and BrowseComp-VL (BC-VL) [12]. Following [49], 10 F. Author et al. 

we keep only image-containing QA pairs for MMSearch. For SimpleVQA, we use all English samples, and for MMSearch-Plus, consistent with [17], we retain only single-image samples. For all other benchmarks, we use the full datasets. 

Tools. Our agent utilizes three primary tools: web search, image search, and visit. (i) For web search, we employ the Google Custom Search API or Serper API to obtain the titles, snippets, and URLs of the top-10 webpages relevant to the query. (ii) For image search, we first use the Serper API to retrieve the URLs of the top-5 webpages and then utilize Crawl4AI [43] to parse the webpage content. Finally, we invoke Qwen3-235B-A22B [54] to summarize the content related to the goal argument and return the generated summary. (iii) For the visit tool, we similarly use Crawl4AI to extract the relevant webpage content, followed by Qwen3-235B-A22B to produce a summary focused on the goal argument. 

Implementation details. POINTS-Seeker initializes its ViT and LLM with Qwen2-VL-ViT [44] and Qwen3-8B-Base [54]. For both Agentic Seeding and SFT, we employ a cosine learning rate scheduler with a warm-up phase [24]. During this warm-up period, the learning rate gradually increases to  5 × 10 −5

over the first 3% of iterations and then steadily decays to  1 × 10 −6. The global batch size is set to 64. Samples within a batch are flattened and concatenated into sequences, with a maximum length of 32k, to enhance training efficiency. For RL training, the clipping ratio parameters are set to 0.2 for the lower bound and 0.28 for the upper bound. We use a global batch size of 64 and perform 8 rollouts per sample. The learning rate is set at  1 × 10 −6, and a KL penalty of 0.001 is applied. Additionally, the λacc , λformat , and λtool are set to 0.7, 0.2, and 0.1, respectively. Further details are provided in the supplementary material.

4.3 Ablations and Analysis Ablation on our core design. To validate the effectiveness of our proposed training strategies and V-Fold, we conduct ablation experiments in Table 2, yielding several key observations. (i) SFT with high-quality trajectory data sub-stantially boosts basic agentic skills. In particular, the model becomes more accurate in tool invocation and achieves strong performance on relatively simple benchmarks (e.g., SimpleVQA and FVQA). In-depth analysis of V-Fold. We conduct an in-depth analysis to in-vestigate how V-Fold addresses per-formance bottlenecks in long-horizon agentic search. By folding extensive textual history into compact visual tokens, it enables the model to more effectively utilize historical cues while bypassing the performance degradation associated with extreme long-context scenarios. 

V-Fold under long-horizon settings. To better demonstrate the effectiveness of our V-Fold, we construct a hard subset for each benchmark using Claude-Sonnet-4-5, selecting VQA instances that require more than ten interaction rounds. As reported in Table 4, V-Fold consistently outperforms these alternatives. This demonstrates that selectively maintaining recent textual context yields a better balance between compression and information retention.

## A Details about the Training Stages 

A.1 Alignment & Pre-training Alignment stage. During this initial phase, we keep the parameters of both the vision encoder and the LLM frozen, updating only the vision projection layer ( i.e. , the MLP adapter). The training corpus for this stage consists of approximately 3M samples curated from Captioning and OCR datasets, primar-ily sourced from Laion-5B [33] and OpenVid [31]. To enhance the quality, the raw metadata from these datasets are re-annotated by state-of-the-art vision-language models ( e.g., InternVL3 [65]). 

Pre-training stage. Following alignment, we perform large-scale pre-training to enhance the model’s multimodal understanding and document-level reason-ing. We download all PDFs from the CC-MAIN-2021-31-PDF-UNTRUNCATED dataset † and filter for documents in Chinese and English. To construct an image-text dataset for training, we develop an automated pipeline comprising layout detection and text extraction. For layout detection, we employ PP-DocLayout [9] to extract all images from a page. Subsequently, we utilize a document OCR model ( i.e. , POINTS-Reader [28]) to extract all text from the page. Finally, the images and text associated with each document are concatenated, with images positioned at the beginning. The resulting pre-training dataset contains approx-imately 400 billion tokens. In contrast to the alignment stage, both the vision projector layer and the LLM are jointly optimized during this phase. 

A.2 Agentic Seeding 

As detailed in Section 3.2 of the main paper, this stage focuses on instilling the model with complex reasoning and planning capabilities. We curate a di-verse training suite comprising AM-DeepSeek-R1-Distilled-1.4M, Toucan-1.5M, Reason-RFT, and VisualWebInstruct. 

• AM-DeepSeek-R1-Distilled-1.4M : This dataset provides approximately 1.4 million high-quality, challenging reasoning problems designed to provoke deep chain-of-thought processing. 

• Toucan-1.5M : A tool-agent dataset featuring 1.5 million trajectories. These are synthesized from 495 real-world Model Context Protocols (MCP), span-ning over 2,000 unique tools to bolster the model’s agentic proficiency.

• Reasoning-RFT : A multimodal reasoning dataset containing over 10,000 samples. Its construction follows a rigorous three-stage pipeline: automated generation, filtering, and human verification to ensure logical groundedness. 

• VisualWebInstruct : Consisting of approximately 900,000 question-answer (QA) pairs, this dataset specifically addresses the scarcity of high-quality, diverse training data for complex multimodal reasoning tasks. 

A.3 Supervised Fine-tuning 

As introduced in Section 3.4, the SFT stage aims to align the model’s intrinsic agentic capabilities with the precise execution of search-related tools. Follow-ing the methodology described in Section 3.3, we construct a multimodal VQA dataset comprising 5,339 high-quality trajectories. To facilitate the V-Fold mech-anism, these trajectories are transformed into a dual-format representation: for each VQA sample, we provide both a text-only trajectory and a rendered visual trajectory where all intermediate tool observations are rendered into images to enable context-efficient visual processing. This data is further augmented with some QA trajectories from the InfoSeek dataset [50] and 15k OCR-specific sam-ples to ensure high-fidelity text recognition. Finally, to maintain broad-spectrum instruction-following capabilities and prevent catastrophic forgetting, we incor-porate Honey-Data-1M [48] as a general multimodal foundation. For samples in Honey-Data containing existing reasoning traces ( i.e. , <think> tags), we em-ploy Qwen3-VL-235B-A22B-Thinking to re-annotate the chain-of-thought con-tent, followed by a rigorous filtering process to exclude any samples where the re-annotated answer deviates from the original response provided in the dataset. The training is conducted using our in-house framework, which is analogous to Megatron [37]. 

A.4 Reinforcement Learning 

Building upon the SFT stage, we perform reinforcement learning to further refine the model’s decision-making and tool-use precision. Our RL framework is im-plemented using the verl [34], integrated with the SGLang [63] inference engine to accelerate the rollout process. The training is conducted for 60 steps, with a maximum trajectory depth of 10 turns per episode. The training corpus for this phase is composed of our self-constructed reasoning tasks and an adapted version of the LiveVQA dataset. To encourage more robust and grounded reasoning, we reformulate a significant portion of the originally multiple-choice LiveVQA sam-ples into open-ended questions. Crucially, we bypass V-Fold during RL training. Empirical results, as detailed in Table 6, demonstrate that employing V-Fold during the RL phase yields performance comparable to training without com-pression. Consequently, we opt for the more generalized uncompressed approach for RL training to maintain simplicity and robustness. Abbreviated paper title 21

## D Limitation and Future Work 

Despite its effectiveness, our approach has several limitations. First, the current context window is constrained to 32k tokens with a maximum of 10 interaction turns, which may limit performance in extremely long-horizon reasoning tasks. Second, our toolset is currently restricted to three core search tools; incorporat-ing more specialized APIs, such as Google Scholar or Google Maps, could further broaden the model’s utility. Finally, due to search API quota constraints, the scale of our RL training was limited. Future work will focus on scaling the re-inforcement learning process and expanding the tool-use diversity to further enhance the model’s autonomous search capabilities.
