Abstract —Spatial reasoning is a fundamental capability for embodied intelligence, especially for fine-grained manipulation tasks such as robotic assembly. While recent vision-language models (VLMs) exhibit preliminary spatial awareness, they largely rely on coarse 2D perception and lack the ability to perform accurate reasoning over 3D geometry, which is crucial for precise assembly operations. To address this limitation, we propose AssemLM, a spatial multimodal large language model tailored for robotic assembly. AssemLM integrates assembly manuals, point clouds, and textual instructions to reason about and predict task-critical 6D assembly poses, enabling explicit geometric understanding throughout the assembly process. To effectively bridge raw 3D perception and high-level reasoning, we adopt a specialized point cloud encoder to capture fine-grained geometric and rotational features, which are then integrated into the multimodal language model to support accurate 3D spatial reasoning for assembly tasks. In addition, we construct AssemBench, a large-scale dataset and benchmark for assembly-oriented spatial reasoning, comprising over 900K multimodal samples with precise 6D pose annotations. AssemBench extends spatial reasoning evaluation beyond 2D and grounding tasks into full 3D geometric inference, filling a critical gap in existing embodied AI benchmarks. Extensive experiments demonstrate that AssemLM achieves state-of-the-art performance in 6D pose reasoning across diverse assembly scenarios. Furthermore, real-robot evaluations show that our model can support fine-grained and multi-step assembly execution in real-world settings, demonstrating its potential for robotic assembly applications.

I. Introduction 

From assembling furniture [4, 19, 24, 41, 42] to repair-ing appliances [17, 28] and reconstructing fragmented ob-jects [21, 34, 36, 47], assembly processes are ubiquitous in daily life. Enabling robots to assist in such tasks can significantly reduce human effort and improve accessibility for non-expert users. These tasks are fundamentally important yet highly challenging for embodied intelligence, as they require fine-grained geometric reasoning over task-critical 6D poses at each step. Achieving this necessitates not only accurate pose prediction, but also the ability to jointly interpret multimodal inputs, including language instructions, point clouds, and vi-sual observations, as well as to generalize across diverse object categories and instance-level variations. Despite recent progress, achieving generalization across diverse object categories and robust multimodal understanding remains challenging. On the one hand, assembly processes are inherently compositional, involving diverse object categories and instance-level geometric variations. Recent works [33, 36, 42, 47, 49] demonstrate promising capabilities in assembly pose prediction, but are largely restricted to specific object categories and primarily rely on single-modal inputs such as point clouds, limiting their generalization across diverse categories. On the other hand, complete assembly inference requires jointly interpreting language instructions, visual cues, and geometric observations to understand the current state. Re-cent spatial reasoning models such as RoboBrain [22, 40] and RoboRefer [51] extend image–language models with depth in-formation, enabling basic spatial understanding. However, they remain insufficient for predicting precise task-critical 6D poses required in assembly tasks. Moreover, large-scale multimodal datasets featuring diverse object categories and precise part poses are essential to train and evaluate models for assembly-oriented spatial reasoning. Existing datasets [4, 35, 38] offer useful samples but are limited in scale and lack comprehensive modality coverage, thus failing to effectively support unified multimodal learning for large-scale model training. To address these challenges, we present AssemLM , a spatial multimodal large language model for robotic assembly rea-soning. AssemLM employs a specialized point cloud encoder to extract fine-grained geometric and equivariant rotational features, addressing the limitations of coarse 2D perception. These 3D representations are integrated into a multimodal transformer backbone that jointly processes manuals, text, and 3D geometries, enabling explicit geometric reasoning and precise 6D pose prediction. To address the critical lack of large-scale multimodal data for robotic assembly, we introduce AssemBench , a compre-hensive benchmark containing over 900K multimodal samples derived from 150K distinct assembly steps, spanning diverse object categories including furniture, daily objects, and frag-ments. We also develop an automated data generation pipeline that converts raw mesh assets and large-scale web-collected visual resources into high-fidelity point clouds, visual manuals, and aligned linguistic instructions. This provides large-scale, high-precision supervision for 6D pose grounding in assembly tasks, enabling models to learn from a diverse range of part geometries and spatial configurations. Our main contributions are as follows:  

> •

We propose AssemLM, a spatial multimodal large lan-guage model designed for robotic assembly that enables accurate prediction of task-critical 6D assembly poses, effectively bridging raw 3D perception and high-level reasoning for robotic assembly.  

> •

We introduce AssemBench, a large-scale multimodal benchmark for assembly-oriented spatial reasoning that jointly includes manuals, point clouds, and textual in-structions, comprising over 900K samples with precise 6D pose annotations across diverse assembly categories.  

> •

We demonstrate state-of-the-art performance across di-verse object categories, highlighting strong generalization capabilities, and further show the model’s potential for real-world robotic assembly through real-robot evalua-tions. 

II. Related Work 

A. Spatial Reasoning with Large Multimodal Models 

Perceiving and reasoning about spatial information is fun-damental for embodied foundation models in real-world ma-nipulation. Prior work on spatial reasoning broadly falls into two categories: 1) Semantic-level spatial reasoning focuses on qualitative relations such as relative position and orientation without explicit metric grounding. Representative methods include SpatialVLM [6] and SpatialRGPT [10], which enable coarse spatial understanding across image and point cloud modalities. 2) Numeric-level spatial reasoning aims to infer explicit quantitative attributes, including position, rotation, and depth. Approaches such as SpatialBot [5], RoboBrain [22, 40], and RoboRefer [51] incorporate metric grounding via RGB-D or multimodal perception. Despite these advances, existing methods remain limited in rotation reasoning [48] and rely heavily on 2D pixel–depth representations [5, 18, 22, 26], which cannot fully capture the 3D geometric structure essential for accurate 6D pose estimation in assembly tasks. Our method addresses this by performing equivariant 3D spatial reasoning directly on point clouds, enabling precise and robust 6D pose prediction in complex assembly tasks. 

B. Assembly Datasets and Benchmarks 

Robotic assembly datasets originate from object reassembly and part-based reconstruction in computer vision. Existing benchmarks can be grouped into four categories. 1) Everyday object assembly includes Assembly101 [35], which provides large-scale multi-view recordings of human toy-vehicle assem-bly with dense action and hand-pose annotations. 2) Furniture assembly is represented by IKEAASM [4], featuring multi-view videos with fine-grained action, segmentation, and pose labels. 3) Fragment reassembly focuses on reconstructing fractured objects, exemplified by BreakingBad [34], which offers over one million simulated broken parts. 4) Robotic Fig. 2: Overview of the AssemLM architecture. AssemLM integrates visual assembly manuals, 3D point clouds, and natural language instructions within a unified multimodal backbone for assembly pose reasoning. Visual inputs are processed by a vision encoder and projected into the language embedding space via a projector, with intermediate visual features injected into early LLM layers using a DeepStack mechanism. Point clouds of the fixed and moving parts are encoded by an SE(3)-aware point cloud encoder, producing both equivariant features 𝐹 and invariant features 𝐺 , which are fused and projected as geometric tokens. All modality-specific embeddings are concatenated with text tokens and jointly processed by the Qwen3-VL backbone. The model autoregressively predicts a sequence of discrete pose tokens representing 3D translation and 6D continuous rotation, which are subsequently converted into assembly poses by a pose de-tokenizer. 

assembly benchmarks typically target narrow tasks such as peg-in-hole or gear assembly, e.g., ReAssemble [38], with limited object diversity and scale. Many recent works leverage vision-language models to generate IKEA-style manuals [27, 42, 45, 49], but remain largely confined to furniture assembly and primarily rely on image or point cloud modalities. In this work, we introduce AssemBench, a large-scale multimodal benchmark comprising over 900K samples and 150K+ assembly steps, enabling systematic research on multimodal robotic assembly at scale. 

C. 6D Assembly Pose Prediction for Robotic Manipulation 

Accurate 6D pose prediction at each assembly step is crucial for successful assembly execution. Most existing methods rely on single modality point cloud inputs and emphasize SE(3) equivariant representations for geometric reasoning. Specifically, SE(3)-Transformer [16] introduces equivariant attention mechanisms for 3D roto-translation symmetry, while Vector Neurons [12] provides a general framework for SO(3)-equivariant learning by encoding features as 3D vectors instead of scalar activations. EPN [7] and 3D EGIF [9] further extend equivariant representations to point cloud analysis and implicit function modeling, respectively. Building upon this line of work, SE(3)-Assembly [47] and TwoByTwo [33] demonstrate the effectiveness of SE(3)-equivariant point cloud reasoning for assembly pose prediction. However, their reliance on point clouds alone limits multimodal integration, and their cross-category generalization is mainly limited to categories within a single broad domain, e.g., within fragments or within daily objects, rather than across diverse assembly domains. To address this, our approach integrates SE(3) equivariant geometric features into a multimodal large language model, enabling unified multi-category, multimodal 6D assembly pose prediction with strong generalization capabilities.

We introduce AssemLM, a 2B-parameter spatial multimodal large model based on Qwen3-VL-2B-Instruct [2], designed to predict precise 6D poses for sequential assembly steps. As illustrated in Fig. 2, we incorporate four key architectural innovations: explicit geometric equivariance in 3D percep-tion, embedding-level multimodal fusion for enhanced spatial grounding, specialized assembly pose tokenizer, and an opti-mized supervised fine-tuning (SFT) pipeline. 

Explicit Geometric Equivariance in 3D Perception. To empower the LLM backbone with robust spatial grounding against arbitrary rigid body transformations, we inject SE(3)-equivariant representations into the reasoning pipeline. Unlike conventional point encoders (e.g., PointNet [32]) that often discard pose information or rely on data augmentation, we employ a specialized Vector Neuron DGCNN [12, 47] to extract features that explicitly track both the orientation and position of assembly parts. Formally, the encoder takes a point cloud 𝑃 ∈ R𝑁 ×3 as input and utilizes dual branches to extract SE(3)-equivariant features 

𝐹 = Eequiv (𝑃 ) and SE(3)-invariant features 𝐺 = Einv (𝑃 ). For any rigid transformation 𝑇 ∈ 𝑆𝐸 (3) composed of rotation 𝑅 

and translation 𝑡 (i.e., 𝑇 (𝑃 ) = 𝑅𝑃 + 𝑡 ), these features satisfy: 

Eequiv (𝑅𝑃 + 𝑡 ) = 𝑅 · E equiv (𝑃 ) + 𝑡, (1) 

Einv (𝑅𝑃 + 𝑡 ) = Einv (𝑃 ). (2) This formulation ensures that 𝐹 faithfully preserves the ob-ject’s 6D pose in the feature space, while 𝐺 encodes pure geometric shape descriptors independent of spatial placement. To ground the assembly alignment, we construct a geometric correlation 𝐶 by modulating the equivariant pose of the moving part ( 𝐹 moving ) with the invariant shape context of the fixed part ( 𝐺 fixed ): 

𝐶 = 𝐺 fixed · 𝐹 moving , 𝐶 ∈ R 𝑓 ×3. (3) Crucially, since 𝐺 fixed is invariant, the correlation 𝐶 retains the SE(3)-equivariance of the moving part. This enables the multimodal LLM to capture the spatial state of the moving component relative to the target geometry, facilitating precise 6D pose prediction. Finally, to resolve cases where the fixed part itself is non-canonical, we incorporate 𝐹 fixed as complementary context. Both 𝐶 and 𝐹 fixed are projected into the LLM embedding space, providing a physics-grounded geometric prior for as-sembly planning. 

Multimodal Fusion and Spatial Grounding. To align visual cues from assembly manuals with 3D geometric reason-ing, we perform multimodal fusion directly at the embedding level, avoiding the overhead of complex cross-attention mod-ules while enabling efficient integration. We employ a vision encoder based on SigLIP-2 [2, 13, 43] to extract dense visual representations, which are projected into the LLM latent space. To preserve both high-level semantics and fine-grained spatial details, we incorporate a DeepStack mechanism [1, 2], which projects and injects intermediate features from multiple encoder layers into early transformer layers. This multi-scale approach ensures that the model maintains the high-fidelity contextual grounding required for precise manipulation. The point cloud embeddings are injected into the sequence via modality-specific placeholders. By preserving the original visual token layout and utilizing M-ROPE (Multimodal Rotary Positional Embedding) [3] derived from the visual grid, we aim to preserve the LLM’s inherent visual-spatial reasoning capability when incorporating 3D inputs. This unified embedding sequence enables the backbone to jointly reason over textual instructions, assembly manuals, and 3D geometries within a consistent spatial reference frame, significantly enhancing long-context stability for complex as-sembly planning. 

Specialized Assembly Pose Tokenization. We cast assembly pose prediction as a language modeling task by extending the Qwen3-VL tokenizer with a dedicated pose vocabulary. Specifically, we introduce 201 discrete tokens 

{<assemble_pose_0> , . . . , <assemble_pose_200> }

to represent coordinate values. To enable stable geometric learning, we represent each 6D assembly pose as a 9D pose vector, composed of 3D translation and a 6D continuous rotation representation [52]. This representation avoids the discontinuities inherent in Euler angles or quaternions, facilitating smoother optimization for the LLM backbone. All nine dimensions are normalized to [− 1, 1] and uni-formly discretized into 201 bins. This uniform tokenization strategy offers several advantages: 1) Seamless Integration: It enables end-to-end autoregressive decoding without requiring auxiliary continuous regression heads or complex pretrained tokenizers. 2) Reduced Predictive Burden: By maintaining a fixed 9-token sequence length for any pose, it avoids the redundancy and variable-length instability of generic subword tokenizers (e.g., FastTokenizer [31]), thereby providing a more stable interface for high-precision grounding. 3) Initializa-tion Stability: The embeddings for new action tokens are initialized using the mean of existing linguistic embeddings, ensuring training stability during the supervised fine-tuning (SFT) phase. 

Optimized Supervised Fine-tuning Pipeline. We imple-ment a two-stage training strategy to decouple geometric fea-ture acquisition from multimodal reasoning, ensuring training stability for complex assembly tasks. During the initial Geometry Warm-up phase, we pre-train the point cloud encoder alone on a targeted subset of As-semBench without linguistic supervision. Concretely, an MLP projector is attached to the encoder to directly predict the assembly pose, supervised by an L1 loss for translation and a Geodesic Distance loss for rotation. The projector is introduced solely for this warm-up stage and is discarded afterward; only the pre-trained encoder is retained in the subsequent multi-modal training stage. This process encourages the encoder to ground its SE(3)-equivariant features in pure geometric structures before multimodal fusion. In the subsequent Full Multimodal Alignment phase, we Fig. 3: Overview of our dataset statistics and data production pipeline. C. AssemBench 

Overview. AssemBench is a comprehensive multimodal benchmark for spatial reasoning in robotic assembly, in-tegrating visual assembly manuals, 3D point clouds, and precise natural language assembly instructions (see Fig. 3). Its key features are: 1) Large Scale. The dataset contains 150K unique assembly steps, totaling over 900K multimodal samples. To enhance both visual and linguistic robustness, we augment each assembly step with diverse renderings and instruction granularities. Specifically, each step is rendered in three styles— Freestyle , Non-Freestyle , and Lineart —where 

Freestyle produces visually realistic images aligned with prac-tical manuals at the cost of higher rendering time, while 

Lineart offers a more efficient but less detailed alternative. In parallel, we provide two levels of textual instructions: precise instructions that describe each step explicitly (e.g., “attach the first footstool leg to the seat”) and vague instructions that follow a generic template (e.g., “Assemble the {category} object”), specifying only the object category. 2) Rich Diversity. 

While existing assembly datasets are limited to categories such as tables and chairs, AssemBench covers over 50 object categories spanning furniture, daily objects, and fragments, fostering broad assembly spatial understanding during SFT. 

3) High Quality. We rigorously ensure data quality: 6D pose labels are generated through deterministic geometric computation, guaranteeing label precision; textual instructions are annotated via rejection sampling with multi-model cross-validation to select high-quality semantic descriptions, ensur-ing multimodal annotation consistency. 4) Easy Scalability. 

Our data generation pipeline seamlessly scales using diverse sources, including existing part-level datasets and extensive web-scale visual assets, enabling continuous expansion. 

Data Generation. Fig. 3 illustrates our progressive data construction pipeline, which combines part-level dataset adap-tation with generative expansion to enable general MM-LLMs to perform assembly-centric spatial reasoning. 1) Part-Level Mesh Preparation. We curate a diverse collection of assembly parts by consolidating and standardizing existing part-level datasets, including PartNet [29], BiAssembly [36], TwoByTwo [33], PartNeXt [44], and IKEA-Manual [45], with unified formats and coordinate systems. As existing datasets alone provide limited category coverage, we further expand part assets using generative models, directly synthesizing part-level meshes via PartPacker [39] to substantially improve diversity. 2) Assembly Data Generation. Given a set of part meshes, we first infer a valid assembly sequence based on spatial coordinates and part connectivity. Then we employ Blender [11] to automatically generate step-by-step assembly manuals that visualize relative part poses at each stage, while simultaneously sampling point clouds for the parts involved in each step, yielding consistent visual and 3D representations. 

3) Rejection Sampling-Based Semantic Annotation. To align visual assembly cues with natural language, we generate semantic descriptions for each part from its manuals and cate-gory attributes, and compose step-level assembly instructions by jointly considering part semantics and assembly manuals. To ensure annotation quality, multiple VLMs (e.g., Qwen3-VL-8B and Qwen3-VL-32B) independently produce candidate instructions, and a stronger model (e.g., GPT-5.2 [37]) serves as a judge to select the optimal annotation, ensuring both correctness and linguistic diversity. Additional details on the data generation pipeline are provided in Appx. B.

A. Benchmark Comparison on Multi-Category Assembly Tasks 

To evaluate AssemLM’s capacity for unified geometric reasoning, we conduct a comprehensive benchmark compar-ison across diverse object categories, including daily objects, furniture, and fragments. 

Data Split and Input Standardization. We conduct all experiments on AssemBench. The dataset is divided into train-ing and testing sets with a 9:1 ratio, with the split performed jointly over object categories and asset sources so that each category-source combination is represented in both sets. For fair comparison across model architectures under the multi-modal design of AssemLM, we standardize the inputs at each assembly step. Specifically, we use only the freestyle-rendered assembly manuals and the corresponding vague instruction, forming a curated training set of approximately 130K unique state–pose pairs. This setting avoids introducing additional visual or linguistic variants for the same assembly step. All input point clouds are fully 𝑆𝑂 (3) randomized. 

Experimental Setup and Baselines. Unlike category-specific methods, we train a single, unified AssemLM model on the aforementioned 130K training samples without per-category fine-tuning. We compare AssemLM against two distinct classes of baselines: 1) Specialized Assembly Models. 

We evaluate TwoByTwo [33], a state-of-the-art framework specifically designed for robotic assembly. It leverages 𝑆𝐸 (3)-equivariant representations to achieve high-precision part mat-ing. 2) General-Purpose Foundation Models. To assess the inherent reasoning ceiling of large-scale models, we include GPT-5.2 [37] and DeepSeek-V3.2 [25]. These foundation models are evaluated by leveraging their pre-trained spatial commonsense and chain-of-thought reasoning capabilities. Ad-ditional baselines are included in Appx. C. 

Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate assembly performance using three complementary metrics: 1) RMSE(T), which mea-sures translation accuracy by computing the root mean squared error between predicted and ground-truth translations. 2) Sym-metric Chamfer Distance (SCD), which is used instead of raw rotation error to account for rotational symmetries. It is defined as twice the Chamfer Distance ( 𝑆𝐶𝐷 = 2 × 𝐶𝐷 ), capturing the bidirectional geometric discrepancy between assembled shapes. 3) Success Rate (SR), which denotes the proportion of successful trials, where an assembly is considered successful if 𝑆𝐶𝐷 < 0.02 (i.e., 𝐶𝐷 < 0.01 ).

V. Conclusion 

We presented AssemLM, a spatial multimodal large lan-guage model tailored for high-precision robotic assembly. Our framework effectively bridges the gap between semantic reasoning and geometric execution by integrating a DeepStack-enhanced vision encoder with an SE(3)-equivariant point cloud encoder. By performing multimodal fusion at the embedding level, we utilize M-ROPE positional indices to maintain con-sistent spatial alignment for visual tokens while seamlessly injecting 3D geometric features. Furthermore, we introduced a specialized assembly pose tokenizer that stabilizes 6D pose prediction through a discrete-vocabulary interface. Validated on AssemBench, our large-scale benchmark with over 900K samples, AssemLM achieves state-of-the-art performance and demonstrates robust zero-shot generalization on unseen sim-ulated benchmarks and strong performance on real-world assembly tasks.

Appendix 

A. Implementation Details of AssemLM 1) Generalization to Real-World Assets: To evaluate the transferability of AssemLM, we extend our framework to physical objects by leveraging the unique characteristics of real-world data acquisition. Unlike web-scale images that are often restricted to a single viewpoint and holistic object-level context, real-world assets provide two distinct advantages for geometric reasoning. First, the ability to capture multi-view imagery facilitates a more comprehensive spatial understand-ing. Second, each constituent part can be independently asso-ciated with its own set of high-resolution images, allowing 3D generative models to produce high-fidelity, part-level meshes with intricate geometry and authentic textured appearances. This granular representation effectively bridges the domain gap between synthetic training and physical deployment. As illustrated in Fig. 5, we employ the Hunyuan3D [50] model to generate high-fidelity 3D models for each part of the task-specific assets used in our experiments. Following the automated dataset construction pipeline described in Appx. B, these real-world assets are further organized into a structured dataset format suitable for training AssemLM. By integrating external pose estimation modules such as FoundationPose [46], it is possible to obtain accurate 6D pose information for each object in real-world environments. Moreover, when combined with grasp prediction methods (e.g., GraspNet [14] and AnyGrasp [15]) and low-level motion planners for robotic manipulators, our framework could poten-tially be extended into a complete real-world robotic assembly pipeline, spanning perception, reasoning, and execution. More importantly, the assembly pose prediction capability exhibited by AssemLM suggests its potential applicability beyond end-to-end policy learning. In particular, it could serve as a high-level spatial reasoning module in non-end-to-end manipulation frameworks [20, 30], as well as in simulation data generation pipelines [8, 23], where determining where to place an object remains a central challenge. 

2) Special Tokens and Chat Template Design: As shown in Fig. 2, to equip the language model backbone with the ability to process multimodal inputs, we extend Qwen3-VL by introducing special tokens specifically designed for point cloud information. In particular, the token <PC_START> is used to indicate the beginning of point cloud input, <PC_END> 

denotes its termination, and additional tokens are employed to encode structured geometric features. 

3) Model Architecture Details: Following Qwen3-VL-2B, our vision encoder adopts the SigLIP-2 architecture, specifi-cally SigLIP2-Large (300M). It comprises a patch embedding layer, positional encoding modules, and 24 stacked vision transformer blocks, with a hidden size of 1024, an MLP Fig. 6: Visualization of the Canonical Coordinate System. intermediate dimension of 4096, and 16 attention heads. We further extract intermediate visual features from vision blocks 5, 11, and 17, process them with three DeepStack merger modules (each consisting of a LayerNorm and a two-layer MLP), and inject them into the first three transformer layers of the large language model backbone. For the point cloud encoder , Vector Neuron DGCNN produces both an SE(3)-equivariant feature 𝐹 and an SE(3)-invariant feature 𝐺 , each of dimension (1024 , 3). Accordingly, the feature hyperparameter 𝑓 introduced in Sec. III-B is set to 1024. After the modality projectors , the two manual images and the two part point clouds are embedded into representa-tions of size (648 , 2048 ) and (512 , 2048 ), respectively, before being injected into the transformer backbone. 

B. AssemBench Dataset and Production Pipeline 

As mentioned in Sec. III-C, we generate a precise and logically ordered assembly sequence for each asset to ensure geometric validity and correct part connectivity throughout the assembly process. To satisfy the training requirements described in Sec. III-A, we further construct multimodal supervision for each assembly step in the sequence, including high-fidelity point clouds, structured assembly manuals, and corresponding textual instructions (see Fig. 11). In the follow-ing, we describe the dataset in detail, organized according to the data generation procedure. 

1) Asset Normalization and Canonical Coordinate System: 

To standardize all assets for downstream processing and model inference, we define a canonical coordinate system that unifies object scale and spatial placement, as illustrated in Fig. 6. Specifically, we first rotate each asset into a consistent z-up orientation. For example, assets from the IKEA-Manual [45] are originally defined in a y-up coordinate system and are therefore rotated accordingly. We then normalize the object scale by rescaling the longest axis to unit length, translate the object such that its center is aligned with the origin in the hor-izontal plane, and place the lowest point of the object at zero height along the vertical axis. This normalization procedure yields a physically plausible configuration that mimics objects resting on the ground in real-world scenes, while providing a unified geometric representation for learning and reasoning.

To enrich the visual diversity of the dataset, we render each assembly step using three complementary rendering styles: Freestyle , Non-Freestyle , and Lineart (see Fig. 10). The Freestyle style performs a topology-aware edge detection after photorealistic rendering, producing line drawings with fine structural details that highlight critical assembly features. While highly informative, this mode incurs a higher rendering cost, with an average rendering time of 8.59 seconds per image, depending on scene complexity and Cycles sampling settings. The Non-Freestyle style disables edge extraction and yields standard pixel-based renderings, offering faster render-ing at an average of 3.35 seconds per image, albeit with less explicit structural emphasis. This approach achieves significantly faster rendering (0.22 seconds per image on average), though the resulting visuals are coarser and may contain noise. Together, these complementary rendering styles balance visual fidelity, structural clarity, and computational efficiency, providing diverse and informative supervision for multimodal assembly reasoning.

2) Additional Experimental Setup: As shown in Tables VI and V, DO, Fur., Frag., and R-T denote Daily Objects, Fur-niture, Fragments, and RMSE(T), respectively, following the same conventions as in §IV-A and §IV-B. To further validate the advantages of AssemLM, we introduce two additional experimental settings. First, compared with §IV-B, we extend Fur.* to the full IKEA datasets [45] by additionally including the shelf , table , and misc categories, where misc consists of a mixture of uncategorized furniture items. This setting poses a more challenging test of the model’s reasoning ability on unseen categories and assets from unseen sources. Second, to better assess rotational prediction, we introduce the metric SCD(R), which measures the Chamfer Distance induced solely by rotational error under accurate translation. We do not adopt Geodesic Distance or RMSE(R) because many assembly parts are symmetric, such that multiple rotations can be equally valid, and these metrics may therefore over-penalize correct predictions. 

3) Ablation Studies: To validate the contributions of differ-ent input modalities and architectural components, we conduct both modality and module ablations. For modality ablation, we train and evaluate the model with one modality removed at a time, and report the results in the top part of Table V. Removing vision or point cloud input leads to substantial degradation in translation and rotation accuracy, respectively, while removing text causes a moderate drop in both. These results highlight the importance of jointly leveraging multiple modalities for accurate assembly pose prediction. For module ablation, the results in the bottom part of Table V show that removing each key design component leads to performance degradation to different extents. This confirms that each module contributes positively to the overall performance of AssemLM. 

4) Additional Comparative Experiments: We additionally include ManualPA [49], which predicts assembly actions from manuals and point clouds, and SE(3)-Assembly [47], which predicts assembly poses using SE(3)-equivariant representa-tions, as supplementary baselines. As shown in Table VI, our method achieves higher prediction accuracy than both base-lines, with particularly notable gains in translation estimation, where it substantially outperforms ManualPA.

The second step inserts the backrest onto the assembled seat, where any residual error from the first step accumulates and further amplifies task difficulty. This task strongly challenges both translational and rotational precision as well as error accumulation across steps. During fine-tuning and evaluation of AssemLM and TwoByTwo on datasets constructed from real-world assets, we apply translational perturbations within a range of 0.1 on the horizontal plane in the canonical coordinate system, together with rotational perturbations of up to 10 ◦, to simulate randomized initial conditions. All models are fine-tuned for 330 epochs with a batch size of 4 and evaluated under 30 randomized initializations with seeds 0–29. In experiments, the grasp poses are predefined, while the assembly poses are predicted by the model, ensuring that the comparison results directly reflect each model’s assembly pose prediction capability.

7) Further Analysis on Data and Design Choices: To fur-ther investigate the factors affecting AssemLM’s performance, we conduct a set of additional analyses on the impact of dataset scale, rotation randomization range, and tokenizer design on geometric reasoning. We consider four experimental settings to examine these factors in a controlled manner, as detailed below:  

> •

AssemLM (Full). The proposed model trained on the full curated dataset of 130K samples with full 𝑆𝑂 (3) rotation randomization.  

> •

AssemLM (350-S). A low-data variant trained exclusively on the 350 samples from the daily object subset of the TwoByTwo dataset, designed to evaluate data efficiency.  

> •

AssemLM (Lim-Rot). A constrained variant where the rotation randomization is restricted to ±45 ◦ during both training and testing, assessing the model’s sensitivity to pose distribution.  

> •

AssemLM (Fast-Tk). A variant utilizing the FastTokenizer architecture instead of our custom pose tokenizer, aimed at evaluating the effectiveness of our discretization strat-egy. As illustrated in Fig. 9, quantitative results indicate that the scale of the training data is the most decisive factor for gener-alization. AssemLM (350-S) , limited to only 350 samples, fails to capture the underlying geometric manifolds, resulting in a high average RMSE(T) of 0.0475. In contrast, AssemLM (Full) 

leverages the 130K samples to achieve a significantly lower error of 0.0317, demonstrating that large-scale multimodal pre-training is essential for learning universal assembly priors. Regarding rotation randomization, while AssemLM (Lim-Rot) 

achieves localized precision in constrained settings, it offers no significant advantage over the Full model, suggesting that full rotation training does not compromise local accuracy while enabling broader generalization. Furthermore, the comparison between AssemLM (Full) and 

AssemLM (Fast-Tk) highlights the importance of the tokenizer design. We observe a distinct performance degradation in the FastTokenizer variant, with RMSE(T) increasing to 0.0366. This suggests that generic subword tokenizers are ill-suited for geometric regression due to the resulting variable-length sequences—often exceeding 9 tokens—and the increased au-toregressive burden. Unlike our training-free, uniform dis-cretization strategy, the FastTokenizer incurs additional train-ing overhead and redundancy without offering accuracy gains, confirming that our custom tokenizer provides a more stable and effective interface for geometric grounding.
