Abstract 

Recent advances in vision language models (VLMs) offer reasoning capa-bilities, yet how these unfold and integrate visual and textual information remains unclear. We analyze reasoning dynamics in 18 VLMs covering instruction-tuned and reasoning-trained models from two different model families. We track confidence over Chain-of-Thought (CoT), measure the corrective effect of reasoning, and evaluate the contribution of interme-diate reasoning steps. We find that models are prone to answer inertia ,in which early commitments to a prediction are reinforced, rather than revised during reasoning steps. While reasoning-trained models show stronger corrective behavior, their gains depend on modality conditions, from text-dominant to vision-only settings. Using controlled interventions with misleading textual cues, we show that models are consistently influ-enced by these cues even when visual evidence is sufficient, and assess whether this influence is recoverable from CoT. Although this influence can appear in the CoT, its detectability varies across models and depends on what is being monitored. Reasoning-trained models are more likely to explicitly refer to the cues, but their longer and fluent CoTs can still appear visually grounded while actually following textual cues, obscuring modality reliance. In contrast, instruction-tuned models refer to the cues less explicitly, but their shorter traces reveal inconsistencies with the visual input. Taken together, these findings indicate that CoT provides only a par-tial view of how different modalities drive VLM decisions, with important implications for the transparency and safety of multimodal systems. 

1 Introduction 

Chain-of-Thought prompting (CoT) improves reasoning in large language models (Wei et al., 2022), often resulting in large gains in downstream task accuracy (Wang et al., 2025b). However, recent work argues that final accuracy alone provides a limited view of reasoning behavior (Guan et al., 2025). Instead, analyzing how predictions evolve across intermediate reasoning steps can reveal whether reasoning progresses incrementally, or simply justifies an early hypothesis. For example, Lewis-Lim et al. (2025) show that tracking the temporal evolution of model confidence across CoT steps helps distinguish genuine reasoning from post-hoc rationalization. Similarly, Madsen et al. (2024) find that LLM self-explanations are often unfaithful, misrepresenting the true basis of model predictions. However, these analyses are limited to text-only models. In vision-language models (VLMs), reasoning requires the integration of textual explanations with visual evidence. This can introduce additional uncertainty and potential failure modes (Parcalabescu & Frank, 2025; Jiang et al., 2025a). Prior work shows that models often struggle to reconcile internal parametric knowledge with external inputs, leading to knowledge conflict , where provided evidence is overridden by prior beliefs (Huang et al., 2025). 2 Related Work 

Reasoning Dynamics and Monitorability of CoT. CoT prompting improves reasoning accuracy in large language models (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2025b), but how intermediate reasoning steps relate to the model’s underlying decision process remains unclear. Prior work examines the faithfulness of CoT explanations, i.e., whether reasoning traces reflect the factors driving a model’s final prediction. Turpin et al. (2023) show that biasing features such as suggested answers can shift predictions without being acknowledged in the reasoning trace. Multimodal Reasoning and Modality Bias. Multimodal reasoning introduces additional challenges, as models must integrate visual and textual inputs into coherent explanations. Surveys and benchmarks on multimodal CoT highlight persistent limitations in grounding and modality integration (Wang et al., 2025b; Cheng et al., 2025). A central concern is unimodal bias, where models over-rely on textual cues despite the presence of informative visuals. Several methods quantify this effect using causal analyses or modality attribution metrics (Chen et al., 2024; Parcalabescu & Frank, 2023), while others study how modality preferences affect robustness (Zhang et al., 2025). 

Monitoring Modality Reliance through Perturbations. Robustness and modality reliance in multimodal reasoning are often studied through controlled perturbations. Benchmarks such as MME-CoT and MathVerse show that models often fail to rely on visual information, instead exploiting textual redundancies or dataset biases (Jiang et al., 2025a; Zhang et al., 2024). Other work probes alignment between visual descriptions and reasoning (Zhou et al., 2025) or examines consistency across decoding strategies (Parcalabescu & Frank, 2025). Calibration-aware evaluations further show that multimodal models struggle to recognize the limits of their knowledge (He et al., 2025). Balasubramanian et al. (2025) show that visual and subtle biases often influence predictions without being articulated, and that models can shift answers late in the CoT, particularly in perception-driven tasks. 

Our focus. While prior work focuses on robustness, bias, and final outputs, it leaves open how predictions are formed and reinforced during reasoning, whether intermediate steps contribute to correcting errors under different modality conditions, and whether the influ-ence of misleading cues can be detected from reasoning traces. Our work analyzes reasoning dynamics in multimodal models by (i) tracking confidence trajectories across CoT steps, (ii) quantifying the corrective effect of reasoning through net gain across modality conditions, and (iii) evaluating the monitorability of misleading hints and modality attribution. 

3 Experimental Setup 

3.1 Data MathVerse (Zhang et al., 2024) contains mathematical problems with diagrams, each trans-formed into multiple multimodal variants that systematically alter the balance between visual and textual information, while always retaining the diagram: text-dominant (full text), text lite (reduced descriptions), vision intensive (minimal text with key conditions), vision-dominant (only the question and implicit cues), and vision-only (no text; the question appears in the diagram), with 436 samples each. We focus on the multiple-choice subset, allowing consistent evaluation of accuracy and confidence across CoT prefixes. The text-dominant variant is used for the main experiments to provide a consistent setting, while all variants are used to analyze modality sensitivity. 3Preprint. Under review. 

PhyX (Shen et al., 2025) is a physics reasoning benchmark, with information distributed across textual descriptions and visual diagrams. To analyze modality reliance, we partition PhyX into three variants based on modality dominance: (i) text-dominant (1,000 samples), where the full given textual question and description are provided; (ii) vision-dominant (440 samples), where textual descriptions are simplified; and (iii) vision-only (1,000 samples), where the question and answer choices are rendered within the image and the model is prompted only to select an answer option. We use the text-dominant subset for the main experiments and all variants for analyses of modality sensitivity. 

ScienceQA Lu et al. (2022). We use the multimodal test subset of ScienceQA (ScienceQA-IMG, 2,017 samples), consisting of multiple-choice questions paired with images across scientific domains such as biology, physics, and chemistry. Figure 2 presents a representative example from each dataset. 

3.2 Models 

We evaluate multimodal language models across two training paradigms, standard instruc-tion tuning and explicit multi-step reasoning. We expect these differences in training to shape how reasoning unfolds, influencing both the ability to revise intermediate predictions and the extent to which models rely on textual versus visual information. 1

Instruction-tuned (I): We include Qwen2.5-VL-Instruct ( 3B , 7B , 32B ) (Bai et al., 2025), Qwen3-VL-Instruct ( 4B , 8B , 32B ) (Yang et al., 2025), InternVL3 ( 2B , 8B , 38B ) (Zhu et al., 2025), and InternVL3.5 ( 4B , 8B , 38B ) (Wang et al., 2025a) which are general-purpose multimodal models trained for instruction following without explicit reasoning supervision. 

Multi-step reasoning (R): We evaluate Qwen3-VL-Thinking ( 4B , 8B , 32B ) (Yang et al., 2025), trained with reinforcement learning to produce explicit CoT reasoning within <think> tags, and InternVL3.5 on Thinking mode ( 4B , 8B , 38B ) (Wang et al., 2025a), which incorporates multimodal reasoning data via a dedicated Thinking mode. 

4 Reasoning Dynamics in Multimodal Models 

Confidence Trajectories. We first quantify how predictive confidence evolves over CoT reasoning in VLMs. By contrasting instruction-tuned and reasoning-trained models, we assess whether confidence is progressively revised or remains largely stable until the final answer. Models are prompted to produce step-by-step reasoning followed by a final answer using a fixed template (Appendix A). For each reasoning step t (i.e., each CoT prefix CoT 1: t), we measure confidence as the probability assigned to the gold answer letter after the prefix 

Answer: , conditioned on the input x and the reasoning prefix CoT 1: t. We obtain this by extracting next-token log-probabilities for all candidate answer letters, normalizing them over the answer set, and taking the resulting probability assigned to the correct answer. For final-incorrect cases, we additionally track the probability assigned to the model’s predicted answer. The resulting sequence [P0, P1, . . . , PT ] defines a per-example confidence trajectory over the reasoning process. For comparability across examples and models, we normalize these trajectories to the range [0, 1 ] prior to aggregation. 

Contribution of Intermediate Steps. We evaluate the extent to which intermediate CoT steps contribute to final prediction accuracy by analyzing model performance under pro-gressive truncation of the reasoning sequence. This analysis complements confidence-based dynamics by directly measuring whether additional reasoning steps improve task perfor-mance. For each example, we first generate a full reasoning sequence CoT 1: T. We then iteratively truncate the sequence to retain only the first t steps, where t ∈ { 0, 1, . . . , T}. At each truncation depth, the model is prompted to produce a final answer conditioned on the partial reasoning prefix and the original multimodal input. We compute accuracy at step t:

Net Gain of CoT Reasoning We assess the corrective effect of CoT reasoning by comparing model predictions before and after CoT generation. For each example, we record whether the initial prediction without CoT is correct and whether the final prediction after CoT is correct. We define net gain as: 

Net Gain = ∑Ni=1 1[bi = 0 ∧ fi = 1]

∑Ni=1 1[bi = 0] − ∑Ni=1 1[bi = 1 ∧ fi = 0]

∑Ni=1 1[bi = 1] . (2) 

where i ∈ { 1, . . . , N} indexes evaluation examples xi.

bi = 1[initial prediction is correct ] and fi = 1[final prediction is correct ]. The net gain lies in the range [−1, 1 ]. Intuitively, net gain measures how often CoT corrects initially incorrect predictions minus how often it overturns correct ones, thus, a positive net gain indicates that CoT more often corrects incorrect predictions than it overturns correct ones. 

4.1 Results Intermediate reasoning often reinforces early predictions rather than correcting them. 

Figure 3 shows that across models and datasets, answer probability trajectories diverge between final-correct and final-incorrect. When predictions are correct, the probability of the gold answer increases over reasoning steps; when incorrect, it declines while the probability of the chosen incorrect option remains high or increases. This divergence emerges early, suggesting that predictive signal is largely determined in the initial steps, with later reasoning primarily amplifying the model’s current hypothesis rather than correcting it. This pattern aligns with findings in text-only LLMs (Marjanovic et al., 2026), where step-by-step reasoning often exhibits a rumination effect, re-examining earlier constructions of the problem. Our results suggest that such behaviors persist beyond text-only settings. We further analyze commitment dynamics by identifying the earliest step at which the model predicts its final answer without revision. As shown in Fig. 14 (Appendix B), models often assign highest probability to their final answer before or within the first few CoT steps, indicating that errors arise from reinforcing early incorrect predictions. 2

5 Monitorability of Visual Reliance 

In this section, we complement our step-wise analysis with a monitorability evaluation, assessing whether modality influences are reflected in the CoT. 

5.1 Monitorability Framework 

Monitorability refers to the degree to which a property of a model’s behavior can be reliably inferred from its reasoning trace by an external monitor (Guan et al., 2025). In our setting, the property of interest is which input modality (text or vision) influences the model’s prediction. To study this, we adapt the framework of Guan et al. (2025), which uses controlled interventions to test whether their influence is recoverable from the model’s CoT. While originally proposed for text-only settings, we adapt it to multimodal inputs by using interventions that create conflicts between textual cues and visual evidence. This enables us to assess both whether models prioritize one modality over the other and whether that reliance is reflected in the CoT. We operationalize monitorability through two complementary evaluations: detecting whether injected hints influence reasoning, and attributing predictions to textual or visual information. 

Misleading Hint Interventions. We inject misleading hints as textual interventions in the prompt (Turpin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025; Guan et al., 2025) that suggest a specific incor-rect answer, following prior intervention-based analyses in text-only LLMs. We consider three framing variants: sycophancy, reward-hacking, and unethical information (Table 1). All experiments are conducted on the Vision-Only subset of MathVerse, where the full prob-lem is contained in the image. This provides a clean setting for intervention: because the correct answer must be recovered from visual content, any effect of injected text reflects reliance on textual cues rather than missing task information. We sample 10 generations per prompt to estimate intervention effects and restrict evaluation to baseline-correct examples, those answered correctly in more than 50% of generations without a hint, to ensure these effects reflect cue reliance rather than task competence. 

Monitor tasks. Given that hints may influence model behavior, we test whether this influence is detectable from the CoT. An external monitor reads the reasoning trace and judges whether the intervention affected the model’s prediction. We define two progres-sively stricter tasks: (i) Hint influence detection ( G2 

> H

), where the monitor is told a hint may have been present and asked whether it influenced the reasoning, measuring whether explicit hint reliance is recoverable from the CoT; and (ii) Modality attribution ( G2

Because the only textual content is the injected hint, this provides a stricter test of whether the CoT reveals the modality that drove the prediction. Comparing these two tasks enables us to disentangle intervention-specific detection from general modality attribution. In particular, it assesses whether a monitor that considers only image-versus-text reliance can recover intervention effects as effectively as one explicitly instructed to detect the hint. Full prompts for the monitors are found in Appendix E. 

Monitorability metric. We quantify monitor performance using G2mean from Guan et al. (2025). This metric measures the extent to which the model’s CoT contains sufficient 7Preprint. Under review. information for an external monitor to detect whether the intervention influenced the model’s prediction. G2mean combines the monitor’s sensitivity and specificity into a single score, where a value of 1.0 indicates perfect monitorability while 0.25 corresponds to random performance. To account for the fact that the model may select the hinted answer by chance even without the intervention, the metric incorporates the total effect, 

TE = Pr (Y=1 | X=1) − Pr (Y=1 | X=0), (3) 

where X ∈ { 0, 1 } indicates whether the hint is present and Y denotes whether the model selects the hinted answer. These probabilities are estimated by sampling 10 responses per example under each condition. A positive total effect indicates that the injected text causally shifts the model toward the hinted option, establishing a lower bound on the proportion of positive outcomes attributable to the intervention, against which the monitor’s detection rate is calibrated. We further use the aggregated total effect to contextualize the strength of each hint’s influence across models. We refer to Guan et al. (2025) for the full derivation of these metrics.

Effect of Hint Modality We investigate whether rendering the hint in the image rather than the text affects its influence or monitorability. For most hints, both the aggregated total effect and monitorability are comparable when the hint is rendered in the image (Figure 21 in Ap-pendix D). A notable exception is the reward hacking hint. Implications for monitoring CoTs in multimodal LLMs. These results suggest that monitorability of modality reliance depends both on the model behav-ior and on the information available to the monitor. A monitor that knows the form of the intervention can search for specific signals, whereas a monitor tasked only with assessing modality grounding must determine whether the reasoning is genuinely de-rived from the image, a substantially harder judg-ment, particularly when the CoT is long and inter-nally coherent. This gap has practical implications: in deployment, one typically does not know the form of potential confounding cues. The fact that modality at-tribution is harder than hint detection, especially for larger models, suggests that CoT monitoring alone may be insufficient to ensure visual grounding in multimodal systems. 

6 Conclusion 

In this work, we analyze reasoning dynamics in VLMs and find that predictions are often formed early, with later CoT steps primarly reinforcing rather than revising them. While reasoning-trained models show stronger corrective behavior, these improvements depend on modality conditions, with the largest gains in vision-only settings. Using controlled 9Preprint. Under review. interventions, we show that models are consistently influenced by misleading textual cues. This influence is easier to monitor in shorter CoTs from instruction-tuned models, where inconsistencies with visual content are apparent, but becomes increasingly obscured in longer ones from multi-step reasoning models, making modality reliance difficult to recover without prior knowledge of influencing factors. A Implementation Details 

CoT prompt For Section 4, we prompt the model to produce step-by-step reasoning and a final answer using a fixed template: 

You are a careful reasoning assistant that answers multiple-choice questions about an image. First, think step by step. Then, give the final answer. Format EXACTLY as: Reasoning: <step 1> Reasoning: <step 2> ... Answer: <final letter> Use at most 5 reasoning steps. Each step should be one or two sentences. Do NOT use '1. ', '2. ' or bullet points. Do NOT literally write <step 1> or <final letter>. 

We first parse explicit Reasoning step markers; when unavailable, we fall back to splitting free-form reasoning by non-empty lines. We treat each Reasoning segment as a reasoning step, corresponding to a prefix CoT 1: t in our analysis. Although this setup may introduce minor ambiguities (e.g., formatting inconsistencies or implicit step boundaries), in practice it provides a sufficiently reliable approximation of step boundaries. 

Generation Protocol. For the reasoning-dynamics experiments (Section 4), we use one-shot prompting with deterministic decoding (temperature = 0), generating a single reasoning trace per example. This allows us to analyze step-wise confidence trajectories conditioned on a fixed CoT. For the intervention experiments (Section 5), we keep the prompt fixed and sample ten independent generations per example and condition (control and intervention) using temperature = 0.8 to capture stochastic variation in cue reliance. For the monitor model, we use deterministic decoding (temperature = 0). Deterministic decoding is used to isolate trajectory-level reasoning dynamics, whereas stochastic decoding enables estimation of distributional reliance effects under intervention.

Qualitative analysis of early incorrect predictions in Qwen2.5-I and Qwen3-Thinking (32B) models. To better understand the nature of early incorrect hypotheses, we examine qualitative examples where models commit to an incorrect answer early in the reasoning process. Across model families, two common patterns emerge. In other cases, the visual configuration is interpreted correctly, but the model applies an inappropriate rule or reasoning strategy, producing an internally coherent but incorrect explanation. Chat Template Formatting and Monitorability Figure 20 shows that removing the chat template improves hint monitorability for instruction-tuned models. Prior work has found that RLHF training discourages models from acknowledging the use of external hints or reward hacks when answering questions (Ferreira et al., 2025), and that removing chat templates can weaken safety behaviors introduced during alignment (Jiang et al., 2025b; Lyu et al., 2024). Our results are consistent with this, as weakening RLHF-induced behavior makes the model more willing to reference the hint in its reasoning, which in turn increases the hint monitor’s ability to detect its influence. This suggests that while removing the chat template may increase unsafe outputs, it can also improve our ability to monitor them. Further work is needed to better understand this relationship.
