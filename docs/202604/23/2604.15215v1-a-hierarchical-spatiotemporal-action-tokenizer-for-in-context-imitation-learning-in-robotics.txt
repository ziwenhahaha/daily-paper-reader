# A Hierarchical Spatiotemporal Action Tokenizer for In-Context Imitation Learning in Robotics 

Fawad Javed Fateh † Ali Shah Ali † Murad Popattia Usman Nizamani Andrey Konin M. Zeeshan Zia Quoc-Huy Tran 

Retrocausal, Inc. Redmond, WA 

www.retrocausal.ai 

Abstract: We present a novel hierarchical spatiotemporal action tokenizer for in-context imitation learning. We first propose a hierarchical approach, which con-sists of two successive levels of vector quantization. In particular, the lower level assigns input actions to fine-grained subclusters, while the higher level further maps fine-grained subclusters to clusters. Our hierarchical approach outperforms the non-hierarchical counterpart, while mainly exploiting spatial information by reconstructing input actions. Furthermore, we extend our approach by utilizing both spatial and temporal cues, forming a hierarchical spatiotemporal action to-kenizer, namely HiST-AT. Specifically, our hierarchical spatiotemporal approach conducts multi-level clustering, while simultaneously recovering input actions and their associated timestamps. Finally, extensive evaluations on multiple simulation and real robotic manipulation benchmarks show that our approach establishes a new state-of-the-art performance in in-context imitation learning. 

Keywords: Robotic manipulation, In-context imitation learning, Action tok-enizer, Hierarchical vector quantization, Spatiotemporal vector quantization 

## 1 Introduction 

Teaching robots to perform actions from demonstrations has received significant research inter-est alongside advances in deep learning. A prominent paradigm, imitation learning (IL), aims to learn generalizable robot policies from expert demonstrations [1]. However, as mentioned in [2], IL suffers from limited generalization due to the scarcity of high-quality demonstrations. Recent large-scale efforts [2, 3, 4] attempt to alleviate this issue; however, adapting to new tasks often still requires collecting additional task-specific data for fine-tuning. Inspired by the in-context learning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) [5, 6, 7, 8, 9], in-context imitation learn-ing (ICIL) [10, 11, 12, 13, 14] has emerged as a promising alternative. ICIL allows robotic policies to perform new tasks from demonstrations provided at inference time, without retraining, enabling flexible and efficient real-world deployment. Fig. 1(a) shows an example ICIL framework [13]. Despite its advantages, ICIL still struggles to learn contextualized action representations from demonstrations [15]. Effective action representations can lead to notable performance gains in ICIL [16]. Several works [17, 18, 19, 3, 20, 21, 13, 14, 22] focus on action tokenizers for dis-cretizing and encoding robot actions, with modeling temporal correlations remaining a key chal-lenge. While positional encoding [23] or vector quantization [24] can be used to preserve temporal order, they often fail to maintain temporal smoothness in action trajectories [25]. As discussed in Mysore et al. [26], temporal smoothness promotes continuity in tokenized actions and reduces noise. ## 2 Related Work 

In-Context Imitation Learning. Robotic manipulation benefits from systems that can adapt to un-seen tasks and scenes without retraining, making in-context imitation learning (ICIL) a promising paradigm for scalable robot learning [35]. Rather than updating model parameters for new scenar-ios, ICIL allows robots to infer task structures directly from contextual demonstrations, inspired by the success of ICIL in large language models (LLMs) [5, 6, 7, 8, 9]. Early approaches rely on state abstractions such as keypoint-based representations of objects and robots [10, 11, 12], enabling ac-2tion inference from context but depending on external modules [36, 37] that generalize poorly across diverse settings. Recent works have shifted to end-to-end transformer frameworks [13, 14], where ICIL is modeled as a sequential prediction task and observation-action tokens are jointly learned. Building on this line of research, we introduce a hierarchical spatiotemporal action tokenizer (HiST-AT) to improve the performance and generalization of ICIL. 

Action Tokenizer. Several research efforts have focused on learning robot action representa-tions [38, 39]. Initial methods [17, 18] discretize each action dimension into bins. ICRT [13] and CAPTURE [14] parameterize actions using neural networks, while Zhao et al. [19] learn low-dimensional action embeddings using a variational autoencoder (VAE). Vision–language–action (VLA) models represent robot actions as sequences of tokens [3, 20, 21, 22]. However, these meth-ods often lack smoothness, which is crucial for stable and successful robotic manipulation. Various approaches have been proposed to enhance smoothness, e.g., Lipschitz regularization [26], Gaus-sian process priors [40], action aggregation [19], and Bayesian optimization [41]. Recently, LipVQ-VAE [27] introduces a vector-quantized VAE (VQ-VAE) [28]-based action tokenizer that relies on flat clustering and spatial reconstruction, enforcing smoothness via Lipschitz regularization. We propose a Lipschitz-smooth hierarchical spatiotemporal action tokenizer that leverages multi-level clustering and spatiotemporal reconstruction to learn smooth and effective action representations. 

Temporal Action Segmentation. Temporal action segmentation [29, 42, 43, 44, 31, 45] involves learning frame representations and clustering them into action segments. Early attempts [29, 30] exploit temporal or spatiotemporal reconstruction for representation learning, while classical or hi-erarchical vector quantization has been applied for clustering in recent works [31, 32]. Moreover, PROGRESSOR [46] utilizes temporal reconstruction as a reward signal for learning robotic manip-ulation policy. Motivated by these works, we develop a hierarchical spatiotemporal vector quantiza-tion framework for action tokenization. Other potential applications of our framework include VLA pretraining [47] and human-like agents [48], which we will explore in our future work. 

## 3 Our Approach 

3.1 In-Context Imitation Learning 

ICIL aims to enable a policy to infer task behaviors from a small set of expert demonstrations provided at inference time without updating model weights. Following ICRT [13], we model ICIL as a next-token prediction problem for robotic manipulation tasks (see Fig. 1(a)). We divide the expert demonstrations into two components: prompt demonstrations and query. Observations and actions are tokenized within the prompt demonstrations, yielding context rich prompt tokens for the model to execute robotic manipulation tasks. A transformer then auto-regressively processes these prompt tokens to predict a sequence of robot actions given query observations. The model can learn to adapt to unseen tasks by conditioning on prompt demonstrations without explicit retraining. Following [27], we employ ResNet-18 [49] to encode RGB-D observations. Optionally, CLIP [50] is used to encode language inputs, while MLPs are used to encode the rest of sensory inputs. An MLP maps the tokenized observations and actions into a shared latent space for dimensional consistency. We then utilize an autoregressive transformer that attends to a sequence of prompt observation and action tokens (hop, hap) and query observation tokens (hoq):

## 4 Experiments 

In this section, we evaluate our HiST-AT approach on simulation robotic manipulation datasets. We conduct simulation experiments in RoboCasa [33] and ManiSkill [34] on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU. In RoboCasa, seven tasks are evaluated with training for 500K itera-tions under the standard protocol, while in ManiSkill we focus on three tasks, training for 30K iter-ations to remain consistent with prior works. We evaluate the performance through success rate, as defined by each environment [33, 34]. We compare our ICRT [13]-based framework with established approaches such as BC-Transformer [1], ACT [19], and MCR [51]; since ACT is computationally intensive and exceeds our hardware capacity, we scale it down to match BC-Transformer for fair comparisons. Within the same ICRT [13]-based framework, we also evaluate against other action tokenizers, including MLP [13], discrete binning [17], FAST [22], VQ-VAE [28], LFQ-VAE [24], and LipVQ-VAE [27], where FAST is further fine-tuned on one million action samples from Robo-Casa and ManiSkill. ## 5 Conclusion 

We propose an in-context imitation learning approach based on HiST-AT, a novel hierarchical spa-tiotemporal action tokenizer. We first introduce a hierarchical approach with a two-level vector quantization hierarchy, i.e., input actions are mapped to fine-grained subclusters at the lower level, while fine-grained subclusters are then assigned to clusters at the higher level. Our hierarchical approach obtains better results than the non-hierarchical baseline, while primarily leveraging spa-tial information through action reconstruction. In addition, we improve our approach by incorpo-rating both spatial and temporal cues, yielding a hierarchical spatiotemporal action tokenizer, i.e., 8multi-level clustering is employed while input actions and their corresponding timestamps are jointly reconstructed. Lastly, we conduct extensive evaluations on several simulation and real robotic ma-nipulation benchmarks to demonstrate our superior performance over previous methods.
