## Abstract 

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have made sig-nificant strides in static image understanding but continue to face critical hurdles in spatiotemporal reasoning. A major bottleneck is “multi-image reasoning hallucination”, where a massive perfor-mance drop between forward and reverse tempo-ral queries reveals a dependence on superficial shortcuts instead of genuine causal understanding. To mitigate this, we first develop a new Chain-of-Thought (CoT) dataset that decomposes intricate reasoning into detailed spatiotemporal steps and definitive judgments. Building on this, we present a progressive training framework: it initiates with supervised pre-training on our CoT dataset to in-still logical structures, followed by fine-tuning with scalable weakly-labeled data for broader gen-eralization. Our experiments demonstrate that this approach not only improves backbone accu-racy but also slashes the forward-backward per-formance gap from over 70% to only 6.53%. This confirms the method’s ability to develop authen-tic dynamic reasoning and reduce the inherent temporal biases of current VLMs.

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Benchmarking Spatio-Temporal Reasoning in Vision-Language Models 

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have evolved from un-derstanding static images to evaluating complex, dynamic scenes. Early benchmarks like CLEVR (ohnson et al., 2016) focused on compositional geometry in synthetic images, while subsequent work like GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019) extended this to complex real-world imagery. Fo-cus later shifted towards 3D and dynamic environments. Datasets like ScanNet (Dai et al., 2017) and Matterport3D (Chang et al., 2017) pioneered 3D understanding, while in-teractive platforms such as Habitat (Savva et al., 2019) and AI2-THOR (Deitke et al., 2022) marked a shift to dynamic tasks requiring navigation and decision-making. Building on this, research emerged to endow VLMs with native 3D perception, leading to models like 3D-LLM, LL3DA, and 2STCR: Progressive Training for Embodied VLMs 

Chat-Scene. This trend was further advanced by models including 3D-LLaVA and Video-3D LLM, which integrate 3D perception with LLM reasoning. Despite these advances, diagnostic tools like the Perception Test (Pavez et al., 2023) indicate that even state-of-the-art models often lack a deep, causal understanding of temporal events. 

2.2. Task Progress Estimation and Reward Model 

Evaluating task progress is crucial, leading to various methods for constructing general-purpose reward models. One approach learns progress representations via time-contrastive learning, where works like (Ma et al., 2022; 2023; Biza et al., 2024) leverage the temporal order of video frames as a self-supervised signal. Another approach uti-lizes the zero-shot capabilities of VLMs, using prompts to score or compare states (Wang et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). Furthermore, other studies measure progress via se-mantic similarity, mapping states and goals to a shared em-bedding space (Mendonca et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2024), or by synthesizing target states for comparison (Zhou et al., 2024). These signals frequently provide feedback for Rein-forcement Learning (Chen et al., 2024b; Luo et al., 2024). 

2.3. Chain-of-Thought for Multimodal Reasoning 

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) enhances LLM reasoning by prompting models to generate intermediate steps before the final answer (Wei et al., 2022). This method effectively handles arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning. Recently, CoT has been extended to the multimodal domain for complex visual tasks. Most multimodal CoT applica-tions operate at inference-time using structured prompts to guide generation (Zhang et al., 2023; Rose et al., 2024). 

## 3. Dataset 

A key vulnerability of vision-language models (VLMs) is their profound sensitivity to input order during spatio-temporal reasoning. This sensitivity manifests as severe hallucinations, particularly when the logical progression of events contradicts the visual sequence presented to the model. For instance, when tasked to determine which of two images is closer to task completion, merely reversing the input order can cause a catastrophic failure in reasoning, a phenomenon termed “multi-image reasoning hallucination”. The root cause lies in the supervisory misalignment: while modern VLMs possess the latent capacity for spatial reason-ing, standard image-caption training encourages them to rely on superficial temporal shortcuts (e.g., file order) rather than causal state analysis. These paradigms typically rely exclu-sively on sparse, end-to-end labels. Such weak supervision is insufficient to counteract the strong “sequence bias” inher-ent from large-scale pre-training. Consequently, the model defaults to an erroneous prior: associating task progression with the second image by default, rather than performing genuine reasoning based on visual evidence. To break this vicious cycle, a dataset with a novel supervisory structure is crucial. Our goal is to replace this inadequate sparse supervi-sion with a dense, structured signal that compels the model to follow a verifiable reasoning process, thereby decoupling its judgment from superficial positional cues. 

3.1. Preprocessing and Sampling 

To construct high-quality training samples, our data pro-cessing pipeline begins with the refinement of the AgiBot (AgiBot-World-Contributors et al., 2025) real-world robot video corpus. First, we employ a multi-granularity sampling method, applying 10x temporal downsampling to filter out visual noise (such as camera shake) and ensure significant state changes between frames. This process compels the model to move beyond simple pattern matching. Second, we utilize multi-granularity sliding windows (with intervals of 5, 10, 12 frames, etc.) to extract image pairs. This multi-granularity design creates a natural difficulty curriculum: smaller windows test the model’s fine-grained visual dis-crimination capabilities, whereas larger windows demand high-level logical inference. Building upon this, we perform a crucial optimization step. To eliminate potential data noise and maximize training efficiency, we programmatically trim redundant operations from the end of many videos that are irrelevant to the core task (e.g., the robotic arm resetting). This refinement process ensures that every image pair ulti-mately selected is focused on a complete and uninterrupted task progression, thereby laying a solid foundation for sub-sequent high-quality annotation. 

3.2. Forward and Inverse Contrasting for Decoupling Order Bias 

We organized the data using an iterative strategy that pairs forward and inverse instances of the same example. At the micro-level of training, this creates immediate cognitive guidance for the model: any shallow heuristic acquired from one sample (e.g., “select the second image”) is instantly invalidated by the subsequent sample. As the model sequen-tially processes both the forward and reverse formulations of the same scenario, the majority of the visual and seman-tic context is held constant. This allows the model to focus its attention exclusively on the single variable of “order”, facilitating highly efficient learning. This immediate logical counterbalancing of forward and inverse examples compels the model to abandon its reliance on the prior knowledge of “order”. Instead, the model is forced to engage in deep and repeated scrutiny of the intrinsic causal logic within the image content to identify a unified judgment criterion valid under both sequences. 3.3. Data Statistics 

The large-scale Spatio-Temporal Causal Reasoning Chain-of-Thought (STCR-CoT) dataset constructed in this study comprises a total of 34.7 million high-quality samples. We have implemented a balanced design of forward and reverse samples at a global level. As illustrated in Figure 2(a), the dataset ensures that forward and reverse sequences each account for 50% through meticulous sequence construction. This global balancing design effectively addresses the issue of temporal bias. The dataset is composed of two parts: (1) Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Samples: These provide strong supervision signals. Each sample includes an image pair, a final answer label, and a structured reasoning chain with spatial details. (2) Tag Samples: These offer weak supervision signals. The data is obtained from numerous manipulation videos, with each sample containing only an image pair and its corresponding binary classification label. The dataset covers 79,610 unique task instances, exhibiting a well-diversified category distribution as shown in Fig-ure 2(b): Pick and Place (25.7%), Packing and Staging (20.9%), Appliance/Object Manipulation (20.5%), Clean-ing and Tidying (12.9%), Garment Manipulation (10.3%), Cooking and Food Preparation (9.0%), and others. Furthermore, a progressive difficulty curriculum is con-structed using a multi-scale sliding window sampling strat-egy (5–16 frames), as depicted in Figure 2(c). Samples from smaller windows (5–9 frames) focus on fine-grained visual discrimination, while those from larger windows (12–16 frames) emphasize macro-level reasoning. The distribution, where the number of samples increases with the window size, provides a systematic learning path for the model. In summary, with its substantial scale (34.7M samples), in-novative global forward-reverse balancing design, diverse task coverage (7 major categories, 79K unique tasks), and multi-scale curriculum planning, the STCR-CoT dataset pro-vides an ideal data foundation for training Vision-Language Models with robust spatio-temporal reasoning capabilities. This holistic balancing design philosophy ensures that the model can learn inherent reasoning abilities that are inde-pendent of the input sequence order. 

## 4. Method 

In end-to-end training, large models tend to learn superficial statistical shortcuts rather than a structured cognitive process that proceeds from precise perception to logical judgment. As a result, their decision-making lacks a solid evidential 4STCR: Progressive Training for Embodied VLMs 

foundation and causes “hallucinations”. To address this chal-lenge, we propose a progressive training paradigm based on Chain-of-Thought (CoT). This paradigm emulates the cog-nitive progression of a student who first intensively studies core knowledge and then engages in extensive practice for deep comprehension and internalization. 

4.1. CoT-Driven Spatial Perception Pre-training: From Shortcut Learning to Structural Grounding 

The fundamental vulnerability of Vision-Language Mod-els (VLMs) in spatiotemporal reasoning lies in Shortcut Learning . When optimized via sparse end-to-end labels, models tend to converge on “statistical coincidences”—such as the sequential order of input frames—rather than internal-izing the underlying physical laws of the environment. To rectify this, Stage 1 of our paradigm does not merely treat Chain-of-Thought (CoT) as a data format, but as a Struc-tural Bottleneck that redefines the model’s optimization trajectory. 

The “Perceive-then-Judge” Cognitive Scaffold. In this phase, we treat the model as a novice required to master a “core textbook” of spatial relationships. By extending the su-pervisory signal from the sparse final judgment to the dense intermediate reasoning chain (the spatial details ), we effectively transform the model’s internal computation. Instead of learning a direct, high-entropy mapping from pixels to answers, the model is compelled to follow a unidi-rectional information flow within its computational graph: 1. The Perception Constraint: The model must first compress raw visual features into verifiable spatial attributes (e.g., object contact, relative distance, and orientation). 2. The Logical Derivation: The final judgment must be derived exclusively from these extracted attributes, acting as a secondary inference layer. This mechanism acts as a cognitive scaffold . In terms of the model’s training dynamics, this dense supervision ensures that the gradients for the “judgment” logic are conditioned on the success of the “perception” module. Consequently, the optimal path for loss minimization is no longer to by-pass visual evidence, but to refine the accuracy of spatial perception. 

Countering the Incentive for Shortcut Discovery. A crit-ical risk in large-scale training is Incentive Conflict : if a model can achieve low training loss by exploiting a brit-tle correlation (e.g., associating the second image position with task completion), it will systematically ignore com-plex causal logic. Our progressive strategy mitigates this by front-loading the “rigorous reasoning” objective. By the time the model encounters larger, weakly-labeled datasets in Stage 2, its internal representation is already anchored in the grounded perceive-then-judge paradigm. This structural initialization makes the model resilient to the seductive but erroneous priors present in raw video data, ensuring that its decision-making process remains interpretable and physically consistent. 

4.2. Scaling Laws for a Scalable Training Paradigm 

After the model masters fine-grained reasoning ability through pre-training, it transitions to a weakly-supervised fine-tuning stage. This stage simulates a student moving from teacher-guided study to independent, extensive prac-tice. We initialize this stage with the model from the first phase and fine-tune it on a large-scale, diverse database containing only final-answer labels (e.g., labels indicating “which state is closer to task completion”). By removing supervision on the intermediate steps, we force the model to abandon its reliance on external “solutions” and instead independently and implicitly reconstruct the reasoning path-ways learned during pre-training. This entire approach is strategically designed to leverage a crucial advantage for scalability: data requiring only final labels is nearly infinite and exceptionally easy to acquire. Consequently, it is es-sential to validate the Scaling Laws to ensure that models can benefit from such massive volumes of data. Our experi-ments reveal a clear and positive performance curve: as the volume of weakly-supervised data increases, model perfor-mance exhibits an upward trend. This finding demonstrates the immense potential of our training paradigm. If the CoT pre-training stage is akin to studying a “text-book with answers and detailed explanations” to build a robust cognitive “framework” that cannot be bypassed with shortcuts, then this stage is analogous to practicing with a “massive problem bank with answers only” to achieve deep comprehension. Throughout this process, our pro-gressive fine-tuning strategy plays a crucial protective and guiding role. It encourages the model to actively apply and consolidate this internal structure in every judgment, thereby efficiently “distilling” the expensive, high-quality CoT knowledge and generalizing it onto the inexpensive, abundant weakly-supervised data.

B.2. Save boundary for imitation policy (VLA) 

A Vision-Language Model’s (VLM) preference output on consecutive frames from a robot’s manipulation process can provide a criterion for both safety protection and error recovery for imitation policies, such as the VLA. This endows fundamentally open-loop imitation policies with a real-time, closed-loop detection and recovery capability. Traditional imitation policies often struggle to recover from failure states during real-world deployment due to compounding errors, which can even lead to physical self-damage from large action errors. Intuitively, when a VLM provides a long sequence of negative preference feedback during a robot policy’s rollout, it indicates that the policy is progressively deviating from the task objective or entering an out-of-control state. By setting a tolerable threshold for the maximum length of a cumulative negative preference sequence, the agent’s erroneous execution can be terminated prematurely if this limit is exceeded.
