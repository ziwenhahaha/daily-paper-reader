Abstract 

While Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has advanced reasoning in Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs), pre-vailing frameworks suffer from a foundational methodological flaw: by distributing identical advantages across all generated tokens, these methods inherently dilute the learning signals essential for optimizing the critical, visually-grounded steps of multimodal reasoning. To bridge this gap, we formulate Token Visual De-pendency , quantifying the causal information gain of visual inputs via the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between visual-conditioned and text-only predictive distributions. Reveal-ing that this dependency is highly sparse and semantically pivotal, we introduce Perception-Grounded Policy Optimization (PGPO), which is a novel fine-grained credit assignment frame-work that dynamically reshapes advantages at the token level. Through a threshold-gated, mass-conserving mechanism, PGPO ac-tively amplifies learning signals for visually-dependent tokens while suppressing gradient noise from linguistic priors. Extensive ex-periments based on the Qwen2.5-VL series across seven challenging multimodal reason-ing benchmarks demonstrate that PGPO boosts models by 18.7% on average. Both theoretical and empirical analyses confirm that PGPO ef-fectively reduces gradient variance, prevents training collapse, and acts as a potent regu-larizer for robust, perception-grounded multi-modal reasoning. Code will be published on https://github.com/Yzk1114/PGPO.

In the context of multimodal reasoning, this poses a foundational flaw: by treating all tokens equally, it fundamentally dilutes the learning signals essential for optimizing those pivotal, perception-grounded reasoning steps. To address this limitation, it is imperative to quantify the causal contribution of the visual modal-ity to each individual reasoning step. We formalize this concept as Token Visual Dependency , mea-sured by the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence be-tween the model’s predictive distributions with and without visual conditioning. To validate the effectiveness of PGPO, we con-duct extensive experiments across seven challeng-ing multimodal reasoning benchmarks. Crucially, by performing fine-grained, token-level advantage re-shaping, PGPO reduces gradient variance, acceler-ates convergence, and increases visual dependency, thereby acting as a potent regularizer for robust, perception-grounded multimodal reasoning. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: • We formulate token visual dependency to quantify the causal impact of visual informa-tion, and empirically validate its sparse distri-bution and correlation with visual semantics. • We propose PGPO, a fine-grained credit as-signment framework that utilizes token-level advantage reshaping to amplify learning sig-nals for visually-dependent tokens while sup-pressing noise. • Extensive empirical evaluations and theoret-ical proofs demonstrate that PGPO achieves state-of-the-art performance on diverse mul-timodal benchmarks, covering mathematical, geometric, logical, multi-discipline reasoning and general VQA. 

2 Related Work 

Multimodal Reasoning. While Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate powerful reasoning capabilities in text-only domains (Guo et al., 2025), Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) (Bai et al., 2025a; Hurst et al., 2024) still exhibit a signif-icant performance gap (Wang et al., 2024b; Dong et al., 2025). Bridging this gap has driven the adap-tation of foundational reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms, such as PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) and DAPO (Yu et al., 2025a), to the multimodal space. Current multi-modal RL strategies predominantly focus on en-hancements external to the core learning algorithm. These broadly divide into data-centric approaches (Bai et al., 2025b; Li et al., 2025d; Chen et al., 2025d; Wei et al., 2025b; Chen et al., 2025b) and reward-centric methods that engineer perception-aware signals (Wang et al., 2025d; Ma et al., 2025), alongside rollout modifications and external vision tools (Liu et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025a). Despite these efforts, existing frameworks still broadcast a uniform learning signal to all tokens, highlighting a critical limitation in addressing the fine-grained demands of multimodal reasoning. 

Credit Assignment. The credit assignment prob-lem (Sutton and Barto, 1998; Arumugam et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2020) is a fundamental chal-lenge in reinforcement learning, seeking to identify which past actions are responsible for observed out-comes. This difficulty is notably exacerbated dur-ing the RLVR of LLMs, where sparse reward sig-nals must be accurately attributed to specific token-level steps within long generated sequences. 3 Preliminaries 

To study the perception in multimodal reasoning, this section first formalizes Token Visual Depen-dency via Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence to quantify the causal information gain from visual inputs at each reasoning step. We then empirically demonstrate that this visual dependency exhibits a highly sparse distribution, strongly correlating with semantic visual anchors and factual truthful-ness. Together, these theoretical and empirical findings expose the fundamental limitations of uni-form sequence-level rewards, directly motivating the core design of our Perception-Grounded Policy Optimization (PGPO) approach. 

3.1 Token Visual dependency 

We define a token’s visual dependency as the infor-mation gain provided by the visual context. This is quantified by computing the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the policy’s predictive distribution conditioned on the original image ver-sus a version without image, formally measuring the distributional shift attributable to visual input. Let I be the visual input, q be the text query, and 

o be the generated text output. At a given state 

st = ( q, o <t ), the visual dependency S at step t is: 

S(st, I ) := DKL (πθ(·| st, I ) ∥ πθ(·| st)) . (1) A high S value indicates that the image provides critical information for the token prediction at step 

t, marking it as a key moment of visually-grounded reasoning. By quantifying Bayesian surprise—the information gained when updating a language prior to a visually-grounded posterior—KL divergence uniquely isolates the causal intervention of the vi-sual modality on each generated token. Further-more, its strict non-negativity, mathematically guar-anteed by Gibbs’ inequality. These favorable theo-retical properties yield a robust credit assignment signal for reinforcement learning. More theoreti-cal analyses can be found in Appendix D, which is fundamentally grounded in the principles of the information theory. 

3.2 Empirical Analysis of Visual Dependency 

Having established the theoretical formulation of token-level visual dependency S, we proceed to empirically validate its behavior in multimodal rea-soning tasks. In this section, we present a com-prehensive analysis of its distributional sparsity and categorize tokens based on their roles in the reasoning process to compare their average S on Qwen2.5-VL-3B. Together, these empirical find-ings substantiate S as a interpretable metric for guiding policy optimization in visual reasoning. Detailed settings are provided in Appendix C. 

Distributional Sparsity of Visual Dependency. 

Using our proposed metric, we analyze the empir-ical distribution of token-level visual dependency. We perform inference on the vision-dominant sub-set of the MathVerse (Zhang et al., 2024) bench-mark, computing S for every token across all trajec-tories. As visualized in Figure 2(a), the frequency drops exponentially as S increases when plotted on 3a logarithmic scale. This highly skewed distribu-tion confirms that only a small fraction of tokens are critical for visually-grounded reasoning. Fur-thermore, Figure 2(b) underscores their semantic importance: content tokens (e.g., numbers, geomet-ric concepts, verbs and adjectives) exhibit a higher average S than non-content tokens. Consequently, applying a uniform learning signal across all tokens dilutes the overall reward by incorrectly crediting irrelevant, non-perceptual steps. 

Higher Dependency for Visual Anchors. Next, we investigate whether S is associated with critical visual information. We utilize generated trajec-tories from the MathVerse vision-dominant sub-set and annotate visual anchor words by GPT-5—defined as tokens that are impossible to prop-erly infer without observing the image. Figure 2(c) reveals that these visual anchor tokens exhibit significantly higher average S compared to non-anchor tokens. This confirms that S effectively captures semantic visual grounding, making it a meaningful metric for credit assignment to enhance visual reasoning in RLVR. 

Lower Dependency in Hallucinations. Finally, we explore the correlation between S and model hallucinations using the CHAIR (Rohrbach et al., 2019) benchmark. By generating image captions, we extract and categorize tokens into truthful and hallucinated. As shown in Figure 2(d), truthful tokens demonstrate higher average S than hallu-cinated ones. This suggests that hallucinated con-cepts are primarily driven by the model’s language prior rather than actual visual input, further validat-ing S as a possible proxy for factual visual reliance.

4.4 Theoretical Analysis 

In this section, we summarize the theoretical prop-erties of our proposed Perception-Grounded Policy Optimization (PGPO) framework, with detailed proofs provided in the Appendix. First, our method strictly mitigates the policy gradient variance inherent in GRPO (Appendix E). By scaling the gradient contribution of visually-independent tokens by ε2 ≪ 1, it acts as a causal filter that isolates valid reasoning signals and sup-presses noise. Second, we prove the necessity of advantage mass conservation ( P|oi|

5 Experiments 

5.1 Setups Models, Data, and Baselines. Following Wang et al. (2025a), we apply PGPO to the Qwen2.5-VL-3B/7B models and train on the ViRL39K

We benchmark our models against a comprehensive suite of open-source reasoning LVLMs, including MM-Eureka-7B (Meng et al., 2025), VL-Rethinker-7B (Wang et al., 2025a), R1-ShareVL-7B (Yao et al., 2025), NoisyRollout-7B (Liu et al., 2025). We reproduce and compare the performance of current strong RLVR methods for multimodal reasoning, includ-ing GRPO, DAPO, PAPO (Wang et al., 2025d), and VPPO (Huang et al., 2026). 

Training Details. Following Wang et al. (2025d), our models are trained for 2 epochs with a learn-ing rate of 1e-6 and a rollout batch size of 384 .We set the maximum response length to 2048 and disable reference KL penalty. PGPO follows the DAPO recipe, incorporating dynamic sampling, clip-higher, and a token-level policy gradient loss. We set the bi-level threshold τ = 0 .4 and the boost-ing coefficient β = 2 .0. More hyperparameter details are available in Appendix A. 

Evaluation Benchmarks. We conduct compre-hensive evaluation on seven diverse multimodal rea-soning benchmarks. Following Wang et al. (2025d), we use an exact-match scoring methodology, elimi-nating reliance on LLM-as-a-judge systems. The benchmarks span mathematical, geometric, logical, and multi-discipline reasoning, including Dyna-Math (Zou et al., 2024), Geo3k (Lu et al., 2021), MathVerse (Zhang et al., 2024), MathVision (Wang et al., 2024a), MMK12 (Meng et al., 2025), Log-icVista (Xiao et al., 2024), and MMMU-Pro (Yue et al., 2024) (see Appendix J for a full breakdown). Furthermore, the necessity of Equation 7 is em-pirically verified by the PGPO w/o Norm. variant. Removing the sum-preserving normalization signif-icantly degrades performance, empirically corrobo-rating our mathematical derivation that unnormal-ized advantage scaling disrupts policy update con-straints via a non-zero mean shift. Ultimately, inte-grating threshold-gated suppression, signal boost-ing, and normalization into the full PGPO configu-ration yields the optimal synergy.

7 Conclusion 

In this work, we propose Perception-Grounded Policy Optimization (PGPO), which is dynami-cally reshapes token-level advantages through a threshold-gated, sum-preserving mechanism, ac-tively amplifying learning signals for perceptual anchors while suppressing modality-independent gradient noise. Extensive evaluations across seven 8complex multimodal benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of PGPO. Both theoretical and em-pirical analyses confirm that PGPO significantly reduces gradient variance and yields highly robust, perception-grounded multimodal reasoning. 

Limitations 

While PGPO significantly advances multimodal RLVR, we acknowledge certain limitations for fu-ture research. While the hyperparameters of our threshold-gated mechanism (threshold τ and boost-ing coefficient β) generalize robustly across our benchmarks, they were optimized for our specific training distribution and may require re-tuning when applied to other datasets or model architec-tures. Furthermore, limited by our computational resources, our experiments validate PGPO on mod-els up to the 7B parameter scale. Although these results indicate a positive scaling trend, its efficacy on large-scale models remains to be verified.

A Implementation Details 

Training Details. Our implementation is built upon the EasyR1 framework (Zheng et al., 2025; Sheng et al., 2024). All experiments were con-ducted using PyTorch 2.8.0 with CUDA 12.6. The base models for our experiments are the open-source Qwen2.5-VL-3B and Qwen2.5-VL-7B. We train all models for two epochs on the ViRL39K 

dataset (Wang et al., 2025a). The vision tower is un-frozen during training. For the online RL process, we generate 5 responses per question. Our overall reward signal is defined as a weighted combination of an accuracy score and a formatting score, for-mulated as: R = 0 .9Sacc + 0 .1Sformat , where Sacc 

is a binary indicator of correctness (1 for correct, 0 otherwise), and Sformat evaluates structural compli-ance (1 if the response adheres to the prescribed for-mat, 0 otherwise). Our training objective follows the DAPO recipe, incorporating dynamic sampling, clip-higher, and a token-level policy gradient loss, without a KL divergence penalty. We reproduced the results of PAPO and VPPO under a unified experimental setup. In our main experiments, we uniformly set the bi-level threshold τ = 0 .4 and the boosting coefficient β = 2 .0 for the training and evaluation of all models. To ensure a fair com-parison, we evaluated DAPO on Qwen2.5-VL-7B using the optimal pre-collapse checkpoint at step 190 (202 in total), as the model suffered from en-tropy explosion during the late stages of training. All models were trained on a cluster of NVIDIA H100 80GB GPUs. All key hyperparameters for the optimizer, RL process, and evaluation are de-tailed in Table 6.

C.3 Hallucination Analysis on the CHAIR Benchmark 

To understand the relationship between visual de-pendency and factual correctness, we extended our analysis to the standard object hallucination bench-mark, CHAIR (Caption Hallucination Assessment with Image Relevance) (Rohrbach et al., 2019). We sampled 5,000 images from the MSCOCO (Lin et al., 2015) 2014 validation set. For each image, we prompted Qwen2.5-VL-3B with: "Describe this image in detail." We employed the official CHAIR evaluation script, leverag-ing MSCOCO ground-truth object annotations (synsets), to assess the generated sequences. First, we parsed the generated caption to extract all men-tioned objects using the standard CHAIR synonym dictionary. Subsequently, these extracted objects are cross-referenced against the image’s ground-truth annotations; tokens corresponding to objects present in the ground truth are classified as truth-ful, whereas those representing absent objects are designated as hallucinated. We isolated these two subsets of tokens across the 5,000 captions and computed their average S

14 values. The finding (Figure 2(d)) that hallucinated tokens exhibit a significantly lower visual depen-dency provides evidence that multimodal hallucina-tions in LVLMs are largely driven by over-reliance on language priors rather than visual processing. D.3 Superiority of KL Divergence 

A seemingly intuitive alternative to quantify vi-sual dependency is to compute the direct reduction in entropy before and after observing the image: 

∆H = H(πθ(· | st)) − H(πθ(· | st, I )) . However, we exclusively employ KL divergence due to two fundamental theoretical advantages: 

Capturing Distributional Shift. Entropy purely measures the volume of uncertainty, ignoring the se-mantic direction of the probability shift. Consider a scenario where the text-only policy is highly con-fident but factually incorrect (e.g., hallucinating a 15 common object based on language priors), yield-ing a very low entropy H(πθ(· | st)) . When the image is introduced, the model correctly grounds its prediction on visual evidence, yielding a differ-ent token but with similarly high confidence (low 

H(πθ(· | st, I )) ). In this case, the entropy differ-ence is near zero ( ∆H ≈ 0), failing to detect the critical intervention of the visual modality. In con-trast, KL divergence measures the cross-entropy penalty; because the original text prior assigned low probability to the visually-grounded truth, the KL divergence will be heavily magnified, perfectly capturing the visual correction. 

Strict Non-Negativity for Stable Credit Assign-ment. Depending on the clarity of the image, the visual input might actually introduce ambigu-ity compared to an overconfident language prior, which makes ∆H negative. Using a metric that can be negative severely complicates advantage reshaping and introduces instability in RL credit assignment. By Gibbs’ inequality, KL divergence mathematically guarantees S(st, I ) ≥ 0. This en-sures a strict, well-behaved lower bound where 0

strictly corresponds to identical distributions (zero visual reliance).

I Analysis of the Training Dataset 

This section details the ViRL39K dataset (Wang et al., 2025a), the foundation of our reinforcement learning experiments. We selected this dataset be-cause its core properties are ideally suited for train-ing robust multimodal reasoning models. 

Topical Diversity and Reasoning Depth. 

ViRL39K exhibits extensive topical diversity, comprising approximately 39,000 queries across challenging domains such as mathematics, physics, chemistry, biology, and chart interpretation. This breadth mitigates overfitting to narrow task distributions and fosters the transferable reasoning skills essential for general-purpose models. 

Suitability for Reinforcement Learning. Cru-cially, ViRL39K ensures strict verifiability. Each programmatically generated instance pairs with an unambiguous ground-truth answer. This determin-ism is indispensable for Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR), facilitating a reli-able, automated reward function. By employing a accuracy signal, the dataset circumvents the need for subjective, model-based evaluation, ensuring the learning process is guided entirely by objec-tive correctness. For comprehensive details on the dataset’s construction and statistical breakdown, we refer readers to the original publication.

We selected this suite to encompass a broad spectrum of chal-lenges—ranging from domain-specific mathemat-ical problem-solving to general logical reason-ing—thereby enabling a comprehensive assessment of the model’s capabilities. 

Mathematical and Geometric Reasoning. This category constitutes the core of our evaluation, fo-cusing on in-depth, domain-specific skills. • DynaMath (Zou et al., 2024) evaluates the 

robustness of visual mathematical reasoning. Utilizing program-based generation, it dynam-ically alters numerical values and functional graphs in seed problems to assess model gen-eralization over memorization. We use the 

sample vriant1 for evaluation. • Geo3k (Lu et al., 2021) is a large-scale bench-mark targeting high-school geometry. Its dense annotations in a formal language make it highly suitable for evaluating interpretable, symbolic reasoning paradigms. • MathVerse (Zhang et al., 2024) investigates the reliance of MLLMs on visual versus tex-tual inputs. By providing six problem variants that systematically shift information from text to diagrams, it mitigates textual redundancy and enables a fine-grained analysis of visual comprehension. We use the multi-choice ques-tions in test-mini subset for evaluation. • MATH-Vision (Wang et al., 2024a) sources problems from authentic mathematics compe-titions (e.g., AMC, Math Kangaroo). Span-ning 16 disciplines and 5 difficulty levels, 21 it serves as a rigorous testbed for advanced, competition-level multimodal reasoning. We use the whole test subset for evaluation. • MMK12 (Meng et al., 2025) focuses on K-12 multimodal mathematical problems, provid-ing a robust evaluation of the foundational reasoning skills requisite for advanced appli-cations. 

Logical Reasoning. To evaluate broader cogni-tive abilities beyond mathematics, we incorporate a benchmark dedicated to formal logic. • LogicVista (Xiao et al., 2024) assesses gen-eral logical cognition across five core dimen-sions: inductive, deductive, numerical, spatial, and mechanical reasoning. Spanning diverse visual formats, it tests the foundational rea-soning capabilities essential for complex mul-timodal tasks. 

Multi-discipline Reasoning. We utilize a rigor-ous college-level benchmark to evaluate complex, cross-disciplinary multimodal integration. • MMMU-Pro (Yue et al., 2024) is an advanced iteration of the MMMU benchmark, explic-itly designed to preclude text-only solutions. By filtering out textual shortcuts, expand-ing multiple-choice options, and introducing a vision-only format, it rigorously tests the model’s capacity to synthesize visual and tex-tual information in academic contexts.
