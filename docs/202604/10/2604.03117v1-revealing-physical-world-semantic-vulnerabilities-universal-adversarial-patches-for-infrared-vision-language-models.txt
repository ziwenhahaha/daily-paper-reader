# Revealing Physical-World Semantic Vulnerabilities: Universal Adversarial Patches for Infrared Vision-Language Models 

## Chengyin Hu Yuxian Dong Yikun Guo Xiang Chen Junqi Wu Jiahuan Long Yiwei Wei Tingsong Jiang Wen Yao 

# Abstract 

Infrared vision-language models (IR-VLMs) are becoming a key paradigm for multimodal perception in low-visibility en-vironments, yet their adversarial robustness remains largely uncharacterized. Existing adversarial patch methods are mainly designed for RGB closed-set recognition and do not directly transfer to the open-ended semantics and physical constraints of infrared VLM deployment. To bridge this gap, we propose Universal Curved-Grid Patch (UCGP), a universal physical adversarial patch framework tailored to IR-VLMs. UCGP combines Curved-Grid Mesh (CGM) parameterization for con-tinuous, low-frequency, and deployable patch generation with a unified representation-driven objective over subspace departure, topology disruption, and stealth. We optimize the patch using Meta Differential Evolution and incorporate EOT-augmented TPS deformation modeling to improve robustness under physical deployment and domain shift. Under the representation-visible interface setting, UCGP learns a single physically deployable patch that can be reused across samples, target regions, and downstream tasks. Instead of manipulating labels or prompts, it attacks visual representation geometry by disrupting the clean-category manifold and weakening cross-modal semantic alignment. Extensive experiments show that this universal patch consistently degrades semantic understanding across diverse IR-VLM architectures, while maintaining non-trivial cross-model transferability, stable cross-dataset generalization, real-world physical effectiveness, and resilience against defenses. Our find-ings provide the first evidence that a universal physical patch can reliably compromise semantic understanding in IR-VLMs, exposing an important robustness blind spot in current infrared multimodal systems. 

Keywords: Infrared vision-language models, Physical adver-sarial attack, Universal curved-grid patch 

# 1 Introduction 

Vision-language models (VLMs) achieve strong performance on retrieval, captioning, and visual question answering by aligning visual and textual representations[ 1 ]. As this paradigm extends to infrared perception, recent work has introduced datasets, models, and benchmarks for infrared semantic understanding[ 2–6]. In this paper, IR-VLM denotes a VLM backbone adapted to infrared tasks such as classification, captioning, and VQA. This shift enlarges the attack surface from recognition errors to semantic deviations in generated descriptions and answers. 

Figure 1: Overview of the attack mechanism. Prior work has shown that carefully designed perturbations can deceive deep visual models in both digital and physical settings[ 7, 8 ]. As multimodal perception evolves from closed-set recognition to open-ended semantic understanding, the security question also changes: failures no longer stop at wrong labels, but can propagate into generated descriptions and an-swers. For infrared vision-language systems, this risk remains largely uncharacterized. Existing efforts provide only a partial picture, and it is still unclear whether a physically deployable shared patch can systematically manipulate semantics once infrared perception is coupled with language reasoning[ 9–12 ]. This gap motivates the central question of this paper: can a universal physical patch induce stable open-ended semantic deviations in IR-VLMs? We answer this question by formu-lating and validating a universal physical adversarial patch setting for IR-VLMs. Compared with prior attacks on RGB VLMs, physically attacking IR-VLMs is harder for two rea-sons. First, infrared imagery has weaker texture and more noise-dominated statistics[ 13 , 14 ], so many visible-domain high-frequency patterns transfer poorly. Second, the attack must break visual-language alignment under open-ended out-puts and physical constraints, which makes robust cross-task objective design substantially more difficult[ 9, 10 , 15 ]. To address these challenges, we propose UCGP (Universal Curved-Grid Patch), a universal physical adversarial patch framework tailored to IR-VLMs. UCGP integrates deployment-friendly patch parameterization, unified objective design, and phys-ical robustness modeling to deliver stable and effective at-tacks. It learns a single shared patch that is reused across samples without per-sample re-optimization. # 2 Related Work 

Infrared physical attacks. Physical adversarial patches reveal that deployable perturbations can remain effective after capture, viewpoint change, and scale variation[ 7 , 8, 16 ]. Recent attack research has also expanded to RGB-image-based VLMs and LVLMs, but this line is still dominated by visible-light digital attacks rather than physically deployable infrared semantic attacks[9, 10, 15]. In infrared scenarios, prior physical attacks have mainly tar-geted pedestrian detectors. Early studies used adversarial cloth-ing or insulating materials to hide thermal signatures[ 17 , 18 ]; more recent works moved toward attachable structured pertur-bations such as blocks, curves, and grid patterns to improve deployability, stealth, and multi-view robustness[ 11 , 12 , 19 ]. These works provide important priors for real-world deploy-ment, but have not yet studied whether a single shared physical patch can systematically manipulate the open-ended semantic understanding of IR-VLMs across classification, captioning, and VQA. In our method, query-based optimization is built on derivative-free search, including differential evolution and related query-based optimizers[20–23]. Physical robustness is modeled separately through EOT and TPS to better match real deployment conditions[7, 24]. 

IR-VLMs and benchmarks. Recent work has extended VLMs and MLLMs to infrared semantic understanding. Infrared-LLaVA and IRGPT advance model adaptation and in-frared image-text data construction[ 2, 3], while RGB-Th-Bench, IF-Bench, and ThermEval benchmark paired understanding, open-ended infrared reasoning, and temperature reasoning[ 4– 6]. These studies ask whether models can understand infrared con-tent; our work asks whether their semantics can be physically manipulated. 

Multimodal security. As multimodal systems become stronger, robustness issues in multimodal reasoning have be-come increasingly clear. Recent studies on RGB VLMs and LVLMs show failures at both the image and cross-modal seman-tic levels[ 9, 10 , 15 ]. Most of these studies, however, operate in visible-light digital settings rather than infrared physical deployment. Rather than optimizing explicit target texts or labels, our method attacks the clean-category manifold in visual representation space, reducing dependence on prompts and supporting a unified objective across classification, captioning, and VQA.

To address this, we propose Curved-Grid Mesh (CGM) parameterization, which constrains the patch to a low-dimensional geometric space composed of sparsely gated curved edges. ## 3.4 Optimization Procedure 

Evaluating a candidate patch involves soft rendering, regional pasting, EOT+TPS transformation, and IR-VLM forward in-ference, making the objective function non-differentiable with respect to 𝜃 . Meanwhile, CGM restricts the parameter space to a low-dimensional structured form where each evaluation is expensive and noisy. Given these problem characteristics, we adopt MetaDE[ 28 ] for derivative-free search to balance explo-ration with stable convergence under a limited query budget. This is the universal patch setting studied in this paper.

## 4.2 Comparison with Baselines 

Classification Results. We compare our method with prior baselines on both classification and cross-modal tasks. Table 1 reports the classification ASR and image naturalness (Nat.) of all methods across four CLIP backbones. Overall, UCGP achieves the best or clearly leading ASR on all four backbones, while ranking first in naturalness on OpenAI CLIP, OpenCLIP, and EVA-CLIP and second on Meta-CLIP. This indicates a better balance between attack effectiveness and stealthiness rather than gaining attack strength at the expense of visual quality. Concretely, on OpenAI CLIP, UCGP reaches 94.33% ASR, far above the strongest baseline at 22.67%, and even on Meta-CLIP, the most robust backbone, it still attains 34.33% ASR versus 20.67% for the best baseline. The ASR differences across backbones also reveal inconsistent representation robustness: OpenAI CLIP is the most vulnerable, Meta-CLIP is the most robust with the lowest ASR, and EVA-CLIP and OpenCLIP fall in between. Notably, UCGP outperforms the strongest baseline by a large margin on every backbone—most prominently on OpenAI CLIP, where the ASR gap exceeds 70%—while still maintaining or improving naturalness scores, suggesting that disrupting the clean-category subspace is a more effective and stealthy strategy than the structured patch baselines considered here. Fig. 5 further provides representative visual cases that corroborate these performance trends. 

Captioning and VQA. To verify that the shared patch dis-rupts not merely a classification decision boundary but rather the more general cross-modal semantic alignment, we further evaluate the same patch on captioning and VQA. Following the LLM-as-a-judge protocol described above, Table 2 reports the relative drops in description consistency and answer correctness; if stable degradation persists across different text interfaces, this indicates that our objective design generalizes well across tasks overall. Fig. 6 provides representative examples. Together with Table 2, the results show that UCGP delivers the strongest attack in all 6 captioning and all 6 VQA settings under the evaluated model combinations. As a representative example, under the OpenAI CLIP encoder, LLaVA-1.5’s captioning score drops by 17.24%, more than three times the strongest baseline drop of 5.03%; the VQA correctness score falls by 16.87%, again clearly exceeding the baseline’s 10.93%. The remaining models follow the same pattern. Fig. 6 further shows that this degradation is not a local lexical effect but a systematic shift in subject category, action attribute, and scene-relation understanding, indicating that our method disrupts cross-modal semantic alignment more effectively than prior baselines rather than merely exploiting a classification decision boundary. 

## 4.3 Physical Attack Experiments 

To verify real-world effectiveness, we conduct physical deploy-ment experiments by attaching the optimized patch to the target clothing region and capturing infrared images from multiple distances and viewpoints in indoor and outdoor scenes. Physical experiment videos are provided in the supplementary material. As shown in Fig. 7, the patch remains visible in infrared and induces semantic deviations, yielding average ASRs of 70.76% indoors and 73.96% outdoors. The attack is strongest at short and medium distances but degrades at the farthest ranges and some views, with the weakest cases around 11.0 m, indicat-ing nontrivial yet viewpoint- and distance-dependent physical robustness. 

## 4.4 Cross-Dataset Transfer 

In real-world deployment, the training and test domains are often mismatched. We therefore optimize the shared patch on one infrared dataset and apply it directly to others without fine-tuning. Fig. 8(a) summarizes ASR, Δ𝑠 target , and 𝑀 adv

## 4.5 Cross-Model Transfer 

Cross-model transfer tests whether the patch depends on a spe-cific backbone. Fig. 8(b) reports ASR, Δ𝑠 target , and 𝑀 adv for all surrogate–victim pairings. The same-backbone diagonal is con-sistently strongest, but heterogeneous transfer remains clearly above near-random behavior. OpenAI CLIP tends to produce the strongest transferable patches, whereas Meta-CLIP and EVA-CLIP show more variable transfer. Detailed pairwise results are provided in the appendix.

# 5 Defense Evaluation 

To evaluate system-level security, we test two practical defenses: adversarial training (AT) and Digital Watermarking (DW). DW is an image-repair defense. In DW-1, the repair model is blind to the patch location, whereas in DW-2 it is given the patch-location prior for guided restoration. We evaluate AT by testing the original patch on the hardened model (AT-1) and adaptively re-optimizing the patch against it (AT-2). Table 3 summarizes the results. Under these two defenses, DW-1 and DW-2 reduce ASR to 45.33% and 24.67%, while AT-1 and AT-2 give 15.33% and 78.00%, respectively. These results show that current defenses can mitigate the attack, but none provides a complete defense. Among the four settings, AT-1 is the strongest. Yet the high residual ASR under AT-2 shows that even after the model learns the previous universal patch, re-optimization keeps the attack highly effective. This gap between AT-1 and AT-2 suggests that robustness learned against a fixed universal patch does not fully transfer to newly searched patch instances under the same threat model. Likewise, the clear advantage of DW-2 over DW-1 indicates that localization priors are important for image-repair defenses in infrared settings, where patch effects are region-constrained rather than globally distributed. Taken together, these findings imply that effective protection against physical semantic attacks on IR-VLMs likely requires combining model-side robustness with patch-aware restoration rather than relying on either strategy alone. 8Table 4: Cross-category extension results.

# 6 Cross-Category Extension 

To verify the proposed method’s generality, we apply the same query-based UCGP optimization pipeline to dog, car, and bicycle in addition to person, without any category-specific redesign (Table 4). Both Δ𝑠 target and 𝑀 adv remain positively shifted across all tested categories, with dog giving the strongest overall result. Specifically, dog reaches 98.67% ASR, with the highest Δ𝑠 target (106.25) and 𝑀 adv (76.43), even exceeding person (94.33%), whereas car and bicycle remain lower at 61.00% and 56.00% ASR. The consistent positive margins also indicate stable semantic displacement across categories. Overall, these results show that UCGP generalizes beyond a single target class and can support broader IR-VLM robustness evaluation. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We present the first systematic study of universal physical ad-versarial patch attacks on infrared vision-language models and introduce UCGP , a universal physical patch framework for this setting. By combining curved-grid parameterization, unified objective design, and EOT+TPS-based robustness modeling, 

UCGP learns a single deployable patch that remains broadly effective across samples, tasks, and physical scenes. Experi-ments show consistent semantic degradation in classification, captioning, and VQA, together with meaningful cross-dataset and cross-model transfer and consistent real-world physical effectiveness. These results establish a concrete evaluation setting for physical semantic attacks on IR-VLMs, reveal a previously underexplored semantic vulnerability, and highlight the need for stronger system-level defenses against cross-modal semantic manipulation.
