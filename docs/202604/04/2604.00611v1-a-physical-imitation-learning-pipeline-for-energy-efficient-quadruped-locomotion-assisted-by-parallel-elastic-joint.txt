# A Physical Imitation Learning Pipeline for Energy-Efficient Quadruped Locomotion Assisted by Parallel Elastic Joint 

Huyue Ma 1, Yurui Jin 1, Helmut Hauser 1, and Rui Wu ∗11School of Engineering Mathematics and Technology, University of Bristol, and the Bristol Robotics Laboratory, BS8 1QU, United Kingdom 

Abstract 

Due to brain-body co-evolution, animals’ intrinsic body dynamics play a crucial role in energy-efficient locomotion, which shares control effort between active muscles and passive body dynamics—a principle known as Embodied Physical Intelligence. In contrast, robot bodies are often designed with one centralised controller that typically suppress the intrinsic body dynamics instead of exploiting it. We introduce Physical Imitation Learning (PIL), which distils a Reinforcement Learning (RL) control policy into physically implementable body responses that can be directly offloaded to passive Parallel Elastic Joints (PEJs), enabling therefore the body to imitate part of the controlled behaviour. Meanwhile, the residual policy commands the motors to recover the RL policy’s performance. The results is an overall reduced energy consumption thanks to outsourcing parts of the control policy to the PEJs. Here we show in simulated quadrupeds, that our PIL approach can offloads up to 87% of mechanical power to PEJs on flat terrain and 18% on rough terrain. Because the body design is distilled from—rather than jointly optimised with—the control policy, PIL realises brain–body co-design without expanding the search space with body design parameters, providing a computationally efficient route to task-specific Embodied Physical Intelligence applicable to a wide range of joint-based robot morphologies. 

Keywords: Brain-body co-evolution, decentralised control, morphological computation, phys-ical intelligence, quadruped, reinforcement learning 

# 1 Introduction 

Animals achieve energy-efficient legged locomotion by offloading part of their control and ac-tuation effort to their bodies’ passive dynamic responses–a phenomenon known as Embodied Physical Intelligence. The intrinsic passive mechanics of the limbs can help generating gait-like motion, while muscles provide additional actuation and control to modulate this motion and compensate for environmental perturbations and task-dependent demands. Animal’s locomo-tion is thereby a compplex interplay between active neuromuscular activities and passive body dynamics [1,2]. This is a natural consequence of brain-body co-evolution that fine-tunes the non-linear compliance of musculoskeletal system to store elastic energy and release it in synchrony with muscle actuation [3–5]. This effective exploitation of Embodied Physical Intelligence has beenidentified as one of the key aspects to animals superior energy efficiency [6–8]. In contrast, legged robots rely mostly on centralised control, where bodies are not designed for it passive dy-namics. Moreover, the controller are often designed to suppress intrinsic body dynamics instead

As a result, today’s quadruped robots still exhibit substantially higher energy demand (often quantified by the dimensionless Cost of Transport, i.e., energy required per unit weight per unit distance: CoT ≈ 0.5–1.5) [9, 10] than comparable terrestrial mammals, which commonly achieve CoT ≈ 0.2–0.5 [11]. Systematically realising Embodied Physical Intelligence has therefore emerged as a central aspiration of robotics research [7, 8, 12]. Currently, Embodied Physical Intelligence in robots are mostly designed based on intuition and/or bio-inspiration. Passive Dynamic Walkers are capable of stable bipedal walking without active control, which is achieved by offloading the stabilisation control to the passive dynamic response of a carefully tuned mechanical body [13–15]. Robot legs that replicate animal’s tendon-coordinated articulation have shown self-regulated motion and high energy efficiency, which offload animal-like low-level control to the body [16–18]. At full-body level, motion patterns extracted from animal locomotion have been embedded into optimised mechanical bodies to mimic animal body motions [19, 20]. Beyond intuition and bio-inspiration, data-driven and learning-based co-design approaches have begun to explore brain–body co-evolution to jointly optimise body and control [21–23]. This allows Embodied Physical Intelligence to be designed for specific control tasks beyond those encountered by animals or achieved by manually tuned mechanisms. However, co-evolution typically requires searching over both the controller parameter and morphological design spaces. Because body and control are strongly coupled, this can lead to a combinatorial or high-dimensional optimisation problem that is susceptible to the curse of dimensionality, and scaling such search efficiently remains an open challenge [7, 24, 25]. The field needs a computationally-lightweight, generalisable design framework that can derive body designs from task-specific target control policies. Dynamic Movement Primitives (DMPs) show how complex movements can be generated by coupling weak control signals with stable nonlinear dynamic systems [26, 27]. Although DMPs is formulated for fully actuated systems, it provides a theoretical basis that a passive nonlinear dynamic body operating in parallel with active control can fully recover a complex movement policy. This insight resonates with the findings in parallel elastic systems, which pro-vide passive assistive force to robot actuators–analogous to passive exoskeletons–for improving energy efficiency [21, 28, 29]. In 2025, we introduced an Elastic Rolling Cam (ERC), which is an inverse-designable mechanical joint capable of producing a wide range of nonlinear torque-angle responses [30]. This enables parallel elastic systems that can be mechanically designed to produce specific physical responses tailored to assist a given control policy. We introduce Physical Imitation Learning (PIL), a computationally-lightweight, generalis-able design pipeline that decentralises RL control policies between nonlinear Parallel Elastic Joints (PEJs) and a central controller, as illustrated in Fig. 1. PIL trains a parent policy through standard RL, then redistribute the actuation effort by decomposing the policy into two components: an active component that commands the motors, and a passive component that can be fully offloaded to the PEJs. The passive component is designed by distilling the parent policy into nonlinear torque-angle responses that can be physically implemented using the Elastic Rolling Cam’s mechanical principle [30]. This enables the robot body to physically imitate the regular movement patterns generated by the parent policy. The residual part of the parent policy forms the active component that output motor commands. During operation, the PEJs function like passive exoskeleton by generating passive torques to assist motor actuation, while the active component naturally synchronises with the PEJs–thanks to distillation–to fully recover the parent policy’s behaviour. In the present study, we demonstrate that PIL can offload up to 87% of mechanical power to PEJs for a simulated Unitree Go2 quadruped robot, without sacrificing robustness and adaptability of the RL policy. PIL provides a generalisable framework to realising task-specific Embodied Physical Intelligence that use PEJ to physically imitate and thereby assist a RL control policy, which can be applied to a wide range of joint-based robot morphologies. 2Figure 1: The Physical Imitation Learning pipeline. # 2 The Physical Imitation Learning Pipeline 

2.1 Simulation Environment 

All experiments are conducted in IsaacLab [31], which provides high-fidelity rigid-body physics and GPU-accelerated massively parallel execution. We simulate 4096 instances of the Unitree Go2 quadruped robot in parallel. The Go2 model comprises 12 actuated degrees of freedom (three per leg: hip abduction/adduction, thigh flexion/extension, and calf flexion/extension) and is parameterised with the manufacturer-specified mass distribution, joint limits, and actu-ator dynamics. The simulation runs at a control frequency of 20 Hz (time step dt =0 .05 s). The terrain follows the default IsaacLab curriculum, in which seven discrete difficulty levels (Level 0–6) are defined as mixtures of five procedurally generated primitives: flat ground, random rough surfaces, box obstacles, pyramid stairs, and inverted pyramid stairs. Higher levels progressively increase the proportion and severity of complex primitives. Terrain Level 0 is effectively flat, while Level 6 features random roughness up to 7.3 cm, box obstacles up to 15 cm, and stair heights up to 17 cm. An agent is promoted to the next difficulty level once it traverses more than half the distance dictated by the maximum episode length and the commanded velocity. The policy network receives observations—joint positions, joint velocities, base orientation, base angular velocity, and a height scan of the local terrain—and outputs target joint positions. Commanded linear velocities are sampled uniformly from 0–2.0 m/s during the curriculum pre-training that trains an initial policy which serves as the starting point for energy-efficiency training. During energy-efficiency training and evaluation (Section 2.3), the range is narrowed to 0.5–2.0 m/s to exclude near-stationary commands where the energy metric becomes 3ill-conditioned. The Parallel Elastic Joints (PEJs) are not physically simulated; they are derived through the distillation process described in Section 2.2 and applied analytically in post-processing. Fig. 1 illustrates the overall PIL pipeline, showing the separation between the RL training loop (left) and the distillation stage (right) in which torque–angle data from each joint are fitted to produce PEJ characteristic curves. 

2.2 Distillation 

The distillation stage extracts the passive component of the parent policy and encodes it into nonlinear PEJ characteristic curves. Because PEJs produce torque as a function of joint angle alone, the goal is to find, for each joint, a torque–angle mapping τPEJ (q) that absorbs as much of the actuator effort as possible, leaving only a residual torque for the motor. 2.3 Training Protocol 

Training proceeds in three stages: pre-training an initial policy via curriculum learning, followed by two parallel continued-training branches—a baseline branch and a co-design branch—that both add a Cost-of-Transport (CoT) penalty but differ in whether PEJs are present. The baseline branch trains with CoT alone and serves as a comparison, while the co-design branch trains with CoT and online PEJ distillation to realise brain–body co-evolution. 42.3.1 Stage 1: Pre-training 

An initial policy is trained from scratch using Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO) [32] via the RSL-RL framework [33]. The policy is a multi-layer perceptron (three hidden layers of 512, 256, and 128 units with ELU activations) that maps observations to target joint positions. Training uses 4096 parallel environments, collecting 24 time steps per environment per PPO iteration (dt =0 .05 s, i.e. 1.2 s of simulated experience per environment per iteration). The initial policy is trained for 10,000 iterations ( ≈10 9 state–action transitions in total) with the terrain curriculum described in Section 2.1; convergence is observed after approximately 6,000 iterations, at which point the curriculum has plateaued at Level 6 and the mean reward fluctuates within a narrow band. On flat terrain, the gait is highly regular and periodic, so a large fraction of the parent policy can be offloaded to the PEJs—echoing the principle underlying passive dynamic walkers [13]. On difficult terrain, the gait becomes less periodic and more reactive, so the policy must retain sufficient actuation margin, leaving less room for passive assistance. For both the baseline and co-design branches, αCoT is set independently for each target terrain level. Starting from the pre-trained checkpoint, αCoT is progressively increased and the policy is continued for 5,000 iterations at each candidate value, with commanded velocities sampled from 0.5–2.0 m/s. The acceptance criterion is that the RMS velocity tracking error (Fig. 4) remains close to that of the pre-trained policy, which achieves approximately 0.2 m/s across terrain levels. The co-design branch, assisted by PEJs, tolerates substantially larger CoT weights before tracking degrades, yielding αCoT = 2 .0 for Level 0, 1.0 for Levels 2–3, 0.5 for Level 4, and 0.2 for Level 6. The baseline branch, lacking PEJ assistance, reaches its tracking-error limit at lower CoT weights: αCoT = 0 .4 for Levels 0–3 and 0.3 for Levels 4 and 6.

# 3 Energy-Efficient Quadruped via PIL 

3.1 Terrain-Specific Power Reduction 

We first illustrate the power distribution for the co-design policies. Fig. 2 decomposes the average positive power of each terrain-specific co-design policy into the active motor contribution (red) and the PEJ contribution (blue) across terrain levels; the dashed curve shows the baseline policy (CoT only, no PEJ) for reference. On flat terrain, the PEJs absorb the majority of the actuation effort (21.0 W out of 24.0 W), leaving only 3.0 W for the motors—substantially below the baseline’s 21.6 W. As terrain difficulty increases, the motor contribution grows while the PEJ contribution shrinks, but passive assistance remains meaningful even at Level 6. Notably, the co-design policy’s total power (solid line) exceeds the baseline (dashed line) at all terrain levels, indicating that the co-design gait has adapted to rely on PEJ assistance and would consume more power than the baseline if the PEJs were removed. Fig. 3 quantifies the offload ratio and power saving for each co-design policy at its training terrain level. The offload ratio (Eq. (9)) measures the fraction of the co-design policy’s actuation effort absorbed by the PEJs. The power saving (Eq. (10)) measures the net energy benefit of the full PIL pipeline relative to a baseline that also uses an energy-efficiency reward but lacks PEJ assistance. On flat terrain, the offload ratio reaches 87% and the power saving 86%. This is consistent with the observation in Section 2.3 that flat-terrain gaits are highly periodic and therefore well suited to passive imitation. As terrain difficulty increases, both metrics decrease monotonically—the offload ratio falls to 18% and the power saving to 8% at Level 6—reflecting 7Figure 3: Power offload ratio and power saving versus terrain level. The offload ratio compares the co-design policy with and without PEJ; the power saving compares the co-design policy (with PEJ) against the baseline (CoT only, no PEJ). the growing need for reactive, non-periodic actuation that cannot be captured by a single torque–angle curve. The decline in both metrics with terrain difficulty is also reflected in the CoT weights se-lected by the protocol of Section 2.3: αCoT =2 .0 for Level 0 versus 0.2 for Level 6. At higher terrain levels, the policy requires greater actuation margin, limiting how aggressively the train-ing objective can favour energy efficiency. Fig. 4 reports the velocity tracking error for each condition, confirming that locomotion quality is maintained close to that of the pre-trained policy at all terrain levels. Fig. 5 presents the distilled PEJ characteristic curves for all four joint groups (columns: front thigh, rear thigh, front calf, rear calf). Joint angles follow the convention of the official IsaacLab Go2 configuration, in which zero corresponds to the fully extended leg posture defined in the URDF; the thigh joints therefore operate in positive angles and the calf joints in negative angles during locomotion. For readability, the calf joint angle axis has been inverted so that all curves are displayed with positive angles. Two trends are visible from Fig. 5. First, the samples cluster more tightly around the PEJ curve under co-design than under the baseline, and more tightly on Level 0 than on Level 6 (Fig. 5 (a)–(d)), indicating that co-designing on easier terrain produces the most periodic gaits and the highest proportion of actuation offloaded to the PEJ—consistent with the declining offload ratio in Fig. 3. Second, at Level 6 the sample scatter widens considerably for both policies, reflecting the diverse joint action required for difficult terrains. The co-design PEJ curves from all five terrain levels in Fig. 5 (e) shows broadly similar shapes, with the primary differences being a wider angular range at more difficult terrains.

# 4 Discussions & Conclusion 

We introduce Physical Imitation Learning (PIL), a computationally-efficient, generalisable brain-body co-design framework where the robot body’s passive dynamic response is designed to imitate active control by replicating a distilled control policy. Specifically, PIL redistributes the actuation effort of a parent Reinforcement Learning (RL) control policy by decomposing it into a passive and an active component. The passive component is static joint angle–torque curves distilled from the parent policy, which is implemented as Parallel Elastic Joints (PEJs) that operate in analogy to passive exoskeletons. The active component is the residual policy after distillation, which commands the joints’ motors. This allows a significant portion of the actuation effort to be offloaded to the passive PEJs, while still fully recovering the parent policy thanks to the active component. Crucially, distillation of the passive component allows the par-ent policy to be decomposed without jointly searching over body and controller design spaces, 9Figure 5: Distilled PEJ curves (dashed lines) for the four symmetric joint groups (columns), overlaid on motor torque–angle samples (dots) recorded from the parent policy during locomo-tion. (a)(b): co-design and baseline policies on Level 0 with 50,000 samples each. (c)(d): same comparison on Level 6. Tighter sample clustering around the curve indicates a more periodic gait and greater passive offloading. (e): co-design PEJ curves overlaid for Levels 0, 2, 3, 4, and 6 (darker shading indicates higher difficulty). thereby avoiding the curse of dimensionality that limits co-evolutionary approaches [7, 24]. In simulation, we applied the PIL pipeline to a Unitree Go2 robot, achieving an 87% power offload ratio on flat terrain and 18% on the most difficult terrain traversable by the parent policy, with corresponding power savings of 86% and 8% relative to a baseline without PEJ— while maintaining traversability. The difference in energy savings across terrain levels indicates that difficult terrains require higher actuation margin for reactive balance recovery, while flat terrain admits regular, periodic gaits that can be more readily imitated by the passive PEJs— echoing the principle of passive dynamic walkers. Cross-terrain evaluation demonstrates that terrain-specific policies generalise to lower difficulty levels, though with reduced power offload-ing. More robust cross-terrain performance combining high offloading on flat terrain with robust traversability on difficult terrain is potentially achievable, supported by the broadly similar PEJ responses trained for different terrain levels (Fig. 5(e)). Although the present study is conducted entirely in simulation, the PEJ curves are manufacturable using existing cam-based mechanical strategies [30, 34–40]. Future work will focus on realising robust cross-terrain performance through more advanced training methods, as well as hardware validation on a physical Unitree Go2 robot. The PIL framework can be applied to a wide range of robots with joint-based morphologies, including 10 Figure 6: Cross-terrain power offload ratio. Each curve represents a co-design policy trained on a specific terrain level and evaluated across all terrain levels. Policies trained on easier terrain achieve higher offload ratios but generalise to fewer levels. humanoids and robot arms. We anticipate PIL to enable future robots whose bodies assist specific control tasks, realising Embodied Physical Intelligence without re-inventing control.
