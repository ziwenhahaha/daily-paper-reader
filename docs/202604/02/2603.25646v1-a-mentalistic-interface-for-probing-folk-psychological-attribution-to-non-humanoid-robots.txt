# A Mentalistic Interface for Probing Folk-Psychological Attribution to Non-Humanoid Robots 

Giulio Pisaneschi 1, Pierpaolo Serio 2, Estelle Gerbier 3, Andrea Dan Ryals 2,Lorenzo Pollini 2, Mario G. C. A. Cimino 2

Abstract — This paper presents an experimental platform for studying intentional-state attribution toward a non-humanoid robot. The system combines a simulated robot, realistic task environments, and large language model-based explanatory layers that can express the same behavior in mentalistic, teleological, or mechanistic terms. By holding behavior constant while varying the explanatory frame, the platform provides a controlled way to investigate how language and framing shape the adoption of the intentional stance in robotics. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Humans explain behavior by telling stories about beliefs, desires, and intentions [1], [2]. That everyday mode of explanation — the intentional stance — is how people make sense of other minds, but in robotics it is rarely treated as something that can be deliberately varied and studied in a controlled way. Research either relies on humanoid form factors that invite mind-reading by appearance, or on mech-anistic explanation layers that foreground internal states and control information rather than folk-psychological language [3]. This paper proposes a platform that flips that script: rather than asking whether people will anthropomorphize a robot, it deliberately addresses them in mentalistic terms and provides a way to study how readily those terms are taken as explanations for behavior. The central idea is to build an agent that communicates about itself using beliefs, desires, and intentions, and use that communicative layer as an experimental lever to probe compliance — how readily do people adopt an intentional stance toward a clearly non-human device when it insists on being interpreted that way? Framed this way, intentionality is not simply inferred from a robot’s body shape or other anthropomorphic cues; rather, it is an interactional product shaped by language and social framing. This distinction is important because the intentional stance is narrower than anthropomorphism, refer-ring specifically to the attribution of mental states, whereas anthropomorphism is a broader and methodologically less stable construct in HRI research. Recent work in robotics and HRI therefore suggests the value of separating intentional-stance attribution from human-likeness, and of studying how

Measuring the gap between what the agent says about itself and what observers believe about it lets us separate rhetorical persuasiveness from genuine mental-state. Why is this important? First, it reframes interpretability as a social-psychological phenomenon, not merely a technical one: the form of explanation matters, and language can be an instrument that manufactures agency. As recently pointed out in the literature about Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) [7], the fruitful adoption of the intentional stance towards AI based agents is likely to be a high-level measure of interpretability. Second, it opens tractable questions for psy-chologists and HRI researchers: under what circumstances do people treat a verbal report as evidence of a mind, or at least as a convenient story? Which linguistic frames (agentive, teleological, mechanistic) that correspond to the three stances (intentional, design, physical) invite predictive models of behavior, and which merely increase trust without improving understanding? Third, treating folk-psychological reporting as an independent variable creates a clear path for ethically rigorous experiments that test attribution, persuasion, and responsibility judgments without relying on deceptive em-bodiments. This paper presents the idea, the normative argument for studying intentional stance in non-humanoid robotics as a design choice, and a concrete proposal for a research plat-form and stimuli set that make those experiments possible. In short: rather than searching for minds in the machinery, let the machine offer the mind and see whether people take the offer. This shifts the scientific question from “Does this robot look like a mind?” to “Does this robot speak and behave like a mind?” The aim of this work is to present an experimental platform for testing the attribution of intentional states to a non-humanoid robot. Selecting a non-humanoid system intentionally reduces confounding effects from anthropo-morphism and empathic engagement. Introducing large lan-guage models (LLMs) enables us to elicit folk-psychological explanations of the robot’s behavior while systematically varying the level of mentalistic commitment. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, this combination of controlled embodiment and LLM-mediated explanation is novel in studies of the intentional stance in robotics and provides a rigorous foundation for future research into how explana-tory language, embodiment, and viewpoint shape intentional attributions.

Recent work has examined the broader role of human-centered AI in robotics, mapping current trends in au-tonomous systems and highlighting the importance of bal-ancing performance, reliability, and human control in robotic design [8]. Complementary research has shown that language models can leverage natural-language feedback in embodied settings, using an “inner monologue” to improve planning and interaction in robotic control [9]. Together, these stud-ies suggest that language-mediated interfaces and human-centered design principles are becoming increasingly relevant for robotics, reinforcing the need to examine how explana-tory framing shapes the interpretation of robot behavior. Currently, the investigation into the conditions under which humans adopt the intentional stance toward artificial agents has evolved into a multifaceted field, employing diverse methodologies to distinguish between mechanistic (design) and mentalistic (intentional) interpretations of behavior [3], [10]–[12], with great attention on the antropomorphic cues of the robotic platform. Humanoid research, primarily utilizing the iCub platform, frequently employs the InStance Test (IST) to measure how pictorial scenarios of daily activities elicit the tendency to attribute mental states [3], [12]–[14]. These studies use socially embodied stimuli to explore how shared experiences, such as watching videos alongside a robot reacting with human-like emotions or participating in collaborative tasks, can modulate the transition from a mech-anistic design stance to an intentional one [12], [14], [15]. Methodologically, humanoid platforms are also used to in-tegrate the intentional stance with joint attention paradigms, such as gaze cueing, and to analyze the effects of erring behavior—comparing human-like mistakes to mechanical loops—on the perception of intentionality [12], [14], [16]. Beyond physical interaction, recent works utilize linguistic and narrative stimuli, including mentalistic text-vignettes and stop-motion films, to demonstrate how imagined or fictional scenarios can prime individuals to simulate a robot’s experience [17], [18]. Conversely, literature involving non-humanoid platforms typically utilizes abstract robotic arms or virtual 3D characters to isolate the influence of motion qualities from physical appearance [11], [19]. These studies focus on how expressive, self-propelled, or surprising behav-ior can trigger the ascription of intentions even in objects lacking human-like morphology [19]–[21]. Finally, research involving non-embodied computer opponents in competitive games like Rock, Paper, Scissors employs neuroimaging to isolate the neural correlates of the intentional stance, finding that the mere belief in an opponent’s rationality activates the mentalizing network [21], [22]. III. I NTENTIONAL STANCE , T HEORY OF MIND AND FOLK PSYCHOLOGY 

Imagine watching a small, non-humanoid robot pause, pivot on, and head for a charger — and immediately con-structing a causal story about what it believed and wanted. That everyday move from kinematics to mentalistic narrative rests on three interlocking concepts. The intentional stance, as articulated by Daniel C. Dennett [1], [2], is a way of choosing the most useful explanatory model: one adopts a mental-state hypothesis because it offers a concise and effective account of behavior, allowing actions to be pre-dicted in a simple and intuitive way. Theory of mind refers to the cognitive capacity that allows observers to represent and reason about other agents’ mental states — such as beliefs, desires, intentions, perceptions, and emotions — and to use those inferred states to explain and predict behavior, including cases in which another agent holds a false belief [23]. Folk psychology provides the conceptual and causal framework through which behavior is understood, organizing explanations around beliefs, desires, and intentions that lead to action. It also supplies the everyday language used to express and communicate these inferences [24]. The common foundation of these frameworks is the notion of mental states: attributed constructs that observers posit to explain and anticipate a system’s behavior. Such states are characterized not by their material implementation but by the role they play in the eye of the observer and by the content they encode [2]. Because they are defined functionally, men-tal states are multiply realizable across substrates, from bio-logical organisms to software architectures. Their legitimacy does not rest on privileged access to internal mechanisms, but on their explanatory and predictive utility: an ascription earns its place when it coherently captures observable regularities and enables reliable forecasts of future behavior [25], [26]. Departing from this definition of mental states, one can argue that such a language is shared across different sub-strates and can function as a common medium allowing diverse agents to communicate. From this view, and relying on the work on AGI [7], we propose here a new notion of interpretability for social-robot behavior: a social robot is interpretable when it expresses itself in folk-psychological terms . However, the intentional stance is often confused with other distinct stances [27]: the moral stance, in which at least one interacting system manifests a moral commitment toward the other, and the linguistic stance, which reduces intention-ality to the ability to express mental states in comprehensible language. In this work we provide a platform that has the potential to isolate true intentional ascriptions by moral/antropomorphic ones, and evaluate the role of linguistic storytelling in folk psychological mental narratives. IV. D ESIGNING AN LLM-BASED NON -H UMANOID INTENTIONAL PLATFORM 

The central idea of this work is to design an experimental setup aimed at testing the attribution of intentional states to a simulated, non-humanoid robot. In this setup, the robot is effectively positioned between two linguistic interfaces based on large language models (LLMs), which render the technical and design-related aspects of the system opaque to the user within a folk-psychological frame. One LLM layer is used to issue commands to the system, while another provides reports about the robot’s current state and its planned actions. The responses generated by the LLMs can be modulated so that the reports are expressed using mentalistic, teleological, or mechanistic terminology, which correspond to the three stances, physical, design and intentional. This setup allows the linguistic stance to be disentangled from the attribu-tion of intentionality. In practice, each dataset item may consist either of a bare video sequence showing the robot performing a task or of the same sequence accompanied by different LLM-generated self-explanations produced in the various explanatory styles. The simulated robot operates within realistic environments (e.g. a flat, a bookshop) and carries out tasks assigned by the designer. A video of the simulation is recorded from two viewpoints — the plan view and the front view — to account for a possible advantage of a personal point of view in attributing intentionality. Each task video is linked to the entire chat with the user, including the LLM responses delivered in the various explanatory styles. Comparing mental-state attributions made from the bare video with those elicited by the LLM-enhanced versions will help classify those attributions as falling under the intentional, linguistic, or moral stances. V. I MPLEMENTATION 

The proposed platform is designed as a computational architecture that enables a mobile robot to produce and communicate explanations of its behavior in terms of beliefs, desires, and intentions. The system integrates a symbolic rep-resentation of internal state with a language generation layer capable of expressing that state under different explanatory frames. The architecture follows a Belief–Desire–Intention (BDI) reasoning paradigm, not as a claim about the robot possessing mental states, but as an operational structure for organizing internal information in a way that can be linguistically reported to human observers. This is consistent with studies where the robot was not even autonomous, but partially controlled from a remote operator [12], separating the issue of ascribing mental states being agnostic about their real existence by the user’s ontological commitment towards those states [6], [26]. This organization makes it possible to manipulate the form of explanations provided by the robot while keeping the underlying behavior constant.

C. Events, Actions, and Decision Policy 

The evolution of the system is driven by events generated through perception and interaction. Let 

E = [ e1, . . . , e n] (7) denote the sequence of events during an interaction ses-sion, where each event is defined as 

e = ( t, η, ψ ) (8) with timestamp t, type η, and payload ψ. Events capture interaction milestones (e.g., user input, goal completion, or frame changes) and trigger updates to the internal state by modifying beliefs, generating desires, or updating intentions. The robot operates over an action set A = {move , chat }.Navigation actions move the robot toward target coordinates 

(x, y ) ∈ R2, updating its spatial state, while communication actions produce utterances in a message space T .At runtime, actions are selected through a policy 

π : S × E → A , (9) implemented via an LLM conditioned on the current belief state, user input, and active explanatory frame. The selected action is at = π(st, e t), with executable parameters given by θt = f (st, a t).

D. Explanation Frames 

A distinctive feature of the architecture is the ability to express the same internal state under different explanatory frames. Formally, let 

F = {agentive , teleological , mechanistic } (10) denote the set of linguistic frames available to the system. A frame-selection function 

ϕ : S × E → F (11) determines which explanatory perspective is used at a given moment. Under the agentive frame, the robot reports its behavior in terms of beliefs and intentions; under the teleological frame, explanations emphasize goals and pur-poses; under the mechanistic frame, descriptions refer to low-level system operations. In all these frames, the behavioral output of the robot remains constant, while the linguistic interpretation of the behavior varies. 

E. System Infrastructure 

The architecture is implemented within the middleware provided by ROS2 [28], which ensures communication among the different system components. The robot platform is simulated in a Gazebo environment and embodied by a TurtleBot3 Waffle mobile base. Navigation capabilities rely on the Nav2 navigation stack [29], which converts high-level spatial goals into low-level control commands, integrates odometry measurements, and provides continuous feedback about navigation progress during the reasoning cycle. Language generation is performed by a locally deployed large language model based on Llama 3.2 with three billion parameters [30]. The model is accessed through a local API and is responsible for generating both conversational responses and explanatory narratives corresponding to the selected linguistic frame. Running the model locally allows the system to operate without external network dependencies while maintaining predictable latency during interaction. The entire system runs on a workstation equipped with an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4060 GPU and an Intel Core i9-13900H processor. Under these conditions, the model requires approximately 4 GB of VRAM, enabling real-time interaction while the navigation and reasoning components operate concurrently. 

F. Logging and Traceability 

To support reproducibility and experimental analysis, the system records all state transitions in a structured log. Each transition is stored as a tuple 

λ = ( e, B, D, I, a )

that captures the triggering event, the belief base, the active desires, the current intention, and the executed action. This logging mechanism enables the reconstruction of the robot’s reasoning trajectory and allows researchers to analyze how explanatory narratives relate to underlying behavioral decisions. VI. C. Other relevant case studies 

The examples presented in the previous sections con-cerned simple commands and questions. The following two examples have been made to let the reader appreciate the capabilities of the platform when stressed with more complex questions under the intentional agent, one for the bookshop and one for the small house. USER: I am a big fan of Tolkien, can you go where the book of that genre are? ROBOT: I believe I know a location related to Tolkien. I intend to move to the fantasy location, as it seems to be the most fitting destination. I want to understand and respond to you better, so I’ll make sure to be close to you. My plan is to navigate to the coordinates (0.66, -4.39) and face the direction that leads there. 

After reaching the fantasy bookshelf: 

USER: I need to get internet access to post about a wellness book. Go to the most suitable place to it. ROBOT: I believe I know exactly where to find the internet. I’m heading to the internet location at (4.58, -5.64). I want to get there quickly so I can help you post about that wellness book. From this dialogue it is easy to appreciate the capabilities of the platform in interacting with the user in a natural, human-like style, by linking the information about Tolkien to the specific genre of the Author. Moreover, the information was not provided directly in the question, but with an indirect premise. Even more promising is the second part of the chat, where the system proved to be capable of distinguishing between the information about the internet location that is relevant to fulfill the task, and the information about the motivation for the task, which, referring to a wellness book, could be confusing. USER: You look dirty, maybe a quick wash at the sink would benefit you, go there. ROBOT: I notice I need to head over to the sink, it seems like I’m in need of a quick wash to clean myself up. I’ll just move over to the sink and get that taken care of. 

After reaching the sink: 

USER: Go to a random place, your choice. ROBOT: I think I’ll go to the table. It’s at a familiar location, and it might be interesting to see what’s on it. Here the platform proves to be capable of producing natural interaction dialogues, that appear coherent with the semantic context provided by the user without explicitly coding a command. VII. C ONCLUSION 

By reframing interpretability as an interactional, language-mediated phenomenon and delivering a concrete LLM-mediated experimental platform for non-humanoid agents, this work shows that intentional attributions can be treated as manipulable variables rather than inevitable byproducts of form. Building on the intentional stance as articulated by Daniel C. Dennett, the platform systematically varies explanatory frames (mentalistic, teleological, mechanistic), viewpoints, and LLM-generated self-reports to separate rhetorical persuasiveness from genuine belief attribution. The resulting protocol and stimulus set offer a tractable route for rigorous experiments that quantify when and why people adopt folk-psychological explanations, probe the relative influence of language versus embodiment, and inform de-sign choices that properly calibrate trust, responsibility, and interpretability. In short, rather than hunting for minds in the machinery, this paper supplies the tools to test when—and by what linguistic and social means—a machine’s own accounts lead humans to treat it as if it had one.
