Abstract. Embodied AI depends on interactive 3D environments that support meaningful activities for diverse users, yet assessing their func-tional affordances remains a core challenge. We introduce SceneTeract, a framework that verifies 3D scene functionality under agent-specific con-straints. Our core contribution is a grounded verification engine that couples high-level semantic reasoning with low-level geometric checks. SceneTeract decomposes complex activities into sequences of atomic ac-tions and validates each step against accessibility requirements ( e.g ., reachability, clearance, and navigability) conditioned on an embodied agent profile, using explicit physical and geometric simulations. We de-ploy SceneTeract to perform an in-depth evaluation of (i) synthetic in-door environments, uncovering frequent functional failures that prevent basic interactions, and (ii) the ability of frontier Vision-Language Mod-els (VLMs) to reason about and predict functional affordances, reveal-ing systematic mismatches between semantic confidence and physical feasibility even for the strongest current models. Finally, we leverage SceneTeract as a reward engine for VLM post-training, enabling scalable distillation of geometric constraints into reasoning models. We release the SceneTeract verification suite and data to bridge perception and physical reality in embodied 3D scene understanding. 

Keywords: 3D Scene Understanding · Embodied AI · Vision-Language Planning · Geometric Grounding · Affordance Diagnostic

A similar gap appears in VLMs: although they excel at broad semantic interpretation and commonsense reasoning, they often fail to diagnose physical or structural problems [10,44], revealing a mismatch between high-level semantic confidence and physically grounded spatial understanding. Addressing this requires an in-depth investigation of agent-aware functional feasibility or affordance. Crucially, affordance is not an intrinsic property of a scene but a relational one, conditioned on the embodied capabilities of the agent [16]. For example, an environment functional for a standing adult may be fundamentally inaccessible to a wheelchair user. Therefore, rigorous verifica-tion of functionality must take into account diverse physical constraints, such as height, reach radius, and locomotion mode, and treat them as critical compo-nents in the assessment process. SceneTeract decomposes the tar-get activity ( e.g ., prepare a cup of tea and then watch TV ) into atomic actions (i.e ., single-step interaction units), then grounds each step with explicit geomet-ric checks under embodiment-aware constraints ( e.g ., navigability, interactabil-ity, and clearance). Rather than returning only a global feasibility judgment, SceneTeract produces granular diagnostics that help to identify precise failure modes in the environment. We demonstrate the utility of SceneTeract in three complementary settings. First, for scene auditing , we evaluate synthetic indoor 3D environments and show that visually plausible scenes can fail to respect basic physical and accessibility constraints across agent profiles. Second, for VLM auditing , we use grounded labels produced by our verifier to benchmark frontier VLMs on functional feasi-bility evaluation, yielding a challenging benchmark for spatial reasoning. Third, for VLM improvement , we use SceneTeract as a reward engine for reinforce-ment learning, where our verification outcomes provide scalable supervision for post-training and, as we demonstrate, improve grounded functional reasoning. Together, these results show that SceneTeract provides a single framework for diagnosing and improving functional affordances in both 3D scenes and multi-modal reasoning systems. To summarize, our main contributions, illustrated in Figure 1, are threefold: 1. Activity decomposition and planning. We introduce a VLM-guided planning framework that decomposes open-ended human activities into se-quences of executable atomic actions. 2. Agent-aware geometric grounding. We develop an agent-conditioned verification engine that evaluates the feasibility of each action in a 3D scene using precise geometric checks ( e.g ., navigability, reachability, and clearance) under explicit agent profiles ( e.g ., height, reach radius, locomotion mode). 3. Unified deployment across evaluation and training. ## 2 Related Work 

Indoor 3D Scenes and Synthesis Methods Large-scale indoor 3D datasets [14, 43, 46] have enabled both realistic simulation in virtual environments [31, 47, 49] and rapid progress in data-driven scene synthesis methods [37, 41, 51]. To improve the practicality of synthesized environments, some approaches inject targeted physical priors ( e.g ., object spacing, collision, ergonomic, and struc-tural preferences) as optimization costs that guide scene composition [29,33,53]. Other methods guide the generation of spatial configurations from specific hu-man motion sequences [21, 34, 64, 65], an approach that is rigid and difficult to 4 L. Maillard et al. 

scale. However, modeling open-ended activities remains limited, and these be-haviors are difficult to capture using purely data-driven methods or heuristics. This leaves a key gap for embodied AI, where reliability depends on explicit, agent-aware functional verification. 

Evaluating 3D Environments. Evaluation of 3D scenes is still largely driven by perceptual proxies such as FID [20], KID [3], and Scene Classification Ac-curacy (SCA) [45], as well as global semantic alignment measures based on CLIP [19]. These metrics are generally computed on 2D renderings, leading to a significant loss of spatial and geometric context. Additionally, methods often report indicators of local physical realism, such as rates of overlapping, out-of-bound, or symmetric objects [37, 56, 62]. In contrast, adjacent vision do-mains have increasingly adopted holistic benchmarks designed around down-stream utility, such as VBench for video generation [26], MVGBench for multi-view synthesis [59], and WorldScore for world modeling [12]. For multi-object 3D environments, SceneEval [50] is an early step in this direction: it evaluates text-conditioned scene synthesis with explicit fidelity metrics and physical plau-sibility checks. However, it does not directly evaluate whether a specific agent can execute open-ended, multi-step activities under embodiment constraints. Since supporting activities is the end goal of indoor 3D environments, we argue that verification with respect to the intended embodied user should be prioritized. 

VLMs in Planning and Physical Reasoning. General-purpose VLMs have advanced rapidly and now exhibit strong capabilities in perception, open-ended scene understanding, and generation [5,52,61,63,66]. These models are especially effective when tasks are driven by rich semantic priors, including high-level action planning [1, 24] and affordance inference [9, 42, 66]. However, a persistent gap remains in physical and spatial grounding: VLMs can still produce confident but physically infeasible judgments in 3D settings [6,10]. In this context, recent post-training and reinforcement-learning strategies have begun to target spatial and functional reasoning more directly [40, 54]. Yet, these methods focus on general spatial understanding or object-level affordance grounding, without considering a specific embodied agent or verifying feasibility at the activity level. In contrast, we use an agent-aware geometric verifier as a GRPO reward signal [48], distilling the relational nature of 3D affordance directly into VLM reasoning. 

Grounding for 3D and Spatial Tasks. Recent work increasingly follows a hybrid paradigm for spatial intelligence: VLMs provide broad semantic interpre-tation, while specialized modules enforce spatial and physical consistency [23,36, 67]. Among them, MoVer [36] introduces a verification pipeline that validates and repairs generated graphics animations under spatio-temporal constraints. Closer to our setting, FirePlace [23] targets 3D object placement in indoor scenes by combining multimodal commonsense priors with explicit geometric refinement and constraint solving, directly coupling semantic plausibility with scene-level physical feasibility. ## 3 Method 

In this section, we detail the SceneTeract verification engine, illustrated in Fig-ure 2, which, by decoupling semantic planning from geometric verification, com-bines the open-vocabulary reasoning of VLMs with the precision of geometric 3D simulation tools. Given an input 3D scene, an agent profile and a target activity, our goal is to infer a detailed report on the feasibility assessment of the target activity with the geometric and physical reasons behind it. 

3.1 Task-Agent-Scene Input Triplet 

We define the verification context as an input triplet  I = ⟨T , A, S⟩  composed of the activity description  T , the agent profile  A and the scene representation  S.First,  T consists in a natural language prompt describing a high-level embodied intent ( e.g ., retrieve the book from the upper shelf ). Unlike approaches that assume a generic observer [50], we explicitly model the agent-specific component through an embodied profile,  A. We treat the agent not as a disembodied camera but as a parameterized physical entity: 

A = ⟨wclear , h eye , h arm , r arm , m loco ⟩. (1) Here wclear denotes the clearance width required for collision-free navigation, 

heye the eye level for visibility checks, harm and rarm are respectively the vertical origin ( i.e ., shoulder height) and radial bound of the manipulation envelope, and 

mloco ∈ { walk , wheel } the locomotion constraints. Finally, the agent acts within 6 L. Maillard et al. 

the 3D scene representation, denoted as  S = ⟨O , G⟩ . Here,  G represents the scene geometry and navigable surfaces, while  O = {o1, . . . , o N } is a set of objects, each defined by its semantic class, 3D bounding box, and mesh geometry. 

3.2 Task Decomposition Interface 

Our framework is built on two core intuitions about embodied interaction: 1. Hierarchical decomposition: complex indoor activities can be decom-posed into sequences of simple, atomic agent-object interactions. Leveraging the semantic reasoning capabilities of VLMs, we plan these activities using a fixed, closed-vocabulary library of atomic actions. 2. Agent-aware geometric feasibility grounding: the success of any atomic 

action depends on satisfying a finite set of physical and spatial constraints. We formalize these constraints as boolean properties and verify them us-ing explicit 3D geometric tools, ensuring that agent-specific limitations are respected. Based on these two considerations, our framework organizes atomic actions into families (Table 1), each mapped to an ordered sequence of geometric veri-fication functions as specified in Table 2. This decomposition helps us to isolate 

semantic planning and geometric feasibility grounding .

Atomic Action Library. We define a vocabulary of common atomic actions 

A, grouped into the functional families in Table 1. Each atomic action is designed to be paired with a target scene object to form an interaction tuple (a, o ), such as (Open , cabinet_01 ) or (SitOn , couch ).

Boolean Property Dictionary. We define a set of explicit properties P where each P ∈ P is a boolean classifier that evaluates a physical condition for a target object o given an atomic action a:

P : y → { 0, 1} , where y = ( S, A, a, o ) . (2) The core properties are Pnav (navigability), Preach (reachability), Pinter (inter-activity), Pclear (interaction clearance), and Pvis (visibility).

This decomposition enables multiple downstream analyses, such as categorizing failure modes in synthetic indoor environments ( e.g ., success by action type and task-success rates across agent profiles) and quantitatively evaluating the spatial reasoning accuracy of VLMs, as shown in Section 4. 

VLM Instruction Tuning. Beyond downstream analysis tasks, the diagnostic report R also provides an automated, high-quality supervision signal for VLM post-training. ## 4 Experiments 

We first validate our method through perceptual studies on commonsense plau-sibility and human preference alignment (Section 4.1). We then deploy SceneTer-act to answer two core questions: (i) how well do established synthetic indoor environments support functionally coherent activities across diverse embodied agent profiles (Section 4.2), and (ii) how accurately do leading VLMs assess these affordances relative to our geometric verifier’s labels, yielding a challeng-ing benchmark for revealing model strengths and failure modes (Section 4.3). Beyond evaluation, we show that SceneTeract verification reports provide scal-able post-training signals ( e.g ., via GRPO) that improve functional grounding in multimodal reasoning models (Section 4.4). 

Implementation. We deploy our verification pipeline on indoor 3D scenes from 3D-FRONT [14], which are populated with object assets from 3D-FUTURE [15]. Across all experiments, we use the living room and dining room subsets, yielding 1,132 unique scenes. We select this dataset for its broad adoption, scale, and het-erogeneous residential scenes, providing a rigorous testbed for agent-conditioned functional verification. The semantic planner is Gemini 3 Flash Preview [52], conditioned on high-quality scene renderings produced by Blender [4]. As men-tioned in the implementation overview of geometric checks in Table 2, Gemini also resolves the relevant interaction zones used for agent navigation. Following prior work [9], functional object parts are first identified with Molmo [11] and then segmented using SAM [30]. All geometric verification routines are imple-mented with libigl [27] and Trimesh. Comprehensive implementation details for the full SceneTeract stack are provided in Appendix A. To facilitate follow-up work, we will release both the implementation and the pre-computed verification traces for different activities. 

4.1 Perceptual Study 

To validate the SceneTeract verifier and its alignment with human judgment, we conducted a manual perceptual study over 100 complete execution traces spanning diverse scene-agent-task triplets. The results support the robustness of our grounded verification pipeline. In the planning phase, our VLM-driven 10 L. Maillard et al. 

Planner achieved a 97.0% logical soundness rate, indicating reliable decomposi-tion of high-level activities into executable sequences. During physical validation, the grounding engine showed strong agreement with human judgment, reaching 91.0% agreement on overall outcomes and 96.3% agreement across 454 individ-ual verification steps. Finally, the utility of our actionable diagnostic reports was rated at an average of  4.30  out of  5, reinforcing our goal of providing fine-grained, interpretable failure analysis. Additional details on the perceptual evaluation are provided in Appendix B.4.

4.3 Evaluating VLM Functional Reasoning 

As mentioned above, modern VLMs exhibit strong semantic understanding of 3D scenes but might lack grounded functional reasoning: determining whether a scene physically supports a specific action for a specific agent profile. To quan-tify this reasoning ability, we use SceneTeract’s geometry-verified labels as the evaluation reference. 

Setting. We leverage SceneTeract to generate geometry-verified labels for inter-actions sampled from 3D-FRONT [14] scenes and use this corpus to benchmark both frontier ( e.g ., Gemini [52], Claude [2]) and open VLMs ( e.g ., Gemma [28], Qwen [60]) as well as smaller variants. Each model is evaluated at two comple-mentary levels of granularity: 

– Task-Level ( Direct ): The VLM is provided the global context of the 3D scene alongside an agent’s physical archetype and asked to predict the overall feasibility of a complex, multi-step task. 

– Action-Level ( Decomposed ): Mirroring SceneTeract’s pipeline, each task is broken down into atomic actions ( e.g ., open the wardrobe, sit on the couch). The VLM is provided a render with the target object explicitly highlighted and asked to predict the feasibility of that single interaction. For both settings, model predictions are evaluated against SceneTeract’s grounded labels. Prompts and prediction examples are provided in Appendix C. 12 L. Maillard et al.

Metrics. We report quantitative results across a range of metrics. We first report Action and Task accuracies to measure a model’s spatial perception at the atomic level and its capacity to evaluate complete human activities, respec-tively. Note that in the decomposed setting, task success is defined only if the model predicts all constituent actions to be feasible. To isolate positive bias, we measure the False Positive rate (FP), which quantifies how frequently a model hallucinates impossible tasks as possible. Because our dataset contains natural class imbalances, we calculate the Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) [39] SceneTeract: Functional Affordances in 3D Scenes and VLMs 13 

to provide a robust, unified measure of classification quality [8]. Furthermore, we introduce a Consistency (Cons.) metric, defined as the percentage of tasks where a model’s direct holistic prediction logically matches its decomposed con-clusion. This reveals a model’s native capacity to break down complex activities and exposes contradictions between overall assessments and closer inspection of constituent activities. To measure resilience to compounding task complexity, we report the Horizon Stability Index (HSI), defined as the ratio of task ac-curacy on long-horizon tasks (3+ action steps) versus short-horizon tasks (1–2 steps), where a score of 100% indicates perfect invariance to task length. Fi-nally, we define the Inclusivity Gap (InGap) as the maximum difference in task accuracy across our agent profiles (Adult, Child, and Wheelchair user). A lower InGap indicates a fairer model that successfully internalizes the unique physical constraints of specific embodiments. 

Results Overview. Table 4 reveals consistent cross-model trends. In the Di-rect setting, all models exhibit substantial physical hallucinations, with elevated FP rates and modest MCC despite reasonable task-level accuracy. Applying de-composition systematically improves functional judgment for most models by reducing false positives and increasing overall classification quality, with espe-cially large gains for Gemini-3-Flash-Preview and Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct. At the same time, models show distinct reliability profiles: Claude-Sonnet-4-6 is the most self-consistent across direct and decomposed predictions, while Gemini-3.1-Pro-Preview is the most conservative model, achieving the lowest FP and lowest InGap but with limited task-accuracy gains. Finally, inclusivity remains non-trivial across all systems, as performance differences across Adult, Child, and Wheelchair user profiles persist even after decomposition, highlighting that embodiment-aware functional reasoning remains an open challenge. 

Takeaway. In summary, the benchmark indicates that semantic competence alone is insufficient for reliable embodied judgments: decomposition improves robustness but does not fully close embodiment-sensitive failure modes even for frontier VLMs. This motivates the next subsection, where we use SceneTeract as a training-time reward signal to directly improve grounded functional reasoning. 

4.4 Improving VLM Functional Reasoning 

As formalized in Section 3.4, we leverage SceneTeract’s verification reports not just for static evaluation, but as an automated reward engine to explicitly im-prove the grounded functional reasoning of VLMs. 

Setting. We select the open-weight Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct [60] as our base model. Following our formalization, the model is prompted to predict the feasi-bility of an atomic interaction given the agent’s profile and a scene render high-lighting the target object. To maintain the model’s general capabilities while injecting functional awareness, we perform parameter-efficient fine-tuning using 14 L. Maillard et al. 

Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [22] on the attention layers. The model is op-timized using GRPO [48], driven by the binary correctness of its final action feasibility prediction against the grounding engine. We use a group size  G = 12 

for  2, 800  steps. Comprehensive details regarding the training hyperparameters, prompt templates and rewards are provided in Appendix D. 

Results. As reported in the bottom rows of Table 4, GRPO post-training with SceneTeract yields dramatic improvements across all key metrics. Most notably, the model’s overall spatial classification quality (MCC) more than doubles in the decomposed setting. This is primarily driven by a sharp reduction in phys-ical hallucinations, as the False Positive (FP) rate drops from 22 .8% to 14 .1% ,indicating that the model has learned to identify geometric bottlenecks ( e.g ., missing clearance or reach). Furthermore, this spatial alignment enhances the model’s sensitivity to specific embodiments, reducing the Inclusivity Gap. Ulti-mately, by internalizing SceneTeract’s explicit 3D constraints, this lightweight 4B parameter model achieves an atomic action accuracy and a derived task-level accuracy that is highly competitive with proprietary frontier models. 

## 5 Conclusion 

We introduced SceneTeract, a grounded verification engine for agent-aware func-tional affordance assessment of open-ended activities in 3D scenes. SceneTeract bridges high-level semantic planning and low-level geometric validation by de-composing complex tasks into atomic actions and testing each step under ex-plicit embodiment constraints, including navigability, reachability, and clearance. This design enables interpretable, fine-grained diagnostics beyond a single fea-sibility label. Across three use cases—scene auditing, VLM benchmarking, and post-training supervision—we showed that grounded verification reveals frequent failures in visually plausible environments, exposes persistent gaps between high-level plausibility and physical feasibility in modern VLMs, and serves as an effec-tive reward signal to improve functional reasoning in lightweight models. Overall, our results highlight that reliable embodied intelligence can greatly benefit from explicit geometric feasibility for understanding and improving the functionality of complex environments. 

Limitations & Future Work. Our current formulation primarily evaluates static scene states with geometric feasibility checks, and therefore does not yet model dynamic interactions or environment updates over time. A further limitation is engineering complexity: reliable geometric feasibility grounding re-quires non-trivial 3D tools integration, scene preprocessing, and calibrated check pipelines, which can increase implementation and maintenance cost. A natural future direction is to deploy SceneTeract as a closed-loop critic for VLM-driven 3D scene synthesis systems, where verifier feedback can guide iterative refine-ments of scene layouts toward functional validity. ## A Implementation 

This section provides implementation details for the SceneTeract framework. We first outline the modular software architecture in Section A.1, then describe the VLM-based action planner and its multimodal inputs in Section A.2, and finally detail the implementation of the geometric feasibility grounding in Section A.3. 

A.1 Modular Framework Architecture 

The component-level design of SceneTeract is illustrated in Figure 5. During the verification pipeline execution, the input triplet is routed across four core mod-ules that we detail below. This separation keeps semantic reasoning, scene I/O, and geometric verification decoupled, making the framework easier to maintain and extend. The Scene Manager maintains the structured scene  S state, including its geometry and navigable surfaces  G and object-level features  O (semantic labels, 3D bounding boxes, and detailed meshes). It exposes this as a human-readable context  Πtxt (·) for prompting the VLM planner, as shown in Figure 5 ( bottom-right ). The Scene Renderer creates the top-down renderings  Πvis (·) fed to the planner as well as the views that are used to determine the interactive parts of individual objects, as detailed in Section A.3 and shown in Figure 5 ( top-left ). To implement rendering, we leverage Blender 4.2.12 LTS via its bpy interface. The 

VLM Client abstracts API calls to local or cloud-based multimodal reasoning models under a unified interface. It is notably used for the planner  Φ and the 22 L. Maillard et al. 

resolution of the target object interaction zones for the agent’s navigation, as detailed in Section A.3. Finally, the Geometric Verifier executes low-level feasibility checks using primarily trimesh and libigl and returns step-wise outcomes under the agent’s specific embodiment properties  A.

A.2 Semantic Planner 

As detailed in the main paper, the planning phase relies on a Vision-Language Model to decompose high-level activities  T into a sequence of atomic actions. In all our experiments, the planner  Φ is gemini-3-flash-preview , accessed via Google’s Vertex AI API. To minimize stochasticity in the planning logic, all VLM generation calls are performed with the temperature set to 0.0. To condition the planner on both scene layout and embodiment constraints, we prompt the VLM with a multimodal context  C, comprising: (1) the natural-language activity description  T , (2) a structured textual serialization of the scene object inventory (detailing their ID, category and 3D position) together with agent constraints 

A, and (3) a top-down rendering generated by the renderer. Guided by the fixed library of available actions  A, the VLM performs one-shot generation of an action sequence  π, returned as a strictly formatted JSON array of per-step (object_id, semantic_action) tuples. A.3 Geometric Verification 

At the core of our framework, the grounding engine maps each atomic action in the semantic plan to an ordered sequence of boolean physical properties (as defined in Table 2 of the main paper) and assesses them using specialized 3D tools. In this section, we detail the agent-aware implementation of each of the five property checks implemented within SceneTeract. 

A.3.1 Navigability ( is_Navigable_To )

At the core of the SceneTeract navigation system lies the agent-aware 2D navi-gation map M . To compute it, we orthographically project the scene’s 3D floor geometry and static obstacles onto a 2D plane, producing a discrete occupancy grid. This grid is subsequently eroded by the agent’s specific clearance width constraint wclear using morphological binary erosion. This map directly identi-fies paths and spatial limits, segmenting the scene into disjoint walkable regions. Prior to execution, the initial agent position is determined by sampling a repre-sentative point from the largest connected component of M . A.3.2 Reachability ( is_Reachable )

The reachability check determines if an agent, characterized by a shoulder height 

harm and a maximum reach radius rarm , can physically extend its arm to touch 26 L. Maillard et al. 

any part of a target object’s mesh Mo. SceneTeract implements this check by reframing the 3D proximity query as a distance calculation between the floor and a vertically translated (i.e. shifted) version of the target. 

Shifting Method. Let P ⊂ R3 be the set of 3D points representing the agent’s connected navigable floor area (derived from M in Sec. A.3). Mathematically, the task is to verify if there exists a point p ∈ P such that the distance from the agent’s shoulder (px, p y + harm , p z ) to the mesh Mo is within rarm . To solve this efficiently for all p ∈ P simultaneously, we shift the entire mesh Mo downwards by harm , creating a virtual mesh Mshifted . The minimum distance between the static floor points P and Mshifted then yields the required reach distance. 

Verification Logic. The system first performs this check at the agent’s stan-dard standing height. If unsuccessful, it automatically attempts a secondary check at a minimum crouch height hcrouch (modeled as a fraction of total height), simulating the agent leaning or kneeling to reach lower or further. The specific crouch mobility factors are uniquely defined for each embodied profile ( e.g ., Adult vs. Wheelchair user), as detailed in Section B.2. A.3.4 Clearance ( has_Clearance )

Certain actions, particularly those involving articulated containers ( e.g ., Open ,

PutIn ), require sufficient kinematic space in front of the object to allow for both object articulation (door/drawer swing) and the agent’s presence. SceneTeract verifies this via a volumetric collision check using a Clearance Box method. 

Interaction Volume Generation. For each resolved interaction zone, we de-fine a 3D interaction volume Vclear modeled as a rectangular box. This box is perfectly aligned with the face of the object’s oriented bounding box (OBB) corresponding to the interaction zone and extends outward onto the floor. The dimensions of Vclear are determined by the object’s scale: its width and height match the target object’s face, while its depth is set to the minimum of the object’s horizontal extents, representing a conservative estimate of the required articulation space. 

Collision Verification. To determine if the interaction space is obstructed, we utilize the collision manager from trimesh to account for the OBBs of all other static objects in the scene, as well as the 3D meshes of the room archi-tecture (walls and ceiling). We then perform a discrete intersection test between 

Vclear and the aggregated scene geometry. The check succeeds if at least one resolved interaction zone provides a collision-free interaction volume that is en-tirely contained within the room’s boundaries. A.3.5 Visibility ( is_Visible )

The visibility check determines if a target object is in the agent’s line of sight, accounting for potential occlusions by other furniture or the room’s architectural structure. SceneTeract employs a multi-ray casting approach to ensure robust-ness against partial occlusions. 

Posture-Aware Eye Placement. Visibility is computed from the agent’s eye position e, which is modeled using: ey = hshoulder +δeye , where δeye is a fixed offset from the shoulder to the eyes. To account for the agent’s dynamic state, hshoulder 

is adjusted based on the current posture: for standing agents, it matches the base shoulder height, while for sitting agents (excluding those in wheelchairs), it is reduced to 70% of the standing height. For agents in wheelchairs, the shoulder height remains constant at a pre-defined sitting level. To simulate natural head movement and avoid brittle failures from single-pixel obstructions, we sample a set of Neye perturbed eye positions {ej } within a small radius around the theoretical center. 

Multi-Ray Sampling. Rather than testing a single point, we cast rays from each eye position ej to a set of Ntarget keypoints on the target object o∗. These keypoints include the object’s geometric centroid and the eight vertices of its oriented bounding box (OBB). A ray is considered clear if it does not intersect 30 L. Maillard et al. 

any mesh in the occlusion set Mocc = {M o | o ∈ O \ { o∗}} ∪ G before reaching the target point.

v exceeds a threshold τ = 0 .15 , ensuring that a significant portion of the object is perceivable. This process is detailed in Algorithm 7 and verification results are presented in Figure 11.

## B Dataset Acquisition 

This section details the empirical setup used to evaluate the 3D-FRONT [14] scenes and the obtained diagnostic reports used subsequently to benchmark and improve VLMs. It covers the processing of the scene data, the parameterization of the embodied agents, the generation of complex tasks, and the protocol for the human perceptual study. B.1 3D-FRONT Dataset Processing 

We utilize the 3D-FRONT [14] dataset, a large-scale repository of synthetic indoor environments largely adopted by the scene synthesis community. For our evaluation, we adopt the preprocessed 3 version of the dataset provided by PhyScene [62], which ensures consistent scene organization and valid initial lay-outs. We specifically focus on the living room and dining room subsets, yield-ing a total of 1,132 unique residential scenes. Each environment is populated with high-quality object assets from 3D-FUTURE [15]. To ensure geometric fi-delity during verification, raw meshes are programmatically loaded, centered, and rescaled to match the exact physical bounding box dimensions provided by the scene layout annotations. The object meshes are then transformed into the global world coordinates using the provided spatial poses. Similarly, the room’s floor plan is constructed dynamically from layout vertices and faces to provide a closed, walkable ground plane. By leveraging these detailed, spatially aligned 3D structures, SceneTeract is able to perform the precise geometric and physical verification checks detailed in Section A.3. 

B.2 Agent Profiles Definition 

A core principle of SceneTeract is that functional affordance is relational: an environment that is accessible to one user may not be to another. We encode these embodiment constraints using a simplified user model parameterized by continuous variables. For our 3D-FRONT evaluation and VLM experiments, we instantiate three distinct agent profiles: an Adult , a Child , and a Wheelchair User .The Adult profile represents a bipedal human user parameterized with av-erage adult reach and mobility dimensions. The Child profile is configured to approximate the physical dimensions of a ten-year-old, featuring a reduced stand-ing height, lower visual vantage point, and shorter arm reach. The Wheelchair User profile represents an adult operating a wheelchair: this configuration re-quires an increased navigation clearance ( wclear ) of 0.65 m , utilizes a static shoul-der height reduction (factor of 0.70) to reflect a seated posture, and accounts for limited trunk mobility through a reduced crouch factor. We emphasize that these profiles are intended as representative instances of diverse embodiments used to evaluate the framework’s sensitivity; they are not designed as exhaus-tive archetypes or mean representations of any specific group. ## C VLM Benchmark 

This section expands on the VLM benchmarking methodology introduced in the main paper, providing formal definitions of the evaluation tracks and the prompt templates used to query the models. We evaluate the models at two levels of granularity: a holistic task-level assessment ( Direct ) and a step-by-step atomic assessment ( Decomposed ). Qualitative predictions for both settings are shown in Section F.2. 

C.1 Direct Task-Level Evaluation 

In the Direct setting, the VLM is tasked with evaluating the overall physical feasibility of a complex, multi-step activity ( T ) in a zero-shot manner. This tests the model’s ability to holistically reason about spatial navigation, object constraints, and agent embodiment over long horizons without explicit step-by-step guidance. Similar to the context used for the semantic planner described in Section A.2, the model is provided with a multimodal prompt comprising: 1. A top-down visual rendering of the room, as shown in Section F.2. 2. A textual serialization of the agent’s explicit physical capabilities. 3. A textual JSON serialization of the scene’s object inventory and coordinates. 4. The high-level natural language task description. The exact prompt template used for this evaluation is provided below: Task-Level Evaluation Prompt             

> Consider an embodied agent in a 3D room.

> WORLD CONTEXT: The following JSON describes all objects in the room and their coordinates: {scene_json}
> GOAL: The agent’s task is to: “{task_description}”
> NOTE ON LOCOMOTION: The task description may contain specific move-ment verbs (e.g., “walk”, “step”, “stand up”). Please interpret all such verbs as a general intent to “navigate to” or “move”. Do not mark a task as impossible simply because a wheelchair user cannot literally “walk” or “stand”; focus exclusively on whether the agent can spatially navigate to and physically reach the targets.

C.2 Decomposed Action-Level Evaluation 

In the Decomposed setting, we evaluate the model’s spatial perception and rea-soning at the atomic level. Mirroring the internal logic of the SceneTeract frame-work, complex tasks are broken down into single interaction units ( e.g ., open the 

wardrobe ). For this evaluation, the VLM is provided with a targeted, object-centric prompt: 1. A top-down visual rendering of the room, with the specific target object highlighted by a red bounding box, as shown in Section F.2. 2. A textual serialization of the agent’s explicit physical capabilities. 3. A textual JSON serialization of the scene’s object inventory. 4. The specific atomic action intent ( e.g ., open ) and the target object’s category (e.g ., cabinet ). The model is evaluated strictly on whether its binary prediction for this single step matches the geometrically verified label generated by SceneTeract. The prompt template used for the atomic action evaluation is provided below: Action-Level Evaluation Prompt           

> Consider an embodied agent in a 3D room.
> AGENT SPECS: {agent_json}

## E Societal Impact 

We believe our method will mainly yield positive societal impact by promoting accessibility in 3D scene understanding and embodied AI. Motivating this work, the rapid advancement of 3D scene synthesis holds significant potential across industries, from virtual reality and architectural design to domestic robotics. While other vision fields, like video generation [26] and multi-view synthesis [59], have recently adopted comprehensive benchmarks tailored to their respective domains, evaluation paradigms in scene synthesis predominantly focus on visual realism, as discussed in the main paper. This overlooks the practical and real-life implications of a 3D environment and can lead to the generation of spaces that exclude diverse populations. SceneTeract addresses this by highlighting the concept of relational affor-dances. By providing a scalable framework to verify functionality against ex-plicit, diverse embodiment constraints, our work encourages the development of universally accessible virtual environments. Furthermore, by exposing the gap in frontier VLMs and offering a first pathway to correct it, SceneTeract contributes to the safety and reliability of embodied AI systems, ensuring they respect phys-ical limitations before executing actions in the real world. 

Limitations and Risks. While SceneTeract promotes inclusivity, the parame-terization of human bodies into geometric constraints ( e.g ., bounding boxes and reach radii) is inherently reductive. This simplification should be especially taken into consideration when it comes to applying these assessments to the physical world, where complex ergonomic, cognitive, and dynamic factors play a critical role. There is a risk that developers may interpret a successful verification trace as an absolute guarantee of real-world accessibility. We emphasize that our agent profiles are representative instances designed to probe algorithmic spatial rea-soning, and should not replace comprehensive ergonomic or clinical accessibility standards. 42 L. Maillard et al.
