# Veo-Act: How Far Can Frontier Video Models Advance Generalizable Robot Manipulation? 

Zhongru Zhang 1,∗, Chenghan Yang 1,∗, Qingzhou Lu 1,∗, Yanjiang Guo 1,∗,†

Jianke Zhang 1, Yucheng Hu 1, Jianyu Chen 11Tsinghua University ∗Equal Contribution †Project Lead 

Abstract —Video generation models have advanced rapidly and are beginning to show a strong understanding of physical dynamics. In this paper, we investigate how far an advanced video generation model such as Veo-3 can support generalizable robotic manipulation. We first study a zero-shot approach in which Veo-3 predicts future image sequences from current robot observations, while an inverse dynamics model (IDM) recovers the corresponding robot actions. The IDM is trained solely on random-play data, requiring neither human supervision nor expert demonstrations. The key intuition is that, if a video model can generate physically plausible future motions in image space, an IDM can translate those visual trajectories into executable robot actions. We evaluate this “Veo-3+IDM” approach in both simulation and the real world using a high-dimensional dexterous hand. We find that, owing to the strong generalization capability of frontier video models, Veo-3+IDM can consistently generate approximately correct task-level trajectories. However, its low-level control accuracy remains insufficient to solve most tasks reliably. Motivated by this observation, we develop a hierarchical framework, Veo-Act , which uses Veo-3 as a high-level motion planner and a VLA policy as the low-level executor, significantly improving the instruction-following performance of a state-of-the-art vision-language-action policy. Overall, our results suggest that, as video generation models continue to improve, video models can be a valuable component for generalizable robot learning. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Achieving generalizable real-world object manipulation across diverse object geometries and open-ended environ-ments remains a longstanding challenge in robotics. Re-cently, vision-language-action (VLA) models built upon large vision-language models (VLMs) have emerged as a promising paradigm for learning generalist robot policies [27, 3, 26, 16, 45, 44, 46]. Building a powerful VLA model, however, hinges on two critical components: collecting large-scale robotic ma-nipulation datasets and effectively preserving and transferring the rich prior knowledge encoded in pretrained VLMs. Both challenges are non-trivial. Collecting large-scale real-world robotic data requires substantial time, cost, and engineering effort [4]. Meanwhile, transferring knowledge from VLMs to VLA models is also difficult; despite recent attempts to co-train VLA models with web-scale data, much of the gener-alization capability of pretrained VLMs still can not be fully preserved in downstream VLA policies [10, 19, 47]. In parallel, video generation models have advanced rapidly and can now produce highly realistic video frames, demon-strating a strong understanding of physical dynamics[21, 39, 29, 5]. We first investigate what fraction of manipulation tasks can be solved in a zero-shot approach using a video gen-eration model. In an ideal setting, if a video model could perfectly predict future visual observations conditioned on the current observation and task instruction[18], low-level actions could be recovered via flow tracking or an inverse dynamics model, potentially enabling direct task execution with zero demonstrations[24, 30]. Notably, such inverse dynamics or tracking models can be learned from robot play data without any human supervision[40]. Our first finding is that, under zero-demonstration settings, current video models can only solve some simple pick-place style tasks, but remain insuf-ficiently accurate for most manipulation tasks, particularly during contact-rich interactions. Despite this lack of precision for low-level control, we observe that Veo-3 consistently generates approximately correct task-level trajectories and follows instructions faithfully [13]. This behavior arises because the “video pre-diction + inverse dynamics” paradigm preserves the full gen-eralization capabilities of video models by operating directly on their native input-output formats. In contrast, VLM-based VLA policies introduce an additional action modality into pretrained VLMs, which often disrupts and partially degrades the pretrained knowledge. Motivated by this observation, we propose a hierarchical manipulation framework in which Veo-3-generated videos serve as a high-level motion planner, while a VLA policy acts as a low-level event executor. In addition, we train an inverse dynamics model that outputs both low-level actions and an interaction indicator, referred to as an interaction detector. This detector determines when to switch between video-based plan-ning and VLA-based execution. In experiments, the integrated Veo-Act pipeline improves the average success rate of a strong VLA baseline, π0.5[26], from 45% to 80% on a simulated platform and a real-world dexterous-hand environment. > •

We find that Veo-3 can already solve tasks involving simple interactions without demonstrations, but lacks the precision required for most manipulation tasks, especially during low-level physical interactions. Nevertheless, it consistently generates approximately correct trajectories.  

> •

A. Video Generation Model for Policy Learning 

Video generation models have advanced rapidly in recent years. State-of-the-art systems such as Sora [5] and Veo [13], along with recent self-supervised and action-conditioned world models such as V-JEPA 2 [1] and Unified World Models [48], are trained on large-scale video or robotic datasets, enabling the emergence of nontrivial physical priors, including object permanence, motion continuity, and coarse collision dynamics. These capabilities have motivated the use of text-conditioned video prediction to generate visual plans for robotic tasks [12, 11, 24, 14, 6]. To translate visual plans into executable control, inverse dynamics models IDMs map state transitions to actions. Early work in Learning from Observation LfO focused on inferring actions from human demonstration videos [41, 36]. More recent approaches have significantly scaled this paradigm by leveraging large collections of unlabeled video data. For exam-ple, VPT [2] demonstrated that IDMs can be used to annotate Minecraft gameplay videos and train agents via behavioral cloning. Subsequent works, including Vidar [12], AnyPos [40], and TC-IDM [32], adopt more expressive IDM architectures and broader task settings. Despite these advances, the performance of IDM-based pipelines remains fundamentally constrained by the fidelity of the underlying video generation models, particularly during contact-rich interactions [15, 9]. These limitations are further amplified in dexterous manipulation scenarios, where high-dimensional action spaces and complex multi-point contact dynamics demand levels of predictive accuracy and physical consistency that exceed the capabilities of current generative models [22, 7]. Motivated by these observations, our framework employs a multi-head inverse dynamics model trained via self-supervised random play with noise injection. This design enables flexible switching between actions inferred from video predictions and actions produced by learned policies, improving robustness under model imperfections. 

B. Vision-Language-Action Models 

The current landscape of generalist robot learning is largely dominated by Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models [25, 17, 8], including RT-2 [49], OpenVLA [27], and more recent flow-matching-based policies such as π0 [3] and π0.5 [26]. VLA models are typically built upon pretrained vision-language models (VLMs), with the goal of transferring the rich seman-tic and physical knowledge encoded in VLM pretraining to downstream robotic control. However, introducing an explicit action modality into a pretrained VLM often degrades its generalization capability and can lead to catastrophic forgetting. Preserving the knowl-edge acquired during large-scale VLM pretraining remains a central challenge in VLA research. Prior work has explored various strategies to mitigate this issue, including co-training with language data [26] and representing actions as language tokens [19]. Nevertheless, existing studies consistently observe that fully retaining VLM generalization in VLA models is difficult, and that stronger VLM backbones do not necessarily translate into stronger VLA policies. In contrast, the video-prediction-plus-inverse-dynamics paradigm preserves the native input-output interface of pre-trained video models, allowing their generalization capabilities to be retained without introducing a new action modality. III. M ETHOD 

We propose a hierarchical planning and control framework that combines Veo3 as a video generation model, a multi-head inverse dynamics model, and a low-level policy. The key idea is to first synthesize a visually plausible future trajectory in image space, then convert it into an executable action chunk via inverse dynamics for instruction following, and finally execute the chunk step by step while monitoring a learned gating signal to decide when to hand over control to a reactive low-level policy for dexterous interaction. Compared with directly using the IDM, the hierarchical planning framework can switch more flexibly between interaction and instruction following, making it better suited for tasks that involve both complex prompt semantics and dexterous manipulation, as shown in Figure 2. In the following sections, we will describe the modules and inference procedure of the framework in detail. Figure 3 illustrates the overall pipeline and notations.

2) Simultaneous Control: In the simultaneous setting, the planned chunk and the low-level policy are both active dur-ing execution, but they control different action subspaces. 2) Dataset: To train the multi-head IDM, we collect 300k frame-pair samples in simulation. V. C ONCLUSION 

In this work, we presented Veo-Act, a hierarchical frame-work for generalizable robotic manipulation that leverages state-of-the-art foundation video generation models. By em-ploying a multi-head inverse dynamics model as a high-level motion planner and a Vision-Language-Action (VLA) policy as a low-level executor, Veo-Act benefits from the strong generalization capabilities of modern video generation models while retaining the ability to robustly handle object interactions through VLA-based control. Our experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines, particularly in complex scenes with rich semantic prompts, highlighting a practical pathway for leveraging modern video models to improve manipulation policies. 

Limitations and future works. Despite these promising results, the current Veo-Act framework remains sensitive to the fidelity and stability of the underlying video generation model. Even minor variations in robot appearance, camera viewpoint, or scene layout can lead to substantially different video predictions, which in turn undermines the reliability of downstream planning. As a result, inverse dynamics alone is insufficient to robustly solve complete manipulation tasks, particularly during contact-rich interaction phases. Looking forward, we believe that the video planning + inverse dynamics paradigm has the potential to support a broad range of manipulation behaviors with minimal reliance on learned low-level control policies. Realizing this vision will require future video models to exhibit stronger consistency under distribution shifts and substantially improved accuracy in modeling contact-rich physical interactions. C. Pure IDM Results 

To better isolate the role of low-level policy execution, we additionally evaluate a pure IDM variant under the same protocol as the main experiments. In this variant, the system executes only the action sequence inferred from the generated video, without switching to the low-level policy during interac-tion. This comparison helps clarify whether the gains of Veo-Act mainly come from better high-level semantic guidance or from the combination of video guidance and policy-based interaction execution. As shown in Table III, pure IDM can still achieve nontrivial instruction-following success in several settings, especially in relatively simple control scenarios. This suggests that the generated video and IDM together already provide useful semantic guidance for target-directed reaching. However, the overall task success remains consistently low across all set-tings, even when instruction-following success is moderate. This gap indicates that pure IDM often reaches the correct object but fails to complete stable grasping and object transfer. We attribute this behavior primarily to the limited physical fidelity of generated hand-object interaction. In particular, although the video model often captures the coarse intent of approaching the correct target, the generated hand motion during the contact-rich stage frequently contains geometric distortion, unstable contact configuration, or temporally incon-sistent finger motion. These errors are then translated by the IDM into inaccurate control commands, which significantly degrades execution quality during grasping and placement. This result supports the design motivation of Veo-Act. Rather than relying on video-derived actions throughout the whole trajectory, Veo-Act uses video prediction mainly to provide high-level semantic guidance, while delegating the interaction-critical stage to the learned low-level policy. In this way, the system can preserve the semantic advantages of video-based planning while avoiding the brittleness of pure video-conditioned action inference during dexterous contact. D. Failure Analysis 

To better understand the remaining errors, we further ana-lyze the failure cases in the experimental settings. Rather than only reporting aggregate success rates, we categorize failures according to the stage at which the execution breaks down. This analysis helps reveal which component is the dominant bottleneck and clarifies why the hierarchical design of Veo-Act is effective. We divide failures into three categories. Video generation failure refers to cases where the generated video itself does not correctly capture the semantic intent of the instruction, for example by moving toward the wrong object or producing a visually implausible task progression. Guidance failure refers to cases where the video semantics are broadly correct, but the inferred IDM actions still fail during the navigation phase before meaningful interaction begins. Interaction failure refers to cases where the system reaches the target region but fails during contact-rich manipulation, typically due to distorted hand motion, unstable grasp formation, or inaccurate fine-grained control around the object. Table IV summarizes the failure counts for pure IDM and Veo-Act. The most striking pattern is that interaction failure dominates the errors of pure IDM. This is consistent with our qualitative observations: even when the generated video provides correct high-level intent, the contact phase remains highly sensitive to motion artifacts and physically inconsistent hand-object interaction. In contrast, Veo-Act substantially re-duces interaction failures by switching to the low-level policy when interaction becomes critical. G. Pi0.5 Training 

We train Pi0.5 on a expert demonstration dataset, which provides synchronized RGB observations and proprioceptive states together with ground-truth action commands for each timestep. The training split is used to compute all normaliza-tion statistics, and the policy is optimized purely from these offline trajectories. We apply mean-std normalization to both the state and action targets using statistics computed from the training set, and Pi0.5 learns in an absolute action space. For visual inputs, we only use essential preprocessing, including blank camera padding and ImageNet normalization, without additional data augmentation beyond the benchmark defaults.
