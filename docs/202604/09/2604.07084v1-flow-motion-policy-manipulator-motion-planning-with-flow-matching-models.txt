Abstract —Open-loop end-to-end neural motion planners have recently been proposed to improve motion planning for robotic manipulators. These methods enable planning directly from sen-sor observations without relying on a privileged collision checker during planning. However, many existing methods generate only a single path for a given workspace across different runs, and do not leverage their open-loop structure for inference-time optimization. To address this limitation, we introduce Flow Mo-tion Policy, an open-loop, end-to-end neural motion planner for robotic manipulators that leverages the stochastic generative for-mulation of flow matching methods to capture the inherent multi-modality of planning datasets. By modeling a distribution over feasible paths, Flow Motion Policy enables efficient inference-time best-of-N sampling. The method generates multiple end-to-end candidate paths, evaluates their collision status after planning, and executes the first collision-free solution. I. I NTRODUCTION 

Motion planning plays an important role in robotic manipu-lation systems, operating alongside high-level control policies to ensure safe and feasible actions. The objective is to compute a valid, collision-free path that connects an initial configuration to a desired goal configuration [3], [4]. Achieving fast and reliable motion planning for manipulators in unstructured environments remains a challenging problem. Decades of research have established many algorithms to achieve this, among which probabilistically complete sampling-based [5] and trajectory optimization [6] algorithms have been widely used for robotic manipulators. Sampling-based planners (SBPs) construct a tree in the con-figuration space to connect the start and goal configurations by iteratively generating random samples within the configuration space, steering towards the newly sampled configurations, and collision checking the resulting connections [7], [8]. Due to their stochastic sampling process, SBPs may generate different

plans for the same planning problem across multiple runs. However, these planners rely on a privileged collision checker during planning, and collision checking is the main bottleneck in motion planning, accounting for up to 90% of the compu-tation time [9], which makes parallelism and inference-time optimization challenging [10], [11]. Although recent advanced variants [12], [13] introduce heuristic-based informed samplers to bias the sampling process towards the goal to reduce the number of collision queries, such heuristics are challenging to design for high-dimensional configuration spaces. Trajectory optimization methods [6], [14] iteratively re-fine an initial trajectory, often a straight-line interpolation between the start and goal configurations, using numerical optimization techniques. These methods enforce constraints, including obstacle avoidance and trajectory smoothness, dur-ing optimization. Although trajectory optimization approaches are amenable to parallelization and can benefit from best-of-

N initialization strategies [15], they still require a privileged collision checker to provide both collision status and gradient information throughout the optimization process. Recently, deep learning methods have been leveraged to address the limitations of sampling-based and trajectory op-timization planners. In sampling-based planning, neural net-works have been proposed as neural informed samplers [1], [16]–[18] to bias tree expansion towards the goal, reducing the number of collision checks. Neural networks have also been used as implicit collision checkers [19], [20], enabling faster collision queries. However, these approaches still re-quire collision querying during planning, which limits their efficiency in best-of-N sampling. For trajectory optimization, learning-based methods have been leveraged to generate high-quality initial trajectories [21], [22], which can help to avoid local minima and accelerate convergence. Nevertheless, these methods also depend on collision checkers to provide collision status and gradient information during optimization. Moreover, another line of work leverages deep learning for open-loop, end-to-end motion planning [2], [23], [24], eliminating the need for a privileged collision checker during planning. These methods learn a direct mapping from the planning problem information (e.g., start configuration, goal configuration, and environment observations) to a feasible path. However, most existing approaches are deterministic policies that produce a single path for a given planning problem. As a result, they cannot exploit best-of-N sampling at inference time. We present Flow Motion Policy, an end-to-end neural mo-tion planner that leverages the stochastic generative formula-tion of flow matching frameworks [25] to enable best-of-N sampling during inference, as demonstrated in Figure 1. Flow Motion Policy generates multiple candidate trajectories and applies a light-weight inference-time optimization to select the first collision-free solution for execution. This design allows Flow Motion Policy to operate both as a direct end-to-end planning policy (without planning), and as a planner enhanced by best-of-N sampling at inference time. Our main contributions are:  

> •

We leverage the stochastic generative formulation of flow matching [25] to enable light-weight inference-time opti-mization for end-to-end motion planning. This capability allows best-of-N sampling during inference to output the first collision-free plan among the planned paths.  

> •

We show that the proposed end-to-end planner can be used both as a direct end-to-end planning policy and as a planner enhanced with inference-time best-of-N

sampling for robotic manipulators’ motion planning.  

> •

We evaluate Flow Motion Policy on held-out evaluation tasks, and demonstrate its effectiveness compared to state-of-the-art motion planners. The rest of the paper is organized as follows. We survey related work on motion planning for robotic manipulators, and outline the main differences of the proposed framework and the state of the art. 

Motion Planning with Collision Checking. Over the years, numerous motion planning algorithms have been developed for robotic manipulators [4], with sampling-based [3] and trajectory optimization [6] planners being the most widely used. Sampling-based planners iteratively build a search tree over the robot’s configuration space, stochastically generate samples and expanding the tree towards the planning goal [5]. Due to their stochastic sampling process, these planners are inherently multi-modal, meaning that different runs can produce different feasible trajectories for the same planning problem. This property can, in principle, be exploited for parallel planning and best-of-N sampling. However, planners require a collision checker during planning, which is the dominant computational bottleneck in planning [10], [11]. Hand-crafted informed [12], [13], [27] and neural informed samplers [1], [16]–[18], [28] have been proposed to guide the planning towards goal, reducing the number of collision queries. However, these informed samplers still inherit the sequential nature of sampling-based planning algorithms and require a collision checker in the tree construction process, which is computationally expensive to perform best-of-N

sampling during inference time [16]. In contrast, Flow Mo-tion Policy performs collision checking in parallel to enable efficient best-of-N sampling during inference. Trajectory optimization planners start from an initial tra-jectory, which may be in collision, and enforce planning constraints and utilize numerical optimization to push the initial path to collision-free spaces while satisfying smoothness constraints [6], [14], [29], [30]. However, these planners depend strongly on the quality of the initial trajectory and can often converge to local minima. To mitigate this issue, deep learning methods have recently been leveraged to learn the underlying distribution of planning datasets to warm-start trajectory optimization [21], [22], [31]. End-to-end Neural Motion Planning. Deep learning ap-proaches have also been explored for end-to-end motion planning, where neural networks plan motions in an open-loop manner without relying on privileged collision check-ers [2], [24], [32], [33]. However, most existing approaches are deterministic motion policies and therefore do not support inference-time optimization through best-of-N sampling. Neu-ral MP [23] addresses this limitation by adopting a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) as the policy output representation to facilitate best-of-N sampling during inference. Nevertheless, GMMs have limited capacity to represent the multi-modality inherent in motion planning problems. In contrast, Flow Mo-tion Policy employs flow matching [25] to model this multi-modality and to support best-of-N sampling during inference. 

Diffusion and flow matching models in robotics and mo-tion planning. Diffusion and flow models have emerged as powerful deep generative models in robotics [34]. Diffusion and flow-based behavior cloning can learn complex policies and and have demonstrated strong performance in real-world robotic applications, spanning from task-specific settings [35] to generalist policies [36], [37]. Diffusion models have also been explored for motion planning, including end-to-end ap-proaches [22] and for warm-starting trajectory optimization algorithms [21]. In this work, we employ a flow-based policy as the decoder head of the proposed neural motion planner, enabling best-of-N sampling at inference time [38]. III. F LOW MOTION POLICY

A. Problem Definition 

We consider the motion planning problem for a robotic manipulator with configuration q ∈ Rn, where n denotes the manipulator’s DOF. Given a start configuration qstart , goal configuration qgoal , and an environment observation represen-tation P, the objective is to generate a collision-free path (q = {qstart , q1, · · · , qgoal }) connecting qstart and qgoal . In this section, we present the deployment of Flow Motion Policy and evaluate it against representative sampling-based and neural motion planning methods. All models are imple-mented using PyTorch [41]. Evaluations and experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA RTX 4080 GPU. 

A. Flow Motion Policy Training 

We leverage an LLM-based workspace generator [2] to create a large number of motion planning environments, and leverage cuRobo [15], a fast, optimization-based motion plan-ner, for planning data collection to train Flow Motion Policy. Algorithm 1 details the training process of the policy. 

B. Benchmarks and Planning Metrics 

Benchmarks. We compare the performance of Flow Motion Policy with a set of representative sampling-based and neural motion planning approaches. The benchmarks considered in this study are summarized as follows:  

> •

Bi-Directional RRT (Bi-RRT) [40]: Bi-RRT grows two trees simultaneously from the start and goal in the con-figuration space. The trees attempt to connect to each other during the planning process to find a feasible path between the start and goal configurations is obtained. We use the Open Motion Planning Library (OMPL) [42] implementation of Bi-RRT for evaluation.  

> •

Batch Informed Trees (BIT*) [13]: BIT* incrementally builds an implicit random geometric graph using batches of samples and performs a heuristic-guided search to explore the configuration space. By utilizing an admissi-ble heuristic, BIT* focuses sampling within an informed subset of the configuration space to improve the current solution and reduce the number of samples and collision queries during planning. We utilize the Open Motion Planning Library (OMPL) [42] implementation of BiT* for evaluation.  

> •

Motion Planning Networks (MPNets) [1]: MPNets is a neural informed sampler that produces goal-oriented samples within sampling-based planning frameworks. We implemented MPNets based on the official implemen-tation and modified it to be compatible with the UR5e robotic manipulator used in our experiments.  

> •

Spatial-informed Motion Planning Network (SIMP-Net) [16]: SIMPNet is a kinematics-aware neural in-formed sampler that generates goal-directed samples for motion planning. The framework uses a cross-attention mechanism to condition the sampling network on workspace embeddings, enabling collision-aware and goal-directed sample generation.  

> •

GAIDE [18]: GAIDE is a kinematics- and spatial-aware neural informed sampler that generates goal-directed samples for motion planning. The framework uses a binary attention mask to encode a graph representation of the robot embodiment and planning space into atransformer-based neural sampling network  

> •

PerFACT [2]: PerFACT is an end-to-end neural mo-tion planner that generates trajectories in an open-loop manner. The framework combines a point cloud encoder with a fusion action-chunking transformer architecture to integrate multiple sensing modalities for efficient motion planning.

Best-of-N sampling. To evaluate the effectiveness of best-of-N sampling for inference time optimization, we adopt and implement Neural MP [23]. Neural MP is an end-to-end neural motion planner that plans in an open-loop manner. This framework utilizes a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)-based decoder to learn the temporal dependencies within the motion planning problems, as well as a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) for inference-time best-of-N sampling. We adopt this framework based on the official implementation to be compatible with our UR5e robotic manipulator. As shown in Figure 3, Flow Motion Policy consistently outperforms the adapted Neural MP baseline [23] across all held-out planning tasks. This improvement can be attributed to two key factors. First, the flow matching formulation provides a more expressive generative modeling framework for path generation. Unlike Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), which approximate the path distribution using a fixed number of Gaussian components, flow matching learns a continuous transformation that maps simple noise distributions to com-plex trajectory distributions. This allows the model to better capture the highly multimodal structure of motion planning problems, resulting in a larger number of feasible candidate paths for inference-time best-of-N sampling. Second, Flow Motion Policy is substantially more lightweight than Neural MP [23]. The proposed model contains approximately 1.4M, whereas Neural MP [23] uses approximately 20M parameters, as reported in Table III. This reduction in model size also leads to faster inference and lower computational overhead during path generation. 0

V. C ONCLUSIONS 

In this paper, we introduced Flow Motion Policy, an open-loop, end-to-end motion planner that leverages the stochastic formulation of flow matching to enable efficient inference-time best-of-N path sampling. A transformer-based encoder encodes planning information and conditions a denoising flow and is trained via supervised learning on oracle-generated optimal paths. At inference time, Flow Motion Policy samples a batch of candidate paths and selects the first collision-free solution, resulting in improved planning success while main-taining planning time. Extensive evaluations demonstrate that Flow Motion Policy consistently outperforms both benchmark sampling-based and neural motion planners, with and without inference-time optimization, across held-out planning tasks. However, several limitations remain. The current formu-lation assumes static environments and does not explicitly account for dynamic obstacles. Extending Flow Motion Policy with reactive controllers, such as Riemannian Motion Poli-cies [44] or Geometric Fabrics [45], could enable motion planning in dynamic environments.
