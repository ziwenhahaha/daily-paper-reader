## Abstract 

Recent multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have begun to support Thinking with Images by invoking visual tools such as zooming and cropping during inference. Yet these systems remain brittle in fine-grained visual reasoning because they must decide where to look before they have access to the evidence needed to make that decision correctly. We identify this circular dependency as the Grounding Paradox . To address it, we propose Test-Time Scaling over Perception (TTSP) , a framework that treats per-ception itself as a scalable inference process. TTSP generates mul-tiple exploratory perception traces, filters unreliable traces using entropy-based confidence estimation, distills validated observations into structured knowledge, and iteratively refines subsequent ex-ploration toward unresolved uncertainty. Extensive experiments on high-resolution and general multimodal reasoning benchmarks show that TTSP consistently outperforms strong baselines across backbone sizes, while also exhibiting favorable scalability and token efficiency. Our results suggest that scaling perception at test time is a promising direction for robust multimodal reasoning under perceptual uncertainty. Code is available here .

Despite this promise, however, Thinking with Images has yet to resolve the central challenge of fine-grained visual reasoning [ 33 ]. In practice, tool-augmented MLLMs still frequently inspect irrele-vant regions, overlook critical evidence, or fail to invoke tools even when detailed information is clearly required. These failures are par-ticularly pronounced in tasks involving small text, subtle attribute distinctions, dense symbolic content, or visually cluttered scenes. The issue, therefore, is not merely that current models lack access to high-resolution inputs. Rather, the deeper problem is that they remain unreliable at deciding where perceptual effort should be allocated in the first place. We argue that this limitation stems from a fundamental obstacle, which we term the Grounding Paradox .

Definition 1.1 (Grounding Paradox). A multimodal model invokes a visual tool because its current global perceptual state Sglobal lacks the fine-grained information needed to answer a query 𝑄 . However, producing an effective tool invocation—in particular, selecting the correct region 𝑅 ⊂ 𝐼 

for further inspection—itself requires fine-grained discrim-inative information that is absent from Sglobal . The model must therefore localize evidence that it cannot yet perceive. The significance of this paradox is twofold. First, it reveals that the main bottleneck in tool-augmented visual reasoning is not merely tool availability, but the absence of a mechanism for grounded

perceptual search under uncertainty. Second, it explains why many current systems fail in a systematic rather than incidental manner: when a model does not know what detailed evidence exists or where it is located, tool use degenerates into speculative probing [ 13 , 29 ]. Mislocalized zooming, redundant inspection, and missed evidence are not isolated errors, but symptoms of a deeper circular depen-dency between perception and action. This perspective also highlights a key limitation of existing ap-proaches. Much of the recent progress in tool-augmented MLLMs has come from improving tool-call formatting, learning when to invoke tools, and enhancing grounding performance on in-domain distributions. While useful, such advances should not be conflated with genuine visual reasoning. A model that has learned to imitate tool-use patterns or exploit familiar grounding priors remains fun-damentally different from one that can reason its way toward the right evidence when its initial perception is incomplete, ambiguous, or erroneous. Genuine visual reasoning requires not only acting on what is already grounded, but also recovering from insufficient grounding through deliberate exploration. Recent work on test-time scaling offers a promising foundation for this idea [ 15 , 23 , 40 ]. In language-only domains, increasing inference-time compute by sampling and aggregating multiple rea-soning trajectories has led to substantial gains in robustness and accuracy [ 20 , 32 , 39 ]. These methods rest on a simple but powerful insight: although any individual trajectory may be unreliable, the joint behavior of many trajectories can reveal consistent structure that supports better decisions. This suggests that additional compu-tation at test time might also help address the uncertainty inherent in visual grounding[7, 37]. However, applying test-time scaling to visual reasoning is not straightforward. Existing methods are largely designed for text-based reasoning, where diversity across trajectories typically re-flects alternative chains of logic over a fixed and fully observed input. In contrast, visual reasoning involves uncertainty not only in how to reason, but also in what has been perceived [ 18 , 27 ]. Differ-ent trajectories may inspect different regions, collect different pieces of visual evidence, and even condition subsequent reasoning on incompatible perceptual observations [ 19 , 31 ]. As a result, naively transplanting self-consistency or majority voting into multimodal settings is inadequate: without explicitly modeling the reliability, complementarity, and conflicts of perceptual evidence, aggregating trajectories can simply amplify noise. To address this gap, we propose Test-Time Scaling over Per-ception (TTSP) , a framework that extends test-time scaling from reasoning over language to active search over visual evidence. As is shown in Figure 1, the central idea is to treat perception itself as a scalable inference process. Rather than committing to a sin-gle tool-use trajectory, TTSP allocates test-time computation to generate multiple exploratory perception paths, applies confidence-aware selection to retain reliable observations, and distills these observations into structured intermediate knowledge that guides subsequent rounds of visual exploration. In this way, TTSP trans-forms tool use from one-shot guesswork into an iterative process of perceptual hypothesis testing: independent explorations probe different candidate regions, stable evidence is consolidated, conflict-ing observations are made explicit, and later inference is directed toward resolving the remaining uncertainty. The final prediction therefore emerges not from a single grounded trajectory, but from progressively refined perceptual consensus. Our contributions are summarized as follows: 

• We identify the Grounding Paradox as a fundamental obstacle in tool-augmented visual reasoning, and show that it provides a principled explanation for the characteristic failure modes of current Thinking with Images systems. 

• We introduce TTSP , a principled framework for test-time scaling over perception that enables multimodal models to explore, validate, and iteratively refine visual evidence un-der uncertainty, rather than relying on a single potentially misgrounded trajectory. 

• We empirically demonstrate that TTSP consistently out-performs existing baselines across diverse visual reasoning benchmarks, providing a stronger foundation for advancing the Thinking with Images paradigm. 

## 2 Related Work 2.1 Test-time Scaling 

Recent work on large language models has shown that increas-ing inference-time computation can substantially improve reason-ing performance [ 10 , 14 ]. A major line of research achieves this through test-time scaling , where models generate multiple reason-ing trajectories and aggregate them via self-consistency [ 2 , 20 , 32 ], reranking [ 34 ], verifier-based selection [ 5], or search [ 39 ]. These ap-proaches have been especially successful in language-only settings, where the input is fixed and fully observed, and where diversity across trajectories mainly reflects alternative latent reasoning paths rather than uncertainty in evidence acquisition. More broadly, this line of work has established that model performance is shaped not only by training-time optimization and model scale, but also by how computation is allocated at inference time [24, 25]. However, existing test-time scaling methods are largely designed for textual reasoning and do not directly address the challenges of multimodal perception. In visual reasoning, different trajectories may inspect different image regions, collect different observations, and condition subsequent reasoning on incomplete or conflicting perceptual evidence, making naive aggregation insufficient and potentially noisy. Our work builds on the central insight of test-time scaling while extending it beyond reasoning over fixed inputs to active evidence acquisition over partially observed visual scenes. In this sense, TTSP differs fundamentally from prior approaches by treating perception itself, rather than only downstream reasoning, as the object of scalable inference. 

## 2.2 Thinking with Images 

Multimodal large language models have recently evolved from passive one-shot image understanding toward more interactive forms of visual reasoning, often described as Thinking with Im-ages [ 1 , 13 , 41 , 43 ]. In this paradigm, models are equipped with visual tools such as zooming, cropping, region selection, and high-resolution reinspection, allowing them to actively query image content during inference instead of relying solely on a single global visual representation [ 4 ]. This direction has improved performance Test-time Scaling over Perception 

on tasks that require localized evidence, including small-text recog-nition, chart and document understanding, dense scene interpreta-tion, and fine-grained question answering, and has also inspired related efforts on iterative grounding, external perception modules, and agent-style multimodal tool use [8, 17, 21, 26, 35]. Nevertheless, current approaches often focus on teaching models when and how to invoke tools, improving grounding accuracy on familiar distributions, or imitating successful tool-use behaviors, while remaining brittle when the correct evidence cannot be reliably localized from coarse initial perception [ 6 , 11 , 12 , 38 ]. As a result, models may inspect irrelevant regions, miss critical evidence, or fail to recover from early misgrounded actions. Our work differs from prior Thinking with Images systems by treating such failures not as incidental tool-use errors but as manifestations of a deeper inference problem. We identify the Grounding Paradox as the core obstacle underlying unreliable visual tool use and propose TTSP as a principled framework for multi-trajectory perceptual exploration, confidence-aware evidence selection, and iterative refinement of grounding under uncertainty.

## 3.2 Framework Overview 

TTSP formulates multimodal inference as iterative search over pos-sible perceptions. Rather than relying on a single tool-use trajectory, it allocates test-time computation across 𝑁 successive rounds, each of which improves the model’s understanding of the image through three coupled steps. First, TTSP samples 𝐾 perception traces in par-allel, allowing different trajectories to explore different regions and collect different evidence. Second, it assigns each trace a reliability score based on token-level entropy and removes low-confidence traces before aggregation. Third, it distills the surviving traces into a structured knowledge memory that separates stable observations from unresolved disagreements. This memory is then fed into the next round so that later traces can build on validated evidence while focusing their exploration on remaining uncertainty. After the final round, TTSP predicts the answer by reliability-weighted aggrega-tion over all retained traces across all rounds. The full procedure is summarized in Figure 2 and Algorithm 1.

## 3.3 Parallel Perceptual Exploration

## 3.5 Knowledge Extraction and Iterative Refinement 

The central mechanism through which TTSP converts additional test-time compute into progressively better perception is its struc-tured knowledge memory , an evolving state that summarizes vali-dated evidence from prior rounds and directs future exploration toward unresolved uncertainty. At the end of each round, TTSP distills the filtered traces into a memory with two complementary components: Confirmed Knowledge and Open Conflicts .Confirmed Knowledge contains observations that are consis-tently supported across independent retained traces and verified against the image. These entries are concise, spatially grounded, and treated as established facts in subsequent rounds, thereby re-ducing redundant re-inspection. Open Conflicts record unresolved disagreements across traces. Each conflict specifies the competing claims and, crucially, a spatial directive indicating which region should be examined to resolve the disagreement. In this way, TTSP converts abstract uncertainty into concrete perceptual actions. This two-tier representation reflects an important asymmetry in the value of information: confirmed facts reduce redundant computa-tion, whereas unresolved conflicts identify the observations most likely to change the final prediction. The memory is updated after each non-terminal round using a conservative yet correctable evidence accumulation policy. Pre-viously confirmed facts are carried forward unless they are con-tradicted by the current round’s evidence, in which case they are demoted to open conflicts. Existing conflicts are promoted to con-firmed knowledge once the new round provides sufficient consen-sus. Newly observed facts are added to the confirmed tier only when they satisfy strict criteria, including cross-trace consistency, direct visual support, compatibility with existing confirmed knowl-edge, and relevance to the question. Newly emerging disagreements are recorded as additional open conflicts with associated spatial directives. This conservative update strategy prioritizes precision in the confirmed tier: falsely confirming an incorrect fact would bias every subsequent round, making false positives substantially more harmful than temporary omissions. Knowledge extraction itself is implemented as a single deter-ministic inference call with greedy decoding. The model is given the image, the question, the previous memory state, and a repre-sentative subset of the current round’s filtered traces, and is asked to synthesize them into updated Confirmed Knowledge and Open Conflicts. Unlike stochastic trace generation, this step is not in-tended to generate diverse hypotheses but to faithfully summarize the evidence already collected. For rounds 𝑛 > 1, the memory K𝑛 −1 is injected into the system prompt of guided traces. Confirmed Knowledge is presented as reliable evidence that need not be re-verified, thereby preserving the exploration budget. Open Conflicts, by contrast, are highlighted as priority investigation targets, prompting guided traces to inspect the relevant regions and adjudicate among competing claims. As a result, later rounds progressively shift from undirected search toward focused investigation of residual uncertainty. ## 4 Experiments 4.1 Experimental Setup 

Benchmarks. We evaluate TTSP on both high-resolution and general multimodal reasoning benchmarks in order to assess its effectiveness across diverse visual reasoning regimes. For high-resolution evaluation, we use V ∗ Bench [ 36 ], HR-Bench [ 31 ] at both 4K and 8K resolutions, and TreeBench [ 29 ], which specifically test the ability to perceive fine-grained details and reason over large visual inputs. To complement these benchmarks, we further evaluate on MME-RealWorld-Lite [ 42 ], which measures more gen-eral perception and reasoning capabilities. Taken together, these datasets provide a diverse and challenging testbed for evaluating whether TTSP improves both evidence acquisition and downstream reasoning. 

Baselines. We compare TTSP against a broad range of strong baselines. First, we include four competitive visual reasoning sys-tems: GPT-4o [ 1 ], Thyme [ 41 ], DeepEyes [ 43 ], and Pixel-Reasoner [ 30 ]. Second, we consider representative inference-time methods span-ning multiple paradigms, including self-consistency approaches [ 28 ,32 ], robust reasoning methods [ 9 , 16 ], and iterative refinement methods [ 22 , 44 ]. We also compare against the concurrent method RTWI [ 19 ], which similarly combines Thinking with Images and inference-time scaling. This benchmark suite allows us to evalu-ate TTSP against both strong general-purpose MLLMs and closely related test-time inference strategies. 

Implementation Details. We instantiate TTSP on top of Qwen3-VL [ 3 ] and report main results with two backbones: Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct and Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct. Unless otherwise specified, the fresh-trace ratio 𝛼 is set to 0.4, the filtering ratio 𝜌 is set to 0.4,the number of rounds is 𝑁 = 4, and the number of traces per round is 𝐾 = 8. For single-round baselines, we use 𝐾 = 32 so that the total number of sampled traces matches that of TTSP, enabling a fair comparison under comparable inference budgets. Additional implementation details are provided in the appendix.

## 4.3 Ablation Study 

We conduct an ablation study to assess the contribution of the key components of TTSP. Specifically, we examine three variants obtained by removing one component at a time: w/o RF , which dis-ables reliability filtering ; w/o SK , which removes the structured knowledge memory and the corresponding iterative knowledge-guided refinement; and w/o WA , which replaces weighted aggre-gation with unweighted voting over the retained traces. We also include standard self-consistency (SC) as a reference baseline. Re-sults are reported in Table 3. Several observations follow. First, removing any single compo-nent leads to a degradation relative to the full TTSP model on most benchmarks, showing that the gains arise from the interaction of multiple design choices rather than from a single isolated factor. In particular, removing structured knowledge ( w/o SK ) yields the clear-est overall drop, indicating that iterative evidence distillation and knowledge-guided refinement are central to TTSP’s effectiveness. Without this mechanism, the model loses the ability to accumu-late validated observations across rounds and to focus subsequent exploration on unresolved uncertainty. Second, reliability filtering also contributes consistently. Al-though the w/o RF variant remains stronger than standard self-consistency, it underperforms the full model overall, suggesting that simply sampling multiple perception traces is insufficient un-less unreliable trajectories are filtered before aggregation. This result supports our design choice to explicitly model trace quality under perceptual uncertainty. Third, weighted aggregation provides additional gains over un-weighted voting. The performance gap between w/o WA and the full model shows that retained traces should not contribute equally to the final decision: traces that resolve their critical decision points with higher confidence are more informative and should be assigned greater weight. ## 4.4 Scalability 

Figure 3 evaluates TTSP under increased model capacity and test-time perceptual computation. We study three complementary di-mensions: backbone size, perception width (the number of sampled traces per round), and perception depth (the number of iterative refinement rounds). For single-round baselines, we match the total number of sampled traces to that of TTSP to ensure a fair compari-son under comparable inference budgets. 

Scaling with Model Size. Figure 3(a) shows that TTSP scales fa-vorably with model size. As the backbone becomes stronger, all methods benefit from improved base perception and reasoning ability, yet TTSP maintains a clear advantage throughout. This result suggests that TTSP is complementary to parameter scaling rather than replaced by it. Increasing model capacity alone does not resolve the core difficulty identified by the Grounding Para-dox, namely how to allocate perceptual effort when the relevant evidence is not yet grounded. TTSP continues to provide gains on larger backbones because it improves the search process through which evidence is acquired, filtered, and consolidated at test time. 

Scaling with Perception Width. Figure 3(b) studies scaling along the perception width , i.e., the number of rollouts sampled per itera-tion. As the width increases, TTSP exhibits a clear upward trend and consistently outperforms alternative test-time scaling strategies. This behavior supports our motivation from the Grounding Para-dox: increasing the number of parallel perception traces improves the chance that at least one trace attends to the correct evidence region. Importantly, TTSP makes better use of additional width than standard self-consistency-style approaches, because unreliable traces are filtered out before aggregation and useful evidence is carried forward through structured knowledge rather than being treated as independent one-shot samples. Computational Complexity Analysis. The computational cost of TTSP is dominated by perception-trace generation. With 𝑁 rounds and 𝐾 traces per round, the total number of model forward passes scales as O( 𝑁 · 𝐾 ·𝑇 max ), where 𝑇 max denotes the maximum number of interaction turns per trace. In addition, knowledge extraction introduces only 𝑁 − 1 extra greedy inference calls, one after each non-terminal round, which are negligible relative to the cost of par-allel trace generation. Reliability filtering is lightweight, requiring only score computation, sorting, and thresholding, with per-round complexity O( 𝐾 log 𝐾 ). Since 𝑁 is small in practice, TTSP does not introduce a qualitatively different asymptotic complexity class com-pared with standard multi-sample inference; rather, it reorganizes inference-time compute into a structured process of exploration, filtering, and refinement. 

Token Efficiency. Beyond asymptotic complexity, we also eval-uate practical efficiency by comparing TTSP with the concurrent method RTWI in terms of the total number of generated tokens during inference. For each dataset, we repeat the measurement five times and report the average token consumption. Figure 4 shows that TTSP consistently requires fewer tokens than RTWI across all evaluated settings. This result is particularly notable because TTSP includes additional knowledge extraction steps. Despite this extra overhead, TTSP reduces overall token usage by explicitly con-solidating shared observations into structured knowledge, thereby avoiding redundant re-inspection of the same regions and enabling later traces to focus on unresolved conflicts. In other words, TTSP improves efficiency not by reducing the amount of reasoning per-formed, but by allocating that reasoning more effectively.

## 5 Conclusion 

We identify the Grounding Paradox as a core challenge in tool-augmented multimodal reasoning: a model must localize evidence that it cannot yet perceive. To address this problem, we propose 

Test-Time Scaling over Perception (TTSP) , which scales percep-tion through parallel exploration, reliability-aware filtering, struc-tured knowledge refinement, and weighted aggregation. Experi-ments across diverse benchmarks show that TTSP consistently outperforms strong baselines while maintaining favorable scalabil-ity and efficiency.

## B Analytic Study 

We further analyze how TTSP changes the model’s inference behav-ior across rounds. In particular, we study two indicators of test-time efficiency: the number of tool calls and the length of generated rea-soning. As shown in Figure 6, both quantities decrease steadily over rounds on multiple benchmarks, suggesting that TTSP effec-tively leverages the structured knowledge accumulated from earlier exploration. 

## B.1 Analytic Study on Tool Usage 

The left panel of Figure 6 plots the average number of tool calls per round. Across all evaluated benchmarks, the number of tool calls decreases consistently as the round index increases. This trend indicates that later rounds rely less on repeated visual inspection and instead make more targeted use of the zoom-in tool. Rather than re-examining the image from scratch, the model is able to build on previously extracted knowledge and focus only on unresolved details. This result provides direct evidence that TTSP improves the efficiency of perceptual exploration by reducing redundant tool usage over time. 

## B.2 Analytic Study on Reasoning Length 

The right panel of Figure 6 shows the average chain-of-thought length across rounds. Similar to tool usage, the reasoning length also decreases steadily as TTSP proceeds. This suggests that the model requires less deliberation in later rounds because part of the uncertainty has already been resolved and consolidated into structured knowledge. In other words, later reasoning is not simply shorter, but more informed: the model can condition on reliable intermediate evidence instead of repeatedly reasoning about the same unresolved perceptual ambiguities. Taken together, these trends show that TTSP not only improves final accuracy, but also changes the dynamics of inference in a more efficient direction. As rounds progress, the model uses fewer tool calls and shorter reasoning traces, indicating that structured knowledge helps reduce redundant exploration and enables more focused decision making.

## C.2 Evaluation Prompts 

To ensure reproducibility, we provide additional details on the prompts used in TTSP during evaluation. The prompting protocol follows the three-stage structure of our framework: (1) parallel perceptual exploration , in which the model actively inspects the image through tool use; (2) knowledge extraction , in which the model critically reviews multiple retained traces and distills them into structured knowledge; and (3) iterative context building , in which the extracted knowledge is prepended to the next round to guide subsequent exploration. The exact prompt templates used in our implementation are provided below. 

Parallel perception prompts. In the perceptual exploration stage, the model is instructed to behave as a visual research assistant that answers image-grounded questions by iteratively observing, zooming, and revising its understanding. The prompt explicitly en-courages a loop of: (i) examining the image and identifying what is already visible, (ii) invoking the zoom-in tool to inspect missing or uncertain details, and (iii) reviewing the returned evidence before deciding the next action. To ensure standardized answer extrac-tion, the final response is required to be wrapped in \boxed{} . For multiple-choice tasks, the model is instructed to output only the option letter. The zoom-in tool is exposed as a function call, which takes as input a normalized bounding box, a textual region label, and an image index. This tool interface enables the model to inspect fine-grained local regions while maintaining a consistent calling format across all benchmarks. 

Knowledge extraction prompts. After trace generation and reli-ability filtering, TTSP performs a separate knowledge extraction step. In this stage, the model is prompted as a critical visual knowl-edge auditor . It receives a set of filtered traces from the current round, together with the previous round’s structured knowledge state when available, and is asked to produce an updated two-tier memory consisting of Confirmed Knowledge and Open Conflicts. The prompt explicitly specifies four operations: inheriting previ-ously confirmed facts unless contradicted, resolving previously open conflicts, adding new confirmed facts only when they sat-isfy strict agreement and grounding criteria, and flagging newly emerged conflicts relevant to the question. To improve precision, the prompt emphasizes conservative ev-idence admission. Confirmed Knowledge is restricted to directly observable, question-relevant facts that are consistent across traces and unlikely to be hallucinated. Open Conflicts, in contrast, explic-itly record disagreements together with the corresponding compet-ing claims and spatial directives indicating where the model should inspect in the next round. 

Iterative context building. From the second round onward, the structured knowledge extracted from previous rounds is prepended to the system prompt of guided traces. This context is organized Zheng Jiang et al. 

into the two tiers above. Confirmed Knowledge is presented as reliable evidence that can be used directly without repeated veri-fication, while Open Conflicts are highlighted as the main targets for further investigation. The prompt therefore encourages later rounds to focus their perceptual effort on unresolved uncertainty rather than restarting from scratch. In contrast, fresh traces are generated without access to prior knowledge in order to preserve exploratory diversity. Overall, the prompting design mirrors the core logic of TTSP: multiple trajectories first explore the image independently, reliable evidence is then distilled into structured intermediate knowledge, and subsequent exploration is steered by this knowledge toward unresolved perceptual ambiguity. 

## D Limitations and Future Work 

Despite its effectiveness, TTSP has several limitations. First, it is still a test-time scaling method and therefore incurs additional inference cost compared with single-trajectory decoding, even though our re-sults show that it uses computation more efficiently than competing multi-step baselines. Second, the effectiveness of TTSP still depends on the capabilities of the underlying multimodal backbone and vi-sual tools: if the base model cannot produce sufficiently diverse or partially correct perceptual hypotheses, the benefits of multi-trajectory exploration may be limited. Third, the current structured knowledge mechanism is implemented through prompting and de-terministic decoding, which, while effective in practice, remains a relatively lightweight and heuristic form of memory construction. These limitations suggest several directions for future work. One promising direction is to develop more adaptive allocation strategies for test-time compute, such as dynamically adjusting exploration width, refinement depth, or filtering strength based on the estimated difficulty of each example. Another is to design more explicit and learnable forms of perceptual memory and evidence consolidation, which could improve both robustness and interpretability. Finally, it would be valuable to extend TTSP beyond the current image-based setting to more complex multimodal scenarios, such as multi-image reasoning, video understanding, and embodied visual agents, where perceptual uncertainty and tool-use planning are even more central. You are a critical visual knowledge auditor for an iterative test -time perception system. 

You will receive: 

- The CURRENT ROUND's independent exploration traces (multiple traces for the same image and question) 

- Optionally, the PREVIOUS ROUND's structured knowledge output (confirmed knowledge + open conflicts) 

Your job is to produce an UPDATED knowledge state by performing four operations: 

(A) INHERIT previous confirmed knowledge. 

- Carry forward each previously confirmed fact UNLESS a current -round trace provides direct visual evidence that contradicts it. 

- If contradicted, move it to OPEN CONFLICTS with the new competing claim. 

- Do NOT drop a previously confirmed fact just because current traces don't mention it — they may have taken it for granted. 

(B) RESOLVE previous open conflicts. 

- Check whether current -round traces now agree on a resolution for any previously open conflict. 

- If resolved: promote to CONFIRMED KNOWLEDGE or remove it. 

- If still unresolved or new evidence makes it more complex: keep in OPEN CONFLICTS with updated details. 

(C) ADD new confirmed facts from the current round. 

- Apply the same four checks: Unanimity, Grounding, Plausibility, Relevance. 

1. Unanimity: Every current -round trace that mentions this topic agrees (or no trace contradicts it). 

2. Grounding: Directly visible in the image, not an inference. 

3. Plausibility: Internally consistent, not a likely hallucination. 

4. Relevance: Directly useful for answering the question. 

- All four must pass. 

(D) FLAG new conflicts from the current round. 

- If current -round traces disagree on a NEW point relevant to the question, add it to OPEN CONFLICTS. 

Rules: 

- Be ruthlessly selective for CONFIRMED KNOWLEDGE. It is far better to confirm 2 solid facts than to let 1 wrong fact slip 

through. Wrong facts poison all subsequent rounds. 

- Prioritize concrete, reusable observations that are directly relevant to resolving the question. Every fact you output should bring 

the system closer to the answer. 

- Do NOT include ANY final answer, option selection, or reasoning toward an answer. 

- Do NOT include subjective interpretations (e.g., "the scene looks peaceful") — only concrete visual observations. 

- Do NOT include generic scene descriptions that do not help answer the question. 

- If all traces agree but the claim seems implausible or suspiciously specific, OMIT it from confirmed knowledge and optionally 

flag it as needing verification under OPEN CONFLICTS. 

- If traces simply discuss different aspects of the image without contradiction, those are NOT conflicts. Only report genuine 

disagreements relevant to the question. 

- If there are no conflicts, the OPEN CONFLICTS section should say "None identified."
