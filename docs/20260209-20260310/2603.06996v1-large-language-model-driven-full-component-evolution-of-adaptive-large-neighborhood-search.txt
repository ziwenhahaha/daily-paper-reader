Abstract 

Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) is a prominent metaheuristic and a widely adopted ap-proach for production and logistics optimization. However, it has long relied on hand-crafted components built on expert experience, which makes development slow and costly to adapt to new problems. This paper proposes a closed-loop, large-language-model-driven evolutionary framework that decouples ALNS and automatically rebuilds all of its components. We break ALNS into seven key modules: destroy, repair, operator selection, weight update, initial solution construction, acceptance rule, and destroy-rate control, and evolve each module through a dedicated task. By incorporating the MAP-Elites mechanism, the frame-work maintains a multi-dimensional elite archive to simultaneously drive the evolution of solution quality and strategic diversity. On TSPLIB benchmarks, the evolved algorithms consistently outperform optimized classic ALNS baselines under both fixed-iteration and fixed-time limits. The gains are especially clear on large-scale instances, where the average optimality gap drops from 3.18% to 0.74%. Code analysis also uncovers several counterintuitive yet meaningful design patterns that emerged naturally during evolution, off ering practical and theoretical insights for future ALNS design. Finally, comparisons across multiple lan-guage models highlight clear di ff erences in their ability to support evolutionary algorithm design, helping guide model selection for real-world engineering use.

1. Introduction 

Combinatorial optimization problems are everywhere in key modern industries such as logistics and supply chains, and electronic design automation for chips. How e ffi ciently we can solve these problems directly a ff ects operating cost and response speed. The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) and its variants are classic NP-hard problems and have long been seen as one of the benchmark “touchstones” for testing optimization algorithms (Pop et al. 2024). When facing large-scale engineering instances, metaheuristic algorithms that can produce high-quality satisfactory solutions in polynomial time, have become one of the mainstream approaches for tackling such problems (Blum et al. 2011). Among them, Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) stands out because it can rebuild solution structures through competing op-erators and dynamically schedule strategies through adaptive mechanisms, achieving excellent performance in complex domains such as TSP and VRP (Voigt 2025). However, building traditional ALNS algorithms has long been limited by a “manual design bottleneck.” Implementing a high-performance algorithm depends heavily on domain experts’ prior knowledge and a tedious trial-and-error process. Designers not only need to handcraft operators tailored to a specific problem structure, but also have to carefully tune the weight-update formula, parameters in the acceptance rule, and the logic that controls the level of destruction, among many other details. This experience-driven trial-and-error paradigm leads to long development cycles, adapts poorly to changing environments, and is constrained by the designer’s own perspective, often failing to discover algorithmic logic that is non-intuitive yet computationally more e ff ective. In recent years, large language models (LLMs) such as GPT, DeepSeek, and Grok have made major breakthroughs in code generation and logical reasoning, opening a new path to break through this algorithm design bottleneck. Studies including DeepMind’s FunSearch (Romera-Paredes et al. 2024) and AlphaE-volve (Novikov et al. 2025), as well as NVIDIA’s Eureka (Ma et al. 2023), suggest that LLMs may surpass human baselines in areas like mathematical discovery and strategy design. This signals a broader shift in algorithm design—from “expert-crafted by humans” to “automatically evolved with the help of large models.” Even so, using LLMs to design complex meta-heuristics is still at an early, exploratory stage. Existing work such as ReEvo (Ye et al. 2024). and LMO (Liu et al. 2025) demonstrates that LLMs can generate heuristic operators, but most of these e ff orts focus on local optimization at the “operator level,” while treat-ing the “decision layer” (e.g., weight updates) and the “control layer” (e.g., acceptance criteria) as fixed. 2This kind of “unbalanced evolution” can easily create bottlenecks: even advanced heuristic operators are constrained by traditional decision logic (such as static weights or fixed thresholds), limiting overall perfor-mance due to the weakest link in the algorithmic chain. In ALNS, performance depends not only on how good the operators are, but also on whether the decision and control layers can adaptively match the oper-ators’ search behavior. Therefore, what is urgently needed is a full-pipeline automated design framework that can systematically redesign everything from low-level operators to high-level control logic. To address these challenges, this study uses the TSP as a testbed and proposes an LLM-based, full-component automated evolution framework for ALNS. We break ALNS down into seven key, cooperating parts: destroy operators, repair operators, an operator selector, a weight updater, an initial-solution gen-erator, an acceptance rule, and a destruction-level controller, and use an LLM as the evolution engine to independently evolve and redesign each part. Moreover, we adopt the MAP-Elites mechanism (Cully and Demiris 2018) to guide LLMs to evolve continuously in the direction of Quality-Diversity. We construct an elite “gene pool” with high behavioral diversity by mapping solutions into di ff erent cells based on behavior-characteristic dimensions that reflect di ff erences among components. This allows us to explore and discover high-performing algorithm structures that are di ffi cult to reach through traditional manual design. In addi-tion, to explore di ff erences in “how” di ff erent model architectures think about algorithm design, we choose several mainstream models, GPT, Grok, and DeepSeek, as evolution cores, and compare and analyze their computational characteristics. The main contributions of this work are as follows: 1. A full-component co-evolution framework for ALNS. We move beyond prior work that only evolves local operators, and expand evolution to seven core components across the operator layer, decision layer, and control layer, enabling a systematic reshaping of the overall algorithm architecture. 2. Discovery of non-intuitive but e ffi cient ALNS logic. Through LLM-driven automated evolution, we uncover non-intuitive yet e ff ective component structures and derived formulas for ALNS, challeng-ing common assumptions and o ff ering building blocks for future ALNS development. Moreover, under both a fixed iteration budget and a fixed time budget, the final evolved ALNS significantly outperforms classic ALNS. 3. Fine-grained evaluation and attribution analysis. We design an evaluator based on controlled-variable methodology to quantify the marginal contribution of each component. We also find that the choice of LLM substantially influences algorithmic evolution, revealing a trade-o ff between early-stage e ffi -3ciency and final solution optimality across di ff erent LLM architectures. While some models quickly locate satisfactory optima, others leverage deeper reasoning to eventually uncover superior global so-lutions. This provides a theoretical basis for selecting code-oriented LLMs for scientific computing tasks. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 introduces the related work, Section 3 is the methodology, Section 4 is numerical experiments, and Section 5 conclude. 

2. Related Work 

2.1. Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search 

ALNS was introduced as a systematic extension of the Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) paradigm (Ropke and Pisinger 2006). Its key idea is to embed a competitive pool of operators and an adaptive scheduling mechanism into the classic destroy–repair loop. By adjusting operator selection probabilities based on their past performance, ALNS can automatically learn e ff ective operator combinations during the search. As a result, it has become a widely used general framework for the Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) and many of its variants. From a systems-engineering viewpoint, ALNS is often described as three tightly coupled layers: the search layer, the adaptive layer, and the control layer. The search layer is the execution part of the algorithm. It includes initial solution construction and a set of destroy and repair operators. Typical destroy operators include Shaw Removal (Shaw 1998) and Worst Removal. Shaw Removal removes related customers based on spatio-temporal relatedness, while Worst Removal targets the most expensive elements in the current solution by identifying and removing edges (or assignments) that contribute most to the objective. On the repair side, a well-known example is Regret-k Insertion (Vidal et al. 2014). Instead of inserting greedily, it measures the opportunity cost of delaying an insertion, which helps reduce the short-sightedness of standard greedy strategies. Voigt (2025) provides detailed comparisons of di ff erent destroy and repair operators and summarizes their strengths and weaknesses. The adaptive layer acts as the decision core (Turkeš et al. 2021). It updates operator weights using historical feedback (such as how much the objective improves when an operator is applied) and then uses these weights to guide future operator selection. A common implementation is roulette-wheel selection, which converts weights into probabilities. Beyond the classic design, Yu et al. (2025, 2026) proposed a new adaptive mechanism and reported clear improvements in solution quality through numerical experiments. 4The control layer mainly defines the acceptance rule and how the neighborhood size is adjusted. Aclassic choice is simulated annealing, which accepts worse solutions with a certain probability to balance exploitation and exploration, reducing the risk of getting trapped in local optima. This layer often also includes a destruction degree controller to adjust the perturbation strength (i.e., neighborhood size) over time. Although ALNS has been widely adopted, an important limitation remains: in many existing de-signs, “adaptiveness” is largely restricted to updating operator selection probabilities. Higher-level meta-strategies—such as the analytical form of the weight update rule, the reward design, the cooling schedule (or other parameter decay functions) in the acceptance criterion, and the logic for controlling destruction strength, are still commonly set by hand and hard-coded. This kind of static control can struggle on complex landscapes that are non-convex, multi-modal, and highly instance-dependent, which in turn limits robust-ness and generalization across instances. 

2.2. LLM-driven algorithm self-evolution and meta-optimization 

In recent years, intelligent paradigms for automatically designing algorithms have been developing rapidly. The core idea is to combine the logical reasoning and code-generation abilities of large language models (LLMs) with evolutionary mechanisms inspired by natural selection, along with gradient-based and meta-optimization strategies. This is shifting algorithm design away from experience-based heuristics and manual tuning, toward a workflow where machines can “propose, test, and refine” interpretable algorithms and update rules on their own (Yu and Liu 2025). First, there is an LLM-driven evolutionary paradigm for discovering better algorithms. Representative methods such as FunSearch (Romera-Paredes et al. 2024) and AlphaEvolve (Novikov et al. 2025) typically start with a population of programs that are functionally correct but not highly e ffi cient (or candidates produced by an LLM). They then iterate through a loop of “selection–mutation–evaluation”: 

• Selection ranks programs by metrics such as runtime and resource consumption, and also considers diversity to choose a set of parent programs. 

• Mutation uses an LLM as an “intelligent mutator,” rewriting code and recombining operators while preserving meaning and maintaining syntactic correctness, so the search can explore the algorithm space more e ff ectively. 5• Evaluation relies on automated testing frameworks to verify correctness on standard cases or in high-fidelity simulation, and to measure performance. Programs that pass verification and deliver meaning-ful improvements are added to a program library and reused as material for later rounds of evolution. This paradigm usually produces human-readable programs or algorithm structures, which makes inte-gration into real systems and safety auditing easier. It is therefore well suited to verified algorithm innova-tion in areas such as mathematical computation, system scheduling, and compiler optimization (Fawzi et al. 2022). Second, there is meta-learning-based automatic discovery of reinforcement learning update rules. In-stead of manually fixing TD targets or policy-gradient loss functions, this direction keeps the overall goal—maximizing long-term return—unchanged, but learns “how to learn” by building an update-rule meta-network (Oh et al. 2025, Jackson et al. 2024). The meta-network takes trajectory data collected from interacting with the environment as input, and outputs abstract predictive quantities and their target val-ues. These outputs are then used to define how the agent updates its policy and internal representations. Multiple agents apply the same learned rule across di ff erent tasks, and their long-term returns serve as the outer objective for judging how good the rule is. By optimizing this outer objective via meta-gradients or evolutionary strategies across tasks and agents, the approach ultimately learns a unified update rule with stronger convergence and better generalization across a wide range of environments. 

3. Methodology 

3.1. Overall Evolution Framework 

Traditional algorithm design often depends heavily on personal experience and repeated trial-and-error, which makes development slow and costly. To address this, this study builds an automated evolution frame-work driven by a Large Language Model (LLM). The goal is to automatically generate and iteratively improve the components of an Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) algorithm. The framework follows a structured “generate–evaluate–feedback” loop. It leverages the LLM’s strength in understanding code and reasoning about logic, and lets the LLM act like an “intelligent mutation opera-tor” that rewrites and improves algorithm components. The proposed framework operates as a closed loop with four key modules. The process starts with MAP-Elites, which samples parent candidates using a “quality–diversity” strategy and sends them to a Prompt Sampler. The sampler uses Context Fusion to dynamically assemble a structured evolution task, 6Figure 1: Closed-loop evolution process based on an LLM 

combining the parent code, historical execution feedback, and hard constraints. This prompt is then fed into the central LLM to produce new candidate code. Next, the generated code is placed into an isolated evaluation environment and tested with other components fixed as “sparring partners.” Finally, the test results are written back to the archive, completing one iteration (as shown in Figure 1). At the prompting level, this work does not use typical meta-prompt optimization. Instead, it adopts a “fixed instruction template + dynamic context injection” strategy: system instructions and hard constraints remain unchanged, so the LLM focuses its e ff ort on improving the algorithm logic and code structure. To more rigorously assess the LLM’s ability to develop algorithmic reasoning from scratch, the early evolutionary stage begins from an intentionally weak baseline with only minimal heuristic guidance. The initial solution is generated as a purely random permutation, and the destroy phase removes elements at 7random without any heuristic signal. The destruction degree is drawn uniformly from a predefined per-centage range, while the repair phase uses a greedy insertion rule based on the one-step marginal increase in cost. Weight updates and acceptance follow classic baseline formulations, and operators are chosen via roulette-wheel selection. However, evolving a complete ALNS pipeline end-to-end creates an extremely large search space, which slows convergence. To address this issue, the study introduces a functionally decoupled framework that orthogonally decomposes ALNS into several cohesive core components that can be optimized indepen-dently. This decomposition substantially reduces the di ffi culty of evolution and improves search e ffi ciency (see Figure 2). These components are grouped into three functional layers, which become the basis for independent evolution tasks: 

• Solution Operations: destroy operators, repair operators, and the initial solution generator. These directly act on the solution encoding and perform neighborhood transformations. 

• Adaptive Mechanism: the operator selector and the weight updater. They allocate computation based on historical operator performance, reflecting online learning. 

• Global Control Strategy: the acceptance rule and the destruction degree controller. They shape the convergence pace and exploration range, balancing exploitation and exploration. With this decoupling, a complex algorithm-design problem becomes a set of clear and focused sub-problems. This helps the LLM concentrate on improving one logical function at a time, while also making evolution more e ffi cient under limited computing resources. In the experimental section, the evolved components are integrated and validated through ablation stud-ies to demonstrate the e ff ectiveness of the proposed method. 

3.2. Prompt Design 

In LLM-driven evolution, prompt design directly determines the quality and direction of the generated code. To support seven di ff erent evolution tasks e ffi ciently and consistently, this study designs a general prompt template from three angles: structure rules, dynamic feedback, and heuristic guidance. To limit uncontrolled generation and ensure the produced code is usable, all tasks follow a unified “role–task–constraints” template: 8Figure 2: Full-component evolution pipeline for ALNS 

9• Role: set the role (e.g., “senior operations research and optimization expert”) to activate domain-relevant knowledge. 

• Task: clearly define the component’s function and optimization goals. 

• Hard constraints: enforce strict rules, such as matching the required function signature, using only Python standard libraries and NumPy (no other third-party libraries), and wrapping code blocks with specific comment formats. This standardized interface ensures that each generated component can be directly swapped into the ALNS main framework under consistent APIs. Beyond the static template, a dynamic context injection mechanism is used to strengthen the LLM’s ability to “learn” from past attempts. When building the prompt, the system automatically retrieves the parent code’s historical performance metrics (such as average gap) and injects them into the context. This helps the LLM connect code logic with performance outcomes. In addition, the prompts include three general types of heuristic design thinking tailored to ALNS components, encouraging the LLM to go beyond random changes and perform deeper logical redesign. 

3.3. Building the Evaluation System 

Given limited computing resources and experiment time, this study sets up an e ffi cient evaluation system based on controlled variables, aiming to measure the performance of ALNS components independently and accurately. Because ALNS performance comes from nonlinear interactions among seven components, this work builds a dedicated Isolated Evaluator for each evolution task to remove interference between components. The evaluator uses a fixed-base design: when evolving one target component, all other components are locked to classic textbook implementations (such as random destroy, greedy repair, and simulated anneal-ing). This provides a controlled environment and improves evaluation e ffi ciency. For quantitative scoring, this study builds a multi-objective quality model to guide the LLM toward code that is both high-performing and robust. While di ff erent tasks emphasize di ff erent metrics (e.g., initial solution generation focuses more on speed, while destroy operators focus more on generalization), the scoring generally follows an additive weighted form for most components. To maintain population diversity, the study uses MAP-Elites to build an elite archive by mapping solu-tions into a behavior feature space (Behavior Descriptors). For common components such as destroy /repair operators, acceptance rules, and weight updaters, a unified 2D feature space is used: solution quality vs. so-lution stability. This highlights the key trade-o ff and helps keep strong candidates along the Pareto frontier. For components with more specific requirements, the feature space is extended: Operator Selector: expanded to 4D, adding “diversity contribution” and “e ffi ciency penalty,” so the archive favors strategies that both maintain diversity and remain computationally e ffi cient. Destruction Degree Controller: expanded to 3D by adding “variance of destruction degree,” distinguish-ing di ff erent levels of strategy fluctuation. Initial Solution Generator: expanded to 3D by adding “average execution time” to measure e ffi ciency. This customized design ensures each component is evolved more precisely on its key performance dimensions. 

3.4. Task Definition and Evaluation Protocols 

Building upon the decoupled framework, we define specific evolution tasks and evaluation protocols for each component. To mitigate the coupling e ff ect between operators, we adopt a modular evaluation strategy, particularly for the Solution Operator Layer. Solution Operator Layer: For operators that directly modify the solution structure, we employ an iso-lation strategy to validate their topological e ffi cacy. Specifically, when evolving the Destroy Operator, the repair mechanism is fixed to a deterministic Greedy Repair. This configuration isolates the destroy opera-tor’s ability to identify local optima and dismantle critical structures without relying on the repair operator’s compensation. Conversely, the Repair Operator is evolved within a Random Destroy environment. By generating high-entropy, unbiased partial solutions, we stress-test the repair operator’s capability to recon-struct high-quality routes from chaotic states. The Initial Solution Generator is subject to a static, single-run evaluation, aimed at guiding the LLM to transition from pure randomization to constructive heuristics. Adaptive and Global Control Layers: The evolution of higher-level logic focuses on state awareness and dynamic responsiveness. In the Adaptive Mechanism layer, the Operator Selector is trained to balance the exploration-exploitation trade-o ff by analyzing historical performance, while the Weight Updater is opti-12 mized to formulate e ff ective reward mechanisms based on relative improvements. Finally, the Global Con-trol layer—comprising the Acceptance Criterion and Destruction Degree Controller—is driven by global state variables (e.g., stagnation counters, search progress). These components are evolved to implement adaptive pacing strategies, such as "coarse-to-fine" search intensity regulation, preventing premature con-vergence. 4. Numerical Experiments 

4.1. Experimental Environment and Dataset 

All experiments were run on a high-performance computing platform equipped with a 2.54 GHz AMD EPYC 7Y43 (48-core) CPU and 256 GB of memory, running a 64-bit Windows operating system. The code was implemented in Python 3.13. The evolutionary experiments integrated multiple large language models, including GPT-5.2, Grok-Code, MiniMax-m2, and DeepSeek-v3.2. 13 During the evolution stage, the ALNS framework used the same general parameter setting throughout: the initial temperature was set to 10,000, the cooling rate to 0.9995, the initial operator weight to 1.0, and the weight-update smoothing factor to 0.6. The LLM sampling temperature was set to 0.7. For most components, evolution was carried out using two independent islands in parallel, with an elite archive size of 20 per island. Since evaluating the initial-solution generation task is much faster than other components, its setup was adjusted to four islands, and the elite archive size was increased to 50 per island. Elite migration was performed every 10 iterations with a migration rate of 10%. Parent selection followed a biased strategy: with 70% probability the process favored exploitation (mutation), and with 30% probability it favored exploration (crossover /exploration). To balance the number of generations (500) with limited computational resources, each candidate com-ponent was evaluated within a budget of 1,000 ALNS iterations per generation. This is a practical trade-o ff 

for two reasons. First, evolution requires evaluating a large number of candidate programs, and running too many iterations per evaluation would make the overall runtime impractically long. Second, prior work sug-gests that how a heuristic performs early in the search often reflects its later—and even final—convergence potential (Schede and Tierney 2025). As a result, 1,000 iterations provide a reasonable balance between e ffi -ciency and evaluation reliability, allowing the method to filter out and retain the most promising algorithmic “genes.” We evaluated the evolutionary approach on instances from TSPLIB, and split the benchmark into an

4.2. Comparison Algorithms 

To quantitatively evaluate the automated algorithm design framework proposed in this work, we con-struct two core comparison algorithms: a baseline algorithm (Baseline-ALNS) and an evolved algorithm (Evolved-ALNS). Both algorithms share the exact same ALNS main loop and execution pipeline. There-fore, any performance di ff erence can be attributed to how the core component set is configured and imple-mented (i.e., the operators and their control logic). Baseline-ALNS is a standard ALNS implementation designed by human experts, built from classic components widely used in the literature, see Table 2. It serves as a conventional and strong reference. To avoid an unfair comparison caused by an under-tuned baseline, we tune its key hyperparameters on the evolution set using grid search. The specific values for the key hyperparameters are set as follows: the initial temperature is 9000 with a cooling rate of 0.9991 for the simulated annealing process. Additionally, the reaction factor for score smoothing is set to 0.45, and the destruction ratio is fixed at 0.5.

4.4. Qualitative Analysis and Mechanism Discovery from the Evolved Code 

Based on the final code evolved by GPT-5.2, we observe several mechanism designs that are both eff ective and insightful. For clarity, we summarize representative findings from three perspectives: adaptive mechanisms, global control strategies, and solution operators. For operator selection, the evolved algorithm employs a Momentum-based Bayesian Bandit. Beyond favoring operators with strong current performance, it more aggressively rewards operators whose perfor-mance is improving rapidly, explicitly injecting a “rate-of-improvement” signal into scheduling. In ad-dition, the code forcibly down-weights the three most recently used operators, deliberately breaking the common “keep using what works” heuristic to mitigate short-term winner–takes–all path dependence. The weight-update rule is also intentionally nonstandard: rather than granting positive credit whenever a worse move is accepted by simulated annealing, the evolved policy assigns a negative reward whenever a move worsens the objective, regardless of whether simulated annealing accepts it. To avoid over-penalization, it further introduces a temperature-dependent risk-adjusted feedback—light penalties at high temperature to encourage exploration, and heavier penalties at low temperature to control risk. This design suppresses the early-annealing failure mode where operators inflate their weights by “farming” acceptances via random walks, concentrating high weights on operators that genuinely drive convergence or enable escapes from 21 local optima. In the acceptance criterion, the evolved code introduces a tolerance bu ff er for controlled worsening. Classic simulated annealing penalizes any degradation ∆ from zero and accepts with probability P ∝ e−∆/T ,which quickly becomes conservative as the temperature decreases; consequently, even moderate degrada-tions are rarely accepted and the search can stall in local optima. By contrast, the evolved strategy sets a dynamic threshold ta: degradations with ∆ ≤ ta are treated as background noise and accepted outright; only when ∆ > ta does it apply a probabilistic penalty to the “excess” ∆ − ta, i.e., P ∝ e−(∆−ta)/T . Conceptually, this shifts the penalty onset from 0 to ta, lowering the escape barrier at low temperature: small fluctuations are mostly passed through, while large deteriorations remain strictly constrained, yielding a more robust balance between convergence and structural exploration. Within the repair (insertion) operator, the evolved code modifies the classic Regret Insertion by “hy-bridizing” its scoring. Standard regret is typically defined as Regret = ∆ f2nd − ∆ fbest , capturing the op-portunity loss of not inserting a node at its best position now. The evolved implementation does not rely on regret alone; instead, it blends regret with the current best insertion cost Cost best = ∆ fbest , produc-ing composite scores such as S core = 1.5 · Regret − 0.25 · Cost best or S core = Regret − 0.15 · Cost best .This unifies two priorities in one ranking rule: prioritize “cannot-wait” nodes (high regret) while preferring cheaper insertions (low Cost best ). Mechanistically, regret drives structurally meaningful decisions, whereas cost damps overly disruptive repairs, leading to smoother repair phases and more sustained downstream improvement; when candidates are nearly tied, using Cost best for tie-breaking further improves robustness and reproducibility. 

5. Conclusion 

This paper tackles the long-standing manual design bottleneck of Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) by proposing a closed-loop evolutionary framework that can redesign the full ALNS component set. We decouple ALNS into seven modules (destroy, repair, operator selection, weight update, initial solution construction, acceptance rule, and destruction-degree control) and evolve them under controlled evaluators. MAP-Elites is used to retain high-performing candidates with di ff erent search behaviors, rather than converging to a single narrow design. On TSPLIB instances, the evolved algorithm consistently outperforms the baseline under both fixed-iteration (1,000) and fixed-time (60s) budgets, with improvements becoming more pronounced on larger 22 instances. Under an extended budget of 25,000 iterations, Evolved-GPT not only reduces the optimality gap to 0.745% (a 76.6% improvement) but also cuts runtime by 69.9%. Wilcoxon signed-rank tests confirm statistical significance (typically p <0.001) for all major comparisons. Ablation analysis reveals that the destroy /repair operators and their intensity control are the primary drivers of improvement—removing the evolved destroy operators degrades performance by over 130%, while removing repair operators or the destruction-degree controller leads to drops of about 70%. The acceptance criterion, initial solution generator, and scheduling components contribute smaller but consistent gains. Comparison of four state-of-the-art LLMs shows that the choice of the underlying model a ff ects per-formance: GPT-5.2 yields the most consistent results under fixed iterations, di ff erent models excel under time constraints, and GPT-5.2 together with Grok-Code demonstrate superior long-run convergence under extended budgets. Qualitative analysis uncovers novel mechanisms in the evolved code—momentum-based Bayesian ban-dit for operator selection, an adaptive tolerance threshold in acceptance that eases escape from local optima, and a hybrid regret insertion balancing cost and opportunity loss—all contributing to enhanced e ffi ciency and robustness. Several practical directions for future research follow naturally: 

• Extension to richer constraints: apply the framework to VRP variants (capacities, time windows, multiple depots) with feasibility-aware operators and evaluation. 

• Co-evolution of interacting modules: move from strictly isolated evolution to joint evolution of strongly coupled pairs (destroy–repair, acceptance–destruction control) to better capture synergy. 

• Lower-cost, more reliable evaluation: develop surrogate scoring, early-stopping criteria, and stronger correctness checks to reduce evaluation variance and computation. Overall, the results suggest that full-component, quality-diversity evolution can make ALNS devel-opment more systematic: it yields algorithms that are both more accurate and more time-e ffi cient, and it produces design patterns that can be reused beyond the specific instances seen during evolution.
